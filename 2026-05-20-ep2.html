Blurb

Brandy Mapes and the All of Us program leadership published the operational definition of who the program is trying to enroll and who counts as "underrepresented in biomedical research." The paper combines a scoping review of 645 articles (170 selected as informative) with a benchmark of 15 large U.S. cohort studies on demographic representation. The output is the All of Us Guide for Diversity and Inclusion: ten categories (race/ethnicity, access to care, age, household income, disability, education, gender identity, geography, sex assigned at birth, sexual orientation) with explicit operational definitions for who is "underrepresented" in each. The direction this episode suggests is to build per-ancestry-stratum replication into a CFTR PheWAS protocol from the start, using All of Us as the candidate non-European replication cohort for CF modifier loci discovered in the European-ancestry-dominated CFF registry. The open question is whether All of Us actually contains enough non-European CF cases to be statistically informative.

Plain-English glossary

  • 21st Century Cures Act: 2016 U.S. legislation that authorized and funded the All of Us program.
  • All of Us: NIH precision-medicine cohort targeting ≥1 million U.S. participants with EHR, surveys, and genomics.
  • Case-control study: design contrasting people with vs. without a disease; differs from a cohort study.
  • Cohort study: design that enrolls a group and follows them over time, collecting outcome data prospectively.
  • EHR (electronic health record): the digital clinical record a healthcare system maintains.
  • FPL (federal poverty level): U.S. government income threshold. 200% of FPL is All of Us's underrepresentation line.
  • NIH: National Institutes of Health.
  • PMI (Precision Medicine Initiative): Obama-era umbrella initiative under which All of Us was launched.
  • Phecode: standardized hierarchical disease classification widely used in PheWAS analyses, derived from ICD codes.
  • SGM (sexual and gender minority): umbrella term covering non-heterosexual, non-cisgender identities.
  • Scoping review: literature-review method that maps the breadth of evidence in a field rather than meta-analyzing it.
  • UBR (underrepresented in biomedical research): All of Us's internal classification flag for participants from historically underenrolled groups.

Methods to lift

  • Scoping review framework — search PubMed/MEDLINE/CINAHL plus federal data sources; screen broadly, select narrowly; document inclusion logic.
  • Cohort benchmark with public-source pull — identify comparable cohorts (here >75K enrollment), pull demographic composition from websites/publications, supplement with author contact when needed.
  • Ten-axis underrepresentation taxonomy — explicit operational definitions for who counts as UBR in each of: race/ethnicity, access to care, age (children <18, adults >65), household income (≤200% FPL), disability, educational attainment (<HS), gender identity (non-binary/transgender/variant), geography (rural/non-metro), sex assigned at birth (intersex), sexual orientation (non-straight).
  • Documented data-collection mapping — for each axis, which survey instrument or EHR field captures it; enables downstream researchers to filter or stratify cleanly.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage:

Pre-register a CFTR PheWAS analysis protocol that includes per-ancestry-stratum association tests for all CFTR-modifier loci identified in the CFF cohort, with All of Us as the candidate external replication cohort for non-European-ancestry CF and matched non-CF samples. Specify in the protocol up front: the ancestry strata to be tested, the minimum effective sample size per stratum required for the replication test to be informative, and the effect-size attenuation threshold at which a discovered locus is declared not transferable.

What to verify first

  • All of Us provides phecode or ICD-aligned phenotype codes that line up with CF complication phenotypes in the CFFPR — phecodes are typically the PheWAS lingua franca but other coding systems are in use.
  • All of Us's controlled-access ("Researcher Workbench") tier offers whole-genome or imputed genotype coverage adequate to test specific CFTR modifier loci, not just program-curated polygenic scores.
  • The actual count of CF cases in All of Us is large enough to be informative. Rough back-of-envelope: ~1 million enrolled × ~1/3000 to 1/10000 CF prevalence = ~100–300 CF cases, of whom only a fraction will be non-European ancestry.
  • CFFPR ancestry data is granular enough for per-stratum testing — not just "white / non-white" binarization, but ideally self-reported race + ethnicity + genetic-PC-derived ancestry.
  • The All of Us data-use agreement permits the type of cross-cohort replication analysis being proposed; some agreements restrict comparison against external clinical registries.
  • Sex-dimorphic CF outcomes (e.g., CF-female lung-function disadvantage) are recorded by both sex assigned at birth and gender identity in the CFFPR; if only one is collected, the analysis is constrained.

Gap-map (CFF-cohort PheWAS lens)

  • Closes: the demographic vocabulary for describing cohort composition and arguing about transferability. The Guide table can be cited directly in a methods section.
  • Leaves open: whether All of Us has, as of 2026, actually achieved the demographic targets described in 2020 (requires more recent enrollment data); whether CF-relevant phenotypes are well-coded; whether the per-stratum sample sizes are adequate for modifier-locus replication.

ALEX Today's second paper is Brandy Mapes and colleagues, "Diversity and inclusion for the All of Us research program: A scoping review," published in PLoS ONE in July 2020. Mapes is at Vanderbilt; senior author Joni Rutter is at the National Center for Advancing Translational Sciences at NIH.

JORDAN This is the policy paper that defines who All of Us is trying to enroll. Why does that matter for a CFTR PheWAS?

ALEX Two reasons. First, All of Us is the U.S. equivalent of UK Biobank — soon to be one of the largest research cohorts in U.S. history, designed with electronic health records, genomics, and questionnaires linked together — and we will eventually need to ask whether All of Us data can serve as an external replication or PheWAS reference for CF modifier signals discovered in the CFF cohort. Second, the CF Foundation Patient Registry has its own demographic skew, and the All of Us design document is the cleanest articulation in the literature of why that skew matters and what to do about it.

JORDAN Before we get into the paper, can we define some of the vocabulary?

ALEX Of course. Let me lay it out so nothing later slips by. "All of Us" is the actual program name — a National Institutes of Health initiative aiming to recruit at least one million U.S. participants, give them surveys, link their electronic health records, draw blood, and eventually sequence their genomes. The Precision Medicine Initiative is the older umbrella name for this effort, dating from the Obama administration. The 21st Century Cures Act is the 2016 legislation that authorized and funded it.

JORDAN And acronyms — UBR.

ALEX UBR stands for "underrepresented in biomedical research." It is a defined classification All of Us uses internally to flag participant groups historically excluded from research cohorts. PMI again is Precision Medicine Initiative. EHR is electronic health record — the digital record your doctor's office keeps. FPL is federal poverty level, a U.S. government threshold for household income — 200% of FPL is the cutoff All of Us picked for an underrepresented designation. SGM is sexual and gender minority. And a scoping review — that is a specific kind of literature review where the authors map the breadth of work in a field rather than synthesizing it into a single statistical answer.

JORDAN One more — cohort study. I have a fuzzy picture.

ALEX A cohort study is a research design where you enroll a group of people, collect data about them, and then follow them over time. The opposite is a case-control study, where you take people who already have the disease and people who do not, and compare them. UK Biobank, All of Us, FinnGen, the Million Veteran Program, and the CFF Patient Registry are all cohort studies. The thing that makes them powerful is the longitudinal follow-up — what was a baseline measurement five years ago is now a predictor of an event that happened last week.

JORDAN What problem motivated this paper?

ALEX Two interlocking problems. First, U.S. biomedical research has historically been overwhelmingly enrolled from non-Hispanic white, middle-class, urban populations. That bias shows up in two visible ways. Drugs come to market with safety profiles that turn out to be wrong for groups that were not in the trials — the paper cites that 80 percent of drugs withdrawn from the U.S. market between 1997 and 2000 were withdrawn because of adverse effects in women, who were underrepresented in the original trials. Clopidogrel, the antiplatelet drug, only works in about half of people of Asian ancestry because of a metabolizing-enzyme variant, and that interaction was not characterized at the original approval. Second, the historical lens on "underrepresented" has been narrow — usually only race and ethnicity, sometimes sex. The authors argue that "underrepresented" needs to span a larger axis: age, household income, educational attainment, disability status, geography, sexual orientation, gender identity, and access to medical care. So the paper's purpose is partly empirical — survey what other cohorts have done — and partly normative — propose a framework All of Us should use.

JORDAN How did they do the work?

ALEX Two parallel pieces. First, a scoping review — search the published literature using PubMed, MEDLINE, and CINAHL, plus the U.S. Census, Health and Human Services Office of Minority Health, and the Centers for Disease Control. They screened roughly 645 articles and selected 170 as informative. Second, a benchmarking exercise — they identified 15 U.S.-and-comparable national cohort studies with enrollment over 75,000 each, including UK Biobank, the Million Veteran Program, the Women's Health Initiative, and a number of NIH-funded biorepositories, and tabulated each cohort's representation of the underrepresented categories. Demographic data was pulled from public sources — websites, datasets, publications — supplemented by direct contact with study authors when public records were thin. ALEX pushes back here: this is a methodology where the rigor is in the framing, not in any statistical estimator. There is no power calculation, no effect size, no confidence interval. The conclusions are categorical — "this group is underrepresented" — and depend on the categorical taxonomy the authors chose. That taxonomy choice is itself contestable, and they acknowledge that limitation in the paper.

JORDAN And what did they propose?

ALEX A Guide for Diversity and Inclusion built around ten demographic categories. Race and ethnicity. Access to care, where the line is "had a needed medical visit in the past 12 months." Age — children under 18 and adults over 65 flagged as underrepresented. Annual household income with the line at 200% of federal poverty level. Disability — physical or cognitive. Educational attainment — high school degree as the line. Gender identity — anyone non-binary, transgender, or gender variant flagged. Geography — rural and non-metropolitan flagged. Sex assigned at birth — intersex flagged. Sexual orientation — anyone non-straight flagged. Then for each category they specified how the All of Us survey instrument captures the variable, so that researchers using All of Us data downstream can filter, stratify, or stratify-then-meta-analyze on each of these dimensions.

JORDAN What did they find when they benchmarked the other cohorts?

ALEX Across all 15 benchmark cohorts, underrepresented groups were underrepresented. The specific numbers: annual household income was unknown or not collected for 13 of the 15. Educational attainment data was available for 7 of 15, and across the U.S. cohorts that did report it, less than 15 percent of participants had less than a high school education. Older adults — 65 and up — were confirmed enrolled in only 5 of 15 cohorts. Children under 18 were enrolled in only 2 of 15. Only one cohort reported disability representation, and it came in at 18 percent of the sample. Of the 14 cohorts that reported sex, females were overrepresented in 5 of the 10 mixed-sex cohorts. None of the 15 reported intersex participants. Five of 15 reported geographic region. Zero of 15 reported gender minority representation. Zero reported sexual orientation breakdown. Did they answer their question? Partially, yes — they showed empirically that the existing biomedical cohort landscape neglects the categories they want All of Us to track, and they produced a framework that operationalizes the inclusion they want. Where they cannot answer — they cannot tell you whether their proposed underrepresentation framework actually improves downstream research validity, because that requires comparing results from a diverse cohort against results from a non-diverse cohort, and that comparison has not yet been done.

JORDAN What does this paper suggest for a CFTR PheWAS?

ALEX Here is one specific direction this episode suggests. The CFF Patient Registry is heavily skewed toward European-ancestry participants — roughly 95 percent — because cystic fibrosis itself is dramatically more common in populations with European ancestry, owing to the F508del founder frequency. That ancestry skew is biological, not procedural, but the consequence is the same: any PheWAS modifier signal Runyu finds in the CFF cohort will, by default, have been discovered in a near-monoethnic sample, and its transferability to non-European CF patients is unknown. The proposed direction this episode suggests is: build into the CFTR PheWAS study design from the start a transferability evaluation across ancestry strata, using All of Us as the candidate external replication cohort for the small but non-trivial number of CF patients of non-European ancestry. Formally: a pre-registered analysis protocol that includes per-ancestry subgroup association tests for all modifier loci identified in the CFF cohort, with All of Us providing the non-European CF and matched-non-CF samples needed for replication and effect-size attenuation estimation.

JORDAN What does this paper close, and what does it leave open?

ALEX What it closes — the demographic vocabulary for talking about transferability is now standardized. We can cite the All of Us Guide for Diversity and Inclusion as the operational definition of what categories matter when describing a cohort's composition, instead of inventing our own. It also closes a question I would not have known to ask: namely, that the Guide explicitly distinguishes sex assigned at birth from gender identity, which matters for some CF outcomes that are sex-dimorphic — CF females have historically had worse lung function trajectories than CF males, an effect that has had several proposed mechanisms and no settled answer, and reporting cleanly on sex assigned at birth vs gender identity matters for that analysis. What it leaves open — the paper does not tell us whether All of Us has, in practice, achieved the representation it set out to achieve. That is a separate empirical question requiring current enrollment data, which would be in a more recent program report. What we would need to check before committing to using All of Us as a replication cohort — first, that All of Us provides searchable phecodes or ICD-aligned phenotype codes that line up with CF complication phenotypes; phecodes are the standardized hierarchical disease codes that PheWAS analyses typically use. Second, that the All of Us workbench's controlled-access tier provides whole-genome or imputed genotype data sufficient to test CFTR modifier loci. Third, that the actual sample size of CF cases inside All of Us is large enough to be informative — CF prevalence is about 1 in 3000 to 1 in 10000 depending on ancestry, so a one-million-participant cohort yields on the order of 100 to 300 CF patients, which is informative for major effects but not for subtle modifier ones. Fourth, that the registry-linked CFFPR ancestry data is recorded with enough granularity to allow per-stratum analyses, not just "white versus non-white." None of these have been verified by Runyu personally and none should be assumed before committing.
