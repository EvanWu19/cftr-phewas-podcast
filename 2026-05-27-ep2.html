Blurb

Lian and colleagues apply a transformer-based unsupervised clustering framework to longitudinal electronic health record data from CPRD Aurum and UK Biobank — over 100,000 AD patients and 45,000 PD patients in CPRD, with several thousand of each in UKB for external validation. They identify five reproducible subtypes per disease, characterized by distinct comorbidity patterns, symptom trajectories, 5-year prognosis, and genetic profiles. The headline result: APOE e4 (rs429358-C) is strongly underrepresented in one AD subtype (OR 0.62, Bonferroni p = 1.9e-6), and APOE e2 (rs7412-T) is enriched in the same cluster — defining a genetically protected AD subtype. PD analogues are nominal-only (LRRK2 rs34637584-A in cluster 2, p = 0.046). The proposed direction it suggests: build a CF Patient Registry sequence dataset, run transformer-embedding + clustering on patient trajectories, then test whether subtypes are differentially enriched for CFTR genotype, modifier-locus variants, and PRS in the WGS subset. Open question: whether the method works at CF cohort scale (≤30K patients vs CPRD's >100K) and how to redefine the "prediagnostic window" for a disease diagnosed in infancy.

Plain-English glossary

  • APOE (apolipoprotein E): a gene on chromosome 19 with three common alleles (e2, e3, e4); e4 is the largest known common-variant risk factor for late-onset AD, e2 is protective.
  • Bonferroni correction: a conservative statistical adjustment for multiple-hypothesis testing; multiply each raw p-value by the number of tests done.
  • CPRD Aurum: the Clinical Practice Research Datalink, Aurum version — a UK GP-records research dataset covering ~20% of the UK population, linked to hospital data and mortality records.

  • GBA1: a gene whose variants are a leading risk factor for Parkinson's disease.
  • HES (Hospital Episode Statistics): the UK secondary-care dataset, linked to CPRD for inpatient records.
  • K-means: a classical clustering algorithm — assign N points to K groups by minimizing within-cluster distance to the centroid.
  • LRRK2: a Parkinson's-associated gene; rs34637584 (G2019S) is the most famous coding risk variant.
  • OR (odds ratio): the ratio of the odds of an outcome in one group versus another; >1 = more risk, <1 = less.
  • PD (Parkinson's disease): a neurodegenerative movement disorder characterised by dopaminergic neuron loss in the substantia nigra.
  • PRS (polygenic risk score): a sum of a person's variant counts weighted by GWAS effect sizes, used to quantify common-variant predisposition.
  • SNP (single nucleotide polymorphism): a common single-letter DNA variant in the population.
  • Subtype: a within-diagnosis grouping of patients who share clinical, genetic, or trajectory features.
  • Tokenization: turning continuous patient-event streams into discrete code units a model can consume.
  • Transformer: the neural-network architecture (attention-based) underlying large language models; here used on patient sequences as if they were sentences.

Methods to lift

  • Patient sequence tokenization — turn each patient's longitudinal record into a time-ordered stream of tokens with timestamp, age, and calendar-year metadata.
  • Transformer-based patient embeddings — train an unsupervised transformer on tokenized sequences to produce a fixed-dimension vector per patient.
  • K-means on embeddings — cluster the patient embedding space into K = 5 subtypes; evaluate stability across resamples.
  • GP-identifier-based train/test split — prevent leakage by keeping all records from the same practice on the same side of the split.
  • Cross-cohort external validation — derive in CPRD, validate in UKB; re-cluster and check whether named subtypes recover.
  • Subtype-by-genetic-anchor overlay — test each subtype against single-SNV genotype calls and PRS to anchor clusters in biology.
  • Bonferroni correction across cluster-SNV tests — standard adjustment to keep cross-cluster SNV tests honest.

ALEX Welcome back. Today's paper is "Subtyping Alzheimer's disease and Parkinson's disease using longitudinal electronic health records," by Lian, Fan, Petrazzini, and colleagues, with senior author Kazem Rahimi at Oxford. Published in Nature Aging in 2026. It's a methods paper that uses transformer models on UK electronic health records to find data-driven subtypes of two big neurodegenerative diseases.

JORDAN Why are we, a CFTR-focused podcast, reading a paper about Alzheimer's and Parkinson's?

ALEX Because the machinery — using long sequences of electronic health record entries to discover clinically meaningful patient subtypes — is exactly the kind of analysis the CF Patient Registry has the raw material for and has not, to my knowledge, attempted at scale. The disease is incidental; the technique transfers.

JORDAN OK. Before we go in, what do I need to know to follow along?

ALEX Let's prime. An EHR — electronic health record — is the routinely-collected, time-stamped dataset that GPs and hospitals enter when a patient is seen: diagnoses, prescriptions, lab values, encounter notes. UK Biobank — UKB — is a half-million-person UK research cohort with linked EHR. CPRD Aurum — the Clinical Practice Research Datalink, Aurum version — is a separate, much larger UK GP-records dataset covering about 20% of the UK population. AD is Alzheimer's disease, PD is Parkinson's disease. A *subtype* in this paper means a cluster of patients with the same diagnosis who actually behave differently — different comorbidities, different progression, different outcomes. A *transformer* is the neural-network architecture that powers large language models — it processes sequences and learns vector representations of each item in context. Here it processes a patient's longitudinal record as a sequence. K-means is a classical clustering algorithm — group N points into K groups by minimizing distance to cluster centroids. PRS — polygenic risk score — sums up a person's genetic predisposition for a disease across many variants, weighted by their effect sizes from a reference GWAS. APOE is the apolipoprotein E gene; its e4 allele is the largest known common-variant risk factor for late-onset Alzheimer's. SNCA, GBA1, and LRRK2 are well-known Parkinson's risk genes. SNP — single nucleotide polymorphism — is a common single-letter variant in the population. An odds ratio, or OR, is the ratio of the odds of having a trait in one group versus another — above 1 means more risk, below 1 means less.

JORDAN That was a lot. So what's the actual scientific question?

ALEX AD and PD are both clinically heterogeneous — different patients with the same diagnosis follow very different trajectories. Some die quickly, some live decades. Some have heavy psychiatric comorbidity, some don't. Drug trials repeatedly fail in part because they pool all these patients together. So the question is: can you take the rich, time-stamped clinical history a patient *accumulates before* they're ever diagnosed with AD or PD, feed it to a deep-learning model that summarizes that history as a vector, cluster patients in that vector space, and recover subtypes that are stable, transferable, and clinically meaningful? That's the core scientific question.

JORDAN And why now?

ALEX Two things converged. The data — CPRD Aurum is now huge enough to give over 100,000 AD cases, and UKB now has linked EHR to serve as an external validation set. The methods — transformer architectures have matured enough that running unsupervised representation learning on patient sequences is feasible. So this is the first reasonably-powered application of EHR-foundation-model thinking to a clinically heterogeneous chronic disease.

JORDAN What problem was unknown before?

ALEX Earlier EHR-subtyping work used hand-engineered features — a list of comorbidities you picked in advance — or it only looked at post-diagnosis trajectories. The contested thing was whether the *prediagnostic* record alone could carry enough signal to recover stable subtypes, and whether subtypes found in one cohort would replicate in another. The skeptical position is "these clusters are statistical artifacts of the model and the dataset." The optimistic position is "patient histories really do contain prognostic structure if you read them as sequences." Lian and colleagues test which one is right.

JORDAN How did they actually tackle it?

ALEX Their pipeline has four pieces. First, cohort and data extraction. CPRD Aurum gave them 113,545 AD cases and 45,825 PD cases after inclusion criteria, plus UKB as external validation with 3,710 AD and 3,732 PD cases. CPRD Aurum, in their study, was split eighty-twenty into a derivation set and an internal validation set based on GP identifier — so patients from the same general practice never cross the train-test boundary. The AD cohort mean age at diagnosis was 82.1 years in CPRD and 74.4 in UKB; PD slightly younger; majority female in CPRD. Second, sequence construction. Each patient's clinical events — diagnoses, prescriptions, GP visits — get tokenized; that means turned into discrete code-units that a model can read. Each token carries timestamp, age, and calendar-year metadata. The window is prediagnostic, meaning the model only sees what happened to the patient *before* their AD or PD diagnosis date. Third, the model. They train a transformer on these tokenized sequences in an unsupervised way to produce a patient embedding — a single dense vector summarizing each patient's prediagnostic trajectory. Unsupervised means the model is not told the disease label or the subtype — it learns purely from the structure of the sequences. Fourth, K-means clustering on those embeddings produces five subtypes per disease. They then characterize each subtype by overlaying comorbidities — measured from prediagnostic records and medication history — symptoms within plus-or-minus five years of diagnosis, 5-year follow-up for mortality and hospitalization, and disease-related PRS and SNPs.

JORDAN Push back on a design choice.

ALEX The choice of K equals five — five subtypes per disease — comes from standard cluster-stability heuristics, but it's still a knob. They argue the subtypes replicate from CPRD into UKB, which is the right check. But the granularity is a modelling decision, not an empirical truth. With K=3 or K=8 they'd get different stories. That said, replication across two cohorts is a real test, and they actually did it.

JORDAN Did they answer the question?

ALEX Yes, with caveats. They report five reproducible subtypes for AD and five for PD. The AD subtypes the authors highlight: a high-mortality subtype with motor and cardiovascular features; a metabolic-inflammatory subtype; a vascular-psychiatric subtype; a relatively low-comorbidity subtype; and a fifth distinguishable by depression and gait disturbance. For PD, one of the subtypes is what they call genetically susceptible but clinically resilient — patients who carry classical PD risk variants but show milder trajectories. The convergence finding is the interesting one: a metabolic-inflammatory phenotype and a vascular-psychiatric phenotype show up in *both* AD and PD subtype maps, hinting at cross-disease mechanisms — same biology, different downstream presentation.

JORDAN Specific numbers and genes?

ALEX For AD, APOE genotype mapped cleanly onto cluster identity. The e4 allele, encoded by rs429358-C, was *underrepresented* in cluster 4 — odds ratio 0.62, 95% confidence interval 0.53 to 0.74, Bonferroni-corrected p-value 1.9 times ten to the minus six. Bonferroni correction is a statistical adjustment for testing many hypotheses at once. APOE e2, rs7412-T, was *enriched* in the same cluster — odds ratio 1.80. That tells you cluster 4 is the genetically protected subtype. For PD, the analogous finding was nominal — LRRK2 rs34637584-A, the most famous PD coding variant, showed an odds ratio of 2.65 in cluster 2 with a p-value of 0.046 — significant before, but not after, multiple-testing correction. So the PD genetic signal is weaker than the AD genetic signal, which fits the broader picture: AD has a single dominant risk gene, PD's genetic architecture is more diffuse.

JORDAN So a real result on AD, a softer one on PD.

ALEX Right. They also computed PRS-by-subtype and found cluster-specific PRS differences for AD, consistent with the SNP-level result. Most importantly, the AD APOE association replicated when they re-ran the pipeline on UKB — the same subtype came out genetically protected. That's the killer check. They also flagged convergent risk patterns — systemic factors like diabetes, depression, and hypertension appearing in subtypes across both AD and PD — suggesting that even when core risk genes differ between the two diseases — APOE and ABCA7 anchoring AD subtypes versus GBA1 and LRRK2 anchoring PD — the *clinical trajectory* drivers can be shared. Whether that convergence is biology or shared diagnostic-coding habits in UK GPs is a question the paper raises but cannot definitively settle from EHR data alone.

JORDAN Now, the proposal it points at for Runyu.

ALEX One specific direction. Build a CF-Patient-Registry sequence dataset: take every registry encounter for every patient — pulmonary exacerbations, FEV1 measurements, infection cultures, modulator initiations, hospitalizations, CFRD diagnosis — tokenize them in time order, train an unsupervised embedding model, and cluster the resulting patient vectors. Then characterize the clusters by CFTR genotype, modifier-locus variant carriage if WGS is available, and outcome trajectories. CFRD stands for cystic-fibrosis-related diabetes. FEV1, by the way, is forced expiratory volume in one second — the standard lung-function measurement reported as a percent predicted for age, sex, and height. The point is to invert the usual CF-cohort analytic approach. Traditional analyses pick a single outcome — say, age at first P. aeruginosa, or rate of FEV1 decline — and look for variants associated with it. The Lian et al. approach instead lets the patient histories tell you which patients group together first, and *then* asks which genetic and clinical variables explain the grouping. It's exploratory, but the discipline of cross-cohort replication keeps it honest. Formally: "In the CFF Patient Registry, derive data-driven CF subtypes from longitudinal prediagnostic and post-diagnostic clinical records using sequence-embedding plus clustering methods; then test whether the subtypes are differentially enriched for known CFTR-modifier variants or polygenic risk scores in the subset with WGS." The cluster-by-WGS overlay is the part that maps directly onto Lian et al.'s APOE-by-cluster finding.

JORDAN What does the paper close, and what does it leave open?

ALEX Closes: the methodological feasibility question. You can run transformer-on-EHR subtyping at over 100,000 patients, the clusters can be made to replicate across cohorts, and genetic anchors like APOE can land cleanly inside them when the disease has a strong common-variant component. It also closes — at least in the AD case — a long-running practical question about whether prediagnostic records carry enough signal: they do, at least for AD. Leaves open: whether the same approach works at CF cohort scale, which is at most about 30,000 patients in the full US CFF Patient Registry — large by CF standards, small by EHR-model standards. Transformer models tend to need more data to be stable; with smaller cohorts, the embeddings might be noisier and the cluster boundaries less reproducible. Also leaves open whether CF's tokenization is well-defined — CFTR modulator history, exacerbation grading, and microbiology cultures are richer than CPRD's GP-code stream but also messier, with center-to-center variation in how things are coded that AD/PD diagnoses don't have. Another piece left open: how to define the analogue of the "prediagnostic window" in CF, where the diagnosis often comes in infancy and the disease is the patient's lifelong context rather than something they accumulate clinical history before. The framing would have to shift — maybe pre-exacerbation, pre-CFRD-onset, or pre-lung-decline rather than pre-diagnosis. What we'd need to check before committing: get a sample of the CFF Patient Registry's actual event schema and confirm that tokenizable longitudinal data exists for individual patients with enough density to feed a transformer; confirm with Alex Elbert and Elizabeth Cromwell whether registry-data extraction at this granularity is even policy-feasible; pilot the pipeline on a single cohort year of CFF data before scaling. And — most importantly — never assume a cross-disease subtype that shows up in AD/PD will show up in CF; the underlying biology is unrelated and the convergence finding is hypothesis-generating only. The APOE-by-cluster check is the result that should be emulated, not the specific subtypes themselves.

JORDAN Before we close — anything practical that would help Runyu decide whether to even prototype this?

ALEX Yes, a small pre-commitment. The biggest single risk in transformer-on-EHR work at small scale is unstable clustering — you get different subtypes every time you re-train. The cheap pre-commitment test is to take the CF registry's existing aggregate variables — encounter counts per year, exacerbation rates, FEV1 trajectory, microbiology culture history — run plain hierarchical clustering on patient-level summary vectors, and ask whether you get reproducible subgroups across a five-fold split. If standard clustering on summary stats fails to produce stable subgroups in CF, a transformer is very unlikely to rescue the situation. If it succeeds, the transformer becomes worth the effort. This is the kind of cheap, day-one experiment that protects against the much more common version of this project — months of model-training with nothing to show.
