Blurb

Mahmoud et al. is the All of Us methodological pilot benchmarking Illumina short-read sequencing against PacBio HiFi and Oxford Nanopore (ONT) long reads on four HapMap reference cell lines (NA24385, HG00514, HG00733, NA19240) plus two anonymized AoU samples (T662828295, T668639440). Headline F-scores on NA24385 vs GIAB v4.2.1: Illumina/DRAGEN 99.47%, HiFi merged 99.87%, ONT merged 98.74%. On the easy 5,027-gene set the three platforms agree at 81.33%; on the hard 386-gene Wagner 2022 panel concordance collapses to 60.74%, with Illumina specifically uncovered on SMN1, CCL3L1, DUX4L1, HLA-DRB1, CRYAA, PRODH, NAIP. SV detection F-scores: Illumina 0.45, HiFi 0.93, ONT 0.91 — the largest single platform-difference in the paper. Proposed direction: a Mahmoud-style platform-concordance pilot on a CFGP subsample restricted to CFTR plus the locked Corvol 2015 modifier-gene panel, asking whether the existing CFGP short-read calls miss known-pathogenic CFTR variants (e.g. CFTRdele2,3, deep intronic splice variants) and what the marginal yield of a HiFi or ONT pilot would be against existing calls as the comparator.

Plain-English glossary (alphabetical)

  • ACMG list — American College of Medical Genetics 73-gene panel; clinical labs report pathogenic findings in these genes regardless of indication.
  • F-score — harmonic mean of precision and recall against a gold-standard truth set; one number summary of variant-caller accuracy.
  • GIAB — Genome in a Bottle consortium; publishes high-confidence reference variant calls (e.g., v4.2.1 on NA24385/HG002).
  • GRCh38 — current human reference genome assembly.
  • Indel — insertion or deletion under 50 bp.
  • Long-read sequencing — PacBio HiFi (~15–20 kb reads, <1% error) or Oxford Nanopore (up to 4 Mb reads, higher historical error rate now narrowing with duplex chemistry).
  • NA24385 / HG002 — the most-characterized GIAB benchmark sample (Ashkenazi male).
  • PromethION — high-throughput ONT instrument.
  • Sequel IIe — PacBio HiFi instrument used in this study (lower throughput than the newer Revio).
  • Short-read sequencing — Illumina chemistry; ~150 bp reads aligned to reference for variant calling.
  • SMN1/SMN2 — paralogous spinal muscular atrophy genes; the canonical example of a region short reads cannot resolve.
  • SNV — single-nucleotide variant; one DNA letter changed.
  • SV — structural variant; ≥50 bp insertion, deletion, inversion, duplication, or rearrangement.
  • Wagner 2022 challenging-gene list — 386 medically relevant genes flagged as hard to assess by short-read sequencing.
  • WGS — whole-genome sequencing.

Utility of long-read sequencing for All of Us

ALEX Welcome back. Today we're reading Mahmoud and colleagues, 2024, in Nature Communications — "Utility of long-read sequencing for All of Us." Senior authors are Fritz Sedlazeck at Baylor and Shawn Levy at HudsonAlpha. The All of Us project does most of its genomes with Illumina short reads at thirty-times coverage, and this is the methods-pilot paper that asks what they're missing.

JORDAN And we're reading it because the same question applies to the CF Genome Project, which is also short-read.

ALEX Exactly. Whether everything we're going to look for in CFF whole-genome data is actually visible at all in the variant calls we have, or whether some real biology is below the resolution of the sequencing technology, is a precondition question for the entire PheWAS plan.

JORDAN Before we go in — terms.

ALEX Yes. Whole-genome sequencing, abbreviated WGS, means reading the entire roughly three-billion-letter human genome rather than just the protein-coding two percent. Short-read sequencing is Illumina's chemistry: each "read" is about 150 letters long, and you stitch them back together by mapping each read to the reference genome. Long-read sequencing is the alternative: each read is fifteen thousand to twenty thousand letters long for PacBio HiFi, sometimes up to four million for Oxford Nanopore — abbreviated ONT.

JORDAN Why does read length matter.

ALEX Because the genome isn't a clean string of unique sequence. About half of it is repetitive — the same short pattern repeated many times, the same gene with a near-identical pseudogene sitting next door, segmental duplications. If your reads are 150 letters and the repeat is a thousand letters long, you can't tell which copy a read came from. Long reads can span the whole repeat and anchor on unique sequence at both ends. The classical example is SMN1 and SMN2 — two essentially identical genes a few thousand bases apart, where loss of SMN1 causes spinal muscular atrophy and short-read pipelines basically can't distinguish them.

JORDAN SNV, indel, SV?

ALEX SNV is single-nucleotide variant — one letter changed. Indel is insertion or deletion under fifty letters. SV is structural variant, anything fifty letters or larger: insertions, deletions, inversions, duplications, rearrangements. SVs are about a hundred times rarer than SNVs per genome but each SV affects far more base pairs, so they collectively impact a comparable amount of the genome. The CFTR gene itself has clinically important structural variants — the CFTRdele2,3 deletion is a well-known pathogenic allele that short-read pipelines can miss.

JORDAN F-score?

ALEX F-score is the harmonic mean of precision and recall, a single number summarizing how well a variant caller does versus a gold-standard truth set. Ninety-nine percent F-score is what you want for clinical work; sixty percent means a lot of the calls are wrong or missing. Precision asks "of the variants you called, how many were right." Recall asks "of the variants that were really there, how many did you find."

JORDAN GIAB, NA24385, ACMG?

ALEX GIAB is Genome in a Bottle, a Nature consortium that publishes high-confidence reference variant calls for a handful of well-characterized samples — the gold standard you score against. NA24385 is the most-used GIAB sample, also called HG002. ACMG is the American College of Medical Genetics; their list of seventy-three genes is what clinical labs are required to report if they find a pathogenic variant during incidental sequencing — BRCA1, BRCA2, MYBPC3, et cetera. Two recent papers expanded this concept: Mandelker 2021 listed a hundred ninety-three genes hard to assess on short reads, and Wagner 2022 extended that to three hundred eighty-six genes flagged as both medically relevant and technologically challenging.

JORDAN Background.

ALEX The framing problem is this. All of Us aims for one million genomes — by 2024 they had released the first hundred thousand. All of those genomes are Illumina short-read at thirty-times coverage, processed through the DRAGEN pipeline version 3.4.12 — the FDA-approved configuration. Researchers downstream are going to mine those calls for everything from drug response to disease risk to ancestry inference. If short reads systematically miss whole categories of variation in medically important genes, then the entire downstream literature inherits that limitation invisibly.

JORDAN And nothing in the published methods literature directly answered the question?

ALEX Not at production-quality coverage on identical samples across all three technologies. There were theoretical arguments — long reads should span repeats, so they should help. There were small-cohort case reports — diagnostic yield for genetic disorders improved when long reads were added. But the question downstream consumers actually need answered is: "at the gene-by-gene level on a clinically actionable gene panel, what's the recall and precision of each technology, and where exactly does Illumina fail?" Before this paper, that table didn't exist. The unknown was a quantitative answer to: which medically relevant genes are recoverable at all by short reads, which require long reads, and within long reads is PacBio HiFi or Oxford Nanopore the better choice. Earlier papers had argued from theory and from small samples that long reads should help; this is the first head-to-head comparison done at production-quality coverage on the same individuals across all three technologies.

JORDAN Methods.

ALEX Three technologies, the same samples, two phases. Phase one — four well-studied HapMap reference cell lines: NA24385, a Caucasian male; HG00514, a Han Chinese female; HG00733, a Puerto Rican female; NA19240, a Yoruba male. They sequence each on Illumina at thirty-times coverage, PacBio HiFi at thirty-five times, and ONT PromethION at forty-five times. They run multiple variant callers — DeepVariant, Clair3, Longshot for short variants; PBSV, Sniffles, CuteSV for SVs — and benchmark every call against the GIAB v4.2.1 truth set on NA24385. Phase two — two anonymized All of Us pilot samples, T662828295 and T668639440, sequenced on all three platforms across multiple tissue sources and extraction methods to see whether real-world All of Us samples behave like the curated HapMap reference samples. They focus the gene-level analysis on two gene sets — the easy 5,027 medically relevant genes and the hard 386-gene set from Wagner 2022 — and report normalized coverage, F-score, and uncovered-gene counts.

JORDAN One design choice to push back on.

ALEX The sample count is tiny. Four HapMap cell lines plus two AoU samples is not a population analysis — it's a benchmarking exercise. They're upfront about it, but it means the variant-frequency claims that downstream readers might want to draw about the All of Us cohort are not in this paper. This is a methods feasibility, not a discovery study.

JORDAN Results.

ALEX Headline F-score: on NA24385 against GIAB v4.2.1, Illumina with DRAGEN gets 99.47%. PacBio HiFi with DeepVariant plus Clair3 merging gets 99.87% — best in class. ONT merged gets 98.74%. So for the bulk of the genome, all three are clinically usable; HiFi is marginally best, ONT slightly worse but with a longer read length advantage in repeat regions.

JORDAN Where does it actually matter.

ALEX The 386 challenging gene set. There the gap opens up. Concordance between platforms on the easy 5,027 genes is 81.33%. Concordance on the 386 hard genes drops to 60.74%. Specific named genes where Illumina is uncovered but long reads work: SMN1 (the spinal muscular atrophy gene), CCL3L1, DUX4L1, HLA-DRB1, CRYAA, PRODH, NAIP. For SV detection, ONT and HiFi both massively outperform Illumina — Illumina F-score for SV detection is 0.45, HiFi is 0.93, ONT is 0.91. They also identified genes where all three technologies fail — H19, KRTAP1-1, MDK — generally because of reference-genome issues like incorrectly duplicated regions in GRCh38. The All of Us-style pilot samples behave essentially like the HapMap reference samples, which is reassuring for generalizability. Did they answer the framing question? Yes, decisively: for the easy genome, short reads are fine; for the hard 386-gene set and for SVs, long reads buy a substantial improvement that is hard-to-impossible to recover with short reads alone.

JORDAN Proposed direction for us.

ALEX One specific possible direction, framed as one option among several. The CFTR locus itself is moderately complex — segmental duplications nearby, structural variants like CFTRdele2,3 and intronic deep variants like 3849+10kbC>T that affect splicing, and a long history of clinically important rearrangements that short-read pipelines have trouble with. A possible direction is a focused short-read versus long-read concordance audit on a small subset of CFF Genome Project samples — say ten to thirty samples spanning F508del homozygotes, F508del compound heterozygotes, and non-F508del genotypes — sequenced on both short reads and PacBio HiFi, with a pre-specified set of CF-relevant gene targets including CFTR itself plus the locked modifier-gene panel from Corvol 2015. In one formal sentence: a Mahmoud-style platform-concordance pilot on a CFF Genome Project subsample, restricted to CFTR plus the locked CF modifier panel, with concordance and uncovered-variant reporting per gene as the primary outcome and structural-variant detection rate within CFTR as a secondary outcome.

JORDAN Gap.

ALEX It closes the question of whether long reads buy clinical-grade improvement on hard medically-relevant genes — yes they do, for the 386-gene panel, and especially for SVs. It closes the platform-choice question for All of Us future strategy — HiFi is best on accuracy, ONT is competitive at longer read lengths and lower per-sample cost. It leaves open several things relevant to us. First, the paper doesn't directly address whether CFTR specifically is in the hard 386-gene set — we'd need to check the supplementary tables. Second, the All of Us samples that come back near-identical to HapMap controls are healthy population samples, not chronically-inflamed CF patients with potential somatic mosaicism or sequencing artefacts from disease-altered tissue. Third, the cost question is not resolved — even if long reads buy a real improvement, the per-sample cost gap matters when you're talking about a fixed cohort budget. What we'd need to check before committing: whether CFTR is on the Wagner 2022 challenging-gene list at all; whether the existing CFF Genome Project short-read variant calls at the CFTR locus have known false-negative rates from prior internal validation; whether any portion of the CFGP samples are stored in a form amenable to long-read re-sequencing; and what the marginal yield of a long-read pilot would be against the existing short-read calls as the comparator, not against a theoretical maximum.

JORDAN One thing to flag about the cost angle.

ALEX ONT needs only one or two flow cells per sample to hit forty-five-times coverage; PacBio HiFi needed four to six flow cells per sample with the Sequel IIe instrument used in this paper, although the newer Revio platform has reduced that gap considerably. So even though HiFi wins on F-score, ONT has historically been the cheaper option per genome and that economic balance keeps shifting. For a CF cohort with five thousand samples already sequenced on Illumina, the realistic question is which subset deserves long-read re-sequencing rather than the whole cohort, and ONT may be the only economically tractable choice at full-cohort scale.

JORDAN Tomorrow.

ALEX Tomorrow we read Faino and colleagues, 2025, Annals of the American Thoracic Society, on a new genetic modifier of chronic Pseudomonas aeruginosa airway infection in the CF Genome Project — a paper on the exact phenotype we care about, on the exact cohort we care about. One-line takeaway for today: Mahmoud shows that for the easy genome short reads are fine, but for the hard 386-gene panel and for structural variants, long reads buy a clinical-grade improvement that's hard to recover any other way.
