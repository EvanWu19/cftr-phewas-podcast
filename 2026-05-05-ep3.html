One-paragraph blurb

Faino, Gordon, Buckingham and colleagues — with senior authorship by Elizabeth Blue (Brotman Baty Institute / University of Washington) and contributing groups from the Cutting (Hopkins), Knowles/Pace (UNC Marsico), and Rosenfeld (Seattle Children's) labs — report a time-to-event genome-wide association study of chronic Pseudomonas aeruginosa infection age in 4,945 CF Genome Project participants with whole-genome sequencing linked to the 2017 Cystic Fibrosis Foundation Patient Registry. They identify rs194810 near CHP2 (calcineurin B homolog protein 2) at P=2.2×10⁻⁸, MAF 43%. People homozygous for the protective A allele acquire chronic Pa on average ~3 years later than people homozygous for the G allele. The mechanistic story is unusually clean: CHP2 activates the sodium/hydrogen exchanger NHE3 (SLC9A3), which already sits at the chr5p13 lung-function modifier locus confirmed by Zhou 2023. The proposed direction for our project is, conditional on inspection of the CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage, a candidate-locus PheWAS of rs194810 (and gene-burden tests of CHP2 rare variants) across the full CFFPR endpoint catalog, treating chronic Pa acquisition age as the established positive control. The key open question is whether CHP2 has effects beyond the Pa endpoint — across non-Pa respiratory infections, lung function trajectory, modulator response, and non-respiratory CF complications.

Plain-English glossary

  • Allele dosage — Number of copies of a particular allele a person carries at a SNP: 0, 1, or 2. The default predictor in additive-model GWAS.
  • Calcineurin — A calcium/calmodulin-dependent protein phosphatase complex. CHP2 is a homolog of one of its subunits.
  • Censoring — In survival analysis, when a participant's follow-up ends before the event occurs. Time-to-event methods handle censored observations rigorously.
  • CFGP (CF Genome Project) — The whole-genome sequencing dataset of ~5,000 CF patients (Cutting, Knowles, Strug, Corvol consortium). Same substrate as Zhou 2023, Aksit 2022, Aksit 2020.
  • CFFPR — Cystic Fibrosis Foundation Patient Registry. Longitudinal clinical data on virtually all U.S. CF patients followed at accredited care centers.
  • Chronic Pa infection — Persistent recovery of Pseudomonas aeruginosa from sputum cultures over time. Definitions: Leeds criteria (>50% of cultures positive in past 12 months) and modified Leeds / U.S. registry variants. Faino et al. test both.
  • Cox proportional hazards model — The standard time-to-event regression. Outputs a hazard ratio per predictor.
  • CHP2 (Calcineurin B Homolog Protein 2) — Small calcium-binding regulatory protein that activates the sodium-hydrogen exchanger NHE3 / SLC9A3. The new locus identified in this paper.
  • Genome-wide significance threshold — Conventional cut-off of P ≤ 5×10⁻⁸, accounting for ~1M independent common-variant tests.
  • Hazard ratio (HR) — In time-to-event analysis, the multiplicative change in instantaneous risk of the event between two groups. HR=1 means no effect; HR<1 means protective.
  • Heritability — Share of phenotypic variation attributable to genetics. Twin studies of CF chronic Pa infection put non-CFTR heritability at ~85%.
  • MAF (Minor Allele Frequency) — Frequency of the less-common allele.
  • Modulator era — The period since 2012–2019 in which small-molecule CFTR modulators (ivacaftor, lumacaftor/ivacaftor, tezacaftor/ivacaftor, elexacaftor/tezacaftor/ivacaftor [Trikafta]) have been progressively approved. Most CFGP follow-up substantially predates Trikafta.
  • NHE3 / SLC9A3 — Sodium-hydrogen exchanger 3. Major intestinal sodium absorption channel; sits at chr5p13 lung-function modifier locus.
  • Pa (Pseudomonas aeruginosa) — Opportunistic respiratory pathogen; the dominant chronic colonizer of CF airways and a major prognostic event.
  • PheWAS (Phenome-Wide Association Study) — Inverse of GWAS — pick a variant or gene, scan thousands of phenotypes for association.
  • Principal components (PCs) — Top axes of genotype variation, included as covariates to adjust for population structure.
  • Time-to-event analysis / survival analysis — Statistical framework that models how long until an event occurs, handling censored follow-up. Used here because the question is when people acquire chronic Pa, not just whether.
  • WGS (Whole Genome Sequencing) — Sequencing the full genome at typically 30× coverage. The CFGP data substrate.

Methods to lift

  • Time-to-event GWAS framework — Cox proportional hazards model with PC adjustment and kinship/relatedness correction, applied to a CF complication endpoint defined from registry longitudinal data. Adopt for any CF endpoint that has a meaningful "time-to" interpretation (CFRD onset, severe lung-function decline, transplant referral, mortality).
  • Dual-definition robustness check — run the analysis under both the Leeds and modified-Leeds definitions of chronic Pa, report concordance. Apply same logic to any disputed endpoint definition (e.g., CFRD criteria, exacerbation definitions).
  • WGS-on-registry-linkage design — the CFGP-to-CFFPR linkage is the analytical substrate this paper depends on; mirror that linkage for any future CF PheWAS.
  • Survival-curve stratification by allele dosage — visualize per-allele effects as separate Kaplan-Meier-style curves in addition to reporting a single HR. Useful for clinical-audience communication.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: a candidate-locus PheWAS of rs194810 (and gene-burden tests of CHP2 rare variants) against the curated CFFPR phecode catalog. Time-to-event analysis for incidence endpoints (chronic Pa = positive control; chronic Staphylococcus aureus, Burkholderia, NTM as related infection endpoints; CFRD onset, transplant referral, mortality as separate axes), quantitative-trait analysis for continuous endpoints (FEV1 trajectory, BMI-z trajectory). Pre-register tests, FDR correction across the actual number of phecodes tested, mandatory pre-modulator vs post-modulator stratification.

What to verify first

  • Whether rs194810 is well-imputed (info score ≥ 0.8) in the older array-genotyped subsets of the CFGP — array-based historical genotypes are likely to be used in any cross-cohort meta-analysis, and TOPMed-imputed quality at this specific SNP needs confirmation before that path is committed to.
  • Whether the CFFPR endpoint catalog stably encodes non-Pa respiratory infections (Staphylococcus aureus colonization status, Burkholderia, NTM) with cross-center harmonized definitions over the planned follow-up window.
  • The pre-modulator vs post-modulator follow-up split in the linked CFFPR data, so any analysis can stratify appropriately — the published CHP2 effect is most defensible as a pre-modulator-era genetic modifier and modulator-era replication is unaddressed.
  • Whether the CHP2 rare-variant burden in CFGP is non-zero — a single common SNP near a gene tells us nothing about whether rare LoF in CHP2 itself causes more extreme phenotypes; we'd want carrier counts before scoping a burden test.
  • Whether CHP2 and chr5p13 SLC9A3 jointly explain lung-function variance in a conditional analysis, or sit on partly independent axes — answers whether they are mechanistically one signal or two.

Gap-map: closes / leaves open

  • Closes: the question of whether common-variant modifiers of chronic Pa acquisition age exist at genome-wide significance in the CFGP WGS cohort. CHP2 is the lead and any re-analysis on the same substrate for the same endpoint will re-find it.
  • Closes: the candidate-gene-era hunt for a single Pa-risk-modifying common variant; CHP2 supersedes the prior MBL2 / HLA candidate-gene literature on this endpoint.
  • Leaves open: CHP2 rare-variant burden, including potential biallelic-LoF carriers and their phenotypes.
  • Leaves open: CHP2 pleiotropy across the rest of the CF complication phenome — non-Pa respiratory infections, lung function trajectory independent of Pa, CFRD, GI complications, modulator response. None of these are tested in the paper.
  • Leaves open: whether the CHP2 effect transfers to the modulator era. Most CFGP follow-up substantially predates Trikafta; whether early-and-continuous modulator therapy abolishes or preserves the CHP2 effect is unknown.
  • Leaves open: the joint architecture of CHP2 (regulator) and SLC9A3 (target) — whether they explain partly overlapping or fully independent variance in CF outcomes is a clean follow-on question.
  • Leaves open: environmental modifiers of the CHP2 effect — gene-by-environment interaction with secondhand smoke, microbiome composition, geographic Pa exposure rates. Not analyzed.

Faino, Gordon, Buckingham and colleagues, Annals of the American Thoracic Society, twenty-twenty-five, titled "CHP2 Modifies Chronic Pseudomonas aeruginosa Airway Infection Risk in Cystic Fibrosis." Senior author is Elizabeth Blue at the Brotman Baty Institute in Seattle, with major contributions from the Cutting group at Hopkins, the Knowles and Pace group at UNC Chapel Hill, and the Rosenfeld group in pediatric pulmonary at Seattle Children's. JORDAN We're reading it because chronic Pseudomonas infection is one of the phenotypes any CF PheWAS would have to include, and because a brand-new locus in this cohort tells us something important about whether that cohort still has signal left to mine.

Today's paper tackles a completely different complication on the same dataset. So this is a useful test of how much novelty is still discoverable in the CF Genome Project for non-pulmonary, non-GI complications.

JORDAN Before we dive in, walk me through some of the terms we'll need.

ALEX A few I want to set up. Pseudomonas aeruginosa, abbreviated as Pa, is a bacterium. It's an opportunistic respiratory pathogen — meaning it doesn't usually cause disease in healthy people, but in cystic fibrosis lungs it colonizes the thick mucus, evades antibiotics, and progressively damages lung tissue. Acquiring chronic Pa infection — meaning, the bacteria persistently grow in the lungs from sputum cultures over many months and visits, rather than coming and going — is one of the major prognostic events in a CF patient's lifetime. The longer you can stay free of chronic Pa, the better your long-term lung function and survival tend to be.

JORDAN How is "chronic" defined?

ALEX That's its own debate. There are at least two widely used definitions. The Leeds criteria — out of the U.K. Leeds CF center — say chronic means more than fifty percent of sputum cultures positive for Pa over the previous twelve months. The other common definition, sometimes called the modified Leeds or the U.S. registry approach, sets a slightly different threshold. Faino et al. test their analysis under both definitions to make sure the result isn't a definition artifact.

JORDAN And the statistical method they use is time-to-event. What does that mean?

ALEX Time-to-event analysis, also called survival analysis even when the event isn't death, models how long someone goes before experiencing an event. The classic tool is the Cox proportional hazards model. The output isn't an odds ratio but a hazard ratio — the ratio of the instantaneous risk of the event between two groups, integrated over time. Time-to-event is the right framework here because the question isn't whether you ever get chronic Pa — many CF patients eventually do — but how long you stay free of it. Time-to-event also gracefully handles people whose follow-up ends before the event occurs, called censoring.

JORDAN One more piece of jargon I want unpacked.

ALEX Allele dosage. At a given SNP, each person has two copies — one from each parent. So your "dosage" of a particular allele can be zero, one, or two. Most modern GWAS treat dosage as a continuous predictor: the model asks whether each additional copy of the allele changes risk by a constant amount. That's also called the additive model and it's the default assumption.

JORDAN OK. Now what was the question they were asking, and why was it open?

ALEX The question: are there host-genome variants outside CFTR that modify the age at which a person with cystic fibrosis acquires chronic Pseudomonas infection? The motivating context is twofold. First, twin and sibling studies of CF — work that has come out of the Hopkins twin and sibling cohort — estimate that roughly eighty-five percent of the variation in chronic Pa infection age is heritable beyond what CFTR genotype explains. So the genetic modifiers exist; we just don't know what they are. Second, we're now in the modulator era. Trikafta and earlier modulators improve CFTR function pharmacologically and reduce exacerbation rates, but they do not eradicate Pseudomonas from people who are already chronically infected. So the genetic modifiers of acquisition timing are still operationally relevant — they tell us who might benefit from earlier or more aggressive infection-prevention strategies.

JORDAN Has nobody looked for these modifiers before?

ALEX There have been smaller candidate-gene studies — MBL2 has been proposed for years, and a few HLA variants — but none of them replicated cleanly. There has not been a well-powered, time-to-event genome-wide association study on chronic Pa in cystic fibrosis until now. The reason is that the cohort assembly is hard: you need both the longitudinal sputum-culture data and the whole-genome sequencing on the same patients, with enough follow-up time to observe the event in many of them. Faino and colleagues are the first to bring all three together at this scale.

JORDAN Let's move into methods. Cohort?

ALEX Four thousand nine hundred forty-five participants in the CF Genome Project — abbreviated CFGP — with whole-genome sequencing linked to longitudinal clinical data from the twenty-seventeen Cystic Fibrosis Foundation Patient Registry. Same WGS substrate as the Zhou twenty-twenty-three lung-function paper and the Aksit twenty-twenty-two CFRD-MI paper.

JORDAN Statistical method?

ALEX Time-to-event genome-wide association study using two definitions of chronic Pa infection. They use a mixed-model framework that adjusts for population structure with principal components and accounts for cryptic relatedness — important because the CFGP includes the twin-and-sibling sub-cohort, so close relatives are present.

JORDAN I want to push back on one design choice. The twenty-seventeen Patient Registry data is now eight years old. The microbiology landscape has shifted — earlier and more aggressive eradication therapy has been standard since the early twenty-tens. Couldn't that change which variants matter?

ALEX That's a real concern and worth flagging on-air. The follow-up window in this paper substantially predates the Trikafta era — many participants reach the event during ages where modulator therapy was either unavailable or partial. So the CHP2 effect they find is most defensible as a "pre-modulator-era genetic modifier of chronic Pa acquisition." Whether the same variant matters in patients who are on Trikafta from infancy is an open empirical question that this paper cannot answer. They do not claim otherwise.

JORDAN All right, what did they find?

ALEX One genome-wide significant association. A SNP called rs194810, located near a gene called CHP2 — calcineurin B homolog protein two — with a p-value of two point two times ten to the minus eight. That just clears the standard genome-wide significance threshold of five times ten to the minus eight. The minor allele, the A allele, has a frequency of forty-three percent in the cohort, so this is a common variant — most people in any CF cohort will carry at least one copy.

JORDAN What's the effect size in human terms?

ALEX Survival curves stratified by allele dosage show that people homozygous for the A allele — meaning two copies of the protective allele — are on average three years older when they acquire chronic Pa infection, compared with people homozygous for the G allele. Three years is clinically meaningful. The intermediate group, the heterozygotes carrying one copy of each, sits between the two homozygous curves.

JORDAN What does CHP2 do?

ALEX Calcineurin B homolog protein two is a small calcium-binding regulatory protein. It activates a sodium-hydrogen exchanger called NHE3 — the same gene we know as SLC9A3, which sits at the chr5p13 lung-function modifier locus that Zhou twenty-twenty-three confirmed. So CHP2 is a regulator of an epithelial ion transporter that's already implicated in CF biology by a completely separate analytical path. That's an unusually clean mechanistic story for a brand-new GWAS hit.

JORDAN Did they answer their question?

ALEX Yes, and with appropriate humility. The question was: are there host-genome modifiers of chronic Pa acquisition age in CF? Answer: yes, at least one — CHP2 — at p just under the standard threshold, with biological plausibility, with consistent effects across the two definitions of chronic infection, and with a non-trivial three-year shift in event timing per allele dosage tier. They do not claim to have explained all the heritability; they explicitly leave open whether other loci will emerge in larger cohorts or under different infection-onset definitions.
