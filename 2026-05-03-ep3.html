Blurb

Taylor and colleagues (2011) introduce KNoRMA — Kulich/Knowles/Rosenfeld Mortality-Adjusted — the lung-function severity score that every cystic-fibrosis genetic-modifier paper since 2011 has used as its outcome variable. Built on three North American CF Modifier Consortium cohorts (n = 4,521 across CGS + TSS + GMS), KNoRMA fixes a fundamental survivor-bias problem in CF genetics: the sickest patients die young and never enter cross-sectional analyses, so naive FEV1-based studies are structurally blind to severe-disease modifier alleles. KNoRMA's mortality adjustment recovers genetic signal that survivor bias was hiding (heritability 0.51, 95% CI 0.28–0.77 in twins/sibs). For our CFTR PheWAS planning: this is the canonical worked example of "phenotype derivation before genetic testing" — and the model we'd hypothetically reproduce on CFFPR longitudinal data, then extend to non-pulmonary endpoints.

Plain-English glossary (concepts used in the episode)

  • Phenotype — an observable trait of a person (height, blood pressure, lung-function score).
  • Genotype — a person's specific DNA letters at a particular spot in the genome.
  • Gene — a stretch of DNA that codes for a protein.
  • CFTR — Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane conductance Regulator, the gene whose mutations cause CF; the gene Trikafta targets.
  • Modifier gene — a gene OTHER than the disease-causing one that influences how the disease shows up.
  • FEV1 — forced expiratory volume in one second, the standard breathing-test severity measure for CF.
  • Cross-sectional study — measures everyone at one moment in time (a snapshot).
  • Longitudinal study — measures the same people repeatedly over years (registry data is longitudinal).
  • Survivor bias — the trap where the sickest people die early and are missing from your analysis, biasing the average to look healthier than the underlying disease really is.
  • Heritability — the fraction of person-to-person variation in a trait explained by genetic differences, on a 0-to-1 scale.
  • GWAS — genome-wide association study; scans the entire genome for DNA spots whose genotype is statistically associated with a phenotype.
  • PheWAS — phenome-wide association study; like a GWAS but scans many phenotypes against many genetic variants.
  • LMM — linear mixed model; a regression that handles related observations (repeated measurements per person, twin pairs, etc.).
  • KNoRMA — Kulich/Knowles/Rosenfeld Mortality-Adjusted; the lung-function severity phenotype this paper introduces.
  • Kulich percentiles — published CF-specific FEV1 reference percentiles by age, used inside KNoRMA so the score is comparable across ages.
  • Probit transform — a math step that converts a percentile into a standard-normal score, the form genetic models prefer.
  • CFF / CFFPR — Cystic Fibrosis Foundation / CFF Patient Registry.
  • WGS — whole-genome sequencing.
  • CFRD — CF-related diabetes (a registry-tracked CF complication).

Methods to lift (for our methods primer)

  • Mortality-adjusted percentile construction: at each age, multiply the patient's age-specific CF-FEV1 percentile by the age-specific cohort survival probability, add (1 − survival) to represent decedents, then probit-transform to a standard-normal score.
  • CF-specific reference curves: use the published Kulich percentiles instead of in-sample LOESS so the phenotype is portable across cohorts that aren't population-based.
  • Three-cohort meta design: pool a population-based cohort (CGS), a family-based cohort (TSS), and an extreme-phenotype cohort (GMS) — KNoRMA's distribution should look different in each by construction, and that's a built-in sanity check.
  • Pairwise-correlation heritability via twins/sibs: with permuted A/B assignment, h² ≈ 2 × (r_MZ − r_DZ/sib). Useful template for any registry-based h² re-estimation.
  • Minimum design footprint: three years of pre-enrollment FEV1 data per patient. Below that, KNoRMA degrades but mixed-model phenotypes degrade much faster.
  • Pancreatic-insufficiency status (88% in CGS) — recorded as a known severity covariate; any modifier-locus analysis must include it as a covariate or stratify on it.

Proposed direction (conditional)

A hypothetical CFFPR/WGS-anchored PheWAS would, before any genetic association testing, reproduce the KNoRMA lung-function phenotype on CFFPR longitudinal data for the WGS sub-cohort — using updated CF-specific reference curves where appropriate — and then extend the same mortality-adjustment logic to derive parallel phenotypes for non-pulmonary outcomes the registry tracks: nutritional status (BMI z-score), pancreatic function, infection burden (e.g., chronic Pseudomonas aeruginosa colonization), and CF-related diabetes onset. The lung-function phenotype is solved as of 2011; the rest of the CF phenome has not received this treatment. Each endpoint needs its own derivation logic before it can defensibly serve as a PheWAS outcome variable.

Gap-map: closes

  • Lung-function phenotype derivation for cross-cohort CF modifier genetics. KNoRMA is the published, peer-reviewed, canonical recipe. Every subsequent CFF-cohort lung-modifier GWAS (Wright 2011, Corvol 2015, Zhou 2023) cites Taylor and uses KNoRMA. No re-derivation is happening at the lung-function endpoint.

Gap-map: leaves open (conditional on the proposed direction)

  • Parallel mortality-adjusted phenotypes for non-pulmonary CF outcomes. No published mortality-adjusted phenotype exists for nutrition, pancreatic function, infection burden, or CFRD onset. The phenotype-design problem is unsolved at every endpoint other than lung function.
  • Post-modulator-era recalibration. Kulich reference percentiles were derived on a 1994–2001 CF cohort; survival estimates use Canadian birth-cohort data that pre-date Trikafta entirely. Modulator-era CF mortality dynamics are sufficiently different that the 2011 model may need updating, or at least separate-stratum analysis, for any modern cohort.
  • Phenome-wide registry endpoint construction. FinnGen built 1,932 endpoints from Finnish registry data (covered yesterday). Nothing analogous exists for CF. KNoRMA is endpoint number one in what would need to be a much larger CFFPR endpoint library before a full PheWAS is defensible.

What to verify first (before any of this becomes a real plan)

  • Does the CFFPR have the per-patient longitudinal FEV1 records — at sufficient density and quality — needed to compute KNoRMA-style scores at scale?
  • Is the WGS sub-cohort linkable, at the patient level, to the longitudinal CFFPR record?
  • Should the post-Trikafta era be analyzed as a separate stratum from pre-modulator data, given the survival-curve change? If yes, the reference percentiles and survival functions need to be re-derived for that stratum.
  • Are non-pulmonary CFFPR endpoints (BMI z-score, PI status, microbiology, CFRD diagnosis) recorded with sufficient longitudinal density and uniformity to support KNoRMA-style derivations?
  • Does the WGS sub-cohort overlap with the CF Modifier Consortium cohorts (CGS, TSS, GMS) used here? If yes, any reproduction is partially circular.

Your average looks healthier than the underlying disease really is. A heritability of 0.5 means about half the person-to-person variation is genetic.

JORDAN And the rest is environment, treatment, luck.

ALEX Right. Two more. GWAS — genome-wide association study — is a study that scans the entire genome looking for DNA spots where the genotype is statistically associated with a phenotype. LMM — linear mixed model — is a regression-style statistical model that handles data where some observations are related to each other, like repeated measurements on the same person, or measurements on twins.

JORDAN And KNoRMA?

ALEX KNoRMA stands for Kulich, Knowles, Rosenfeld Mortality-Adjusted. It's the lung-function phenotype this paper introduces. The "K" honors Michael Kulich, who built the CF-specific FEV1 reference curves they use. Knowles and Rosenfeld are the senior CF investigators whose framework this builds on. "Mortality-Adjusted" is the whole point of the paper.

JORDAN Okay. With that vocabulary in hand — what problem were Taylor and colleagues trying to solve?

ALEX By 2011, CF genetic-modifier research was already a field. Investigators had assembled large cohorts to find modifier genes — genes other than CFTR that influence how severe a person's CF lung disease becomes. The dominant approach was the genome-wide association study, the GWAS, where you scan the genome looking for DNA variants that statistically track with severity.

JORDAN What's the catch?

ALEX The catch is the phenotype — the severity measure — that the GWAS uses as its outcome. The naive choice is FEV1 percent predicted, measured cross-sectionally — a single recent value. Two problems. One, FEV1 declines with age in CF, so a 10-year-old at 60% predicted is much sicker than a 30-year-old at 60% predicted, but the raw number is identical. Two, and worse: survivor bias. The 30-year-olds you measure today are the survivors of their birth cohort. The patients with the worst lung disease from that birth cohort already died. Your sample is systematically missing the severe end of the distribution at older ages.

JORDAN So a naive cross-sectional FEV1 number is biased toward looking healthy as patients age.

ALEX Right. And here's the consequence for genetics. If a rare genetic variant pushes lung disease toward severe, the patients carrying that variant are the ones most likely to have died before you measured them. Your GWAS, which only sees the survivors, is structurally blind to exactly the alleles you most want to find. Survivor bias eats your statistical power for severe-disease modifiers.

JORDAN That's a problem. And the inverse — a protective allele that helps patients live longer — would look artificially common, because those patients stay in the dataset.

ALEX Right. Survivor bias distorts the genetic signal in both directions, and worst at exactly the older ages where the natural-history data is most accumulated. If you don't fix it, your modifier hits are biased toward variants that act mildly across the whole disease range, and biased against the variants with strong effects on severe-end outcomes. Those strong-effect rare alleles are also the most clinically actionable ones — they're the ones a drug target would hang off of. So this isn't an academic statistics problem; it directly distorts which biology you discover.

JORDAN Got it. What had the field tried before this paper?

ALEX Mary Schluchter and colleagues had built an earlier mortality-aware approach using a mixed model — a regression that handles repeated measurements per person — that jointly modeled FEV1 over time alongside age at death. Schluchter and colleagues recommended a phenotype called FEVest20, which is the model's predicted FEV1 at age 20, derived from each patient's own FEV1 trajectory. It worked, but it had a weakness: the estimate degrades the further a patient is from age 20. A four-year-old or a forty-year-old, the FEVest20 estimate is shaky because you're extrapolating a trajectory across a long age gap. Taylor and colleagues set out to design a phenotype that handles mortality attrition without that age-extreme weakness, and that travels well across cohorts with different sampling designs — population-based studies, family-based studies, extreme-phenotype studies. The portability requirement is what makes it usable as a shared outcome variable across the consortium.

JORDAN Walk me through how they actually built KNoRMA.

ALEX Three pieces. Piece one — they pooled longitudinal FEV1 data from three North American CF cohorts, which together became the North American Cystic Fibrosis Modifier Consortium. We'll come back to those cohorts. Piece two — for each patient, at each age, they used a previously published set of CF-specific FEV1 percentile curves, the Kulich percentiles, to compute where that patient's FEV1 sits relative to all CF patients of the same age. So instead of saying "60% predicted," you say "this patient is at the 30th percentile of CF patients their age." That handles the age-dependence problem.

JORDAN That's the K in KNoRMA.

ALEX Right. Piece three — the mortality adjustment. For each age, they multiplied the patient's percentile by the probability that a CF patient of that age is still alive, then added one minus that survival probability to represent the dead. Mathematically, the dead are placed at the bottom of the distribution. Then they probit-transform — that's a math trick that converts a percentile into a standard normal score, the kind of bell-curve number genetics models like to work with. The output is one number per patient — their KNoRMA score — that represents lung-disease severity adjusted for both age and the fact that some of their birth cohort has already died.
