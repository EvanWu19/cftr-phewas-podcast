Blurb

Taylor et al. (2011) define the KNoRMA phenotype — Kulich-percentile-based, survival-adjusted, mortality-attrition-aware FEV1 score — that the CF modifier-genetics field has used as the lung-disease outcome ever since. Built on three North American CF Modifier Consortium cohorts (n = 4,521 across CGS + TSS + GMS) using only three years of follow-up per patient, KNoRMA is a portable summary score, not a trajectory phenotype. For our CFTR PheWAS, this paper is exactly what makes the longitudinal-trajectory gap (gap-map Gap 3) defensible: KNoRMA was designed to summarize away the slope-and-trajectory information that CFFPR's quarterly FEV1 measurements would let us test directly.

Methods to lift (add to methods primer)

  • Mortality-adjusted percentile construction: age-specific FEV1 percentile × age-specific cohort survival probability + (1 − survival), then probit-transform to a normal score.
  • LOESS-based age-specific reference curves where population-based sampling permits (NoRMA) vs. published CF-specific Kulich percentiles for portable cross-cohort use (KNoRMA).

Concept-note paste

Existing CF modifier-genetics literature uses summary-score phenotypes (KNoRMA, Schluchter FEVest20) by convention to enable cross-cohort harmonization (Taylor et al., 2011). The CFFPR registry layer beneath the CFGP WGS cohort permits longitudinal mixed-effects FEV1 slope analysis with random subject- and family-level effects, jointly estimated with modulator-initiation timing — a phenotype only newly possible because of post-2020 modulator-era follow-up and not previously tested at genome-wide resolution.

JORDAN CFTR-WGS slot today is Taylor et al., Pediatric Pulmonology 2011, "A novel lung disease phenotype adjusted for mortality attrition for cystic fibrosis genetic modifier studies." PMID 21462361, PMCID PMC3130075. This is the KNoRMA paper.

ALEX Yes — and I want to flag at the top that this paper is one of the methodological hinges in our gap map. Gap 3 in our gap-map memo is "longitudinal/trajectory phenotypes," and the literature uses summary-score phenotypes — KNoRMA, the Schluchter mixed-model approach, and so on — by convention. Taylor 2011 is the paper that defines KNoRMA. To make a defensible argument for trajectory phenotypes in our concept note, we have to know exactly what KNoRMA does and why the field adopted it.

JORDAN Set up the problem they're solving.

ALEX CF is a Mendelian disease but lung-disease severity varies enormously even between patients with the same CFTR genotype. Heritability of lung function in CF is well-established — references 1 through 4 in their introduction cite the Twin and Sibling Study, the Canadian Consortium, the UNC/Case Gene Modifier Study. So there are modifier loci to find. The question is: what phenotype should the modifier-locus GWAS use as its outcome?

JORDAN Why not just FEV1 percent predicted?

ALEX That fails for a deep reason: mortality attrition. Patients with severe lung disease die younger. So if you compare a 30-year-old's FEV1pp to other 30-year-olds, you are comparing them only against survivors — patients from the same birth cohort with worse lung disease have already dropped out of the data. A 30-year-old at 60% FEV1pp is, in cohort terms, doing reasonably well because most of the original cohort with worse lung function is no longer alive. A 10-year-old at 60% FEV1pp is severely affected — almost everyone in their birth cohort is still alive and has better function.

JORDAN So a naive cross-sectional analysis would call these two patients equivalent.

ALEX Right, and Schluchter and colleagues — reference 5 in their paper — built the original mortality-aware framework using a mixed model that jointly estimates FEV1 slope, FEV1 intercept, and age at death. Schluchter recommended FEVest20 — predicted FEV1pp at age 20 — as the modifier-study phenotype. That worked but it has a critical problem. The estimate degrades the further you are from age 20.

JORDAN Which is the problem Taylor is fixing.

ALEX Exactly. They build two phenotypes. First, NoRMA — Normal Residual Mortality Adjusted — which uses LOESS regression on FEV1pp by age within the CGS sample to compute age-specific percentiles, then multiplies by the age-specific cohort survival probability and adds one minus survival to account for the dead. Then probit-transforms to get a normal score. Second, KNoRMA — the "K" stands for Kulich — uses Kulich's previously published CF-specific FEV1 percentiles instead of the in-sample LOESS, which makes the phenotype portable across cohorts that aren't population-based.

JORDAN Walk me through the cohort numbers.

ALEX They apply this to the North American CF Modifier Consortium — three cohorts, three different ascertainment schemes. Canadian Consortium for CF Genetic Studies (CGS): population-based, 2,061 analyzable patients, mean age 20.5, all 37 Canadian CF centers between 2000 and 2004. Johns Hopkins CF Twin and Sibling Study (TSS): 1,323 patients from 693 families, family-based, mean age 16.3 — that's NCT00676169 in our memory file, the Cutting lab's recruitment that lets them estimate heritability. UNC/Case Western Gene Modifier Study (GMS): 1,137 patients homozygous for F508del, extreme-phenotype design, mean age 23.1.

JORDAN Total patient count is roughly 4,500.

ALEX Right. And — this is the methods bit that matters for our cohort — these three studies are exactly the cohorts that became the CF Genome Project's ascertainment base, which then became Zhou 2023's WGS sample. So when our gap map says "Zhou 2023 used KNoRMA as a single phenotype, by explicit construction," this paper is where KNoRMA comes from.

JORDAN Heritability estimate?

ALEX They use 67 monozygous twin pairs and 138 dizygous twin/same-sex sib pairs, with permuted A/B assignment. KNoRMA correlation in MZ twins is 0.79; in DZ/sib pairs is 0.53. Heritability is twice the difference: h² = 0.51, with 95% CI 0.28 to 0.77.

JORDAN That's a big confidence interval.

ALEX Yes — and worth flagging on-air. With 67 MZ pairs, you do not get a tight heritability estimate. The point estimate is consistent with prior work using just the Kulich percentile without mortality adjustment, but anyone who tells you "the heritability of CF lung disease is 0.51, full stop" is reading the point estimate without the interval. The interval includes 0.28, which would be a much weaker genetic-modifier story.

JORDAN Correlation between KNoRMA and the Schluchter phenotypes?

ALEX In the CGS sample restricted to ages 10-30, the correlations are 0.90 to 0.94. In TSS, KNoRMA correlates 0.67 with AgeDied%ile and 0.57 with FEVest20. In GMS, 0.79 and 0.81. The TSS numbers are weaker because TSS skews younger, and the mixed-model phenotypes degrade at the age extremes. KNoRMA is more robust.

JORDAN Why does that matter for our PheWAS?

ALEX KNoRMA was explicitly designed to handle short follow-up windows — the paper analyzes only three years of data per patient. That's the reason Zhou 2023 could harmonize across heterogeneous WGS sub-cohorts. But the design choice has a cost, and that cost is exactly the gap our concept note is trying to argue for.

JORDAN Tell me more.

ALEX Look at Table 1 in our gap map. Gap 3 reads: "No published genetic-modifier paper analyzes FEV1 slope directly in mixed-effects longitudinal models, exacerbation frequency over time, or trajectory phenotypes from longitudinal registry data." KNoRMA collapses the longitudinal data into a single age-adjusted, mortality-adjusted, percentile score. It is built for portability. It is not built for trajectory analysis. CFFPR has FEV1 measurements roughly four times per year per patient — that is a much richer longitudinal substrate than three years of consortium-recruited visits.

JORDAN So the concept note's argument writes itself.

ALEX It does. The argument is: KNoRMA is the right phenotype for the cross-cohort modifier discovery the field has done so far, because it travels well between studies with different ascertainment. But for a CFFPR-anchored analysis, where every patient has decades of homogeneous registry data, the slope-and-trajectory phenotypes that KNoRMA was designed to summarize away are the phenotypes we should test directly.

ALEX This paper closes nothing — it is the methodological substrate that defines what's been done. It leaves Gap 3 wide open by design: KNoRMA is a summary score, not a trajectory phenotype. JORDAN Limitations to flag for our use?

ALEX Three. One, KNoRMA's reference equations are Kulich's, which were derived on a 1994-2001 CF cohort. Lung function in CF has improved markedly since then. Taylor explicitly acknowledges this "known bias in a known direction." For a modulator-era analysis that bias is no longer ignorable. Two, the mortality estimates use Canadian birth-cohort survival data; they checked against US registry data through age 22 and found no significant difference, but they did not check at older ages where US-Canadian survival differences are documented. Three, survival estimates are not sex-stratified despite known CF sex disparity in survival.

JORDAN One more thing I want to make sure we name. The TSS sample being family-based was actually load-bearing for the heritability estimate, but it also means TSS contributes the relatedness structure that any GWAS in the merged consortium has to handle. For our PheWAS planning, this is the relatedness signal that pushes us toward SAIGE or BOLT-LMM with a properly constructed GRM rather than naive logistic regression.

ALEX Right — that connects directly back to today's first episode. The Twin and Sibling Study sub-recruitment under NCT00676169 is exactly why our cohort's relatedness exceeds UKBB's 30.3%, and it's the reason we cannot simply assume independence in any modifier-locus analysis. It also matters for trajectory phenotyping: family-based covariance structure in longitudinal mixed-effects models is non-trivial — twins and same-age sibs have correlated environment as well as correlated genotype, which our slope-phenotype model needs to absorb.

JORDAN That's a methods-primer addition: any trajectory mixed-effects GWAS we propose has to declare its random-effect structure for both subject and family.

ALEX Add it. The KNoRMA framework dodged this by collapsing to a per-subject summary that you could feed into a relatedness-aware single-variant test. Once we move to slope phenotypes, the family random effect is no longer hidden in the summary — it's something the model has to fit explicitly. That's an honest analytical complication and we should name it before a reviewer does.

JORDAN Before the artifact — one number I want to lock down. They quote 88% pancreatic insufficiency in the CGS sample as "consistent with previous reports." This matters because PI status is a strong predictor of severe lung phenotype, and our cohort's PI fraction will differ from the consortium samples.

ALEX Right, and CFFPR records PI status explicitly. We will need to either include it as a covariate or stratify, and either way our results will not be directly comparable to KNoRMA-based modifier studies that did not condition on it. That is a methods-section sentence: PI status will be included as a covariate in all single-variant tests and reported as an effect modifier in primary modifier-locus analyses.
