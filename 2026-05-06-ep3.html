Blurb

Polineni and colleagues integrate nasal-mucosa RNA-seq from 134 CF patients with GWAS data from 5,659 CF patients (CF Genome Modifier Study cohort) to ask which biological pathways mediate the heritable component of CF lung-disease severity. They show that gene expression itself is heritable in this CF cohort (the precondition for the integrative analysis) and find convergent pathway-level signals across the transcriptomics and GWAS arms, organized around what they call airway mucosal host defense: viral infection, inflammation/inflammatory signaling, lipid metabolism, apoptosis, ion transport, F508del CFTR processing, and innate immune responses (HLA genes). Ion transport, CFTR processing, and HLA genes appear in both arms. The proposed direction this episode opens up for the CFTR PheWAS project is an airway-epithelium-tissue-restricted TWAS on the CFGP WGS cohort using the seven Polineni-pathway gene sets against the CFFPR phecode panel. Open questions: whether the same pathway architecture mediates non-pulmonary CF complications, whether the modulator era preserves the architecture, and whether HLA's appearance reflects CF-specific biology or shared airway-disease machinery.

Plain-English glossary

  • CF GMS cohort (CF Genome Modifier Study) — North American CF Modifier Consortium GWAS cohort; the n=5,659 GWAS arm of this paper is part of this cohort, the same family of cohorts that contribute to the CFGP WGS work.
  • eQTL (expression Quantitative Trait Locus) — A SNP whose genotype is statistically associated with the expression level of some gene; how GWAS hits are linked to specific candidate causal genes.
  • F508del CFTR processing — The cellular pathway that handles the F508del-mutant CFTR protein, which misfolds at the endoplasmic reticulum and is targeted for degradation; rescue of this processing pathway is the mechanism of corrector-class CFTR modulators (lumacaftor, tezacaftor, elexacaftor).
  • Gene Ontology (GO) — Standardized vocabulary of biological processes, molecular functions, and cellular components; the source of most pathway gene-set annotations.
  • GSEA (Gene Set Enrichment Analysis) — Statistical test for whether a pre-defined gene set is enriched at the top of a ranked list of genes; output is a per-pathway p-value.
  • GTEx — Genotype-Tissue Expression consortium; public eQTL resource across ~49 tissues from ~1,000 donors. The standard reference for tissue-specific gene-expression prediction models.
  • HLA (Human Leukocyte Antigen) — A gene complex on chromosome 6 encoding the MHC molecules central to adaptive immunity; the most polymorphic region of the human genome and the source of many GWAS signals across immune-mediated diseases.
  • KNoRMA (Kulich-normal residual mortality-adjusted FEV1) — Standard CF-cohort survival-aware composite measure of lung-disease severity; covered in detail Episode 5/3/ep3 (Taylor 2011).
  • Methionine salvage pathway — A metabolic pathway recycling methylthioadenosine back to methionine; APIP encodes a key enzyme. Polineni 2018 connects this pathway to CF inflammatory biology at the chr11p13 modifier locus.
  • Pathway analysis — Asking whether a list of significant genes is enriched for genes belonging to pre-defined biological functions (Gene Ontology, KEGG, Reactome).
  • RNA-seq (RNA sequencing) — Wet-lab technique that measures, for a given tissue sample, the abundance of messenger RNA transcripts for each gene.
  • Transcriptome / transcriptomics — The set of all messenger RNA transcripts in a cell or tissue; the field that studies it.
  • TWAS (Transcriptome-Wide Association Study) — Method that uses GTEx-trained models to predict tissue-specific gene expression from a person's genotypes, then tests predicted expression against a phenotype. Output: one p-value per gene per tissue.

Methods to lift

  • Nasal-mucosa RNA-seq protocol with Matrix eQTL — Less invasive than bronchoscopy; 134-patient sample feasibility documented; Matrix eQTL (Shabalin 2012) for fast SNP-by-gene eQTL scans.
  • SVA package for batch-effect removal (Leek et al 2012) — Standard in any large transcriptomics integration; would be needed for any cross-cohort airway expression work.
  • Heritability-of-expression test as the precondition for integrative analysis — Demonstrate gene expression is heritable in the cohort before running TWAS-style integration; pre-registers a check step worth replicating.
  • Pathway-level reporting frame (Polineni's 7 mucosal-host-defense pathways) — Viral infection, inflammation, lipid metabolism, apoptosis, ion transport, F508del CFTR processing, innate immune/HLA. Reusable as the prior gene-set list for any CFGP TWAS-style follow-on.
  • Convergence-across-arms reporting — Reporting only pathways that show signal in both transcriptomics and GWAS arms (rather than treating either alone as discovery); reduces false-positive risk from any single data type.
  • eQTL test of previously identified CF GWAS risk alleles against expression of nearby genes — Targeted complement to the genome-wide pathway analysis; useful template for any "do CFGP modifier-locus SNPs have eQTL effects on airway pathway genes" question.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: airway-epithelium-tissue-restricted TWAS of the seven mucosal-host-defense pathway gene sets identified by Polineni 2018 (viral infection, inflammation/inflammatory signaling, lipid metabolism, apoptosis, ion transport, F508del CFTR processing, innate immune/HLA), conducted on the CFGP WGS cohort against (a) the CFFPR phecode panel and (b) continuous lung-function trajectory phenotypes, with the HLA region treated as a separate analysis because of its complex LD structure and ancestry-confounding risk.

What to verify first

  • That the GTEx airway-epithelium expression models are well-calibrated in CF patients, who differ from healthy GTEx donors in chronic-inflammation state — model bias on the CF substrate is not characterized.
  • That CFGP WGS coverage is adequate for the relevant eQTL SNPs in the seven pathways — we have not personally inspected the CFGP variant call coverage at airway-epithelium eQTL loci.
  • That the seven pathway annotations are stable across Gene Ontology versions used by Polineni 2018 vs current GO; pathway membership drifts year to year.
  • That an HLA-region TWAS on a Northern-European-skewed CF cohort can be controlled for ancestry confounding before any HLA hit is reported as CF-specific.

ALEX Welcome back. This is Episode 3 for today and we are reading "Airway Mucosal Host Defense Is Key to Genomic Regulation of Cystic Fibrosis Lung Disease Severity," published January 1, 2018 in the American Journal of Respiratory and Critical Care Medicine, volume 197, issue 1, pages 79 through 93, by Deepika Polineni, Hong Dang, Paul Gallins, and colleagues, with senior author Michael Knowles. We are reading it because it is one of the few CF Genome Modifier Study cohort papers that pairs gene expression with GWAS — the closest thing in the CF literature to the kind of integrative analysis the broader biobank field has been doing for a decade.

JORDAN Slow down. Define transcriptomics, RNA-seq, and eQTL. I have heard the terms but I cannot use them in a sentence.

ALEX Each cell in the body carries the same DNA, but different cells turn different genes on and off — that pattern of which genes are active is called the transcriptome. RNA-sequencing, or RNA-seq, is a wet-lab technique that measures, for a given tissue sample, how much messenger RNA is being made for each gene. Higher RNA usually means more protein being produced. So when you run RNA-seq on, say, nasal epithelial cells from one hundred and thirty-four CF patients, you get one hundred and thirty-four expression profiles, each one a vector of about twenty thousand gene-level expression values. An eQTL — an expression quantitative trait locus — is a SNP that is statistically associated with the expression level of some gene. So an eQTL analysis asks, for each SNP, does its genotype predict the expression of some nearby or distant gene. eQTLs are how you connect a GWAS hit — which is just a position in the genome — to a specific gene that the variant is plausibly regulating. The biggest public eQTL resource is GTEx, the Genotype-Tissue Expression consortium, which mapped eQTLs across about 49 tissues from roughly a thousand donors. Polineni and colleagues didn't use GTEx directly because they wanted CF-specific airway tissue, but the methodology is the same — gene expression and genotype, joined SNP by SNP.

JORDAN One more — pathway analysis and GSEA.

ALEX When a GWAS or transcriptomics study spits out a ranked list of genes, you can ask, are the genes near the top of this list enriched for some pre-defined biological function. The pre-defined functions live in databases like Gene Ontology, KEGG, and Reactome. Each function is a gene set — a list of genes annotated as belonging to, say, innate immune response or lipid metabolism. Gene Set Enrichment Analysis, GSEA, is the specific statistical test that asks whether a gene set is enriched at the top of a ranked list more than chance would predict. The output is a per-pathway p-value. The advantage over single-gene analysis is biological interpretability — instead of twenty unrelated genes, you get this whole pathway is involved. We've talked about GSEA before in the context of Zhou 2023's branching-morphogenesis finding; same machinery, different question.

JORDAN Okay. What was the question Polineni was trying to answer?

ALEX The starting point is a known fact about CF: even among patients homozygous for the F508del mutation — the most common CFTR variant, accounting for about seventy percent of CF chromosomes worldwide — lung-disease severity varies enormously. Heritability studies — twin and sibling work, the Vanscoy 2007 paper they cite — have shown that more than fifty percent of that variability reflects genetic variation outside the CFTR gene itself. The CF GWAS literature has identified a handful of modifier loci — chr11p13 around EHF and APIP, chr5p13 around SLC9A3, and others. But knowing where the loci are doesn't tell you the biology. Polineni's question is, can we identify the specific cellular pathways that mediate that modifier biology, by joining gene-expression data with the GWAS signal.

JORDAN How did they do it?

ALEX Three pieces. First, they collected nasal mucosal tissue from 134 CF patients and ran RNA-seq, generating a per-patient transcriptomic profile. Why nasal? Because nasal epithelium is reasonably similar to bronchial epithelium for the cellular processes they care about — there's prior work cited in the references showing nasal cells track bronchial inflammation reasonably well — and nasal sampling is far less invasive than bronchoscopy. Second, they tested for association between gene expression and a quantitative measure of CF lung-disease severity in those 134 patients, identifying genes whose expression tracks how sick a patient's lungs are. Third, they pulled GWAS data from a separate, much larger cohort — 5,659 CF patients — and ran pathway analysis on both data streams to ask whether any biological pathway shows convergent signal in both the gene expression and the GWAS results. They also explicitly tested whether previously identified CF GWAS risk alleles were associated with the expression of nearby genes — that's the eQTL leg of the integration.

JORDAN Push back on a design choice.

ALEX The choice to use nasal epithelium instead of lung. The paper makes a defensible case — nasal cells are accessible, sample sizes are larger, and prior literature shows reasonable concordance — but the actual disease site is the lower airway, and there are CF-specific aspects of lung biology, particularly the mucus-bacteria interface in the small airways, that nasal sampling will not fully capture. They don't have a side-by-side validation in matched bronchial tissue, so the inference that nasal expression patterns reflect bronchial biology is borrowed from prior literature, not directly demonstrated here.

JORDAN Results.

ALEX They found significant evidence that gene expression is itself heritable in this CF cohort, which is the precondition for the entire integrative analysis. They then identified pathways that were significantly associated with CF lung-disease severity and reproducible across the transcriptomics and GWAS arms. The pathways converge on what they call airway mucosal host defense: viral-infection response, inflammation and inflammatory signaling, lipid metabolism, apoptosis, ion transport, F508del CFTR processing, and innate immune responses including the human leukocyte antigen — HLA — genes. The convergent signal is most striking for ion transport and CFTR processing pathways, and for HLA genes, which appear in both the differential gene expression results and the GWAS pathway analysis. They did not nominate one single new modifier gene at genome-wide significance — that wasn't the design — but they did localize the modifier biology to a defined pathway architecture, which is a substantively new and useful claim. They also discuss APIP, the methionine salvage pathway gene at the chr11p13 locus, in the context of broader inflammation biology — that thread is one of the more provocative pieces of the paper because it connects a known CF modifier locus to a candidate metabolic mechanism rather than leaving APIP as a hand-waved annotation.

JORDAN One concrete research direction this paper opens up?

ALEX One direction would be a TWAS-style analysis — TWAS stands for transcriptome-wide association study, the method that uses GTEx-trained models to predict tissue-specific gene expression from a person's genotypes and then tests predicted expression against a phenotype, giving you one p-value per gene per tissue — built on the CFGP whole-genome sequencing cohort, using GTEx-trained airway-epithelial expression models — or, if available, CF-specific airway expression models like the ones Polineni's group has continued to build — to predict, for each of the roughly five thousand CFGP participants, gene-level expression across the airway-mucosal-host-defense pathway, and then to associate that predicted expression with both pulmonary and non-pulmonary CFFPR endpoints. In plain English: we don't sequence anyone's mucosa, but we can use the WGS we already have plus public expression models to ask, does each person's predicted airway-defense gene expression predict their lung-function trajectory, their CFRD risk, their infection burden, their modulator response. Formal one-sentence version: airway-epithelium-tissue-restricted TWAS of the seven mucosal-host-defense pathway gene sets identified by Polineni 2018, conducted on the CFGP WGS cohort against the CFFPR phecode panel and continuous lung-function phenotypes, with HLA region treated as a separate analysis because of its complex LD structure.

JORDAN Conditional on that, what does Polineni close, what does it leave open, and what would we need to check?

ALEX It closes — within the CF GMS cohort and within nasal epithelium — the question of whether modifier biology converges on a definable pathway architecture rather than scattering randomly across the genome.
