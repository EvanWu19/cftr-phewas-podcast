How did this episode land for you?

Blurb

Lee, Hujoel, Loh, Pasaniuc, and Sankararaman (Nat Genet, 2025) introduce LTPI — a liability-threshold framework that converts binary phecodes into continuous per-individual liability estimates by integrating other phecodes, family history, and continuous lab/biomarker fields, weighted by their genetic correlations to the target. The method generalises LTFH (which used only family history). On eMERGE chronic-kidney-disease data LTPI reached AUROC 0.954 for advanced CKD; on UK Biobank CAD it identified 139 independent risk loci vs 51 for binary-PheCode GWAS and 120 for LTFH, with 275 of 280 novel LTPI-only loci showing concordant direction in external replication GWASes. Proposed direction for CFTR PheWAS: stop treating CF complications as binary registry flags and instead build LTPI-style continuous-liability phenotypes by integrating diagnosis flags with CFFPR continuous fields (lung-function trajectory, anthropometrics, microbiology positivity, lab panels), then GWAS-scan against these continuous phenotypes. Open: LTPI assumes pre-existing population-scale genetic correlations (PheWeb/Pan-UK); CFFPR-specific traits don't have them, and the method's behaviour under weaker correlation inputs has not been characterised.

Plain-English glossary

  • ATSA — automatic trait selection algorithm; LTPI's algorithm for picking which nontarget traits to feed in, ranked by genetic contribution to the target.
  • AUROC — area under the receiver operating characteristic curve. 0.5 = chance, 1.0 = perfect discrimination.
  • Bonferroni correction / FPR — multiple-testing alpha adjustment / false positive rate.
  • Cauchy combination test — a way of combining p-values from non-independent tests; used to combine LTPI and LTFH evidence.
  • CFRD / CFLD / MI — CF-related diabetes / CF liver disease / meconium ileus (CF complication phenotypes).
  • eMERGE — Electronic Medical Records and Genomics Network; multi-site EHR+DNA consortium.
  • FH — family history. Five UKBB field IDs encode disease in father (20107), mother (20110), siblings (20111), plus sibling counts (1873, 1883).
  • GHK algorithm — Geweke-Hajivassiliou-Keane importance sampler; the numerical engine for high-dimensional conditional Gaussian integrals inside LTPI.
  • GWAS — genome-wide association study.
  • h² / heritability — fraction of trait variance attributable to genetics; on liability scale for binary traits.
  • HRC — Haplotype Reference Consortium imputation panel.
  • LDSC — linkage-disequilibrium score regression; estimates heritability and genetic correlation from GWAS summary stats alone.
  • Liability threshold model — yes/no diagnosis treated as a Bernoulli outcome of an unobserved continuous liability crossing a threshold.
  • LTFH — Liability Threshold model conditional on case-control status and Family History (Hujoel et al. 2020); LTPI generalises this.
  • LTPI / LTPI-b / LTPI-c — Liability Threshold Phenotypic Integration; -b component uses binary nontargets, -c uses continuous nontargets, full LTPI composites both.
  • MNAR — missing not at random; the hardest missing-data regime.
  • Pan-UK — Pan-UK Biobank summary-statistic resource for multi-ancestry GWAS.
  • PheCode / PheCode-FH — phecode = ICD-bucket disease category; PheCode-FH adds family-history and self-report fields.
  • PheWeb — public catalogue of pre-computed SAIGE GWAS summary statistics across 1,402 phecodes from >400K UKBB samples with 20M HRC-imputed variants.
  • PRS / PRSpleiotropy — polygenic risk score / a metric for how much a PRS smears across nontarget traits.
  • Regenie — fast biobank-scale whole-genome regression GWAS toolkit.
  • r_g — genetic correlation between two traits; values −1 to 1.
  • R² (R²_LTPI, R²_GWAS) — variance explained, for LTPI score vs the simulated genetic-liability ground truth, and for binary case-control as baseline.
  • SAIGE — scalable mixed-model GWAS method; used to produce PheWeb summary stats.
  • SoftImpute / AutoComplete — phenotype-matrix imputation methods (low-rank approximation and deep autoencoder, respectively); used as comparison baselines.
  • SNP — single-nucleotide polymorphism.

Methods to lift

  • Liability-threshold reframing — convert every yes/no phecode into an unobserved continuous liability with a threshold; treat the liability as the GWAS phenotype.
  • GHK importance sampler — Geweke-Hajivassiliou-Keane recipe for high-dimensional conditional Gaussian moments; used to compute posterior mean genetic liability efficiently.
  • Automatic trait selection algorithm (ATSA) — rank nontarget traits by their genetic contribution to the target (proportion of target's genetic variance the nontarget can explain via r_g); take top-N with prevalence>0.01 and h²-p<0.05 filters.
  • PheWeb-sourced r_g matrix — pull pre-computed genetic correlations from public catalogues (PheWeb, Pan-UK) via LDSC rather than re-estimating in-cohort.
  • Cauchy combination of LTPI and LTFH — combine the two p-values when both family history and other EHR data are available; further power gain over either alone.
  • Regenie for biobank-scale GWAS — whole-genome regression with leave-one-chromosome-out polygenic prediction; covariates: age, sex, PCs, batch.
  • Sensitivity check via consequence-trait removal — recompute LTPI after dropping nontargets that are downstream complications rather than upstream predictors of the target (Manual vs Manual-32 in the paper).
  • Validation against external GWAS datasets — replicate novel hits in independent published case-control summary stats for the same trait; check replication slope ≈ 1 and effect-direction concordance.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: construct LTPI-analogue continuous-liability phenotypes for the canonical CF complication set — CFRD, CFLD, meconium ileus, distal intestinal obstruction syndrome, nontuberculous mycobacterial infection — by integrating CFFPR-recorded binary diagnoses with continuous CFFPR fields (longitudinal FEV1/KNoRMA, BMI z-score, sweat chloride, microbiology positivity counts, hepatic lab panels). Perform Regenie-based GWAS modifier scans against these continuous-liability phenotypes in the CFF WGS cohort. Compare loci recovered to those recovered by standard binary-flag modifier GWAS on the same n≈5,000 cohort, to test whether the continuous reframing buys back enough power to compensate for the small sample size.

What to verify first

  • Whether LTPI's genetic-correlation inputs (PheWeb, Pan-UK) cover any CFFPR-specific phenotypes — most are population-cohort general-medicine traits, not CF complications, so the standard pipeline may not be plug-and-play.
  • Whether a stripped-down LTPI variant using observed phenotypic correlations (not LDSC genetic correlations) is well-defined and how badly performance degrades — published method requires r_g.
  • Which CFFPR fields are recorded with sufficient longitudinal depth to function as continuous nontarget traits; need to inspect registry-data-dictionary completeness rates.
  • Whether continuous-liability reframing changes the modulator-era stratification problem — pre/post-modulator continuous phenotype distributions are not simply scaled versions of each other.
  • Whether the consequence-vs-predictor distinction (Manual-32 in the paper) is operationally tractable in CFFPR fields where complications cascade — does lung function predict CFRD, or does CFRD predict lung function?
  • Whether the n≈5,000 CFF WGS cohort gives LTPI enough effective sample size for any real power gain — Lee et al. ran on 405K UKBB participants; the LTPI power-gain curves may not extrapolate downward this far.
  • Computational footprint of constructing CFFPR-specific genetic-correlation estimates from in-cohort GWAS, if PheWeb r_g cannot be reused.

Lee, Hujoel, Loh, Pasaniuc, and Sankararaman, 2025, in Nature Genetics: "Liability threshold model-based disease risk prediction based on electronic health record phenotypes." The method they introduce is called LTPI, liability threshold phenotypic integration. It is brand-new — published this past November, zero citations as of the database snapshot, so we're reading at the frontier here.

JORDAN Before we go in, give me the term primer. What's a liability threshold model? What's heritability? What's a phecode? What's LDSC? Half of this is alphabet soup if you haven't worked with it.

ALEX Yes. So the liability threshold model is a way of thinking about a yes-no disease — you have schizophrenia or you don't, you have CF-related diabetes or you don't — by imagining there's a hidden continuous score behind the scenes, called the liability, and you cross the diagnosis threshold when your liability score climbs above some cutoff. Liability is the sum of two components: a genetic component, the heritable part driven by your DNA, and an environmental component, the everything-else part. Heritability, written h², is the fraction of variation in the liability that's explained by genetics. For CF-related diabetes it's somewhere in the moderate range; for schizophrenia it's around eighty percent. The crucial intuition is that two people who both meet the diagnosis are not necessarily equally liable — one might be just over the threshold, one might be way over. Binary case-or-control labels throw all of that gradient away.

JORDAN OK. And the rest of the acronyms?

ALEX PheCode — same as yesterday — a curated bucket of ICD codes that map to one biologically meaningful disease, about eighteen hundred sixty in the standard v1.2 scheme. EHR is electronic health record. UKBB is UK Biobank, the British half-million-person research cohort with genotypes, lab measurements, family history, and linked health records. eMERGE is the U.S. multi-site EHR-plus-DNA network we mentioned yesterday. LDSC stands for linkage-disequilibrium score regression, a statistical technique that takes GWAS summary statistics for two traits and estimates how genetically correlated they are without needing the underlying genotypes. Genetic correlation, r_g, is a number between minus-one and one that asks: are the variants pushing trait A up the same variants pushing trait B up? GWAS is genome-wide association study. PRS is polygenic risk score. FH is family history. LTFH is a published method from 2020 that uses family history to upgrade a binary case-control label into a continuous score. LTPI extends LTFH by using anything in the EHR, not just family history. Regenie is a fast biobank-scale GWAS toolkit. ATSA is the paper's automatic trait selection algorithm. PRSpleiotropy is their metric for how much a PRS smears across traits other than the target.

JORDAN And the GHK algorithm name they keep mentioning?

ALEX Geweke-Hajivassiliou-Keane importance sampling. It's a numerical recipe for computing high-dimensional conditional Gaussian integrals. Don't memorise it — just know it's the inner solver that lets LTPI do its maths efficiently.

JORDAN Good. So what's the problem they're attacking?

ALEX The Zheutlin paper from yesterday is the exemplar of the problem. Take a binary phecode label, call it case-versus-control, regress against genotype. The data are doing two things wrong. First, they're throwing away information — a person diagnosed with type-two diabetes who also has elevated haemoglobin-A-one-c plus a family history of diabetes plus diagnosed coronary artery disease is more diabetic-liable than a person who has the diagnosis alone. Phecode binarization treats them identically. Second, when phecodes are missing, you don't know whether the person genuinely doesn't have the disease or just hasn't been to that specialty clinic. The PheCode approach handles all of that crudely. LTPI proposes a principled fix: estimate the underlying continuous liability for the target disease using everything else in the EHR — other phecodes, family history when available, quantitative lab values, biomarkers — and use that continuous estimate as the GWAS phenotype instead of the binary label. The conceptual ancestor is LTFH, which used family-history field IDs from the UKBB to do the same upgrade. LTPI generalises that.

JORDAN That's clever. How does it work mechanically?

ALEX Two steps. Step one — for every other binary trait in the EHR that is genetically correlated with the target, fit a multivariate Gaussian liability model across all of them, use the GHK importance sampler to compute the posterior mean genetic liability conditional on the observed yes-no pattern. Call that LTPI-b. Step two — for continuous traits, lab measurements, biomarkers, fold them in with a maximum-likelihood estimator, call that LTPI-c. The final LTPI score is a composite. The math depends on having genetic correlations between traits, which they pull from PheWeb — a public catalogue of pre-computed GWAS summary statistics — using SAIGE-derived summaries for fourteen hundred two phecodes built from over four hundred thousand UKBB samples, twenty million variants imputed with the HRC reference panel. They estimate r_g via LDSC and Pan-UK. The automatic trait selection algorithm, ATSA, then picks which nontarget traits to feed in — its rule is the genetic contribution to the target. The paper compares LTPI against four competitors: standard binary-PheCode GWAS, LTFH using only family history, SoftImpute, which is a low-rank phenotype-matrix imputer, and AutoComplete, a deep-learning autoencoder approach. They evaluate on simulations of one hundred thousand parent-offspring trios with two hundred traits, then on real data from eMERGE-III with ninety-nine thousand seven hundred thirty-seven samples and UKBB with four hundred four thousand nine hundred eighty-one white British participants across nine target diseases.

ALEX One design choice I want to push on. The whole framework rests on the genetic-correlation estimates from LDSC being trustworthy, and LDSC needs reasonably-large discovery GWASes for both traits in the pair. For rare diseases, or for traits that have not been GWAS'd at scale, that input quality breaks down. The paper sidesteps this by sticking to common diseases with rich UKBB summary stats — CAD, type-two diabetes, breast cancer, prostate cancer, COPD, hypertension, depression, stroke, colorectal cancer. The method's assumption is, in effect, that someone else has already done the hard work of estimating every relevant pairwise genetic correlation.

JORDAN That's a real limit. Did the method actually do better?

ALEX Yes, consistently. In simulations with one hundred binary plus five continuous nontarget traits at genetic correlation zero-point-nine, LTPI R² to the simulated genetic liability was zero-point-six-two versus zero-point-four-three for the binary-only baseline, a forty-two-point-five-three-percent improvement. GWAS power tracked the prediction improvement — at r_g equals zero-point-seven and zero missingness, LTPI had fifty-one-point-eight percent power to detect causal SNPs at genome-wide significance versus forty-three-point-three-five for LTFH and thirty-five-point-five for binary GWAS, a forty-six-percent power gain over the binary baseline. Combining LTPI and family-history p-values via the Cauchy combination test pushed it to fifty-eight-point-three-five percent power, sixty-four-point-five percent better than binary. False-positive rate stayed calibrated under the null. Real-data applications were as good or better. In eMERGE on chronic kidney disease, LTPI achieved AUROC zero-point-nine-five-four for advanced-stage CKD and zero-point-seven-nine-eight for moderate CKD, versus phecode R² of zero-point-two-five-two versus LTPI R² of zero-point-three-two-five. In the UKBB on coronary artery disease, LTPI identified one hundred thirty-nine independent risk loci versus one hundred twenty for LTFH and just fifty-one for standard GWAS. They validated the novel LTPI loci against external GWAS datasets for CAD, type-two diabetes, breast cancer, and prostate cancer — of two hundred eighty loci unique to LTPI, two hundred seventy-five replicated effect direction. They did answer the question they posed.

JORDAN That's a clean result. Now translate to a CF context.

ALEX One possible direction. For a CFTR PheWAS, the target traits we'd care about — CF-related diabetes, cystic fibrosis liver disease, meconium ileus, distal intestinal obstruction syndrome, nontuberculous mycobacterial infection — are by definition rare in the general population. The UKBB barely contains any CF cases. So the LTPI machinery as stated can't be plugged in directly because the cross-trait genetic correlations aren't available from PheWeb. But the architectural lift is: stop treating CF complications as binary case-versus-control, and instead build a multimodal phenotypic-integration score that combines the binary registry flag with continuous CFFPR fields — lab values, lung function trajectories, anthropometric z-scores, microbiology-positivity counts — into a per-patient continuous-liability estimate, then use that as the GWAS phenotype against the CFTR-WGS variant set. Plain English first: instead of asking "did this patient ever get a CFRD diagnosis," ask "what is this patient's underlying CFRD liability given everything we know about them," and run the modifier GWAS on the continuous answer. Formal version: in the CFF WGS cohort linked to CFFPR longitudinal data, construct LTPI-style continuous-liability phenotypes for the canonical CF complication set by integrating registry-recorded diagnoses with continuous lab measurements, anthropometric trajectories, and microbiology-positivity histories, then perform Regenie-based GWAS modifier scans against these continuous-liability phenotypes rather than the binary registry flags.

JORDAN And what does the paper close, what does it leave open for that direction?

ALEX What it closes: the case that going from binary to continuous phenotypes consistently improves modifier-GWAS power. It also closes the case that the trait-selection step can be automated rather than expert-curated. What it leaves open: first, LTPI assumes population-scale GWAS summaries exist for the genetic-correlation inputs. The CFFPR-CFGP cohort is too small to generate those internally and too phenotypically specialised to import them from UKBB. The substantive question for us is whether the LTPI machinery degrades gracefully when fed weaker correlation inputs — that needs to be checked, possibly by a stripped-down LTPI variant using observed phenotypic correlations rather than genetic correlations. Second, we'd need to verify which CFFPR fields are recorded with sufficient depth and longitudinal coverage to be useful as continuous nontarget traits — KNoRMA is published for FEV1 trajectory, but the analogous integrations for nutrition, microbiology, and hepatobiliary panels would need to be confirmed extant or constructed. Third, the modulator-era confound applies again — pre- and post-modulator phenotype trajectories are different distributions, and any continuous-liability construction needs to either condition on modulator exposure or restrict to a pre-modulator era stratum. Fourth, LTPI is computationally cheap when the genetic-correlation matrix already exists, but constructing one from scratch for CFFPR-specific phenotypes would be a substantial upstream effort. Fifth — and this is a gating question — the AUROCs in the paper are pulled toward the ceiling partly because nontarget traits include things that are downstream of the target disease, not just predictors of it; they explicitly recompute "Manual-thirty-two" after dropping thirty likely-consequence traits and the AUROC barely moves, but that issue would need re-evaluation in a CF setting where complications cascade tightly.
