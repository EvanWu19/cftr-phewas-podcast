One-paragraph blurb

Lennon and colleagues describe the eMERGE Network's end-to-end pipeline for returning polygenic-risk-score-based genome-informed risk assessments to twenty-five thousand diverse U.S. patients across ten chronic diseases — asthma, atrial fibrillation, breast cancer, chronic kidney disease, coronary heart disease, hypercholesterolemia, obesity, prostate cancer, and type-one and type-two diabetes. The paper covers the multi-stage funnel that narrowed twenty-three candidate conditions to ten, the principal-component-based ancestry calibration step trained on thirteen-thousand-four-hundred-seventy-five All-of-Us participants, the CLIA-grade clinical lab validation, and the deployment outcomes of the first twenty-five-hundred samples processed between July 2022 and May 2023. One possible direction this episode suggests is a CFFPR-linked clinical-grade complication-risk-assessment pipeline modeled on this template, combining locked CFGP-WGS-supported modifier loci with rare-pathogenic-variant carrier status, returned through a CLIA-grade pipeline with ancestry calibration anchored to a CFGP-internal reference panel. The open question is whether a five-thousand-person CFGP discovery cohort produces effect-size estimates stable enough to support per-individual clinical return rather than research-only output.

Plain-English glossary

  • CLIA: Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments — the U.S. federal regulatory framework that any lab returning clinical-grade results to a patient must operate under.
  • eMERGE: Electronic Medical Records and Genomics Network — the NIH-funded multi-center consortium that conducted this study.
  • GIRA: Genome-Informed Risk Assessment — the eMERGE name for the integrated clinical report combining PRS, monogenic, and clinical risk factors.
  • GWAS: Genome-Wide Association Study — a population-scale scan that compares allele frequencies between cases and controls across the whole genome.
  • Linkage Disequilibrium (LD): The statistical correlation between nearby genetic variants on the same chromosome, which differs between ancestral populations.
  • Monogenic vs polygenic: Monogenic disease is caused primarily by variants in a single gene (CFTR for cystic fibrosis); polygenic risk is the additive contribution of many small-effect variants spread across the genome.
  • PCA: Principal Component Analysis — the statistical method used to summarize genetic ancestry from genome-wide genotype data.
  • PRS: Polygenic Risk Score — a weighted sum of an individual's risk alleles across many variants, producing a single number summarizing inherited risk for a complex trait.
  • SNP: Single-Nucleotide Polymorphism — a one-letter difference in DNA between individuals.
  • z-score: A standardized statistical score expressed in units of standard deviation from the population mean.

ALEX Good morning. Today we're reading Lennon and colleagues, 2024, published in Nature Medicine, titled "Selection, optimization and validation of ten chronic disease polygenic risk scores for clinical implementation in diverse US populations." The senior author group spans the Electronic Medical Records and Genomics Network — eMERGE — which is the NIH-funded consortium that has been pushing genomic medicine into actual clinics for almost twenty years.

JORDAN And why are we reading this today, specifically?

ALEX Because this is the paper that describes — soup-to-nuts — what it actually takes to send a genetic risk score back to a real doctor at a real clinic, in a real laboratory that complies with U.S. clinical-lab regulations. If we ever want to imagine putting any kind of CFTR-related polygenic prediction in front of a CF clinician through the CF Foundation Patient Registry pipeline, this is the operational template we'd be following.

JORDAN Before we get into the paper, slow me down. What's a polygenic risk score?

ALEX A polygenic risk score — usually abbreviated PRS — is a single number that summarizes a person's predicted risk for a common disease, computed by adding up the contributions of many, many genetic variants across the whole genome. Each variant on its own typically has a tiny effect. Together, weighted by their effect sizes from a genome-wide association study, they can give a meaningful estimate of inherited risk.

JORDAN So it's the opposite of CFTR.

ALEX Yes, almost exactly the opposite. CFTR is a *monogenic* gene — one gene, very large effects, two bad copies and you have cystic fibrosis. A PRS for, say, coronary heart disease pulls signal from thousands of variants, each contributing tiny shifts. The math is "add them all up." The shorthand contrast in the field is monogenic versus polygenic.

JORDAN And what's a GWAS?

ALEX GWAS is genome-wide association study — you take a cohort, you measure a trait or disease, you genotype everybody, and you scan every common variant in the genome looking for ones that differ between cases and controls. The output is millions of SNP-by-disease association statistics, and PRS construction takes those statistics as input. SNP just means single-nucleotide polymorphism — a one-letter difference in DNA.

JORDAN What about CLIA?

ALEX CLIA stands for Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments. It's the U.S. federal regulatory framework that any lab returning clinical-grade results to a patient has to operate under. If you generate a number on a research server in a basement, it's not CLIA-compliant. If a CFFPR-linked PRS were ever returned to a CF clinician for a real patient decision, it would have to come out of a CLIA-certified pipeline. That distinction matters because most of the methods we've been reading for this podcast are not CLIA-compliant — they're research workflows. This paper is one of the very few that does both.

JORDAN And eMERGE itself?

ALEX eMERGE is a multi-center consortium of U.S. health systems with linked electronic medical records and biobanks. They've been running for about eighteen years. The third major phase of eMERGE focused on polygenic risk score implementation. That's what this paper reports.

ALEX The background is that polygenic risk scores have been getting better and better in predictive performance, but several real-world barriers have blocked them from clinical use. The first barrier is that almost all the source genome-wide association studies were done in people of European ancestry. So when you compute a PRS for a person of African or Hispanic or Asian descent, the score is less accurate. Sometimes much less accurate. That's a published, well-characterized problem.

JORDAN Which means deploying these scores carelessly could actually make health disparities worse, not better.

ALEX That's exactly the worry the eMERGE steering committee starts the paper with. They cite the Martin and colleagues 2019 paper which laid out this disparity-amplification risk in detail. The second barrier is interpretation — even when a score is statistically valid, the clinician receiving it has to know what to do with it, the patient has to understand it, and the regulator has to bless the workflow. The third barrier is calibration: PRS distributions have different means and standard deviations in different ancestry groups, partly for true biological reasons and partly because of differences in allele frequencies and linkage-disequilibrium patterns between populations.

JORDAN Linkage disequilibrium?

ALEX Two nearby variants tend to travel together on the same chromosome across generations. The correlation structure between variants in a region is called linkage disequilibrium, or LD. The LD structure is different in different ancestry groups, which means a PRS trained on European-LD patterns will fit imperfectly when applied to a different ancestry. The eMERGE Network set out to take a stack of published PRS models, audit them, optimize them where possible, and build a pipeline that returns a clinically validated score to twenty-five thousand participants enrolled across diverse U.S. healthcare-system populations, with six of the ten recruitment sites targeting seventy-five percent minority enrollment.

JORDAN Okay, what did they actually do?

ALEX They ran a multi-stage funnel. They started with twenty-three candidate conditions. The audit considered analytic viability — does the original PRS model have what we need to deploy it? Feasibility — do we have diverse validation cohorts available? Clinical actionability — is there a screening or treatment strategy that changes for a high-risk patient? Translatability — does the score work across diverse populations? Heritability — published SNP-based heritability for the ten in-scope conditions ranged from three percent to fifty-eight percent. They considered conditions for both pediatric and adult populations with disease onset ages from birth to sixty-five-plus.

JORDAN And the funnel narrowed.

ALEX In a first review they dropped six conditions for reasons like low prevalence in some ancestral groups for age-related macular degeneration, lack of diverse validation datasets for primary open-angle glaucoma and Crohn's disease and rheumatoid arthritis, and lack of an EHR-extractable phenotype algorithm for bone mineral density. A few months later they dropped five more — depression, ischemic stroke, hypertension, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease, and lupus — for various reasons including low predictive value and ethical concerns about returning genetic risk for low-prevalence conditions. They ended up with twelve candidates, eventually narrowed to ten implemented. The final ten are asthma, atrial fibrillation, breast cancer, chronic kidney disease, coronary heart disease, hypercholesterolemia, obesity, prostate cancer, type 1 diabetes, and type 2 diabetes.

JORDAN How did they handle the ancestry calibration problem you mentioned?

ALEX This is the cleverest part of the methods. They used a principal-component-analysis-based calibration that models *both* the mean and the variance of each PRS score as a function of genetic ancestry. The prior PCA-based method from Khera and colleagues 2018 calibrated only the mean — they extended it to variance because they found that some conditions show ancestry-dependent variance, which would have produced wrongly-many or wrongly-few "high-PRS" calls in some ancestry groups. The calibration parameters were trained and tested using thirteen-thousand-four-hundred-seventy-five All of Us Research Program participants, with permission from the AoU program. The output is a z-score that's been adjusted for ancestry, and an individual's high-risk status is defined by crossing a per-condition percentile threshold.

JORDAN Push back on something for me. Wouldn't ancestry-specific scores — separate scores tuned to each population — be more accurate?

ALEX That's a fair pushback and the authors anticipated it. Their reasoning for going with calibration rather than ancestry-specific scores is operational. Returning ancestry-specific risk estimates requires patients to self-report ancestry accurately, which they often can't, and requires multiple ancestry-specific reports for each condition. And for patients of mixed ancestry, there's no clean ancestry-specific report at all. So they made a deliberate tradeoff: less statistical optimality per-population, but a single coherent report pipeline that handles admixed individuals consistently. Whether that's the right tradeoff probably depends on the magnitude of the per-population improvement you'd get, which they don't fully quantify here.

JORDAN What were the results?

ALEX The clinical-pipeline validation hit very high technical precision and accuracy. Pearson correlation between PRS computed from imputed array genotypes versus 30X whole genome sequencing on seventy reference samples ran from ninety-three percent for breast cancer up to ninety-nine-point-five percent for obesity and type-one-diabetes. Pipeline repeatability was one hundred percent across ten end-to-end pipeline runs of the same seventy specimens. Reproducibility z-score standard deviations were all below 0.005, which is technically negligible. Site missingness — the fraction of the original score's SNPs that drop out of the final imputed dataset — ran from 0.32 percent for breast cancer to 2.97 percent for prostate cancer and type-one-diabetes. The odds ratios for high versus not-high PRS were statistically significant in at least two ancestry groups for every condition, with some standout numbers like a high-PRS prostate cancer odds ratio above twelve in European-ancestry men and above twenty in African-American men — type-one-diabetes also shows very large odds ratios, consistent with the strong known HLA-region contribution to T1D.

JORDAN And the deployment.

ALEX Between July 2022 and May 2023 the pipeline processed twenty-five-hundred clinical samples — about ten percent of the planned cohort. Of those, just under twenty-one percent had a high PRS for at least one condition; just under three percent had a high PRS for two conditions; and a handful had high PRS for three. Self-reported race-ethnicity distribution was deliberately diverse: thirty-three percent White, thirty-three percent Black or African American, twenty-five percent Hispanic, five percent Asian, and the remainder various smaller groups. The observed-versus-expected high-PRS counts per condition were largely concordant, with the only statistically significant deviation being type-two-diabetes where fewer high-PRS calls were observed than expected, possibly reflecting cohort enrichment for healthier-than-average participants.

ALEX It closes the workflow question.
