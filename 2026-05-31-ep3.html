Blurb

Gong, Wang, Sun, Strug and colleagues (PLoS Genetics, 2019) ran a meconium-ileus GWAS in 6,770 patients from the International CF Gene Modifier Consortium (Canada/US/France) and integrated it with tissue-specific expression to ask not just which genes modify cystic fibrosis but in which tissue they act. They confirmed SLC6A14 (chrX) and SLC26A9 (chr1), discovered a new meconium-ileus locus at ATP12A (chr13, p=3.83×10⁻¹⁰), and flagged PRSS1, TARS, and CEBPB at FDR significance. The methodological contribution is the Simple Sum (SS), a frequentist, summary-statistics-based colocalization framework that can contrast tissues or genes — answering "does this colocalize more in pancreas than intestine?" Key result: for SLC6A14, SLC26A9, and ATP12A the meconium-ileus signal colocalized with pancreatic expression (not intestinal), and SLC6A14 split by trait — meconium ileus → pancreas eQTL, CF lung severity → nasal-airway eQTL. Same gene, two CF traits, two tissues. Proposed direction for the CFTR project: pair any pleiotropic modifier from a phenome-wide scan with SS-style tissue-stratified colocalization to distinguish one-tissue from multi-tissue mechanisms. Open: whether phenome-wide CF complications have the case counts and tissue-matched eQTL data (airway panel here was only 63 people) to support colocalization.

Methods to lift

  • Simple Sum (SS) — frequentist, summary-statistics-only colocalization with analytic p-value; uniquely can contrast tissues or genes (more colocalization in tissue A vs B). Successor SS2 used in Wang 2022.
  • GEE with exchangeable correlation — GWAS regression that accounts for sibling relatedness; adjusts for site, platform, principal components.
  • Trait × tissue eQTL integration — overlay GWAS LocusZoom signal with tissue-specific eQTL tracks (GTEx v7 + custom-computed v6-only genes) to read off tissue of action.
  • CF-specific airway eQTL — RNA-seq of nasal epithelia (n=63 CF patients) as an airway-surrogate panel, since GTEx lung-collection protocol misses CF-relevant surface epithelium.
  • F508del-homozygote sensitivity analysis — restricting to 4,343 F508del homozygotes increased ATP12A effect size (OR 1.7 vs 1.55); CFTR-genotype dependence flagged for follow-up (GxG interaction p=0.057, not significant).
  • Gene-set / apical-membrane enrichment — replicates prior finding that apical-plasma-membrane gene set is enriched for meconium-ileus variants.

Proposed direction (conditional)

ALEX Episode three, and today we come home to cystic fibrosis. The paper is Gong, Wang, Sun, Strug and colleagues, 2019, in PLoS Genetics. The title is "Genetic association and transcriptome integration identify contributing genes and tissues at cystic fibrosis modifier loci." This one is load-bearing for the project, because it introduces the colocalization method this CF group has built its whole modifier programme around, and because it asks a question most genetics papers skip: not just which gene, but in which tissue.

JORDAN Then start from zero. What is a modifier gene in cystic fibrosis, what is meconium ileus, and what is an eQTL? Don't assume I know any of it.

ALEX Good list. Cystic fibrosis is caused by mutations in one gene, CFTR — the cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator, a channel that moves chloride across cell surfaces. But here's the puzzle: two patients with the exact same CFTR mutation can have wildly different disease — one with mild lungs, one with severe, one with a gut blockage at birth, one without. The CFTR mutation sets the stage, but other genes elsewhere in the genome tune the severity. Those tuning genes are modifier genes. Meconium ileus is one of the traits they modify: meconium is a newborn's first stool, and in about 15% of CF babies it's so thick it blocks the intestine at birth — a surgical emergency. It's heavily genetic, heritability around 88%, and it can differ even between CF siblings with the identical CFTR mutation, which is exactly why it's a clean target for modifier hunting.

JORDAN And eQTL?

ALEX An eQTL is an expression quantitative trait locus. Plain version: it's a spot in the genome where which DNA letter you carry changes how much a nearby gene is switched on — how much messenger RNA it makes — in a particular tissue. It matters here because most disease-associated variants don't sit inside a gene changing the protein; they sit in regulatory DNA, nudging gene dosage up or down. So if a CF-modifier variant is also an eQTL for a nearby gene, that's a clue the variant works by tuning that gene's expression. And the punchline of this paper is that the tissue where the eQTL acts tells you where the biology happens.

JORDAN So how did they set it up?

ALEX The substrate is the International CF Gene Modifier Consortium — 6,770 people with CF from Canada, the US, and France. They ran a genome-wide association study, a GWAS, for meconium ileus: scanning roughly 8.7 million genotyped-and-imputed variants — imputed meaning statistically filled in from a reference panel — testing each for association with the trait. Because the data include siblings, they used a method called generalized estimating equations with an exchangeable correlation structure, which is just a way of running the regression that accounts for the fact that related people aren't statistically independent. They adjusted for study site, genotyping platform, and population structure. Then, for the loci that came up, they pulled in tissue-specific expression data — from GTEx, a public atlas of eQTLs across human tissues — plus their own RNA sequencing of nasal-airway cells from 63 of the CF patients, and asked whether the disease signal and the expression signal line up at the same variants.

JORDAN And how do you tell whether two signals "line up"? That sounds slippery.

ALEX It is, and that's the methodological contribution. The danger is that a disease variant and an eQTL just happen to sit near each other by chance, in a region where lots of variants are correlated — high linkage disequilibrium. Asking whether they're truly the same signal is called colocalization. Existing tools were mostly Bayesian and made strong assumptions about a single causal variant. Gong and colleagues built a frequentist alternative they call the Simple Sum, the SS. It uses only summary statistics, computes a colocalization p-value analytically, and — the genuinely useful part — it can contrast tissues or genes against each other, letting you ask not just "does this colocalize" but "does it colocalize more in pancreas than in intestine." That comparison ability is what makes the tissue question answerable.

JORDAN Push back on a design choice for me.

ALEX The nasal-epithelium eQTL panel was 63 people. That's a real, CF-relevant airway tissue — far better than GTEx lung, which is collected in a way that mostly misses the airway surface cells that matter in CF — but 63 is small for eQTL discovery, so power to detect airway-specific regulation is limited, and a null result there can't be read as "no effect." They're transparent about it, but it's the soft spot: the airway story rests on a thin sample even as the meconium-ileus GWAS rests on thousands.

JORDAN What did they find?

ALEX Three genome-wide-significant meconium-ileus loci, near the genes SLC6A14 on the X chromosome, SLC26A9 on chromosome 1, and a new one — ATP12A on chromosome 13, at a p-value of 3.8 times ten-to-the-minus-10. ATP12A encodes a proton pump, and it was a fresh discovery for meconium ileus. Three more loci passed a softer false-discovery-rate cut: PRSS1, TARS, and CEBPB. Then the tissue integration, which is the real story. For SLC6A14, SLC26A9, and ATP12A, the expression signals that colocalized with the meconium-ileus association were in the pancreas — even though meconium ileus is a disease of the intestine. The intestinal eQTLs didn't line up; the pancreatic ones did. And at the PRSS1 locus — PRSS1 is cationic trypsinogen, a pancreatic digestive enzyme — the meconium-ileus risk allele was the same allele tied to lower PRSS1 expression and previously linked to pancreatitis, reinforcing a pancreatic mechanism for a gut phenotype.

JORDAN And the lung connection you mentioned?

ALEX That's the cleanest demonstration of the tissue idea. The SLC6A14 locus on the X chromosome is associated with both meconium ileus and CF lung-disease severity — but the two associations peak at slightly different places. Using the Simple Sum, they showed the meconium-ileus signal colocalizes with SLC6A14 regulation in the pancreas, while the lung-disease signal colocalizes with SLC6A14 regulation in the nasal airway epithelium. Same gene, two CF traits, two different tissues of action. That's the headline: a modifier isn't just a gene, it's a gene-in-a-tissue, and pleiotropy across CF traits can run through entirely different organs. So did they answer their question? Yes — they identified contributing genes and, for several, named the likely tissue, which is more than a standard GWAS delivers.

JORDAN So what concrete direction does this hand the CFTR project?

ALEX Plain English first. If Runyu ever finds that a single modifier variant is associated with several different CF complications — the essence of a phenome-wide scan — the obvious question will be whether that's one mechanism touching many organs, or different mechanisms in different tissues that happen to share a variant. This paper shows you can actually test that, by colocalizing each trait's signal against tissue-specific expression. The concrete direction: for any pleiotropic modifier surfaced in a CF phenome scan, follow up with tissue-resolved colocalization — using the Simple Sum framework and tissue-matched expression panels — to ask whether the shared variant acts through one tissue or several. Formal one-sentence version: one possible direction this episode suggests is to pair any phenome-wide CFTR-modifier association with Simple-Sum-style, tissue-stratified eQTL colocalization to distinguish single-tissue from multi-tissue mechanisms underlying cross-trait pleiotropy.

JORDAN Conditional on that direction — what does the paper close, and what's left open?

ALEX Conditional on a tissue-resolved-colocalization direction, it closes the methods gap: the Simple Sum is published, summary-statistics-based, and explicitly designed to contrast tissues — and we've already seen its successor, SS2, applied in a later CF paper, so the lineage is established and usable. It also gives concrete prior expectations — pancreas for several GI-relevant modifiers, airway for lung severity — that a new analysis could test against. What it leaves open is everything about scale and substrate. This was meconium ileus, a high-heritability trait with thousands of cases; most phenome-wide CF complications will have far fewer events and weaker signals, and colocalization needs a decently powered association signal to even begin. It also depends entirely on having tissue-matched eQTL data — and the most CF-relevant tissue, airway epithelium, was only 63 people here. What Runyu would need to check, before committing, is whether the CF cohort he'd work in has, or can be linked to, eQTL data in the relevant tissues at usable sample sizes; whether the modifier signals in a phenome scan would be strong enough to colocalize at all; and whether the traits of interest have the case counts to support it. None of that is settled, and none of it presumes anyone has yet examined the CF sequence data.

ALEX Tomorrow we'll pick up another thread from the biobank-methods stack — likely a rare-variant or interaction-mapping paper — and keep building the toolkit. Today's one-line takeaway: a CF modifier is not just a gene but a gene-in-a-tissue, and the Simple Sum is how this field tells which organ a shared variant is really working in.
