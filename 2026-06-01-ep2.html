Blurb

Zou et al. (2026, Nature) present the largest copy-number-variant phenome-wide association study to date: CNVs called from 470,727 UK Biobank whole genomes tested against 2,941 plasma proteins, 13,336 binary clinical phenotypes, and 1,911 quantitative traits. After DRAGEN calling, strict QC, and orthogonal validation (935 trios, HWE, LD with small variants), they report hundreds of CNV–protein, CNV–disease, and CNV–trait associations, recover textbook positive controls (PMP22/CMT1A; PDZK1/urate; MSH2/colorectal cancer), and introduce a CNV+PTV gene-collapsing model that adds thousands of associations and clarifies causal genes within multigene CNVs. The proposed direction for this project: build and validate a CNV call set for CFTR and known CF modifier loci in the CFF WGS cohort, then run a focused CNV PheWAS against registry phenotypes — asking whether copy-number variation, not just SNVs, modifies CF. Central open question: whether short-read CF WGS can call CFTR-region CNVs reliably, and whether a few-thousand-patient cohort is powered for any CNV testing beyond gene-collapsed bundles.

Plain-English glossary (alphabetical)

  • cis / trans (pQTL): genetic spot near the protein's own gene (cis) vs far away/another chromosome (trans).
  • CNV (copy number variant): a stretch of DNA present in too many (duplication) or too few (deletion) copies; kilobases to megabases long.
  • Collapsing (gene-level) analysis: pooling CNVs (or variants) hitting the same gene to gain statistical power.
  • CNV+PTV model: combining copy-number deletions with protein-truncating point mutations in one gene, since both disrupt function.
  • DRAGEN: hardware-accelerated genome analysis pipeline used to call the CNVs.
  • Gene cluster / passenger gene: a large CNV overlaps several genes; reporting at cluster level guards against crediting innocent neighbors.
  • HWE (Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium): expected population genotype proportions; used as a CNV-quality check.
  • LD (linkage disequilibrium): correlation between nearby variants; common CNVs tracked tightly (R²≈0.96) with small variants.
  • PheWAS (phenome-wide association study): test one genetic feature against the entire catalog of traits/diseases.
  • pQTL (protein quantitative trait locus): genetic spot influencing a blood protein's abundance.
  • PTV (protein-truncating variant): a small mutation that cuts a protein short, usually breaking the gene.
  • Trio validation: checking CNV inheritance in parent–child families to estimate error (Mendelian-violation rate).
  • WGS: whole genome sequencing — reads more of the genome and maps CNV breakpoints better than exome/array.

Methods to lift

  • DRAGEN v3.7.8 CNV calling — germline CNV calls from WGS; restricted to >10 kb after QC (80,147 dups / 102,717 dels).
  • Trio / orthogonal validation — 935 parent–child trios (median 4.1% Mendelian violations), HWE checks, LD-with-small-variant concordance, NCBI common-CNV frequency agreement.
  • Three genetic models — dominant deletion, recessive deletion, dominant duplication, tested across proteins/binary/quantitative phenotypes.
  • Gene-level collapsing — aggregate CNVs per gene; report at gene-cluster level to mitigate passenger-gene effects.
  • CNV+PTV integrated collapsing — merges CNV deletions with qualifying PTVs per gene; recovered associations missed by either alone and attenuated passenger signals.
  • Pan-ancestry meta-analysis — ancestry-stratified PheWAS + meta-analysis surfacing ancestry-specific signals (e.g., sickle-cell in AFR, α-thalassaemia in EAS).
  • CNV–SNP LD / conditional analyses — testing whether CNV signals are tagged by known GWAS variants.

ALEX Welcome back. Today's paper is a 2026 Nature article, first author Xiangzhen Zou and colleagues, titled "Phenome-wide analysis of copy number variants in 470,727 UK Biobank genomes." It is, basically, the biggest map yet of how big chunks of duplicated or deleted DNA connect to human disease.

ALEX Because it's the gold-standard design at gold-standard scale, and it covers a kind of genetic variation we haven't talked about much — copy number variants. If we ever build a CFTR phenome-wide scan, this is the template to benchmark against. It even flags that CF-region duplications and deletions are worth checking. So today is partly "learn the method," partly "see what the frontier looks like."

JORDAN Then let's define everything. What's a copy number variant?

ALEX A copy number variant — CNV — is when a stretch of DNA is present in too many or too few copies. Instead of a single misspelled letter, a whole paragraph is duplicated or deleted. They range from thousands to millions of letters long. A deletion removes a stretch; a duplication adds an extra copy. Because they're big, they can hit several genes at once and have outsized effects. Now the other terms. PheWAS — phenome-wide association study — flips the usual genetics question around: instead of picking one disease and scanning the genome, you take one genetic feature and test it against the entire catalog of traits and diseases at once. The "phenome" is that whole catalog of measurable traits. WGS, whole genome sequencing, is reading all the DNA. A pQTL — protein quantitative trait locus — is a genetic spot that influences how much of a particular protein floats in your blood; cis means the spot is near the gene for that protein, trans means it's far away or on another chromosome. A PTV, protein-truncating variant, is a small mutation that cuts a protein short — usually breaking it; CNV deletions often act like PTVs because both knock out gene function. And odds ratio and p-value we've covered — risk multiplier and surprise meter, with the genome-wide bar near ten-to-the-minus-eight.

JORDAN So the whole trick is: one CNV, tested against thousands of things at once?

ALEX Exactly. And they tested against three things at once: 2,941 blood protein levels, 13,336 yes/no clinical diagnoses, and 1,911 quantitative measurements like blood counts and biomarkers.

JORDAN Why bother with the protein measurements? Most genetics studies I've heard about just use diseases.

ALEX Great question, and the proteins are secretly the cleverest part. A disease association tells you a CNV matters but not why. A protein association tells you the mechanism — if deleting a stretch of DNA lowers a specific protein in the blood, you've caught the gene in the act of doing less. They call these protein associations a validation layer: deletions should generally lower the nearby protein, duplications raise it, because you've changed the gene dosage. When that dosage logic holds, you trust the call. So the proteins do double duty — they confirm the CNV calls are real and they hand you a mechanism. For us, the lesson is that pairing a genetic scan with a molecular readout like protein levels turns a list of associations into a list of explanations.

JORDAN What was the actual problem they were solving? Haven't people studied CNVs before?

ALEX They have, but with weaker tools — microarrays or exome sequencing, which read only the gene-coding slices. Let me unpack why that's a limitation. A microarray is an older genotyping chip that checks a fixed menu of pre-chosen spots — it can sense roughly that a region is duplicated or deleted, but it's blurry about exactly where the CNV starts and stops. Exome sequencing reads only the one to two percent of the genome that codes for protein, so it's blind to CNVs that sit between genes or in regulatory DNA. Whole genome sequencing reads it all, so you can map a CNV's exact boundaries and catch the non-coding ones. CNVs were hard to study for three reasons the authors name: the calls were often low quality, the diseases they cause are heterogeneous — meaning the same CNV can produce different problems in different people — and rare CNVs need enormous samples to detect. UK Biobank with half a million whole genomes plus linked health records — and blood proteins on about fifty thousand of them — is uniquely able to beat all three problems at once. The unknown they're attacking: across the full sweep of human disease, which duplications and deletions matter, and can we read their effects through the proteins they change?

JORDAN Why would a CNV cause different problems in different people? Same DNA change, same result, no?

ALEX You'd think so, but it's one of the deep puzzles in genetics — it's called variable expressivity, and it's central to our whole CF project too. The same deletion can give one person a serious illness and barely touch another, because the rest of their genome, their other variants, their environment, all modulate the outcome. CF itself is the poster child: the same CFTR mutation, wildly different disease severity. So a CNV that's "heterogeneous" is really a CNV whose effect is being tuned by modifiers — the exact thing we hunt. Seeing how this paper handles that blur at scale is part of why it's worth our time.

JORDAN How did they actually get the CNVs out of the genomes?

ALEX They called CNVs in 470,727 whole genomes using a tool called DRAGEN — a fast hardware-accelerated genome analysis pipeline — then applied strict quality filters and kept only CNVs bigger than ten thousand letters. That left about 80,000 duplications and 103,000 deletions across the non-sex chromosomes. ALEX's pushback here is actually praise for their honesty: they didn't just trust the calls. They validated against 935 parent-child trios — families where you can check whether a child's CNV plausibly came from a parent — and found a low error rate, about four percent Mendelian violations. They checked that common CNVs sat in expected population proportions and tracked tightly with nearby single-letter variants. That orthogonal checking is the part to copy.

ALEX Three genetic models — does one deletion copy matter, do you need both copies deleted, does a duplication matter — tested against all those proteins and traits. And a clever twist they call CNV-plus-PTV — combining copy-number deletions with protein-truncating point mutations in the same gene, since both break the gene, to squeeze out more signal.

JORDAN Hold on — what's a "hotspot," and what's 16p11.2?

ALEX A hotspot is a region of the genome where the local DNA architecture makes deletions and duplications happen over and over, in unrelated people, because repeated sequences there cause the copying machinery to slip. 16p11.2 — chromosome 16, short arm, band 11.2 — is the most famous one; recurrent CNVs there are linked to autism, developmental delay, and obesity. 17p12 is another, home to the nerve-disease gene we'll mention. The point is these aren't random — certain neighborhoods churn out CNVs, so they dominate any CNV scan.

JORDAN Before we wrap — that CNV-plus-PTV idea. Walk me through why combining two variant types helps, because it sounds like it could just add noise.

ALEX It's the most transferable trick in the paper, so let's nail it. Picture a gene. It can be broken two ways: a copy-number deletion wipes out the whole stretch, or a protein-truncating point mutation cuts the protein short mid-sequence. Different molecular events, same end result — a non-working gene. If you test those two kinds of damage separately, you split your evidence in half and might miss the signal in both. Pool every person whose copy of that gene is broken either way, and you get one bigger, cleaner group of "this gene is knocked out" carriers to test. That's the boost. The example they give: deleting a gene called IFI35 only showed its protein effect when they combined the CNV and PTV carriers — neither alone reached the bar.
