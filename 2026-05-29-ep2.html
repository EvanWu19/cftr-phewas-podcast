Blurb

Lisa Bastarache, sole author and one of the original architects of phecodes at Vanderbilt, reviews the design, validation, and applications of phecodes — manually curated groupings of ICD codes into ~1,867 clinically meaningful phenotypes (version 1.2). The review traces phecodes from their PheWAS origins, through the ICD-10-CM remapping, to PheRS (phenotype risk score), which combines OMIM clinical descriptions, HPO mapping, and phecode rollup into a continuous similarity score against any Mendelian disease. The cystic fibrosis example is used throughout: in one cited study, 8 of 10 adults eventually diagnosed with CF had a CF-PheRS above the 95th percentile prior to diagnosis. One proposed direction: implement a CFTR-PheRS framework across the CFF Patient Registry-linked WGS cohort as both phenotyping QC and a continuous analytic outcome. Open question: whether the CFFPR carries longitudinal ICD codes at sufficient granularity for phecode computation, or whether a registry-native HPO-to-CF-complication mapping is required.

Plain-English glossary

  • CMS (Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services): U.S. agency overseeing medical-coding standards and reimbursement.
  • CM (Clinical Modification): U.S. extension to ICD with finer disease-specific coding granularity.
  • EHR (electronic health record): Hospital's digital patient record with visits, diagnoses, labs, medications, notes.
  • Exclude range: Phecode field defining the set of related phecodes whose presence makes a patient ineligible to serve as a control for the index phecode.
  • F1 score: Harmonic mean of precision and recall; a single metric balancing false positives and false negatives.
  • GWAS (genome-wide association study): Scan of many SNPs against one phenotype.
  • Hierarchical phecodes: Three-digit parent (e.g., 008 intestinal infection), child (008.5 bacterial enteritis), grandchild (008.52 C. difficile infection).
  • HPO (Human Phenotype Ontology): Controlled vocabulary for clinical features, originally built to annotate OMIM.
  • ICD-9-CM / ICD-10-CM: Successive U.S.-extended versions of WHO's diagnostic coding system; transition occurred October 2015.
  • Leaf code: Phecode with no children — the most specific level in the hierarchy.
  • Mendelian disease: Single-gene disease with high penetrance, traditionally studied in families.
  • OMIM (Online Mendelian Inheritance in Man): Catalog of inherited human diseases with clinical descriptions; >5,000 gene-disease entries.
  • Phecode: Manually curated ICD-code group representing a clinically meaningful phenotype.
  • PheNorm / MAP / PheCAP: Machine-learning hybrid phenotyping methods that integrate ICD codes with labs and clinical notes.
  • PheProb: Method that computes a per-patient probability of a phenotype from diagnosis-code counts, replacing binary case-cutoffs.
  • PheRS (phenotype risk score): Continuous score measuring an individual's phecode similarity to an OMIM Mendelian disease description.
  • PheWAS (phenome-wide association study): Scan of one variable (usually a SNP) against many phecodes.
  • Pleiotropy: One genetic variant influences multiple phenotypes.
  • Rule of two: Default case definition requiring a patient to have the phecode on two or more unique encounter dates.
  • SNP (single-nucleotide polymorphism): Single-base genetic variant.
  • Upcoding: Inappropriate assignment of a higher-reimbursement diagnosis code; a major source of ICD inaccuracy.
  • UMLS (Unified Medical Language System): NIH master mapping resource between medical vocabularies.

Methods to lift

  • Phecode catalog v1.2 — 1,867 phenotypes, hierarchical, freely available at https://www.phewascatalog.org/phecodes.
  • Rule of two — default phecode case definition; require 2+ unique-date phecode instances. Default in PheWAS R package; tune for acute conditions.
  • PheWAS scan — one SNP × ~1,500 phecodes with sufficient cases, Bonferroni or FDR multiple-testing correction; default toolkit is the PheWAS R package.
  • Cox-regression PheWAS — replaces logistic regression with time-to-event modeling; improves replication of known genetic associations by exploiting longitudinal EHR data.
  • HPO-to-phecode mapping — the bridge that lets OMIM Mendelian disease descriptions be auto-translated into EHR-extractable phecodes.
  • PheRS — prevalence-weighted, continuous Mendelian-similarity score per patient; tolerates partial phenotypic overlap and missing EHR data.
  • PheNorm / MAP / PheCAP — hybrid ML-based phenotyping methods that layer labs and NLP-extracted features on top of phecodes.
  • PheProb — replaces binary phecode case cutoffs with a continuous probability based on diagnosis-code counts.

GWAS, genome-wide association study, is the inverse, scanning one phenotype across many SNPs. SNP is single-nucleotide polymorphism, a single-base genetic variant. HPO, the Human Phenotype Ontology, is a controlled vocabulary built originally to annotate the clinical descriptions in OMIM. PheRS, phenotype risk score, is a continuous score measuring how closely an individual's phecode profile matches the clinical description of a Mendelian disease. CMS is the U.S. Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services. UMLS is the Unified Medical Language System, the NIH's master mapping resource between medical vocabularies.

JORDAN Okay. So now what is the problem.

ALEX The problem is that EHR data was built for two things: patient care and billing. Researchers want a third thing, phenotype definitions for genetic studies. Pulling that out of the EHR is hard because the data is fragmented, contradictory, noisy, and uses raw ICD codes that have far too much granularity for most genetic questions. Type 1 diabetes alone has twenty ICD-9-CM codes and one hundred thirteen ICD-10-CM codes. If you want to do a genetic association study with a single "type 1 diabetes" phenotype, you need a way to collapse all those codes into one binary case-control label. Doing it manually for every phenotype across thousands of phenotypes is impossible. Phecodes solve that problem.

JORDAN That makes sense. What is the history?

ALEX Phecodes started at Vanderbilt around twenty ten as "PheWAS codes," used as a quick way to define large numbers of phenotypes at once for biobank analyses. They renamed them in twenty fifteen. The grouping was done manually by clinicians and researchers, informed by an existing schema called the Clinical Classification Software, CCS, and by the actual frequencies of codes in EHRs at multiple medical centers. When U.S. hospitals switched from ICD-9 to ICD-10 in twenty fifteen, the team had to build a new map; they did that automatically using the CCS general equivalence mappings, UMLS mappings, and then validated the new map with genetic replication studies. The current version, 1.2, condenses about fifteen thousand five hundred ICD-9 codes and ninety thousand ICD-10 codes into one thousand eight hundred sixty-seven phecodes. The codes are hierarchical: a three-digit parent like 008 means "intestinal infection," 008.5 is the child "bacterial enteritis," 008.52 is the grandchild "C. difficile infection." Phecodes without children are called leaf codes. Each phecode also carries an exclude range, defining who is ineligible to be a control.

JORDAN And how do you actually decide who is a case?

ALEX That is a key methodological choice. ICD codes are inaccurate, sometimes wildly so, with reported accuracies ranging from below fifty percent to nearly one hundred. Errors come from typos, upcoding for reimbursement, and the fundamental fact that a diagnosis is a probabilistic statement that evolves over time. The default phecode case definition uses the rule of two: require a patient to have the phecode on two or more unique encounter dates. In a six thousand patient validation study, that maximized the average F1 score, a measure that balances precision and recall. ALEX flag, pushback: the rule of two only maximized F1 for one of the four tested phenotypes in that original study. It can be a poor choice for acute or time-limited conditions, where one encounter is the truth. The author calls out PheProb as a method that replaces the binary cutoff with a continuous probability based on diagnosis-code counts.

JORDAN So PheWAS itself. What is the loop?

ALEX A PheWAS takes one input variable, usually a single SNP, and tests it for association against every phecode in the cohort with enough cases. Each test gets a p-value; you correct for multiple testing across roughly fifteen hundred phecodes using Bonferroni or false discovery rate. Hits reveal pleiotropy, where one variant influences multiple phenotypes. PheWAS has extended past SNPs: it has been run on HLA alleles, predicted gene expression, functional variants, and Mendelian randomization instruments. People also run PheWAS on nongenetic variables, sleep quality, race, healthcare cost, even health among musicians. Recent methodological extensions add temporal information through Cox regression instead of logistic regression and account for clinic-visit context.

JORDAN And PheRS, the phenotype risk score?

ALEX PheRS flips the direction. Instead of scanning the EHR for associations with a variant, you take a known Mendelian disease, look up its phenotypic description in OMIM, map each clinical feature through HPO to a phecode, and then compute a continuous score per patient measuring how closely their phecode profile matches that disease description. Features are weighted by their prevalence so that rare findings carry more signal. Crucially, PheRS allows partial matches. The cystic fibrosis example is the one Bastarache uses repeatedly: the HPO term "exocrine pancreatic insufficiency" maps to the broader phecode 577, "diseases of the pancreas," because there is no specific phecode for exocrine pancreatic insufficiency. That collapses specificity but preserves sensitivity. In the proof-of-concept study, PheRS for one thousand two hundred four Mendelian diseases was used to scan six thousand rare genetic variants, replicated in external cohorts. In one striking case, eight out of ten adults eventually diagnosed with cystic fibrosis already had a CF PheRS above the ninety-fifth percentile before the formal diagnosis was made.

JORDAN Why does CF keep showing up as the example in a methods review?

ALEX Two reasons, both deliberate. First, CFTR is the gene that put Mendelian disease research on the map in nineteen eighty-nine, so it is a natural reference point for any author thinking about how to bridge Mendelian genetics and biobank-scale phenotyping. Second, CF has variable expressivity that PheRS handles gracefully: some CFTR variants cause classical CF with lung, pancreatic, and reproductive features, while others cause only single-organ disease like congenital absence of the vas deferens or chronic pancreatitis. A binary case-control phenotype loses that gradient entirely, but a continuous score preserves it. A later citation in the review, Zhong and colleagues, found that genetically predicted CFTR expression correlated with CF PheRS even in people without a CF diagnosis, which is a clean piece of evidence that the score is picking up real biological signal not just diagnostic noise.
