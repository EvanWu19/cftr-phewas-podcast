Blurb

Virolainen et al. 2023 (Genes & Immunity) is a Cincinnati Children's review of gene-environment interactions (G × E) across four environmental categories — environmental toxicants, pollution, viruses, and sex chromosome composition. Anchored on Ottman's four-model framework, the review focuses on the synergistic model where genotype and environment together produce more risk than either alone. Examples include AS3MT variants × arsenic exposure for arsenic toxicity, DAPP1 × diesel exhaust for asthma, EBNA2 × human autoimmune-risk variants for lupus/MS, IRF7 rare loss-of-function × influenza/COVID for severe respiratory disease, and XX-versus-XY × infectious-disease severity. The episode suggests one possible direction for the CFTR PheWAS: a locked-locus G × E scan in CF Genome Project WGS anchored on previously-published CF modifier loci, tested against pre-specified environmental categories using CFFPR longitudinal data — conditional on a sample-size audit, environmental-variable inventory, and a modulator-era stratification plan.

Plain-English glossary

  • Complex disease — disease shaped by many genes + environment, opposite of monogenic.
  • eQTL — expression Quantitative Trait Locus; a variant that changes gene-expression level.
  • G × E (gene × environment) interaction — case where genotype changes response to an environmental factor, or vice versa, beyond simple addition.
  • GxEMM, StructLMM — modern mixed-model frameworks built specifically to estimate genome-environment interaction variance.
  • GWAS — Genome-Wide Association Study.
  • Linkage disequilibrium (LD) — tendency of nearby variants to be inherited together.
  • Monogenic — caused by a single gene's mutation (e.g., CFTR for CF).
  • Multiple testing burden — statistical penalty for running many tests at once; interaction tests carry a larger burden than main-effect tests.
  • Ottman's four models — synergy / risk-genotype-exacerbates / exposure-exacerbates / both-required.
  • Penetrance — fraction of carriers of a disease mutation who actually develop the disease.
  • PM2.5 — particulate matter ≤2.5 µm in diameter, the standard air-pollution metric.
  • QTL — Quantitative Trait Locus; a region of the genome statistically tied to a measurable quantity.
  • Somatic mutation — DNA change occurring after fertilization, not inherited.
  • Synergistic — combined effect of two factors greater than the sum of each alone.

Methods to lift

  • Ottman four-model G × E taxonomy — synergy / risk-genotype-exacerbates-environment / environment-exacerbates-genotype / both-required-for-risk; pre-register which model an analysis plan targets.
  • Locked-locus interaction-only scan — restrict G × E testing to a small, pre-specified locus set (drawn from prior main-effect discovery) rather than genome-wide; reduces multiple-testing burden to a tractable level.
  • Environmental exposure linkage — link individual-level health-record longitudinal exposure variables (smoke status, ZIP-derived PM2.5, viral-infection history) to genotype data as covariates and interaction terms.
  • Genotype-stratified mechanistic follow-up — pair every population-level G × E hit with a genotype-stratified molecular phenotyping plan (e.g., chromatin accessibility, expression QTL, transcription-factor binding) to demonstrate the interaction mechanism.
  • Sex-chromosome composition as a discrete G × E covariate — XX/XY (and XXX/XXY/X0 when available) coded as a categorical covariate in any infection-related or autoimmune-related model.

What to verify first

  • Variance-inflation cost and statistical-power floor of running interaction terms on a ~5,000-person CFGP substrate; whether StructLMM, GxEMM, or REGENIE-with-interaction-flag is tractable.
  • Which environmental-exposure variables are actually in CFFPR with longitudinal date resolution — secondhand smoke is reportedly captured; PM2.5 via ZIP-code linkage and systematic viral-infection history likely require external joins.
  • Multiple-testing posture: at twelve locked modifier loci × four environmental categories = 48 interaction tests, Bonferroni alone gives α≈10⁻³; whether to pre-register one environmental category or accept reduced power.
  • Modulator-era stratification: pollution and secondhand-smoke G × E effects were measured pre-Trikafta; modern airway biology under highly-effective modulators may interact with environment differently.
  • Sex-chromosome variant calling: X-chromosome coverage uniformity in males vs females in CFGP WGS, before any sex-chromosome G × E test is run.
  • Generalizability across modifier-locus ancestry: CFGP is largely European, but pollution and smoke effects vary by socioeconomic context; the G × E module may inherit the ancestry-imbalance limitations of the underlying discovery substrate.

ALEX Today's second episode is Virolainen, VonHandorf, Viel, Weirauch, and Kottyan, 2023, in Genes and Immunity — "Gene-environment interactions and their impact on human health." It's a review out of the Cincinnati Children's Center for Autoimmune Genomics and Etiology. We're reading a review today because yesterday's episode ended with the gene-by-environment Collaco 2008 paper on secondhand smoke times CFTR, and we need a wider frame before we can decide whether gene-environment interactions belong in a CFTR PheWAS at all.

JORDAN Stop me before we go further. I want every piece of jargon in that cold open and in this paper's title nailed down.

ALEX Right. Three terms. "Gene-environment interaction" is the technical name — the paper uses the shorthand "G × E" or sometimes "GxE" — for a situation where a genetic variant changes a person's response to an environmental factor, or where the environmental factor changes the apparent effect of the genetic variant. It is not the same as "genetics plus environment, both matter." That's just both contributing additively. The interaction case is when the two together produce more or less risk than you would predict by adding them up separately. The technical word is "synergistic" or "antagonistic." The second term is "complex disease." That's the catch-all for diseases like asthma, type 2 diabetes, hypertension, autoimmune disease — diseases where dozens or hundreds of genetic variants each contribute small effects, mixed with environment. The opposite is "monogenic" or "Mendelian," which means a single gene's mutation does the work. Cystic fibrosis is a monogenic disease — one gene, CFTR. But — and this is where it gets interesting for our podcast — CF severity within CF patients is a complex trait. A third term: "penetrance." That's the fraction of people carrying a disease mutation who actually get the disease. Many "causal" mutations are "incompletely penetrant" — only some carriers actually develop the disease — and one explanation for the people who don't is gene-environment interaction. The review also uses GWAS, the Genome-Wide Association Study, which we covered yesterday, and QTL, which stands for quantitative trait locus — a region of the genome statistically tied to a measurable quantity, like the expression level of a particular gene. The most common subtype is eQTL, expression quantitative trait locus, which is a variant that changes how much of a gene gets made.

JORDAN What's the structural problem this review is trying to solve?

ALEX For about twenty years, GWAS has been the standard genome-side tool, and it has done magnificent work cataloguing risk variants for hundreds of complex diseases. The most recent GWAS catalog they cite contains over 5,000 independent GWAS datasets across 70,000-plus variant-trait associations. But there are well-known failure modes. First, GWAS to date have focused disproportionately, more than 78 percent, on European-ancestry samples — the generalizability story is broken. Second, GWAS variants are typically not the variants that actually cause the biological effect, they're just statistically tagged to the causal variant through linkage disequilibrium — the tendency of nearby variants to travel together on haplotypes. Third, the additive model is the default — which we hit hard yesterday. And fourth, even with all of GWAS's catalogued common-variant hits added together, you cannot explain the full heritability of most complex diseases. Some of that missing heritability is rare variation. Some is non-additive architecture. And some, this review argues, is gene-environment interaction. The authors anchor the review in Ottman's four-model framework for GxE — the risk genotype exacerbates the environmental risk; the environmental exposure exacerbates the genotype's risk; both are needed for risk to appear; or both contribute independently and the combination is greater than the sum. The review focuses on the last, synergistic model, and walks through four environmental factor categories.

JORDAN Walk me through them.

ALEX Category one is environmental toxicants. The clearest established examples are heavy metals. Variants at the 10q24.32 locus near AS3MT — that's arsenic methyltransferase, the enzyme that detoxifies arsenic — change how efficiently a person can clear arsenic from their body. Carriers of inefficient-metabolizer variants who live in areas with arsenic-contaminated drinking water, of which the WHO estimates over 100 million people worldwide, have meaningfully higher disease risk than non-carriers with the same exposure. A second example is the metallothionein 2A gene, MT2A, where a variant in the gene's promoter alters how much metal-binding protein the cell produces, and that in turn changes how much cadmium and copper accumulate. The pattern is the same in both cases: genetic variant changes detoxification efficiency, environmental exposure provides the toxin, the interaction lands somewhere along a metabolic pathway. Toxicants are also unique in that they can directly mutate the genome — somatic mutations from carcinogens — which is a kind of bidirectional gene-environment interaction.

ALEX Category two is pollution, especially air pollution. The lead example is PM2.5 — particulate matter under 2.5 microns in diameter, which is small enough to penetrate the lower airways. The Lancet Commission on Pollution and Health attributes roughly 4 to 9 million annual deaths to PM2.5 and ozone exposure. The cleanest documented genetic interaction is at a chromosome 3 locus encoding DAPP1, where a GWAS-identified variant interacts with diesel exhaust exposure to alter airway hyperreactivity, and the same interaction was demonstrated in mice independently. Asthma prevalence is higher among low-income African-American children, who are more likely to live near highways — so the GxE interaction at DAPP1 has direct disparity implications. Category three is viruses. The deepest example is Epstein-Barr virus, EBV — a herpes virus that infects more than 90 percent of adults worldwide and persists as a latent infection in B cells for life. During latency EBV produces a viral protein called EBNA2 — Epstein-Barr nuclear antigen 2 — which directly binds the human genome at thousands of sites and acts as a transcription regulator. Critically, EBNA2 binding intersects nearly half of known lupus and multiple sclerosis risk variants, and the binding is genotype-dependent — meaning that a person's specific risk allele changes how EBNA2 alters that region's gene expression. So a lifelong latent virus is, in effect, a continuous environmental modifier of the human genome. Another viral example is IRF7, interferon regulatory factor 7, where rare loss-of-function mutations underlie a small but striking fraction of life-threatening influenza and COVID-19 cases — a single rare host variant collides with a specific viral exposure to produce severe disease. Category four is sex chromosome composition and sex hormones. The XX-versus-XY difference shapes infectious-disease severity, autoimmune incidence, and vaccine response. The review notes that XY individuals are more likely to die from COVID-19 and XX individuals mount stronger antibody responses to vaccines. The review also briefly covers somatic mutations in inflammatory disease — UBA1 in late-onset inflammatory syndromes, NLRP3 in autoinflammatory disorders, MYD88 in Schnitzler's syndrome — as a parallel mechanism by which environment-shaped mutation accumulation drives disease late in life.

JORDAN Push back on one design choice in this review.

ALEX The whole review consciously focuses on the synergistic model — Ottman's fourth model — and excludes the other three. That's a defensible scope decision, but it has a consequence: any case where the environment is necessary and the genotype is purely permissive — model three — gets dropped from the discussion. That model three pattern probably describes a lot of infection biology, including, plausibly, the CF airway phenotype where Pseudomonas colonization is the necessary environmental trigger and CF-modifier-genotypes shape only the response. The review's scope quietly de-emphasizes a model that may be the most relevant one for us.

JORDAN Did they answer the question they set out to answer?

ALEX Partially. They successfully demonstrate that gene-environment interaction is real and consequential across at least four environmental categories — toxicants, pollution, viruses, and sex chromosome composition. They successfully argue that targeting the environmental side of these interactions is often more practical than targeting the genetic side, because environments are modifiable through policy and behavior. What they sidestep is statistical methodology. They acknowledge that multiple testing burden is "a major challenge" in GxE research because you need very large sample sizes to detect interaction terms — interactions need roughly four times the sample size of main effects at the same effect-size scale — but they don't prescribe specific tools or pipelines beyond a passing nod to machine-learning approaches. So as a tool-shopping document for designing a GxE arm of a CFTR PheWAS, this review tells you what's worth measuring but not how to measure it.

The genetic background is fixed — every patient has CF — but CF disease severity varies enormously between patients, and the environmental exposures that drive that variation are well known: respiratory pathogens, secondhand smoke, air pollution, viral infections, and probably climate-driven aeroallergen exposure. We have one prior episode worth of evidence — Collaco 2008 — that secondhand smoke times TGFβ1 promoter genotype interacts on FEV1. The Virolainen framework suggests one specific extension. JORDAN Given that direction, what does Virolainen close and leave open?

ALEX It closes the credibility question for GxE as a research category — after this review you cannot dismiss interaction-term analyses as exotic. It closes the framework question by giving us Ottman's four-model taxonomy to write any future analysis plan against. It closes one specific cystic-fibrosis-relevant choice by reminding us that for CF, the genetic background — CFTR — is fixed within the cohort, which means the cohort is in many ways an ideal natural experiment for environmental main effects and for modifier-times-environment interactions. What it leaves open is the methodology. We'd need to check several things before committing. First, the statistical methods question: what's the actual variance-inflation cost of running interaction terms at every modifier locus on a ~5,000-person cohort, and are tools like the StructLMM framework or GxEMM tractable on this substrate. Second, the environmental data question: which environmental exposure variables actually live in CFFPR with longitudinal date-resolution? Secondhand smoke status is captured. Pollution exposure can in principle be derived from ZIP code linkage but we'd need to verify it isn't currently in CFFPR. Viral infection history is captured episodically but probably not systematically. Third, the multiple-testing question: even locked to a dozen modifier loci, four environmental categories give 48 interaction tests, and the standard correction is brutal — we'd need to think about whether to pre-register one environmental factor or accept the reduced power. Fourth, the modulator-era confounding: pollution and secondhand smoke effects on CF lung function were measured pre-Trikafta, and the modern airway biology under modulators may interact with environment differently. None of these are deal-breakers; each is a "what we'd need to check" item.
