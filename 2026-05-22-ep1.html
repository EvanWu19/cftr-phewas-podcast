One-paragraph blurb

This Nature Communications paper from the Marsh lab at Edinburgh asks whether the standard variant prioritization machinery — variant effect predictors and protein stability predictors — handles mechanism class fairly across loss-of-function (LOF), dominant-negative (DN), and gain-of-function (GOF) disease variants. Using 13,050 ClinVar pathogenic missense mutations and 211,266 gnomAD putatively benign variants across 1,261 Mendelian genes, mapped onto Protein Data Bank structures and scored with FoldX v5, they show that LOF variants are dramatically more structurally damaging than DN and GOF variants, that 45 of the field's variant effect predictors all underperform on DN and GOF variants, and that a new three-dimensional spatial clustering metric (Extent of Disease Clustering, EDC) discriminates non-LOF from LOF genes. One possible direction the episode suggests for the CFTR PheWAS work: stratify CFTR rare variants by mechanism class — using the established class-I through class-VI scheme — and test whether mechanism-class-stratified burden tests produce different downstream phenotype patterns than the unstratified burden. Open: whether the CF class scheme maps cleanly onto LOF/HI/DN/GOF, and whether novel uncharacterized CFTR variants can be assigned at all.

Plain-English glossary

  • AD (autosomal dominant): mode of inheritance where one broken copy of a gene is enough to cause disease.
  • AR (autosomal recessive): mode of inheritance where both copies of a gene must be broken to cause disease. CFTR works this way.
  • ClinVar: public database of clinically interpreted genetic variants.
  • DN (dominant-negative): a variant where the mutant protein actively interferes with the function of the wild-type protein, usually inside multi-subunit complexes.
  • EDC (Extent of Disease Clustering): ratio metric defined in the paper, >1 = pathogenic residues cluster in 3D protein space, ~1 = randomly distributed.
  • FoldX: structure-based protein stability predictor that estimates ΔΔG.
  • GOF (gain-of-function): a variant that gives the protein a new or amplified activity.
  • gnomAD: Genome Aggregation Database, the field's reference catalog of ~140,000 ostensibly healthy individuals' variants.
  • HI (haploinsufficient): a flavor of LOF where one functional copy of a gene is insufficient; gene is dosage-sensitive.
  • LOF (loss-of-function): a variant that breaks the protein's normal job.
  • Missense variant: single-letter DNA change that swaps one amino acid for another in the protein.
  • OMIM: Online Mendelian Inheritance in Man, curated database of Mendelian-disease genes and inheritance patterns.
  • PDB (Protein Data Bank): repository of experimentally determined 3D protein structures.
  • VEP (variant effect predictor): software that scores a variant's likely damaging-ness — examples: SIFT, PolyPhen2, REVEL, CADD, ClinPred, BayesDel.
  • ΔΔG: change in Gibbs free energy of protein folding upon mutation; higher absolute ΔΔG = more destabilizing.

Methods to lift

  • FoldX v5 ΔΔG on full biological assemblies rather than monomers — the gain is small but consistent and the assembly setup is what allows interface-position annotation.
  • OMIM + ClinGen + manual curation as a gene-level mechanism-class assignment pipeline, with the explicit acknowledgement that one-mechanism-per-gene misclassifies mixed-mechanism genes like CFTR.
  • EDC metric (Extent of Disease Clustering) — a simple per-protein ratio of disease residue clustering vs. random distribution, computed in 3D protein space. Implementation does not require running a VEP.
  • ROC-AUC across 45 VEPs with stratification by mechanism class — the methodological template for evaluating whether any new CFTR-specific predictor handles mechanism class fairly.
  • Variant-level vs gene-level mechanism annotation cross-check — the paper repeats its main result on a smaller variant-level dataset to defend the gene-level analysis.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: stratify CFTR rare missense variants in the CFF whole-genome sequencing data by the CF community's class-I through class-VI mechanism scheme, then run mechanism-class-stratified rare-variant burden tests against CFFPR pulmonary, gastrointestinal, endocrine, and hepatobiliary phenotypes, asking whether mechanism class modulates phenotypic risk profile independent of overall variant severity.

What to verify first

  • Whether published CFTR class annotations are comprehensive enough to cover the variants present in the CFF whole-genome sequencing data.
  • Whether class assignments are stable and consistent across the canonical references (CFTR2, Sosnay, McCague, etc.).
  • Whether novel rare CFTR variants without prior functional characterization can be assigned a mechanism class at all, or must be left in an unclassified bucket.
  • Whether the CFFPR phenotype catalog has the breadth and granularity needed to distinguish class-specific phenotype patterns from baseline disease severity.
  • Whether the EDC metric is meaningful for CFTR specifically — CFTR has multiple functional domains (NBD1, NBD2, MSD1, MSD2, R-domain) and class-by-class spatial clustering inside CFTR may or may not be informative.

ALEX Welcome back to the CFTR PheWAS reading podcast. Today's first paper is Gerasimavicius, Livesey, and Marsh, 2022, published in Nature Communications, titled "Loss-of-function, gain-of-function and dominant-negative mutations have profoundly different effects on protein structure." The reason we're reading it: every CFTR PheWAS has to decide which DNA variants to lump together and which to keep separate, and this paper says that the standard ways of doing that lumping are systematically missing a big category of disease variants. That matters directly for how we'd score CFTR variants in a registry analysis.

JORDAN Before we get into the paper itself, can we slow down? You said "missense variant," "loss-of-function," "dominant-negative" — I need a primer.

ALEX Good. Let me lay down the vocabulary. A "missense" variant is a single-letter DNA change that swaps one amino acid in a protein for a different amino acid. The protein still gets made — it just has the wrong building block at one position. That's different from a "nonsense" or "frameshift" variant where the protein is truncated or scrambled. Now: "loss-of-function," abbreviated LOF, means the variant breaks the protein so it can no longer do its normal job. "Gain-of-function," GOF, means the variant gives the protein a new or amplified activity it shouldn't have. "Dominant-negative," DN, is sneakier: the broken protein actively interferes with the working protein, usually because proteins often work as multi-subunit complexes and one bad subunit poisons the whole assembly. And one more: "haploinsufficiency," HI, means you need both copies of a gene working, so losing one copy is enough to cause disease — it's a kind of LOF that only shows up when the gene is dosage-sensitive.

JORDAN And the "AR" and "AD" you'll be using?

ALEX AR is autosomal recessive — you need two broken copies of a gene to get sick. CFTR works this way. AD is autosomal dominant — one broken copy is enough. AR diseases are mostly LOF. AD diseases are a mix: some are LOF via haploinsufficiency, but others are dominant-negative or gain-of-function. That distinction is the whole heart of this paper.

JORDAN One more — you said the paper is about "variant effect predictors."

ALEX Right. A variant effect predictor, or VEP, is a piece of software that takes a DNA variant and outputs a number saying how likely that variant is to be damaging. PolyPhen, SIFT, REVEL, CADD, AlphaMissense — these are all VEPs. They are everywhere in clinical genetics pipelines because we get far more rare variants out of sequencing than we can experimentally test, so we use VEPs to prioritize which ones probably matter.

JORDAN Okay. Now — what's the background and why is this question worth a whole paper?

ALEX Genetic medicine has a triage problem. When you sequence a person, you find thousands of rare variants. Most of those are harmless, but some are disease-causing. The dominant way the field decides which ones matter is to filter on rarity — anything seen in less than zero point one percent of the population goes onto the suspect list — and then to score each variant with a VEP. The implicit assumption baked into almost every VEP is that pathogenic variants damage the protein. They destabilize its fold, or they disrupt a binding interface, or they sit at an evolutionarily conserved residue. That assumption works fine for loss-of-function variants, where the protein has been broken. But the authors point out it should fail for gain-of-function and dominant-negative variants, because those variants don't have to break the protein at all — in fact they can't, in the dominant-negative case, because the mutant protein has to stay folded and assembled well enough to poison the wild-type subunits it's interacting with.

JORDAN So the worry is that variant prioritization pipelines are blind to a whole class of real disease variants?

ALEX That's the worry. The authors set out to test it on a much larger scale than anyone had before. Their other angle is on protein stability prediction. There's a class of prediction tools called stability predictors — FoldX is the famous one — that estimate how much a missense variant changes the thermodynamic stability of the protein in kilocalories per mole, abbreviated ΔΔG. Higher absolute ΔΔG means the variant destabilizes the protein more. These tools are widely used as a tiebreaker on top of VEPs. The paper asks whether ΔΔG, and VEPs more broadly, behave differently across LOF, GOF, and DN variants.

ALEX Now, methods. They start by assembling a dataset of 13,050 pathogenic missense mutations from ClinVar — ClinVar is a public database where labs deposit clinical variant interpretations — across 1,261 Mendelian disease genes. They contrast that pathogenic set against 211,266 supposedly benign missense variants from gnomAD, the Genome Aggregation Database, which is the reference catalog of variants observed in roughly 140,000 ostensibly healthy people. Every variant gets mapped to a three-dimensional protein structure from the Protein Data Bank. Then they run FoldX version five on every variant in two modes: monomer ΔΔG, treating the protein as an isolated chain; and full ΔΔG, including all the interaction partners in the biological assembly.

JORDAN Wait — why two modes?

ALEX Because a lot of pathogenic variants sit at protein-protein interfaces, and a monomer-only calculation misses that part of the energy bookkeeping. The first headline result is: using the full assembly bumps the area-under-the-ROC-curve, which is the standard accuracy metric, from 0.652 to 0.677 for separating ClinVar pathogenic from gnomAD benign. Small in absolute terms, but it confirms that interface effects matter and sets up the rest of the analyses.

ALEX To get at mechanism, they bring in inheritance pattern from OMIM, the Online Mendelian Inheritance in Man database. Genes get sorted into autosomal recessive — 726 genes — and autosomal dominant — 535 genes. Then, within autosomal dominant, they go further and classify each gene as haploinsufficient, dominant-negative, or gain-of-function, using a combination of OMIM keyword searches, ClinGen dosage-sensitivity annotations, and manual curation. ALEX caveats this approach because it assigns one mechanism per gene, and many genes really do have mixed mechanisms — that's a real limitation we should keep in mind.

JORDAN All right, what did they find?

ALEX Three findings, all in the same direction. First, autosomal recessive variants are much more structurally damaging than autosomal dominant variants. Mean monomer ΔΔG is around 4 kilocalories per mole for AR and around 3 for AD. AR variants are 58 percent buried in the protein interior, AD only 43 percent, and AD variants are enriched at protein interfaces — 31 percent of AD variants sit at interfaces, versus 27 percent for AR. Second, when they decompose the AD genes by mechanism, the picture sharpens dramatically. Haploinsufficient genes — which are mechanistically just AD-flavored loss-of-function — look exactly like AR genes: high ΔΔG, mean about 4.18. Dominant-negative and gain-of-function genes are much milder, mean ΔΔG around 2.5 to 2.7. So if you treat all AD variants the same, you're averaging two very different signal regimes. Third, and this is the big punchline, they tested 45 different VEPs — covering everything from older conservation-based tools like SIFT and PolyPhen2 to recent meta-predictors like REVEL, ClinPred, and BayesDel — and almost all of them perform worse on dominant-negative and gain-of-function variants than they do on loss-of-function variants. Even predictors based only on evolutionary conservation showed the gap.

ALEX I want to push back on one design choice. By classifying mechanism at the gene level rather than the variant level, the authors guarantee themselves some misclassification. CFTR is a good example — it has loss-of-function class-I and class-II variants, gating mutations that behave more like gain-of-function on chloride permeability, and the canonical p.Phe508del which is a class-II processing defect. If you assigned CFTR one mechanism, you'd be wrong for a quarter of the variants. The authors do acknowledge this, and they redo the analysis using a smaller variant-level dataset with annotated mechanisms — and they get the same answer, which is reassuring. But the gene-level limitation is still load-bearing.

ALEX They then ask whether there's any feature that does pick up dominant-negative and gain-of-function variants. They define a metric called EDC, Extent of Disease Clustering, which is the ratio of how tightly disease-causing residues cluster in three-dimensional protein space compared to random expectation. EDC greater than one means the disease residues are clumped together; EDC near one means they're scattered. DN and GOF genes have significantly higher EDC than HI genes — pathogenic variants in DN and GOF genes do tend to cluster spatially. Two illustrative examples: ADSL, an autosomal recessive enzyme deficiency, has an EDC of 0.89, meaning its disease variants are spread all over the protein, as you'd expect from generic destabilization. KCTD1, which causes dominant disease, has an EDC of 1.90 — its disease variants pile up in one structural region, consistent with a dominant-negative mechanism.

ALEX They posed two questions and answered both partially. Question one: do current variant prioritization pipelines miss non-LOF disease variants? Answer: yes, by quite a lot, and the result is consistent across forty-five different tools. Question two: is there anything that could rescue prediction for non-LOF variants? Answer: spatial clustering, EDC, looks promising but is an aggregate property of a gene, not a per-variant score, so it's a research-grade tool not a clinical-grade tool yet.
