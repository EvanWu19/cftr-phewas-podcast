How did this episode land for you?

Blurb

Zhou and colleagues threw the largest, deepest CF genetic dataset assembled — 4,248 whole-genome-sequenced patients plus 3,592 imputed-array patients, 7,840 total, ~15.9 million SNPs — at the lung-disease modifier question, using the field-standard KNoRMA phenotype on pre-modulator data. They confirmed and finely mapped the previously known SLC9A3/CEP72 (chr5p13) and EHF/APIP (chr11p13) signals, but explicitly reported "no novel primary associations with common single variants or rare variants." For follow-up work this is a clean negative result: it closes the door on novel common single-variant lung-function modifier discovery in the CF Gene Modifier Consortium WGS at this scale, and points downstream work toward phenome-wide, post-modulator, longitudinal, and rare-variant gene-burden directions.

Plain-English glossary (alphabetical)

  • Case-control study — a study design comparing people who have a trait (cases) with people who do not (controls). Zhou used a quantitative-trait variant rather than a binary case-control design, but the underlying statistical framework is closely related.
  • CFTR (cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator) — the single gene whose mutations cause cystic fibrosis. Encodes a chloride ion channel; broken CFTR produces thick mucus in the lungs and other organs.
  • Common variant — a genetic variant present in more than ~1-5% of the population. Well-powered for single-variant GWAS.
  • FEV1 (forced expiratory volume in 1 second) — how much air a person can blow out in the first second of a hard exhale. The standard lung-function measurement; lower means more obstructed lungs.
  • Gene-burden test — a rare-variant statistical method that collapses all qualifying rare variants in a gene into one count per person, then asks whether the count tracks with the trait. Different statistical regime from single-variant testing.
  • GWAS (genome-wide association study) — testing millions of genetic variants one at a time across thousands of patients to find variants associated with a trait.
  • Imputation — statistically inferring genotypes at variants not directly measured on a genotyping array, using a reference panel of fully sequenced genomes.
  • KNoRMA (Kulich Normal Residual Mortality-Adjusted lung function) — a CF-specific quantitative lung-function phenotype derived from FEV1, age- and sex-adjusted, with a correction for survival bias (sicker patients die or transplant out of cross-sectional samples). Field-standard outcome for CF lung-modifier studies; from Taylor et al. 2011.
  • Modifier gene — a gene elsewhere in the genome that influences how severe a single-gene disease becomes. CFTR causes CF; modifier genes shift severity up or down.
  • Modulator drugs — small molecules (ivacaftor, lumacaftor, tezacaftor, elexacaftor — Trikafta is the elexacaftor-tezacaftor-ivacaftor combination) that partially restore CFTR protein function. Broadly available from ~2019-2020. Lung-function data after modulator initiation no longer reflects the natural genetic architecture of CF lung disease.
  • Pre-modulator era — clinical data collected before patients had access to or initiated CFTR modulator therapy. Zhou's dataset is entirely pre-modulator era.
  • Rare variant — a genetic variant present in fewer than ~1% of the population. Underpowered for single-variant testing; usually analyzed via gene-burden methods.
  • Single-variant test — testing one SNP at a time for association with a trait. The classic GWAS approach.
  • SNP (single-nucleotide polymorphism) — a single-letter difference in DNA between people; the most common type of genetic variant.
  • TWAS (transcriptome-wide association study) — uses gene-expression reference data to predict each gene's expression from genotype, then tests predicted expression against the trait.
  • WGS (whole-genome sequencing) — reading every base of the genome, capturing rare, deep intronic, regulatory, and structural variants that genotyping arrays miss.

Methods to lift

  • KNoRMA as the lung-function phenotype — established, mortality-adjusted, comparable across cohorts.
  • WGS-plus-imputed-array meta-analysis — sample-size strategy when WGS alone is too small.
  • Mixed-ancestry GWAS framework rather than European-only restriction.
  • Layered analysis stack: single-variant GWAS, then gene-level test, then TWAS, then pathway enrichment, on the same harmonized dataset.
  • Pancreatic-insufficiency restriction to enrich for severe-phenotype, more genetically homogeneous cases.
  • Cross-cohort effect-size ordering as an internal sanity check (and a hint at age/birth-cohort effects).

Proposed direction (conditional)

If verification confirms it is feasible, the natural next move for our side-project is a CFTR-anchored phenome-wide association study (PheWAS) on Cystic Fibrosis Foundation Patient Registry (CFFPR) phecodes using the WGS sub-cohort, focusing on registry-derived non-lung phenotypes (gastrointestinal, hepatic, infectious, endocrine, growth, mental health). Zhou closed the lung-function single-variant door cleanly; a phenome-wide scan of established CF modifier loci across registry phenotypes is one of the few defensible directions left that does not duplicate published work.

Gap-map: closes

  • Novel common single-variant lung-function modifier discovery in the pre-modulator CF Gene Modifier Consortium WGS at n=7,840 using KNoRMA.
  • Novel rare single-variant lung-function modifier discovery at the same scale (per their abstract; rare gene-burden as a primary framework is a different question — see "leaves open").
  • The "is there hidden signal at WGS resolution beyond what arrays caught" hypothesis for this specific phenotype/cohort — answered, mostly no.
  • Fine-mapping of the SLC9A3/CEP72 and EHF/APIP loci at WGS resolution — done.

Gap-map: leaves open

  • (a) Phenome-wide scans of established loci. What do SLC9A3, CEP72, EHF, and APIP do across non-lung phenotypes — gut, pancreas, infection, growth, mental health — within a CF cohort? Not systematically tested.
  • (b) Post-modulator era recalibration. Modifier effects may shift once CFTR function is partially restored by Trikafta-era therapy. A new analytical question; Zhou's data cannot address it.
  • (c) Longitudinal and survival phenotypes. KNoRMA is a snapshot. Rate of FEV1 decline, time to first Pseudomonas, time to transplant, age at first severe exacerbation — different phenotypes, untested here.
  • (d) Rare-variant gene-burden as primary discovery. Zhou's testing was single-variant-focused. REGENIE/SAIGE-style gene-burden testing is a different statistical regime and remains underexplored on CF WGS.

What to verify first

  • Overlap between the CFFPR-linkable WGS sub-cohort (~5K samples, per project notes) and Zhou's 4,248 WGS samples. Substantial overlap means the lung-modifier story has already been told on those exact people; the PheWAS angle still works, but any lung-phenotype reanalysis would be redundant.
  • Post-Trikafta clinical-phenotyping depth in the CFFPR linkable to WGS samples — how many years, how many encounters per patient. Determines whether post-modulator recalibration is a now-question or a future-question.
  • Inventory of registry-derived non-lung phenotypes (phecodes, lab values, growth metrics, prescription records) with stable definitions and clean linkage to WGS sample IDs.
  • Whether longitudinal phenotypes (rate of decline, time-to-event endpoints) are computable from CFFPR with sufficient follow-up depth.
  • Governance and data-access path for running a PheWAS on the WGS sub-cohort — WGS sits with the Therapy department, not Clinical Affairs.

JORDAN Cystic fibrosis, CF, is a recessive disease caused by mutations in a single gene called CFTR — cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator. CFTR is a chloride channel; broken CFTR means thick mucus, especially in the lungs and pancreas. Lung disease is what kills most people with CF.

ALEX Right. And the central puzzle for thirty years has been: two patients can have the exact same CFTR mutation — say, both homozygous for F508del — and one ends up needing a lung transplant at 25, the other has near-normal lung function at 50. Same broken gene. Wildly different lungs.

JORDAN That is the modifier gene question. A modifier gene is a gene somewhere else in the genome that nudges how severe a single-gene disease becomes. CFTR is the cause; modifiers are the volume knob.

ALEX Exactly. Now the tools. GWAS, genome-wide association study, is when you take thousands of patients, line up several million genetic variants, and ask, statistically, which variants track with the trait you care about. Classic GWAS uses SNP arrays — chips that read maybe a million pre-chosen common variants — plus imputation, which is statistical guessing of the variants the chip didn't directly measure, using a reference panel.

JORDAN And whole-genome sequencing, WGS, is the upgrade. Instead of guessing, you read every base. So you see rare variants, deep intronic variants, regulatory variants, structural variants — the stuff arrays miss.

ALEX That is the bet behind this paper. If we go from imputed array data to actual WGS, do new modifier signals fall out? Two more terms before we get to the question. KNoRMA — Kulich Normal Residual Mortality-Adjusted lung function — is the CF field's specialty phenotype. The problem with CF lung function is that the sickest patients die or get transplanted, which biases any cross-sectional FEV1 — that is, forced expiratory volume in one second, the standard lung-function measurement. KNoRMA, from Taylor and colleagues 2011, is essentially an age- and sex-adjusted FEV1 percentile that handles the survival bias. It is the field-standard quantitative lung phenotype for modifier work.

JORDAN And modulators?

ALEX Modulator drugs are the small molecules that partially fix the CFTR protein itself — ivacaftor, lumacaftor, tezacaftor, and most importantly elexacaftor-tezacaftor-ivacaftor, brand name Trikafta, which became broadly available around 2019 to 2020. They dramatically improve lung function in eligible patients, which means lung-function data collected after a patient starts a modulator no longer reflects the natural genetic architecture of the disease. So modifier studies built on pre-modulator data and modifier studies built on post-modulator data are answering subtly different questions.

JORDAN Got it. And one more — single-variant testing versus gene-burden testing.

ALEX Single-variant tests one SNP at a time. Powerful for common variants, weak for rare ones because each rare variant individually has too few carriers. Gene-burden tests collapse all the rare variants in a gene into one count and ask whether carriers as a group differ from non-carriers. Different statistical regime, different things you can find.

JORDAN Okay. Now — what was the actual question?

ALEX The question is the natural next step after Wright 2011 and Corvol 2015. Those earlier CF lung-modifier GWAS studies used SNP arrays plus imputation in cohorts of a few thousand. They surfaced a handful of replicated modifier loci — the cleanest being a region on chromosome 5p13 near SLC9A3 and CEP72, and a region on chromosome 11p13 near EHF and APIP. Those loci were real, but the variance in lung function explained was small, and a lot of signal was presumably hiding beneath the array's resolution.

JORDAN So the field's open hypothesis was, if we could see every variant, the rare and the regulatory ones, more modifier loci should emerge.

ALEX Right. Whole-genome sequencing was the obvious test. The Cystic Fibrosis Foundation, NHLBI, and the Genome Sequencing Program funded WGS on roughly four thousand pancreatic-insufficient — meaning more severe — CF patients drawn from the U.S., Canadian, and French modifier cohorts. The Knowles group then combined those WGS samples with the older imputed-array samples in a meta-analysis. That is the dataset behind Zhou 2023.

JORDAN And critically, this is all pre-modulator era data?

ALEX Critically. The lung function values feeding into KNoRMA were collected before modulators were on the market or before each particular patient started one. That is not a flaw — modulators barely existed when most of these data points were captured — but it is a fixed property of the dataset that matters a lot for what comes after.

JORDAN Methods, then. What did they actually do?

ALEX Four-thousand-two-hundred-forty-eight patients with WGS plus three-thousand-five-hundred-ninety-two patients with imputed array data, total seven-thousand-eight-hundred-forty pancreatic-insufficient patients. They harmonized everything to KNoRMA as the lung-function outcome. They tested approximately fifteen-point-nine million SNPs.

JORDAN That is a lot of variants.

ALEX It is the whole genome. They ran mixed-ancestry GWAS, meaning they did not restrict to European-ancestry samples only — they used statistical methods that handle population structure across mixed ancestries — though most of the cohort is still European, which is a known limitation. They did this in the WGS sub-cohort, did it in the imputed sub-cohort, and meta-analyzed the two.

ALEX They layered three more analyses. A transcriptome-wide association study, TWAS, which uses gene-expression reference data to ask which genes' predicted expression tracks with the phenotype. JORDAN So they were not just doing one test. They were trying every legitimate angle on the same data.

ALEX Yes. If there were modifier signal hiding in the WGS, they gave it many ways to come out.

JORDAN And the results?

ALEX The two previously known multi-locus signals — chromosome 5p13 around SLC9A3 and CEP72, and chromosome 11p13 around EHF and APIP — were confirmed and refined. Variant effect sizes at those known loci were consistently ordered across the cohorts, which they note suggests possible age or birth-cohort effects on the magnitude of the modifier effect. JORDAN But the headline?

ALEX The headline is the sentence in their abstract: quote, "no novel primary associations with common single variants or rare variants were found," end quote. After all that data, all that resolution, all those tests — no new primary single-variant modifier hit at genome-wide significance.
