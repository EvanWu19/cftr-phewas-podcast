Blurb

Jumentier, Qu, Manousaki and colleagues (Diabetologia, 2026) built and validated a trans-ancestry polygenic risk score (TA-PS) for type 1 diabetes that performs comparably across ancestries — fixing the well-known failure of the European gold-standard GRS2 score (AUROC 0.92 in Europeans, ~0.81 in African Americans, with ancestry-shifted score distributions that break uniform screening thresholds). They kept GRS2's HLA component and rebuilt only the non-HLA part using PRS-CSx and JointPRS, trained on large European + East Asian + meta-analysed African American/Hispanic GWAS, generating 35 candidate scores tested in a 4,657-person multi-ancestry Montreal cohort. The weighted TA-PS beat GRS2 across all tested groups (e.g., EUR 0.88 vs 0.86, AFR 0.86 vs 0.83, HIS 0.86 vs 0.81; pooled 0.89 vs 0.85, p=4.67×10⁻³¹). Most importantly, at a single uniform 90th-percentile threshold defined in European controls, average sensitivity rose from 0.53 (GRS2) to 0.74 (TA-PS) with specificity ≥0.83. Validated in CHOP-CAG, GRACE (China), All of Us, and UK Biobank. Proposed direction for the CFTR project: any CF-complication PRS built on the European-heavy CF cohort must be tested for cross-ancestry transferability with PRS-CSx-style methods and reported per-ancestry, not pooled. Open: whether CF modifier traits have enough non-European cases to estimate ancestry-specific weights at all (~5,000-person, mostly-European CF cohort), and whether CF outcomes are polygenic enough to score.

Plain-English glossary

  • AUROC — area under the receiver operating curve; discrimination metric from 0.5 (coin-flip) to 1.0 (perfect case/control separation).
  • DeLong test — significance test comparing two AUROCs in the same sample.
  • F1-score — harmonic mean of precision and recall; a single balance metric for a chosen threshold.
  • GRS2 / GRS2x — the 67-SNP European type 1 diabetes genetic risk score (HLA + 32 non-HLA SNPs + HLA interactions); GRS2x is its WGS/TOPMed-imputation-adapted extension.
  • GWAS summary statistics — per-variant effect-size estimates from a genome-wide association study; the input weights for a PRS.
  • HLA region — human leukocyte antigen cluster on chr6; immune-presentation genes; dominant type 1 diabetes risk locus and the most polymorphic region in the genome.
  • JointPRS — multi-ancestry PRS method used for comparison.
  • LDpred2 — older single-population Bayesian PRS method; used as a European baseline.
  • Linkage disequilibrium (LD) — non-random correlation of nearby variants; its pattern differs by ancestry, which is why European PRS transfer poorly.
  • MAF / info-score — minor allele frequency / imputation-quality score; QC filters (MAF>0.001, info>0.4).
  • PRS / polygenic risk score — per-person weighted sum of risk-allele dosages.
  • PRS-CSx — Bayesian continuous-shrinkage method that jointly re-estimates PRS weights across multiple ancestries (the core engine here); phi = shrinkage tuning parameter.
  • Sensitivity / specificity — true-positive rate among cases / true-negative rate among controls, at a chosen cutoff.
  • TA-PS — the paper's trans-ancestry polygenic score; "(S)" = weighted version with scale transformation, the best performer.
  • Trans-ancestry — engineered to perform comparably across ancestry groups, not just the training population.
  • Type 1 diabetes — autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells; ~50% heritable.

Methods to lift

  • PRS-CSx — multi-ancestry Bayesian continuous-shrinkage PRS construction from per-ancestry GWAS summary statistics + ancestry-matched LD panels; sweep phi tuning parameter.
  • Modular HLA + non-HLA architecture — keep a validated HLA component, rebuild only the non-HLA part; exclude MHC (hg19 chr6:28,477,797–33,448,354) + 1 Mb flank to avoid overlap.
  • HLA-interaction toolkit (USF-HII/HLA-prs-toolkit, Sharp et al.) — models HLA DR-DQ gene-gene interaction via SNPs in strong LD with HLA haplotypes.
  • Regression-weighted score combination — estimate component coefficients (non-HLA, HLA-interaction, HLA) by logistic regression in one reference population; apply scale or MinMax transformations.
  • Fixed-percentile threshold evaluation — define cutoffs (95/90/85/80/75th centiles) in reference-population controls; report sensitivity/specificity/F1 with bootstrap CIs; compare with Z-test.
  • TOPMed (R3) imputation + plink --score — imputation and dosage scoring backbone; METAL for GWAS meta-analysis; PCAmatchR for PC-based case-control matching.
  • Pooled non-European analysis — combine small ancestry groups to recover statistical power when per-ancestry case counts are too low.

Proposed direction (conditional)

What to verify first

  • Whether any external non-European CF cohort exists for independent validation (the entire approach here depends on multiple ancestry-diverse cohorts).
  • Whether the target CF outcome (CFRD, lung-function decline) has a genuinely polygenic architecture worth scoring, versus being dominated by a few large-effect modifier loci (in which case a PRS is the wrong tool).
  • Whether CF-relevant GWAS summary statistics exist by ancestry at all — PRS-CSx needs per-ancestry GWAS inputs that the CF field may simply not have.
  • Whether a fixed-threshold screening framing even applies to CF complications, or whether the relevant question is within-patient risk stratification rather than population screening.

ALEX Episode two. Today's paper is Jumentier, Qu, Manousaki and colleagues, 2026, in Diabetologia, the European diabetes journal. The title is "Development and validation of a trans-ancestry polygenic risk score for type 1 diabetes." We're reading it because it is a current, careful demonstration of how you build a genetic risk score that actually holds up across ancestries — and that "across ancestries" problem is the quiet threat hanging over any score Runyu might one day build on the mostly-European cystic fibrosis cohort.

JORDAN Right, stop there. I need the vocabulary before the argument. What is a polygenic risk score, what is type 1 diabetes genetically, and what does "trans-ancestry" mean?

ALEX Let's build it up. Type 1 diabetes is the autoimmune form — the immune system destroys the insulin-making beta cells in the pancreas, so it's distinct from the lifestyle-and-metabolism type 2. It's strongly genetic; family studies suggest about half the risk is inherited. Most of that inherited risk sits in one place: the HLA region on chromosome 6. HLA stands for human leukocyte antigen — it's the cluster of immune-system genes that present molecular fragments to T-cells, and it's the most variable region in the whole genome. The rest of the risk is spread across roughly a hundred other single-letter variants, SNPs, in genes like INS, the insulin gene, and PTPN22, an immune-regulation gene. Now — a polygenic risk score, a PRS, is a single number you compute for a person by adding up the risk variants they carry, each weighted by how strongly that variant is associated with the disease. High score, high genetic risk.

JORDAN And where do those weights come from?

ALEX From a GWAS — a genome-wide association study — which scans hundreds of thousands of people, cases and controls, and estimates an effect size for every variant. Those effect sizes become the PRS weights. Here's the problem and the whole reason for this paper. Almost all the big GWAS were done in European-ancestry people. Linkage disequilibrium — the pattern of which variants travel together on a chromosome — differs between ancestries, and allele frequencies differ too. So a PRS trained on Europeans systematically performs worse in people of African, East Asian, or Hispanic ancestry. "Trans-ancestry," then, means a score deliberately engineered to perform comparably across all those groups rather than just the population it was trained in.

JORDAN How bad is the existing tool, and what's their target?

ALEX The existing gold standard is a European score called GRS2 — 67 variants, including the HLA part plus 32 non-HLA SNPs — which hits an area-under-the-curve of 0.92 in Europeans. That's a measure I should define: the AUROC, area under the receiver operating curve, runs from 0.5, which is a coin-flip, to 1.0, which is perfect separation of cases from controls. 0.92 is excellent. But in African Americans that same GRS2 drops to about 0.81, and — just as bad for a screening programme — its score distribution shifts by ancestry, so a "top 10%" cutoff in Europeans is not the top 10% in everyone else. The authors' goal was two things: build a score with comparable performance across ancestries, and find a single uniform high-risk threshold that works for everyone without first having to classify each person's ancestry.

JORDAN So what did they actually do?

ALEX They kept the HLA part of the existing European score — HLA is so dominant and so shared that re-engineering it buys little — and rebuilt only the non-HLA component. To do that they used two modern trans-ancestry PRS methods. PRS-CSx is the main one: it's a Bayesian method that takes GWAS summary statistics from multiple ancestries at once and jointly re-estimates the variant weights, sharing information across populations while respecting each one's linkage structure. JointPRS is a similar multi-ancestry method they used for comparison, and LDpred2 is an older single-population method they used as a European baseline. They fed in large GWAS — a European study with about 19,000 cases, an East Asian one, and meta-analysed African American and Hispanic studies — and generated 35 candidate scores. They then tested them in a multi-ancestry case-control cohort from Montreal of about 4,657 people, including 556 non-Europeans, and combined the rebuilt non-HLA part with the HLA part using weights estimated by logistic regression in the European subset.

JORDAN Push back somewhere — what's a soft spot in this design?

ALEX The non-European numbers. The headline claim is "works across ancestries," but the Montreal test cohort had only 139 Africans, 236 Hispanics, 56 East Asians, 125 South Asians. Fifty-six East Asian cases is a thin basis for an AUROC comparison, and the authors are candid that for several ancestries the small case counts meant they couldn't tell their weighted and unweighted versions apart statistically. They lean hard on combining everyone into one pooled non-European group of 556 to claw back statistical power, and they validate in bigger external cohorts — but the per-ancestry resolution is genuinely limited, and a reader should hold the "comparable across all ancestries" claim a little loosely for the smallest groups.

JORDAN Did the new score win?

ALEX Largely yes, and they were specific. Their best version is called TA-PS — the trans-ancestry polygenic score — in a weighted form. Across the testing populations it beat or matched GRS2 everywhere: AUROC 0.88 versus 0.86 in Europeans, 0.86 versus 0.83 in Africans, 0.86 versus 0.81 in Hispanics, 0.82 versus 0.81 in East Asians, 0.90 versus 0.87 in South Asians. In the pooled multi-ancestry set the weighted TA-PS hit 0.89 against GRS2's 0.85, and that difference was overwhelmingly significant. But the most important number for a screening tool is sensitivity at a fixed threshold. At a single uniform cutoff — the 90th percentile defined in European controls — the average sensitivity across ancestries was 0.74 for the new score versus only 0.53 for GRS2, and the African-ancestry sensitivity, the worst case, rose from 0.32 to a much more even level. Specificity stayed acceptable, at or above 0.83 everywhere. Then they validated in four independent cohorts — the Children's Hospital of Philadelphia, a Chinese cohort, All of Us, and UK Biobank — and the advantage held: in UK Biobank, for example, AUROC 0.86 versus 0.81, and sensitivity 0.67 versus 0.53 at the same cutoff.

JORDAN So they answered both of their questions?

ALEX They did, with a caveat I respect them for stating. The score performs more evenly across ancestries, and a single uniform threshold is now defensible — that was the whole point, because needing a different cutoff per ancestry breaks population screening for mixed-ancestry people. The honest caveat is that "more even" is not "equal": African-ancestry sensitivity in some validation subsets, like the Philadelphia cohort, was still lower than European even as it improved, and the small-sample uncertainty is real. But as a direction of travel — yes, they answered it.

JORDAN So what's the one concrete direction this hands the CFTR project?

ALEX Plain English first. The cystic fibrosis cohort Runyu might work in is overwhelmingly of European ancestry. If anyone ever built a polygenic predictor on it — say, a score for which CF patients develop CF-related diabetes, or who gets severe lung disease — the lesson here is that you cannot assume that score transfers to a Black or Hispanic or Asian CF patient, and you should test that explicitly rather than hope. The concrete direction: any CF-complication PRS built mainly on European CF patients should be evaluated for transferability across ancestry, ideally using a trans-ancestry construction method like PRS-CSx, and reported with per-ancestry AUROC and sensitivity at a fixed threshold — not a single pooled number. Formal one-sentence version: one possible direction this episode suggests is to assess and, if needed, re-engineer the cross-ancestry transferability of any CF-modifier or CFRD polygenic score using multi-ancestry methods such as PRS-CSx, reporting ancestry-stratified discrimination and fixed-threshold sensitivity rather than a pooled metric.

JORDAN And conditional on that direction — what does the paper close, and what stays open?

ALEX Conditional on a transferability-focused PRS direction, the paper closes the methods question cleanly: it gives a concrete recipe — keep HLA, rebuild the non-HLA part with PRS-CSx, combine with regression weights, and validate with AUROC, sensitivity, specificity, and F1 at fixed percentile thresholds defined in a reference population. That's a ready-made template. What it leaves open is whether any of it is even reachable in cystic fibrosis. Type 1 diabetes has GWAS with tens of thousands of cases across multiple ancestries; CF modifier traits do not — the entire CF whole-genome cohort is around 5,000 people and heavily European, so there may simply be too few non-European CF patients to estimate ancestry-specific weights at all. What Runyu would need to check, before committing, is how many non-European patients exist in the CF cohort and registry, whether any external non-European CF cohort exists for validation, and whether the relevant CF outcome — CFRD, lung-function decline — even has a polygenic architecture worth scoring, as opposed to being dominated by a handful of modifier loci. None of that is settled, and none of it assumes anyone has yet inspected the CF sequence data or its ancestry breakdown.
