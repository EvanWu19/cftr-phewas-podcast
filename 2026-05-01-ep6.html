Blurb

The prequel to Aksit 2020. Blackman and colleagues ran the founding genome-wide association study for cystic-fibrosis-related diabetes (CFRD) — discovery cohort of 3,059 CF patients (644 with CFRD), replication cohort of 694 (124 with CFRD). Two prongs: a candidate-SNP replication of general-population type-2-diabetes loci, and a genome-wide screen for CF-specific modifiers. They confirmed TCF7L2 plus three more type-2-diabetes loci (CDKAL1, CDKN2A/B, IGF2BP2) as also relevant in CFRD, and discovered SLC26A9 — an epithelial chloride/bicarbonate channel that physically interacts with CFTR — as a novel CF-specific CFRD modifier (HR=1.38, P=3.6e-8 discovery; HR=1.47, P=0.007 replication; combined P=9.8e-10). Five loci together: 8.3% variance explained, 68% population attributable risk. This is the paper that started the SLC26A9 story Aksit 2020 later extended.

Plain-English glossary (alphabetical)

  • Allele — which version of the DNA letter sits at a given genomic location.
  • Candidate-SNP replication — testing a small, pre-specified list of variants (typically borrowed from someone else's GWAS hits) in your cohort, rather than scanning the whole genome. Much smaller multiple-testing burden than a fresh GWAS.
  • CFRD (cystic-fibrosis-related diabetes) — the diabetes that develops in CF patients due to pancreatic damage. Distinct from type 1 and classic type 2 diabetes; affects roughly 40-50% of CF adults by age 30.
  • Discovery cohort vs replication cohort — find your candidate hits in cohort 1; re-test those specific hits in an independent cohort 2 before you believe them.
  • GWAS (genome-wide association study) — genotype thousands of people across hundreds of thousands of common variants, ask which variants are systematically more common in cases than controls. Significance threshold P < 5e-8.
  • Hazard ratio (HR) — a time-aware relative-risk measure. HR=1.38 means risk-allele carriers are 38% more likely to develop the disease at any given moment than non-carriers. Appropriate when disease onset is age-dependent.
  • Locus — a specific location on the genome.
  • Modifier gene — a gene that does not cause the disease itself but changes how severe it is or which complications appear. Volume knob on top of a main switch (here, CFTR).
  • Population attributable risk (PAR) — the fraction of disease cases that would disappear if a risk variant's effect vanished. Population-level interpretation; usually larger than variance explained.
  • SLC26A9 — gene encoding an epithelial chloride/bicarbonate channel that physically interacts with CFTR. Identified in this paper as a CF-specific CFRD modifier.
  • Variance explained — the fraction of patient-to-patient differences in the phenotype that the genetic markers can predict.

Methods to lift

  • Multi-cohort consortium GWAS design (CFF Modifier Consortium): aggregating Johns Hopkins twin/sibling, UNC, Toronto, and Case Western cohorts to reach n~3,700.
  • Two-prong analysis: candidate-SNP replication of external T2D GWAS hits + genome-wide screen for novel CF-specific modifiers.
  • Survival-analysis (time-to-CFRD) framework rather than logistic case/control — appropriate for age-dependent complications.
  • Independent discovery + replication cohort with combined-cohort meta-analysis.
  • Reporting both variance explained and population attributable risk for the lead variant set.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Phenome-wide scan of SLC26A9 carrier status across CFFPR (CF Foundation Patient Registry) phenotypes — lung-function trajectories, pancreatic exocrine function, Pseudomonas colonization age, CFRD status — using the WGS sub-cohort. Reverse-orientation analysis: fix the locus, scan the phenome.

Gap-map: closes

  • The CFRD branch of the SLC26A9 phenome. SLC26A9-CFRD association is replicated (Blackman 2013) and extended (Aksit 2020). Re-testing the CFRD endpoint adds nothing.

Gap-map: leaves open

  • Every non-CFRD branch of the SLC26A9 phenome — lung-function trajectories, pancreatic-sufficient vs pancreatic-insufficient status, Pseudomonas aeruginosa colonization age, CF liver disease, nutritional outcomes.
  • Methods generation gap: Blackman 2013 is candidate-SNP-replication era. Modern polygenic-risk-score transfer methods (LDpred2, PRS-CS) integrate evidence across many variants jointly and have not been applied here.
  • Joint genotype-phenotype architecture across complications (does SLC26A9 modify multiple CF outcomes, or is the diabetes effect isolated?).

What to verify first

  • Is SLC26A9 well-genotyped in the CFF WGS variant calls? Read depth, callable region, lead-variant coverage.
  • Does CFFPR have the non-pulmonary phenotype catalog at sufficient granularity — pancreatic exocrine function, Pseudomonas colonization timing, hepatobiliary outcomes?
  • WGS sub-cohort overlap with the Blackman 2013 discovery cohort. If a substantial fraction of WGS patients were in the original GWAS, double-counting will falsely re-confirm an already-replicated signal; analysis must condition on inclusion or restrict to the non-overlapping subset.

JORDAN Hundreds of thousands of statistical tests at once.

ALEX Yes, which is why the significance threshold is so strict — five times ten-to-the-minus-eight. Sort of a volume knob on top of the main switch.

JORDAN Got it. Locus?

ALEX A locus is just a location on the genome — a specific spot. An allele is which version of the DNA letter you have at that spot. Hazard ratio, abbreviated HR, is a relative-risk measure that accounts for time. A hazard ratio of one-point-three-eight means that at any given moment, people carrying the risk allele are thirty-eight percent more likely to develop the disease than people who don't.

JORDAN It accounts for the fact that CFRD develops over decades.

ALEX Right. Variance explained is what fraction of the differences between patients you can predict from those genetic markers. And population attributable risk — abbreviated PAR — is a more dramatic statistic. It asks: if these risk alleles vanished from the population entirely, what fraction of disease cases would disappear with them?

JORDAN So PAR is bigger than variance explained.

ALEX Usually much bigger. Two more terms. Discovery cohort versus replication cohort — you find your hits in cohort one, then you re-test those same hits in a separate cohort two before you believe them. Much smaller multiple-testing burden.

JORDAN Last one — SLC26A9.

ALEX SLC26A9 is a gene encoding an epithelial chloride and bicarbonate channel. It sits on the surface of secretory cells. The interesting fact is that it physically interacts with CFTR, the protein that's broken in CF. So it's not a random gene — it's a partner of the disease-causing gene.

JORDAN Okay. So what was the question Blackman and colleagues set out to answer?

ALEX It's a two-part question, and both parts are interesting. Part one — by 2013, the type-2-diabetes GWAS field was mature. Researchers had identified roughly seventy genome-wide significant loci for ordinary type 2 diabetes in the general population. The question was: do any of those general-population type-2-diabetes risk variants also raise the risk of CFRD?

JORDAN Because it's not obvious they would.

ALEX Right. CFRD is mechanistically different. It starts with the pancreas being scarred and clogged by CF mucus, which kills off the islet cells that make insulin. So you might think — different mechanism, different genetics. Or you might think — once you're in a state of insulin insufficiency, the same modifiers that push someone in the general population over the edge into type 2 diabetes might also push a CF patient over the edge into CFRD. Open question.

JORDAN And part two of the question?

ALEX Part two — are there CF-specific genetic modifiers of CFRD that have nothing to do with general-population type 2 diabetes? Variants you'd only see by looking inside a CF cohort?

JORDAN How would those even arise?

ALEX Think about it this way. If a variant changes how badly your pancreas scars from CF mucus, that variant has nothing to do with type 2 diabetes in the general population — because the general population doesn't have CF mucus. You'd only ever detect it by running a GWAS where everyone in the study already has CF.

JORDAN A modifier of the CF process itself.

ALEX Exactly. And that's the more original scientific contribution they were after. Type-2-diabetes overlap is interesting and useful, but it's a translation question. CF-specific modifiers — that's where you find biology nobody else can find.

JORDAN Why didn't anyone do this in 2008 or 2010?

ALEX Sample size. CF is a rare disease — about thirty thousand patients in the US. To run a credible GWAS you need thousands. The CFF Modifier Consortium spent years aggregating cohorts across multiple centers — the Johns Hopkins twin and sibling study, the University of North Carolina cohort, the Toronto cohort, the Case Western cohort. ALEX They had two cohorts. The discovery cohort was three thousand fifty-nine individuals with CF, six hundred forty-four of whom had developed CFRD. The replication cohort was an independent six hundred ninety-four CF individuals, with one hundred twenty-four CFRD cases.

ALEX Right — extraordinary by 2013 CF-genetics standards. The analysis had two prongs. Prong one — candidate-SNP replication. JORDAN And prong two was the genome-wide screen.

ALEX Yes. They genotyped common variants across the whole genome and ran a survival-analysis association test. JORDAN And then they replicated.

ALEX Anything that crossed genome-wide significance in the discovery cohort got re-tested in the independent six-hundred-ninety-four-person replication cohort.
