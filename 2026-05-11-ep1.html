Blurb

Vuckovic et al. assemble the largest blood-cell trait GWAS to date — 563,085 European-ancestry participants from UK Biobank plus the Blood Cell Consortium — and use it to put common-variant and rare-variant genetics on a single canvas for 29 blood phenotypes. They discover 5,106 new sentinel variants (16,900 total conditionally independent associations across 7,122 loci), confirm an omnigenic-flavored architecture empirically, identify 109 putative splice-altering variants in 6 genes, and validate a curated catalog of 314 Mendelian blood disorder genes by showing that common GWAS signal is enriched in and near them. Polygenic scores built from the common variants add meaningful prediction on top of rare pathogenic variant carrier status for several blood phenotypes — the central methodological move we'd want to learn from for a CF modifier PheWAS. The proposed direction this paper suggests: build a pre-registered Mendelian-CF-modifier gene catalog (anchored on Corvol 2015 plus phenocopy genes plus ciliopathy genes), then ask whether registry-wide PRS adds incremental prediction over rare-pathogenic-variant carrier status across the CF Genome Project. Open question: can a 5,000-person CF cohort generate signal at the scale Vuckovic gets at 563,000.

Plain-English glossary (alphabetical)

  • Blood cell trait — a number measured in a routine blood test (red/white/platelet counts, MCV, reticulocytes, etc.).
  • Fine-mapping — narrowing a significant GWAS region from many correlated variants to one or two causal candidates.
  • GWAS — genome-wide association study; tests millions of variants against a phenotype with a 5×10⁻⁸ significance threshold.
  • Hematopoiesis — production of blood cells in the bone marrow.
  • Linkage disequilibrium (LD) — non-independence of nearby variants because recombination between them is rare.
  • MAF — minor allele frequency; fraction of chromosomes carrying the less-common version of a variant.
  • Mendelian disease — single gene, single severe variant, classical inheritance (CF, sickle cell, hemochromatosis).
  • Omnigenic model — 2017 hypothesis (Pritchard) that almost every gene expressed in the relevant tissue contributes to a complex trait via gene-network propagation from a small core set.
  • PRS (polygenic risk score) — weighted sum of common-variant effects estimating a person's genetic burden for a trait.
  • Sentinel variant — the representative variant used to tag an independent locus after LD clumping.
  • Splice-altering variant — DNA change that alters how mRNA is cut and joined, producing a different protein.

ALEX Welcome back. Today we're reading Vuckovic and colleagues, 2020, in Cell — "The Polygenic and Monogenic Basis of Blood Traits and Diseases." Senior authors are Vijay Sankaran at Boston Children's and Nicole Soranzo at the Wellcome Sanger Institute. It's a five-hundred-and-sixty-three-thousand-person UK Biobank study of blood cell traits.

JORDAN And we're reading it because it does something specific and unusual that we want to learn from.

ALEX Yes. It puts common-variant and rare-variant genetics into a single picture for the same set of clinical phenotypes. That's the move we eventually want to make for cystic fibrosis modifier biology, and this paper is one of the cleanest templates in the literature.

JORDAN Before we go into what it does, can we slow down? There are a lot of terms in this abstract that I want pinned down before they accumulate.

ALEX Please. Let's start at the top. A blood cell trait is a number that gets measured every time you get a routine blood test — your red blood cell count, your white cell count, your platelet count, the average size of a red cell, the proportion of immature reticulocytes. Twenty-nine of these traits, in this paper. Hematopoiesis just means the biological process of making blood cells in the bone marrow.

JORDAN GWAS?

ALEX GWAS stands for genome-wide association study. You take a few hundred thousand people, you measure something about them — height, blood pressure, platelet count — and you ask, at each of millions of positions in the genome, does the version of the DNA at that position correlate with the trait. You correct for multiple testing because you tested millions of positions. The significance threshold is five times ten to the minus eight, written five-e-minus-eight. Variants that cross it are called genome-wide-significant.

JORDAN MAF? PRS? Mendelian?

ALEX MAF is minor allele frequency — how common the less-common version of the variant is in the population. If a variant has MAF one percent, one out of every hundred chromosomes carries it. PRS is polygenic risk score — you take all the small-effect common variants associated with a trait, add them up weighted by their effects, and you get a single number per person estimating their genetic burden. Mendelian disease is the textbook kind — a single gene, a single severe variant, a clear inheritance pattern. Cystic fibrosis is Mendelian. So is sickle cell disease.

JORDAN And omnigenic, splice-altering, fine-mapping.

ALEX The omnigenic model is a 2017 hypothesis from Pritchard's lab proposing that for many complex traits, essentially every gene expressed in the relevant tissue contributes something — there are a few core genes and a vast number of peripheral genes whose effects flow through gene-regulatory networks. A splice-altering variant changes how the cell's machinery cuts and joins the messenger RNA — same DNA letter change, but the protein that comes out is different. Fine-mapping is the post-GWAS step where you take a region with a significant signal and try to narrow it down to one or two actually-causal variants rather than the hundreds in linkage disequilibrium with them.

JORDAN Linkage disequilibrium?

ALEX Two variants near each other on a chromosome tend to be inherited together because recombination is rare on short distances. That non-independence is linkage disequilibrium, abbreviated LD.

JORDAN Good. Background now. Why this paper?

ALEX Three pressures converged. First, by 2020, GWAS had been around for a decade and had found tens of thousands of variants associated with thousands of traits. But almost all of those signals were common variants with tiny effects. The clinical relevance was murky. Second, exome sequencing in biobank-scale cohorts was just becoming available, which let researchers see rare protein-coding variants — the kind that cause Mendelian disease. Third, blood cell traits are an extraordinarily well-suited testbed for genetics because they are quantitative, they are measured routinely, they have decades of curated Mendelian gene catalogs behind them — hemoglobinopathies, platelet disorders, bone marrow failure — and the cells whose biology you're trying to understand can be isolated and assayed directly. So the question this paper sets up is: if you really push GWAS as hard as you can on a clinically rich set of traits, what does the architecture actually look like, and does it touch the Mendelian-disease world in a useful way.

JORDAN And the unknown before this paper was what exactly?

ALEX Several things at once. Whether the omnigenic model is empirically supported, or whether instead you can find a tidy core set of causal genes. Whether splicing changes are a major hidden mechanism that earlier GWAS missed because most fine-mapping pipelines look for missense changes and regulatory variants. Whether a polygenic score built from common variants can predict who is at risk for a Mendelian-looking blood phenotype like iron deficiency or a thrombocytopenia. And whether the rare large-effect variants — the kind clinical labs report when they sequence a patient — sit on a continuum with the common-variant signal or on a separate axis. Before this paper, those four questions had partial answers from smaller studies. This is the first time anyone put them all on one substrate.

JORDAN Methods. How do they tackle it?

ALEX A meta-analysis. The discovery cohort is UK Biobank — about four hundred thousand European-ancestry participants with blood counts measured at recruitment. They add a previously published Blood Cell Consortium meta-analysis to push the total European-ancestry sample size to five hundred and sixty-three thousand. They run GWAS on each of twenty-nine blood cell phenotypes. They use linkage disequilibrium clumping to collapse the millions of significant variants into independent loci. The number that comes out is seven thousand one hundred and twenty-two loci, of which five thousand one hundred and six had never been reported before. They build a curated catalog of three hundred and fourteen genes known to cause Mendelian blood disorders — that catalog is built by expert clinicians from the U.K. National Institute for Health Research Rare Diseases sequencing project. They then check whether GWAS sentinel variants near or in those genes are enriched compared to random expectation, fine-map every locus, annotate variants for splicing impact, and finally build polygenic scores and ask whether the polygenic score adds predictive value on top of the rare pathogenic variants in the same gene.

JORDAN Design choice you'd push back on?

ALEX European-ancestry only. They acknowledge it but the design forecloses any direct cross-ancestry replication. You can argue that's the cost of using meta-analyzed BCX data the way they did, but the trade-off is real and not framed strongly enough in the paper.

JORDAN Results.

ALEX Headline numbers first. Sixteen thousand nine hundred conditionally independent associations across the twenty-nine traits, organized into the seven-thousand-one-hundred-and-twenty-two loci, of which five thousand one hundred and six were genuinely new. They confirm the omnigenic-model prediction empirically: every gene expressed in hematopoietic tissue has some statistical support for affecting at least one blood trait, with a long tail of small effects converging on a smaller set of core regulators. They identify a hundred and nine variants with putative splicing consequences in six genes that have plausible mechanistic stories — variants that change how the gene is spliced rather than what amino acid the protein contains. The Mendelian-gene catalog gets validated — common GWAS variants are enriched in or near the three hundred and fourteen Mendelian blood disorder genes far above chance.

JORDAN Specific named hits.

ALEX Known clinical-genetics gene-disease pairs come up exactly where you'd want them. TMPRSS6 with iron-refractory iron deficiency anemia, abbreviated IRIDA — that's an inherited iron-absorption defect. MPL with congenital amegakaryocytic thrombocytopenia — a rare bleeding disorder of newborns. PIEZO1 with dehydrated stomatocytosis — a red-cell membrane disease. HBB with beta-thalassemia. GP9 with Bernard-Soulier syndrome — the classical inherited platelet bleeding disorder. JAK2, the somatic-driver gene for the myeloproliferative neoplasms, appears with the expected V617F-region signal on platelet and red-cell counts. GATA1, a master hematopoietic transcription factor, comes up too, as does HFE — the gene that causes hereditary hemochromatosis, the common iron-overload disease. Did they answer the framing question? Yes, mostly. The architecture is omnigenic in flavor, splicing is a quantitatively important mechanism, Mendelian genes do sit on the common-variant signal, and polygenic scores add meaningful prediction on top of the rare pathogenic variants for several blood phenotypes — not just better than the common-variant score alone, but better than the rare-variant carrier-status indicator alone.

JORDAN One possible direction this opens for a CF modifier PheWAS.

ALEX Here's a concrete one — and it's deliberately framed as one possible direction, not a chosen plan. Build a curated catalog of Mendelian-style cystic-fibrosis-relevant genes — the modifier list locked from prior consortium GWAS like Corvol 2015 and Wright 2011 augmented with CF-Mendelian phenocopy genes like SCNN1A, SCNN1B, SCNN1G, the bicarbonate-transporter genes, and the ciliopathy genes — and ask, on the CF Genome Project whole-genome data, whether a polygenic score built from registry-wide phenome-wide associations adds predictive value on top of the rare pathogenic variants in the same gene set, for phenotypes like lung function decline, chronic Pseudomonas infection, CF-related diabetes, and exocrine pancreatic insufficiency. In one formal sentence: a Vuckovic-style integrated polygenic-plus-rare-variant decomposition of the CF Genome Project whole-genome cohort against a pre-registered Mendelian-CF-modifier gene catalog and a registry-wide phenotype panel, reporting per-phenotype incremental predictive accuracy of the polygenic component over rare-pathogenic-variant carrier status.

JORDAN Gap. What does Vuckovic close, leave open, and what would we need to verify before committing?

ALEX It closes the proof of concept that you can integrate polygenic and monogenic genetics on a single substrate and get something more clinically useful than either alone. It also closes the methodological worry that splicing would be a confounder hidden in every GWAS — they show it's tractable. It leaves open three things directly relevant to us. First, this is European-only at five hundred thousand people; we don't know if the same architecture description holds in non-European-ancestry CF subpopulations. Second, blood cell traits are unusually well-powered because of measurement frequency and quantitative resolution; CF endpoints are coarser and rarer. Third, the Mendelian-gene catalog they use is curated by U.K. clinicians for blood disease — there's no equivalent locked Mendelian-CF-modifier gene catalog yet, and assembling one is a non-trivial scientific task in its own right. What we'd need to check before committing: whether the CF Genome Project sample size can power even a tenth of the per-trait sentinel-variant count Vuckovic gets at half a million; whether the registry phenotypes are quantitative enough to admit Vuckovic-style continuous-trait GWAS or whether we'll be stuck with binary endpoints; and whether the Mendelian-CF gene catalog can be assembled and pre-registered before the analysis begins, to avoid the post-hoc-gene-list problem that has burned earlier CF candidate-gene studies.

JORDAN One more thing on the gap — the polygenic-plus-monogenic decomposition. Why does that specifically matter for CF.

ALEX Because CF historically gets framed as pure Mendelian — one gene, two recessive alleles, severe phenotype. But the registry shows enormous variability in lung function trajectory, infection age, pancreatic involvement, and survival even within F508del homozygotes. Vuckovic's framework lets you say explicitly: some of that variance is monogenic — a second hit in a modifier gene — and some of it is polygenic background — the cumulative effect of many small variants across the genome. Separating those quantitatively, on the same patients, is the kind of move that hasn't been published for CF yet. Whether it's feasible at our cohort size is the open question.
