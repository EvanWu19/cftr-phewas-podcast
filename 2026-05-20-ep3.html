Blurb

Christopher Morrow and colleagues from Johns Hopkins ran a questionnaire-based exposure study in 703 CF patients from the U.S. CF Twin-Sibling Study (NCT00037778), linking self-reported cat and dog ownership to respiratory outcomes drawn from chart review and the CFFPR. After adjustment for demographic and genotype confounders using generalized estimating equations clustered by family, cat ownership was associated with a 1.66-fold increased odds of nasal polyps (P=0.024) and combined cat-and-dog ownership with a 2.01-fold increased odds of wheezing (P=0.009). No association with FEV1, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, or MRSA acquisition. The direction this episode suggests is a CFTR PheWAS that models environmental exposures as a coequal covariate dimension to genotype, with explicit gene-by-environment interaction tests rather than nuisance adjustment. The open question is whether interaction-term analyses at the scale of a 5000-patient CF cohort have sufficient power to detect anything modest.

Plain-English glossary

  • ABPA (allergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis): allergic reaction to inhaled Aspergillus mold spores; affects ~10% of CF patients.
  • CF (cystic fibrosis): recessive multi-system disease caused by pathogenic variants in CFTR.
  • CFTR: Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane conductance Regulator — chloride/bicarbonate channel in epithelial cells.
  • CFFPR (CF Foundation Patient Registry): longitudinal U.S. CF registry that links clinical, microbiology, and treatment data.
  • CF Twin-Sibling Study: observational cohort (Cutting/JHU; NCT00037778) of CF patients recruited with a CF twin or sibling, 2000–2013.
  • CF-specific FEV1: FEV1 normalized using Kulich equations derived from 287,000 PFTs in 21,000 CF patients, allowing within-CF comparisons across ages.
  • F508del (ΔF508, p.Phe508del): the most common pathogenic CFTR variant; three-nucleotide deletion removing phenylalanine at position 508.
  • FEV1 (forced expiratory volume in 1 second): volume of air exhaled in the first second of a forced expiration; primary spirometric measure of obstructive lung disease severity.
  • GEE (generalized estimating equations): regression framework that accommodates clustered observations (e.g., siblings within a family).
  • Kaplan-Meier with log-rank test: time-to-event analysis comparing acquisition curves between groups.
  • MRSA (methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus): drug-resistant Staph bacterium that colonizes CF airways.
  • Nasal polyps: benign mucosal growths in the nose; common upper-airway complication in CF.
  • PFT (pulmonary function test): spirometric measurement of lung function including FEV1.
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa (P. aeruginosa): opportunistic bacterial pathogen; chronic colonization strongly worsens CF lung function trajectory.

Methods to lift

  • CF Twin-Sibling Study cohort — 703 CF patients recruited with a CF twin or sibling; Cutting/JHU; IRB NA_00035659; NCT00037778.
  • CF-specific FEV1 (Kulich equations) — within-CF normalized reference; superior to general-population %predicted for within-CF comparison.
  • 3-year mean PFT, minimum 4 PFTs over 2 years for decline calculation — standard operational definition for CF FEV1 trajectory.
  • GEE with independent correlation structure, family-clustered — accommodates twin/sibling pair structure; adjusts standard errors.
  • Kaplan-Meier + log-rank — for age-of-acquisition outcomes (Pseudomonas, MRSA).
  • Pancreatic status by genotype + clinical fallback — two-pancreatic-sufficient mutations vs. two-pancreatic-insufficient; clinical adjudication if genotype indeterminate.
  • Race/ethnicity binarization (any non-white reported = non-white) — used for regression adjustment; coarsely operationalized for this analysis.
  • Stata IC 11.0 — analysis platform.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage:

Design a CFTR PheWAS that explicitly models a panel of environmental exposures — secondhand smoke exposure, household income, insurance status, urban-vs-rural residence, pet exposure — as a coequal covariate dimension to genotype, with formal gene-by-environment interaction tests for each candidate modifier locus against each environmental variable. Outcomes drawn from canonically defined clusters of correlated CF complication traits, statistical framework upgraded from GEE (single-locus) to a linear-mixed-model implementation (genome-wide) with a genetic relatedness matrix capturing the multiplex-family structure in the CFFPR/Twin-Sib subset.

What to verify first

  • The CFFPR captures the environmental exposure variables of interest (smoke exposure, income, insurance, pet ownership, urbanicity, ambient air pollution), at what longitudinal density.
  • For cross-sectional exposure variables, the snapshot is informative enough to model interactions despite the bias the authors flag.
  • Interaction-term GWAS at n=5000 has any statistical power; interaction tests typically require 3–4× the sample size of main-effect tests for the same effect size.
  • LMM with GRM software (GCTA, BOLT-LMM, SAIGE, REGENIE) supports the multiplex-family structure in the Twin-Sib subset; some pipelines reject high-relatedness samples by default.
  • The complication-trait outcome definitions used here (chart-review-based nasal polyps, sinus disease, wheezing, ABPA, etc.) are reproducible from registry phenotype tables without going back to original chart-review forms.

Gap-map (CFF-cohort PheWAS lens)

ALEX Today's third paper is Morrow and colleagues, "Cat and dog exposure and respiratory morbidities in cystic fibrosis," published in The Journal of Pediatrics in October 2014. First author Christopher Morrow at Johns Hopkins, senior author Joseph Collaco, with the Cutting lab as the genetics anchor.

JORDAN We have been reading mostly biobank methods papers and CF modifier-gene papers. This one is about pets. Why is it on the reading list?

ALEX Because it teaches three things that turn out to matter for a CFTR PheWAS: how the CF Twin-Sibling Study cohort actually operationalizes outcomes, how environmental covariates get adjusted for in a CF analysis, and where the limits of cross-sectional questionnaire data lie. Plus, environmental exposures are confounders for any genetic modifier study run on the same patients. If the genetic effect we want to attribute to a SNP is partly an effect of cat ownership tracking with that SNP's ancestry distribution, the genetic estimate is biased. So the environmental data is methodologically relevant whether or not we care about pets.

JORDAN I want vocabulary first. What is the CF Twin-Sibling Study?

ALEX It is a long-running observational cohort that recruited CF patients from U.S. CF centers between October 2000 and March 2013, with the recruitment criterion being that the participant had to have CF and a twin or sibling who also had CF. Why twins and siblings? Because if you are studying gene-environment interactions in CF, you want pairs of people who share the major genetic insult — most CF patients carry F508del at least once, often homozygously — and ideally share a lot of background genome through family relatedness, while differing in the modifier genome and in environmental exposures. The pair design lets you test things you cannot test in unrelated individuals. The study is anchored at Johns Hopkins, in the Cutting lab. The IRB protocol number is NA_00035659. The clinicaltrials.gov anchor is NCT00037778.

JORDAN Acronyms. CF, CFTR, F508del.

ALEX CF is cystic fibrosis, a life-shortening recessive disease caused by mutations in the CFTR gene — that gene encodes the cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator, a chloride and bicarbonate channel embedded in the apical surface of epithelial cells throughout the airways, pancreas, intestine, sweat glands, and reproductive tract. F508del — sometimes written delta-F508 or p.Phe508del — is a three-nucleotide deletion that removes phenylalanine at position 508 of the protein. It is the most common pathogenic CFTR variant; roughly half of patients in U.S. registries are F508del homozygous.

JORDAN And the clinical outcomes mentioned — FEV1, ABPA, MRSA.

ALEX FEV1 is forced expiratory volume in one second — the volume of air a person can blow out in the first second of a forced exhale during a pulmonary function test, the PFT. FEV1 percent predicted is FEV1 expressed as a percentage of what you would expect for a healthy person of the same age, sex, height, and ethnicity. In CF you also see CF-specific FEV1, which uses CF-specific reference equations — the Kulich equations, derived from over 287,000 PFTs in 21,000 CF patients — because comparing CF FEV1 against general-population reference equations gives you mostly noise about how sick CF is rather than about how much variation is between patients. ABPA is allergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis, an allergic reaction to inhaled Aspergillus mold spores that occurs in about 10 percent of CF patients. MRSA is methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus — a bacterial pathogen that often colonizes the CF airway. Pseudomonas aeruginosa, P. aeruginosa, is the other big airway pathogen in CF, chronic colonization with which dramatically worsens lung function trajectory.

JORDAN One more. GEE.

ALEX Generalized estimating equations. It is a statistical regression framework that lets you fit a model to data where observations are clustered — like family members in this study — while accounting for the fact that observations inside a cluster correlate. The naive alternative, ordinary regression, would treat siblings as independent and inflate the certainty of your estimates. GEE corrects for that.

JORDAN What was the question, and what was known before?

ALEX The setup question is the one a CF parent asks the pulmonologist: should we have a cat? Should we have a dog? Will it make my child's lungs worse, give them more sinus infections, transmit Pseudomonas? The published evidence going in was thin and contradictory. Pet allergens are linked to worse outcomes in asthma. A small case series of 20 CF families found shared bacteria and fungi between pets and patients. Case reports describe interspecies transmission of Pseudomonas from a CF patient to a pet cat, and Bordetella bronchiseptica from a kitten to a CF patient, and MRSA transmission in both directions. But case reports do not give you population-level risk. The Twin-Sibling cohort is one of the only datasets large enough and well-phenotyped enough to give a clinical answer.

JORDAN How did they do it?

ALEX 703 CF patients in the Twin-Sibling cohort. Self-reported questionnaire about pet ownership at baseline. Cat owners and dog owners were tracked separately and as combined cat-plus-dog ownership. Outcomes from chart review plus the CF Foundation Patient Registry: presence of nasal polyps, sinus disease, wheezing, environmental allergies, ABPA, and Pseudomonas and MRSA infection status with age of acquisition. Lung function: CF-specific FEV1 derived from a three-year mean of PFTs after questionnaire completion, with at least four PFTs over two years required for the rate-of-decline calculation. Subjects who owned cats and dogs were on average two to three years older than non-owners, more likely to be white, and dog owners specifically were more likely to be F508del homozygous and pancreatic insufficient — those are confounders that have to be adjusted for. They used GEE with an independent correlation structure clustered by family, adjusted for age, race/ethnicity, and pancreatic function. Kaplan-Meier with log-rank tests for age-of-acquisition. All analyses in Stata IC 11.0. ALEX pushes back here: the questionnaire captures pet ownership at one moment in time. A child whose parents got rid of the dog two years before questionnaire completion is in the no-dog group, and a child whose parents got the dog the week before is in the dog group, and those are not equivalent exposures. The authors flag this themselves as the biggest limitation.

JORDAN What did they find?

ALEX Forty-seven percent of subjects owned a dog. Twenty-eight percent owned a cat. The U.S. general-population rates are about 36 percent dogs, 30 percent cats — so CF families own more dogs than baseline, similar cats. After adjustment for demographics: dog ownership was associated with nothing — no respiratory outcomes, no Pseudomonas, no MRSA. Cat ownership was associated with a 1.66-fold increased odds of nasal polyps, adjusted p-value 0.024. Combined cat-and-dog ownership was associated with a 2.01-fold increased odds of wheezing, adjusted p-value 0.009. There was no FEV1 effect — but that was cross-sectional FEV1, not a longitudinal decline measurement carried forward. There was no effect on either prevalence or age of acquisition for Pseudomonas or MRSA. Did they answer the parent's question? Mostly yes, with hedges. The clinical answer is: cats are associated with nasal polyps, cat-plus-dog is associated with wheezing, neither is associated with FEV1 in this analysis, and pets do not appear to be a Pseudomonas or MRSA acquisition route. The hedges: cross-sectional, cannot ascribe causality, cannot estimate duration of exposure, cannot capture changes over time.

JORDAN What does this paper suggest for a CFTR PheWAS?

ALEX Here is one specific direction this episode suggests. The genetic-modifier literature in CF has tended to treat environmental variables as nuisance covariates — adjust for smoke exposure, adjust for socioeconomic status, then estimate the SNP effect on FEV1 and report. But the genetic and environmental effects do not have to be independent. F508del homozygous patients were significantly enriched among dog owners in this cohort. That is almost certainly confounding by ancestry and SES — F508del homozygosity tracks with northern European ancestry which tracks with pet-ownership rates — but the joint statistical structure has not been characterized. The proposed direction this episode suggests is: a CFTR PheWAS that explicitly models environmental exposure as a covariate dimension equal in weight to the genetic dimension, looking for gene-by-environment interactions in CF complication phenotypes, rather than treating environment as a nuisance regressor. Formally: an interaction-term GWAS or modifier-locus survey across CF complication endpoints in the CFF cohort that tests every candidate modifier variant for interaction with a defined panel of environmental exposure variables — secondhand smoke, household income, insurance status, urban-vs-rural, pet exposure — recorded in the registry.

JORDAN What does this paper close, what does it leave open?

ALEX What it closes — it documents the operational definitions the Twin-Sibling cohort uses for nasal polyps, sinus disease, wheezing, ABPA, and Pseudomonas and MRSA acquisition. Those definitions can be reused. It also documents one of the cleanest worked examples of CF-specific FEV1 referencing using the Kulich equations, which Runyu should adopt rather than general-population FEV1 percent predicted. And it documents GEE with family clustering as a viable analysis framework for the Twin-Sibling subset. What it leaves open — the most consequential limitation is that the environmental exposure of interest is captured at one questionnaire moment, not longitudinally. For a real gene-by-environment PheWAS you would want time-varying exposure, which the registry does collect for some variables (insurance status, household income, BMI) but not for others (pet ownership probably is not asked repeatedly, smoke exposure is asked but the granularity varies). What we would need to check before committing to that direction — first, that the CFFPR captures the environmental exposure variables Runyu would want to interact with genetics, and at what longitudinal density. Second, that for the variables that are captured cross-sectionally, the cross-sectional snapshot is informative enough to model interactions, given the bias the authors flag here. Third, that an interaction GWAS at the scale of a 5000-patient CF cohort has any statistical power at all — interaction tests typically need three to four times the sample size that main-effect tests need to detect the same effect size, and we are not in a UK Biobank power regime. Fourth, that the GEE family-clustered framework extends to a genome-wide setting, since GEE was not built for millions of tests in parallel — a linear mixed model with a genetic relatedness matrix is the typical scale-friendly substitute. None of these have been verified by Runyu personally and none should be assumed.
