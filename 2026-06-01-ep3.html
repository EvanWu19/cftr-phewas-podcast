Blurb

Lin et al. (2024, Eur Respir J) report the first genome-wide association study of Pseudomonas aeruginosa infection susceptibility in cystic fibrosis. In 1,037 Canadians (CF Gene Modifier Study), restricted to pancreatic-insufficient, modulator-naïve patients, they analyzed an age-residual chronic-infection phenotype with a linear mixed model (GENESIS). SNP-based heritability was ~46% for chronic-infection age (vs ~22%, non-significant, for first infection), echoing prior twin estimates (85% vs 0%). Two novel genome-wide-significant loci emerged: 5p12 (rs62369766; NNT eQTL, near FGF10; validated in 501 French patients) and 13q12.12 (rs927553; no single candidate gene named in text), jointly explaining ~3% of variance. A CF lung-function PRS predicted infection age (p=0.002); colocalization appeared at the X-linked SLC6A14/AGTR2 locus; bidirectional MR supported lung function → infection timing (β≈0.78 yr) but not the reverse (weak instrument, F≈9). Proposed direction: replicate and extend these loci in the larger CFF WGS cohort, adding rare/structural variation to chase the high-but-largely-unexplained heritability. Central open questions: whether CFF registry culture data supports Lin's chronic-infection definition, and whether enough modulator-naïve, pancreatic-insufficient patients remain to power replication.

Plain-English glossary (alphabetical)

  • Candidate gene: the gene at an associated locus you suspect is responsible; a GWAS points at the neighborhood, not the house.
  • CFTR: the cystic fibrosis gene; when broken, airway salt/water balance fails and mucus thickens.
  • Chronic Pseudomonas infection (here): ≥3 consecutive years of cultures with positivity in ≥2 of the 3.
  • Colocalization: statistical evidence that two traits share the same causal signal at a locus.
  • eQTL (expression quantitative trait locus): a variant that changes how much a nearby gene is expressed.
  • GWAS (genome-wide association study): scans common variants genome-wide for trait associations.
  • Heritability: fraction of trait variation attributable to genetics (twin estimate: 85% chronic, ~0% first infection).
  • Linear mixed model: association method accounting for relatedness (siblings) — used via GENESIS.
  • Locus (pl. loci): a genomic neighborhood associated with the trait.
  • Mendelian randomization (MR): uses genetic variants as proxies for an exposure to test causation.
  • Modifier gene: a gene elsewhere that tunes severity of a single-gene disease like CF.
  • Modulator-naïve: not on CFTR-correcting drugs, which would alter the disease and blur genetic signal.
  • Pancreatic-insufficient genotype: the more uniformly severe CFTR class; restricting to it reduces CFTR confounding.
  • PheWAS: testing one variant against many phenotypes (here, in general-population lung-function data).
  • PRS (polygenic risk score): sums many small-effect variants into one predisposition score.
  • SNP ("snip"): single-nucleotide polymorphism — a single-letter site of variation; the GWAS test unit.
  • 5×10⁻⁸: genome-wide significance threshold.

Methods to lift

  • Phenotype engineering — age-of-chronic-infection regressed on current age; symmetric residual used as outcome (handles right-skew, K–S symmetry p=0.223).
  • Confounder control by design — restrict to pancreatic-insufficient CFTR genotypes (reduce CFTR confounding) and modulator-naïve patients (avoid treatment effect).
  • GENESIS linear mixed model — association testing accounting for sibling relatedness.
  • GCTA — SNP-based heritability estimation (46% chronic vs 22% first infection).
  • Independent replication — French CF Modifier Gene Study (n=501) validated the 5p12 locus.
  • Cross-trait C+T PRS — 7,756 LD-pruned SaKnorm lung-function SNPs scored against infection age (p=0.002; 3.6% variance).
  • Bidirectional MR (IVW + sensitivity) — lung function → infection age supported; reverse direction weak-instrument (F≈9), not robust.
  • GWAS Atlas PheWAS — leading SNP cross-checked against 4,756 phenotypes (FVC, FEV1 hits at rs62369766).
  • Colocalization — infection GWAS vs CF lung-disease GWAS at SLC6A14/AGTR2 (chrXq22–q23).

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: in the CFF WGS cohort, derive a chronic-Pseudomonas-infection-age phenotype matched to Lin's three-of-three-years culture definition, test for replication of the 5p12 (rs62369766) and 13q12.12 (rs927553) signals, and extend to rare-variant and structural variation in candidate regions including NNT, FGF10, and the SLC6A14/AGTR2 X-linked locus — asking whether whole-genome data recovers more of the high twin-study heritability than common-SNP GWAS.

What to verify first

  • Whether CFF registry holds longitudinal Pseudomonas culture data detailed enough to build the three-of-three-years chronic-infection definition.
  • Whether enough modulator-naïve patients remain (modulator era is far more advanced than this Canadian cohort's), or whether the window is historical only.
  • Whether restricting to pancreatic-insufficient CFTR genotypes still leaves adequate sample size.
  • Whether several-thousand CF patients can replicate a ~3%-variance common-SNP signal, especially split by ancestry.
  • Whether the 13q12.12 locus has since been resolved to a candidate gene elsewhere (this paper names none in text).

Gap-map (CFTR-WGS lens)

  • Closes: a validated, copy-able chronic-infection-age phenotype; two anchor loci (one replicated); a worked PRS + MR + colocalization template; NNT/FGF10 chr5 candidates; SLC6A14/AGTR2 colocalization revisit.
  • Leaves open: the causal gene at both loci (esp. 13q12.12); the missing heritability (85% heritable, ~3% explained); whether the phenotype/restrictions are reproducible in CFF registry data; rare/structural variant contribution untested.

ALEX Welcome back. Today we're reading a 2024 paper in the European Respiratory Journal, first author Bowei Lin and colleagues from the Strug group in Toronto with a Paris collaboration, titled "Genome-wide association study of susceptibility to Pseudomonas aeruginosa infection in cystic fibrosis." This is a CF-cohort paper, and it goes right at a phenotype we've flagged as high-priority — who gets a key chronic lung infection, and how early.

JORDAN Pseudomonas — remind me why that bug matters so much in CF.

ALEX Pseudomonas aeruginosa is a bacterium that loves the CF airway. Once it sets up a permanent, chronic infection, lung function declines faster and survival drops. Up to sixty percent of people with CF eventually get chronically infected — but the age that happens varies wildly between people who have the very same CFTR mutation. That variation is the whole story. It means something beyond the CF-causing gene is setting the timing.

JORDAN Let's get the vocabulary down before the details. Start from zero.

ALEX Good. CFTR is the cystic fibrosis gene — when it's broken, the salt-and-water balance on airway surfaces goes wrong and mucus turns thick and sticky. A GWAS — genome-wide association study — scans common DNA variants across the whole genome to find spots that associate with a trait. A SNP — single nucleotide polymorphism, say it "snip" — is a single-letter spot where people differ; that's the unit a GWAS tests. Heritability is the fraction of person-to-person variation in a trait that's due to genetics rather than environment — so if a trait is 85% heritable, most of the differences between people trace to their DNA. A modifier or modifier gene is a gene elsewhere in the genome that tunes how severe a single-gene disease like CF turns out. A locus is a genomic neighborhood; a candidate gene is the gene at that neighborhood you suspect is responsible, though a GWAS points at the neighborhood, not the house. An eQTL — expression quantitative trait locus — is a variant that changes how much a nearby gene is switched on. A polygenic risk score, PRS, adds up many small-effect variants into one number estimating genetic predisposition. Mendelian randomization, MR, is a clever trick that uses genetic variants as natural stand-ins for an exposure to test whether that exposure causes an outcome, rather than just correlating with it. And the p-value and the 5×10⁻⁸ genome-wide bar we know — surprise meter, strict threshold.

JORDAN That's a lot, but the modifier idea is the heart of it, right? CFTR causes the disease, but other genes set the dial.

ALEX Precisely. And here's the striking number that motivates the study: twin studies suggest that genetic factors other than CFTR account for about 85% of the variation in the age of chronic Pseudomonas infection. That's enormously heritable — but until this paper, nobody had run a genome-wide scan to find those susceptibility loci. Candidate-gene guesses and one exome study existed, but no GWAS. That's the gap they fill.

JORDAN So how did they do it? Who's in the study?

ALEX The discovery cohort is the Canadian CF Gene Modifier Study — they analyzed 1,037 Canadians with CF for the main phenotype. Two design choices ALEX wants to highlight as smart. First, to keep the CFTR genotype from confounding things, they included only people with pancreatic-insufficient genotypes — the more uniformly severe CFTR class — so differences in timing aren't just CFTR differences. Second, they used only modulator-naïve individuals — people not yet on the modern CFTR-correcting drugs — because those drugs change the disease and would muddy a genetic signal. They defined chronic infection rigorously: three consecutive years with cultures, positive in at least two of the three. Then — and this is a nice statistical move — the age-of-infection numbers were badly skewed, so they regressed out current age and analyzed the symmetric residual instead. The association testing used a linear mixed model in the GENESIS package, which handles the fact that the cohort includes siblings.

JORDAN And they checked it somewhere else?

ALEX Yes — an independent French cohort, the French CF Modifier Gene Study, 501 people, for validation. Replication in a separate cohort is what separates a real hit from a fluke. ALEX wants to underline why that matters so much in a study this size. A thousand people is small for a GWAS — most modern scans have tens or hundreds of thousands. With small samples, chance can throw up a spuriously strong signal. So a hit that re-appears, same direction, in a completely separate French cohort collected by different clinicians is worth far more than its p-value alone suggests. The flip side: a real signal that fails to replicate in only 501 people might just be underpowered there, not false — so they're careful about what "didn't validate" means.

JORDAN Did the heritability hold up in their own data?

ALEX Partly, and informatively. Using a tool called GCTA to estimate SNP-based heritability, they got about 46% for chronic-infection age — high, and statistically significant — versus only about 22% and not significant for the age of first infection. That mirrors the old twin study, which found basically zero heritability for first infection but 85% for chronic. So ALEX's reading: first infection looks opportunistic — luck and exposure — while chronic infection is where the genetics lives. They wisely focused on the chronic phenotype.

JORDAN The actual hits — what did they find?

ALEX Two novel genome-wide-significant loci for chronic-infection age. One on chromosome 5, region 5p12, lead SNP rs62369766, p equals 1.98×10⁻⁸ in the combined sample — and this is the one that validated in the French cohort. The other on chromosome 13, region 13q12.12, lead SNP rs927553, p equals 1.91×10⁻⁸. The two SNPs together explain about 3% of the variation. Now, honesty as the SKILL demands: a GWAS names neighborhoods, not genes. For the chromosome 5 hit, they point to candidate genes by function and eQTL evidence — rs62369766 is a weak eQTL for NNT, a mitochondrial gene involved in cellular redox balance and immune response, and it also sits near FGF10, a growth factor that shapes how the airway branches during development. For the chromosome 13 locus, the paper does not pin down a single named candidate gene in the text — so we leave that one as a location, not a gene, and say so out loud.

JORDAN Did they connect this to lung function, since the two are obviously linked?

ALEX They did, and this is the richest part. First, a PheWAS — phenome-wide lookup — of the chromosome 5 SNP in general-population data showed it's associated with lung-function measures, forced vital capacity and FEV1, in a 400,000-person lung-function meta-analysis. So a CF-infection variant also tunes breathing in people without CF. Second, they built a polygenic risk score from CF lung-function-associated SNPs and found it significantly predicted chronic-infection age — p equals 0.002 — meaning the genetics of lung disease and the genetics of infection timing overlap. Third, they found colocalization — shared signal — between their infection GWAS and CF lung-disease GWAS at the X-chromosome locus that carries SLC6A14 and AGTR2, a modifier region we've catalogued before. Finally, bidirectional Mendelian randomization to ask which causes which.

JORDAN And which does cause which?

ALEX This is where ALEX insists on caution, because the paper does. In one direction they found a statistically significant causal effect of lung function on infection age — better lung function, later chronic infection, beta around 0.78 years. But in the reverse direction — infection driving lung function — the instrument was statistically weak, F-statistic around nine, and sensitivity analyses that guard against bias did not support a strong effect. So the honest conclusion is: evidence is stronger for lung function shaping infection timing than the reverse, but the causal claim is tentative, and the authors say so.

JORDAN Did they answer the question they set out to answer?

ALEX Largely yes, for a first-of-kind study. They asked: is chronic-infection age heritable, is it polygenic, and are the genetic contributors CF-specific or shared with general lung biology? They showed it's heritable, found two loci with one replicated, demonstrated a shared polygenic component with lung function, and offered a tentative causal direction. What they did not deliver is a confirmed causal gene or a big fraction of that 85% heritability — two SNPs explaining 3% leaves the lion's share of "missing heritability" unaccounted for.

JORDAN So as a springboard — what concrete direction does this hand us?

ALEX A clean one. This paper defines and validates the chronic-Pseudomonas-infection-age phenotype and its first genetic anchors in a roughly 1,000-person Canadian-French effort. The CFF cohort is several times larger. So a possible direction: treat chronic-Pseudomonas-infection age as a primary phenotype in the CFF WGS cohort and attempt to replicate and extend these two loci — and, because the heritability is so high but so little is explained, layer in the rarer variation that whole-genome sequencing captures and a GWAS chip misses.

JORDAN The formal version?

ALEX Conditional one-sentence form: in the CFF WGS cohort, derive a chronic-Pseudomonas-infection-age phenotype matched to Lin's definition, test for replication of the 5p12 and 13q12.12 signals, and extend to rare-variant and structural variation in candidate regions including NNT, FGF10, and the SLC6A14/AGTR2 X-linked locus — asking whether whole-genome data recovers more of the high twin-study heritability than common-SNP GWAS has.

JORDAN And the gaps — what's closed, what would we have to check first?

ALEX What this closes for us: a rigorous, copy-able phenotype definition — the three-of-three-years culture rule, the age-residual transform, the pancreatic-insufficient-only and modulator-naïve restrictions — plus two anchor loci and a worked PRS-and-MR template. What stays open, as questions: first, does the CFF registry hold longitudinal Pseudomonas culture data detailed enough to build that exact chronic-infection definition — we have not inspected this and must not assume it. Second, the modulator problem is now worse than it was for this Canadian cohort: most current CF patients are on modulators, so a modulator-naïve window may be small or historical — would we have enough such patients? Fourth, replicating a 3%-variance signal needs real power — is several-thousand patients enough, especially split by ancestry? Every one of those is a check we'd run before committing, not something we know.

JORDAN One thing I want to make sure I understand — the colocalization at SLC6A14 and AGTR2. Why does it matter that the infection signal and the lung-disease signal sit at the same X-chromosome spot?

ALEX Because it tells you the two CF problems may share a root cause rather than one simply following the other. Colocalization is the statistical test for "is this the same underlying signal, or just two separate signals that happen to be neighbors?" When the infection-age GWAS and the lung-disease GWAS both peak at the SLC6A14/AGTR2 locus and the test says they coincide, the most economical explanation is one modifier influencing both traits. We've catalogued that locus before as one of the most pleiotropic CF modifier regions — pleiotropy meaning one gene affecting many traits. This paper adds infection timing to its résumé, which is exactly the kind of one-gene-many-CF-traits pattern a phenome-wide scan is built to find.
