Karczewski and colleagues took 394,841 UK Biobank exomes, ran SAIGE-GENE gene-burden tests and single-variant tests against 4,529 harmonized phenotypes, and put the entire results matrix on a free public portal called Genebass (app.genebass.org). Plain-English glossary (alphabetical)

  • Effect size — How strong an association is, separate from how confident you are it is real (the p-value handles confidence).
  • Exome — The roughly 1–2 percent of the genome that codes for proteins.
  • Gene-based test (gene-burden test) — A statistical test that pools all rare damaging variants in a single gene and asks whether carriers as a group differ on a trait.
  • Genebass — The public portal Karczewski and colleagues built, hosting precomputed gene-trait association statistics from 394,841 UK Biobank exomes against 4,529 phenotypes (app.genebass.org).
  • GWAS (genome-wide association study) — Pick one trait, scan the whole genome for variants tied to it.
  • PheWAS (phenome-wide association study) — Pick one gene or variant, scan thousands of traits to see which it is tied to.
  • Public portal — A free website that lets anyone query a precomputed dataset without downloading or recomputing it.
  • p-value — A number between 0 and 1; smaller means the association is less likely to be due to chance alone.
  • Quantitative phenotype — A trait measured on a continuous scale (e.g., HDL cholesterol level).
  • Binary phenotype — A yes/no trait (e.g., does the person have asthma).
  • SAIGE-GENE — A statistical tool for gene-burden testing at biobank scale; handles imbalanced case/control ratios and related individuals.
  • SAIGE-GENE+ — A refinement of SAIGE-GENE with better false-positive control when very rare variants are included.
  • Single-variant test — A statistical test asking whether one specific DNA letter change is associated with a trait.
  • Summary statistics — The output table from association tests: for each gene-trait or variant-trait pair, an effect size and a p-value.
  • WES (whole-exome sequencing) — A sequencing technique that reads only the protein-coding part of the genome instead of the full genome.

Methods to lift

  • Use SAIGE-GENE / SAIGE-GENE+ as the engine for any gene-burden test — it is now the field-standard tool for biobank-scale rare-variant analysis.
  • Run both single-variant and gene-burden tests in parallel; the gene-burden test catches signals the single-variant test misses for genes with many distinct rare alleles.
  • Stratify variants by predicted consequence (loss-of-function, missense, synonymous) and report each category separately rather than pooling them.
  • Treat synonymous variants as a calibration negative control: if you see strong signal there, your pipeline has a problem.
  • Harmonize phenotypes consistently across the entire matrix (binary and quantitative on standardized scales) so results across phenotypes are comparable.
  • Precompute and publish the full summary-statistics matrix where possible, so downstream users do not have to re-run the pipeline to ask basic lookup questions.

ALEX Good plan. First, exome. Your genome is about three billion DNA letters long, but only about one to two percent of it codes for proteins. That protein-coding slice is called the exome. WES, which stands for whole-exome sequencing, is a technique that reads only those protein-coding regions instead of the whole genome. It is cheaper than full whole-genome sequencing, and it captures the variants most likely to break a protein.

JORDAN Got it — exome equals the protein-coding part, WES equals reading just that part.

ALEX Right. Next, two flavors of statistical test. A single-variant test asks: does one specific DNA letter change associate with a trait? A gene-based test, sometimes called a gene-burden test, instead pools together all the rare damaging variants in a single gene and asks: do people who carry any of these rare hits in this gene differ on the trait?

JORDAN Why pool them?

ALEX Because rare variants are, by definition, rare. Any single one might appear in only a handful of people, too few to detect a signal alone. But if you bundle all the rare hits across a whole gene together, you get enough carriers to actually test something. The specific tool they use for the gene-based test is called SAIGE-GENE. Think of SAIGE-GENE as a statistical recipe designed for biobank-scale data, which has to handle two awkward problems: most diseases are rare, so cases and controls are very imbalanced, and people in biobanks are sometimes related to each other. SAIGE-GENE handles both. SAIGE-GENE+ is a refinement of SAIGE-GENE that controls false positives even better when you include very rare variants.

JORDAN And PheWAS?

ALEX PheWAS stands for phenome-wide association study. A regular GWAS — genome-wide association study — picks one trait and scans the whole genome for variants tied to it. A PheWAS flips that around: pick one gene or variant, and scan thousands of traits to see which ones it is tied to. Last term: summary statistics. When you run an association test, the raw output is a table — for each gene-trait pair, an effect size and a p-value. A public portal is just a website that lets anyone query that table without having to download or recompute anything.

JORDAN Okay, ready for the actual paper.

ALEX Great. So here is the question Karczewski and colleagues were trying to answer. By 2022, two big groups had already done huge UK Biobank exome analyses. The Regeneron team — Backman and colleagues — had run association tests on roughly 450,000 exomes. The Wang and colleagues paper had done similar work. Both produced enormous tables of gene-trait associations.

JORDAN So what was missing?

ALEX The summary statistics were not freely browseable. The numbers existed somewhere, but they were not in a form a working scientist could just look up.

They decided to apply the same philosophy here. Take UK Biobank exomes — they had access to 394,841 of them — run a comprehensive single-variant and gene-based association scan against thousands of harmonized phenotypes, and then put everything behind a free public portal. They called it Genebass, short for "gene-biobank association summary statistics," and it lives at app.genebass.org.

JORDAN So the contribution is not really a new biological discovery — it is making existing-style analyses queryable.

JORDAN Walk me through how they actually built it.

ALEX Three big pieces. First, the cohort. They started from the UK Biobank exome release, and after quality control they landed on 394,841 individuals with usable exome data. Second, phenotypes. UK Biobank collects an enormous amount of health information — ICD-10 hospital diagnoses, self-reported conditions, lab values, imaging measurements, questionnaire answers. They harmonized all of that into 4,529 distinct phenotypes. Some are binary, meaning yes-or-no, like "does the person have asthma." Others are quantitative, meaning a continuous number, like "what is the person's HDL cholesterol level." Each phenotype gets the same standardized treatment so the results across phenotypes are comparable.

JORDAN Why does that harmonization matter?

ALEX Because if every phenotype is processed differently, you cannot compare a result for gene A on diabetes to a result for gene B on cholesterol. Consistency is what makes the matrix usable. Third piece: the analysis. They ran SAIGE-GENE on every gene against every phenotype — that is the gene-burden test — and they also ran a single-variant test on every individual variant against every phenotype. They tested multiple variant categories: predicted loss-of-function variants, missense variants, synonymous variants, each grouped separately so you can ask different biological questions.

ALEX Right — the engineering. The output is essentially a gigantic matrix of summary statistics: roughly nineteen thousand genes by 4,529 phenotypes, times multiple variant categories, times two test types. They precomputed all of those association statistics and built a web interface that lets anyone query it: type in a gene, get the PheWAS plot across all 4,529 traits; type in a phenotype, get the genes most associated with it. Bulk download is also available for people who want the whole table.
