How did this episode land for you?

One-paragraph blurb

Mastromatteo and colleagues from Lisa Strug's group sequenced 477 Canadian CF patients with 10x Genomics linked-read sequencing, achieving a 4.39 Mb phase block N50. They use the phased data to resolve the long-known chr7q35 trypsinogen modifier signal for CF meconium ileus, showing that two independent polymorphisms — a 20-kb deletion polymorphism removing PRSS3P2 and TRY7, and a PRSS2 missense variant p.Thr8Ile (rs62473563) — each independently reduce PRSS2 pancreas expression and independently increase meconium ileus risk in CF, with the missense variant having been invisible to prior GWAS because PRSS2 was missing from the GRCh37 reference. The proposed direction is to apply phasing-aware reanalysis to the CFF cohort WGS at published CF modifier loci to convert unresolved GWAS hits into specific coding- or regulatory-variant hypotheses; the open question is which phasing approach is feasible on existing short-read CFF data and whether the trypsinogen-style reference-bias correction has already been applied during variant calling.

Plain-English glossary

  • CFTR — Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane conductance Regulator. The gene that, when both copies are broken, causes CF.
  • eQTL — expression quantitative trait locus. A variant that changes how much of a nearby gene gets made (its mRNA expression level), in a given tissue.
  • F508del — the most common CF-causing mutation; deletes the phenylalanine at position 508 of CFTR.
  • GRCh37 / GRCh38 — two versions of the human reference genome. GRCh37 is older and at chr7q35 is structurally incorrect.
  • GTEx — Genotype-Tissue Expression project; a public database of how genetic variants affect gene expression across human tissues.
  • GWAS — genome-wide association study. Statistical scan of millions of common variants across the genome to find ones linked to a trait.
  • Haplotype — the set of variants riding together on a single parental copy of a chromosome.
  • KI270803.1 — an alternative reference contig that represents the non-deleted form of the chr7q35 trypsinogen locus.
  • Linkage disequilibrium (LD) — non-random co-inheritance of nearby variants. High LD means variants travel together.
  • Linked-read sequencing — a short-read sequencing approach where every read derived from the same long DNA molecule shares a barcode, enabling long-range phasing.
  • Meconium ileus (MI) — intestinal blockage at birth in 13–21% of CF newborns; marker of severe early disease.
  • Missense variant — a single-nucleotide change that swaps one amino acid for another in the encoded protein.
  • Paralog — a gene copy that arose by duplication from a common ancestor and now sits elsewhere in the genome.
  • Phase / phasing — assigning each heterozygous variant to one of the two parental chromosome copies.
  • Phase block N50 — half of the phased genome is in blocks of at least this length.
  • PRSS1, PRSS2 — active trypsinogen-encoding genes in the chr7q35 cluster (pancreatic enzyme precursors).
  • PRSS3P1, PRSS3P2, TRY7 — pseudogenes in the same chr7q35 cluster.
  • Pseudogene — a sequence that looks like a gene but does not produce a functional protein.
  • Signal peptide — the first ~15–30 amino acids of a secreted protein, used to target it to the endoplasmic reticulum for secretion.
  • SNP — single nucleotide polymorphism; a one-letter DNA difference common in the population.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage:

In the CFF cohort whole-genome sequencing dataset (n≈5,000), perform phasing-aware reanalysis of published CF modifier loci — at minimum SLC26A9 (chr1q32.1), SLC6A14 (Xq23-24), SLC9A3 (chr5p13), EHF/APIP at chr11p13, the chr20q13.2 region from Wright 2011, and the chr7q35 trypsinogen cluster — to test whether resolution of allelic heterogeneity within each region yields independent contributing variants (coding, eQTL, or structural) for any CFFPR-recorded complication phenotype (FEV1 trajectory, BMI percentile, CFRD onset, CFLD severity, MI status, sweat chloride). Pair the phased call set with pancreas/intestine/airway eQTL data from GTEx (with locus-specific reference-bias corrections where indicated) and test for colocalization between each independent CF modifier signal and a tissue-relevant eQTL.

What to verify first

  • Whether the CFF cohort variant-calling pipeline aligned to GRCh38 main contigs alone, or whether alternative contigs (e.g., KI270803.1 at chr7q35) were used — and if not, whether trypsinogen-region calls are affected by the same reference-bias issue Mastromatteo describes.
  • The actual phasing reach achievable on the existing CFF short-read WGS via population-based statistical phasing using a CF-appropriate or TOPMed reference panel — at what point does switch error rate become unacceptable for the loci of interest.
  • The fraction of the CFF cohort that is F508del homozygous (which has limited heterozygosity in cis to CFTR and therefore is hard to phase locally) versus compound heterozygous.
  • Whether any CF modifier locus of interest sits in a structurally complex region requiring its own alternative-contig realignment.
  • Whether a subset of the CFF cohort could plausibly be re-sequenced using a long-read or alternative linked-read technology (TELL-Seq, PacBio HiFi, Oxford Nanopore) given that 10x Genomics has discontinued the original linked-read product.
  • Whether CFFPR phenotype linkage supports stratified analysis at locus-specific carrier counts after PI-only or pancreatic-status-stratified subgroups are applied.

Gap-map: closes / leaves open

This is a biobank-methods episode, so the standard CFTR-WGS gap-map does not strictly apply. However, the paper has a CF-cohort-specific contribution worth flagging:

Closes

  • That the chr7q35 trypsinogen-cluster GWAS signal for meconium ileus is explained by two independent variants — the 20-kb deletion polymorphism and the PRSS2 p.Thr8Ile missense — both reducing PRSS2 pancreas expression. This converts a long-standing unresolved GWAS suggestive locus into a specific mechanistic hypothesis (more PRSS2 → more pancreatic trypsin → less protein-rich meconium → less obstruction).

Leaves open

  • Whether PRSS2 expression in non-pancreas tissues (intestine, in particular) is similarly affected by these variants.
  • Whether the same direction-of-effect holds in non-European-ancestry CF patients (only six admixed-African-ancestry individuals were present, and were excluded from the locus analysis due to distinct haplotype structure).
  • Whether the chr7q35 locus contributes to any non-MI CF complication (e.g., later pancreatic exocrine status, CFRD), which would require PheWAS testing in a larger CF cohort.
  • How the 10x-Genomics-discontinued status of linked-reads will be replaced for next-generation CF phasing studies (TELL-Seq, PacBio HiFi, Oxford Nanopore are candidate replacements but were not benchmarked on this cohort).

ALEX Welcome back. Today's first paper is Mastromatteo, Chen, Gong, Lin, and colleagues, with senior author Lisa Strug at SickKids, published in 2023 in Human Genetics and Genomics Advances. The title is "High-quality read-based phasing of cystic fibrosis cohort informs genetic understanding of disease modification."

JORDAN That is a mouthful. Why are we reading it?

ALEX Because it does two things at once that are both relevant to you. First, it demonstrates a sequencing technology that lets the authors figure out, for each person, which of their two copies of a gene a given variant is sitting on. Second, it uses that information to crack open a piece of CF genetics that the field has been staring at for years without being able to fully resolve — a stretch of DNA on chromosome 7 that influences meconium ileus risk in CF.

JORDAN Before you go further, can we slow down? You said two copies of a gene. What does "phasing" actually mean?

ALEX Sure. Every person inherits two copies of every chromosome — one from each biological parent. When you sequence someone's DNA and find two variants in the same gene, an obvious question is: are those two variants on the same physical copy, or on different copies? The answer matters. If both are on the same copy, the other copy is normal. If they're on different copies, then every copy of the gene is hit. Phasing is the act of assigning each variant to one of the two parental copies — figuring out which variants travel together along a single inherited stretch of DNA, which geneticists call a haplotype.

JORDAN And we usually cannot do that?

ALEX With ordinary short-read sequencing — which is the default in most labs — you mostly can't. A short read is only about 150 bases long. If two variants sit a thousand bases apart, no single short read covers both, so the read can't tell you they came from the same parental copy. Researchers work around this by using statistical methods that look at large reference populations and guess which variants tend to travel together. That's called population-based phasing. It works on average, but it makes errors, and it struggles with rare variants. The alternative is to introduce some physical information into the sequencing process itself that links nearby reads together. That's what this paper uses.

JORDAN Got it. What technology specifically?

ALEX Something called 10x Genomics linked-read sequencing. The idea is clever: you take long stretches of DNA — tens of kilobases each — and you tag every short read that came from the same long molecule with a shared barcode. Then later, when you see two reads share a barcode and they fall in the same neighborhood, you know they came from the same original parental chromosome. Effectively, you get the cost and accuracy of short-read sequencing with much of the phasing power of long reads. Two more terms you'll hear: GWAS, genome-wide association study, is when you scan millions of common variants across the genome to see which are statistically linked to a disease. eQTL, expression quantitative trait locus, is a variant that influences how much a nearby gene gets expressed. Pancreas eQTL just means: in pancreas tissue, this variant changes how much of some gene's mRNA is made. And one piece of CF jargon: meconium ileus, often written MI, is an intestinal blockage at birth that happens in roughly 13 to 21 percent of babies born with CF. It's an early, severe complication and it tends to mark a more severe overall disease course.

ALEX Background and motivation. The biggest open puzzle here is that even when two CF patients carry the same CF-causing mutation in the CFTR gene, their disease can look very different — different lung trajectories, different responses to therapy, different complications. CF is officially a single-gene disease, but its course is anything but uniform. The field has spent two decades hunting for modifier genes — other genes elsewhere in the genome that nudge the disease one way or the other. Several GWAS of meconium ileus have been done, and one of the loci that keeps coming up is a region on chromosome 7, band q35, that contains the trypsinogen gene cluster.

JORDAN Trypsinogen. That's a pancreas enzyme, right?

ALEX Right. Trypsinogen is the precursor of trypsin, which is the workhorse protease the pancreas secretes into the gut to break down protein. There are five paralogs — copies that descended from a common ancestor — clustered together at chr7q35: PRSS1, PRSS3P1, PRSS3P2, TRY7, and PRSS2. PRSS1 and PRSS2 make active enzymes; the other three are pseudogenes, meaning they look like genes but don't make functional protein. The region is notorious for a structural quirk. About 41 percent of people of European ancestry carry a roughly 20-kilobase deletion that removes two of those pseudogenes. So depending on which chromosome you sequence, the locus may or may not be missing 20 kilobases. And here's the trap: the GRCh38 reference genome that almost everyone aligns reads to actually represents the deleted version. If a sample carries the non-deleted version, the extra 20 kilobases has nowhere to go — reads from it pile up in the wrong place and produce spurious variant calls. The older reference, GRCh37, is even worse: it was assembled incorrectly and is missing PRSS2 entirely. So past GWAS work on this region was reading a partially scrambled book.

JORDAN Okay, so the field knew the locus mattered but couldn't see it clearly.

ALEX Exactly. And on top of that, even when you do call variants accurately, you still need to know phase — which variants are riding together on the same haplotype — to tell whether the GWAS signal is being driven by a single variant or by a combination.

ALEX Methods. The authors sequenced 477 individuals with CF from the Canadian Cystic Fibrosis Gene Modifier Study Consortium, which they abbreviate CGMS. They used the 10x Genomics linked-read technology at about thirty-times coverage. They paid careful attention to DNA extraction — they tested four methods and found that one called MagAttract gave the longest DNA molecules, averaging about 59 kilobases per molecule. Long molecules are what give linked-reads their phasing reach. Their average phase block N50 was 4.39 megabases, which means at least half of the genome was phased in stretches of at least 4.39 megabases — a very long reach for a short-read-based method.

JORDAN Pushback?

ALEX Worth flagging: the authors themselves note that during the study, 10x Genomics announced they were discontinuing this linked-read product. So the specific technology used here is now in run-down mode. The methodology generalizes — long-read and other linked-read alternatives like PacBio HiFi, Oxford Nanopore, and TELL-Seq exist — but anyone trying to replicate the exact 10XG pipeline today will have to substitute. Also, a substantive limitation specific to CF: about half the cohort were F508del homozygotes, meaning both copies of CFTR carry the same single deletion. Their CFTR region is essentially identical on both chromosomes, so there's almost no heterozygous variation to phase — the median F508del-homozygous person had only ten heterozygous variants across the 389-kilobase CFTR window. For the recessive-disease-with-a-dominant-mutation case, phasing has less to work with right at the locus you most want to understand.

JORDAN And they fixed the alignment problem at chr7q35?

ALEX Yes. They realigned reads to an alternative contig called KI270803.1, which represents the non-deleted version of the trypsinogen locus. This eliminated the spurious variant calls. They could also unambiguously call the 20-kilobase deletion itself as a single polymorphism for each individual.

ALEX Results. They confirm two distinct association peaks at chr7q35 for meconium ileus risk, in different linkage disequilibrium blocks. The first peak centers on a variant called rs3757377 — that single nucleotide change is highly correlated with the 20-kilobase deletion polymorphism. The second peak centers on rs1799886, which is not strongly correlated with the deletion. With phase information in hand, the authors find that rs1799886 is in near-perfect linkage with a protein-coding variant in PRSS2 called p.Thr8Ile — written as rs62473563. That coding variant changes the eighth amino acid of the PRSS2 trypsinogen protein from threonine to isoleucine, and it sits in the signal peptide that targets the protein to the endoplasmic reticulum for secretion. So the GWAS signal at the second peak was actually tagging a missense variant in PRSS2 that had been invisible to earlier studies because PRSS2 wasn't in the GRCh37 reference.

JORDAN How did they connect this back to function?

ALEX They queried the Genotype-Tissue Expression project, GTEx, which is a public database of variant-to-gene-expression effects across tissues. After they corrected for the same 20-kilobase deletion alignment issue, two PRSS2 pancreas eQTLs emerged: the 20-kilobase deletion itself with p equals 7.8 times ten to the minus five, and rs62473563 — the p.Thr8Ile missense — as an independent signal that became more significant when they conditioned on the deletion: p equals 9.5 times ten to the minus seven. Conditioning on both together eliminated the eQTL signal. Both variants reduce PRSS2 expression in pancreas. Then they tested the same two variants for meconium ileus association in 309 of their CF subjects with linked-read data and MI status: the deletion p equals 0.0028, and p.Thr8Ile p equals 0.011 after conditioning on the deletion. So both contribute independently. And the direction is consistent: less PRSS2 expression, higher meconium ileus risk. They propose a tidy mechanism — trypsin in the gut breaks down protein in meconium and discourages the thick blockage. Less trypsin, more blockage. The PRSS2 protein eQTL effect estimate is also opposite in direction to non-CF pancreatitis biology, where more trypsin activity is the problem.

ALEX Beat six, proposed direction. One specific concrete research idea this paper opens up for Runyu: take the same phasing-aware approach to the CFF cohort's existing whole-genome sequencing data — currently around five thousand individuals — and re-examine the published CF modifier loci where the GWAS hit has never been resolved to a clear causal variant. Plain English: instead of treating each variant in isolation, look at which variants ride together along each chromosome at known modifier regions, and ask whether the apparent GWAS signal is actually being driven by a coding or regulatory variant that travels with the GWAS hit. As a formal one-sentence version: in the CFF WGS cohort, perform phased haplotype reconstruction at published CF modifier loci including SLC26A9, SLC6A14, SLC9A3, EHF/APIP at chr11p13, and the chr20q13.2 region from Wright 2011, and test whether resolution of allelic heterogeneity yields independent contributing variants — coding, eQTL, or structural — for any CFF Patient Registry complication phenotype.

JORDAN And beat seven — what's the gap, conditional on that proposal?

ALEX What this paper closes: it shows that phase information can convert an unexplained GWAS signal into a specific coding-variant hypothesis, and it gives a worked template for handling reference-bias problems at structurally complex loci. What it leaves open for Runyu's question: first, the CFF cohort sequencing isn't linked-read — it's standard short-read WGS. So we'd need to check what phasing approach is even possible on the existing CFF data: whether population-based phasing using a CF-appropriate reference panel reaches enough phase block length to resolve the regions of interest, or whether a subset would need to be re-sequenced with a long-read or linked-read alternative. Second, we'd need to verify whether the chr7q35 trypsinogen locus is being called correctly in the CFF variant set — that is, whether the alignment pipeline used the GRCh38 main contig or accounted for the alternative contig KI270803.1. If it didn't, the spurious-variant problem this paper describes is present in CFF too. Third, the F508del-homozygous-fraction phasing limitation matters: the proportion of homozygous F508del patients in CFF would constrain how many CFTR-locus phase blocks could be reconstructed. Fourth, several of the modifier loci of interest are themselves in structurally complex regions, so the same KI270803-style realignment exercise would have to be repeated locus by locus. None of these are reasons not to pursue the direction — they're things that would need to be checked before committing.

One-line takeaway from today: when a GWAS hit refuses to resolve, the problem may not be statistical power — it may be that you don't actually know which variants are riding together on the same chromosome.
