Blurb

Bradley, Blackman, Watson, Doshi, and Cutting 2012 (Am J Clin Nutr) is the foundational genome-wide modifier-gene linkage study for pediatric CF nutritional status. Substrate: 1,124 CF twins and siblings from the CF Twin-Sibling Study (NCT00037778) and CFFPR, 2000–2010. Phenotype: average BMI z-score from age 5–10 (BMI-z_5to10), adjusted for sex, birth cohort, pancreatic insufficiency, and meconium ileus history. Same-sex twin-only heritability estimates 0.54–0.82 — substantial genetic contribution beyond CFTR itself. Genome-wide significant linkage at chromosome 1p36.1 (LOD 5.3) and chromosome 5q14 (LOD 5.1) in 358 pancreatic-insufficient sibling pairs. After winner's-curse correction, these loci explained ≥16% and ≥15% of BMI variance respectively. 1p36.1 was lung-function-correlated (signal attenuated when adjusting for FEV1); 5q14 was nutrition-specific (signal preserved). ARRDC3 (arrestin-domain-containing 3) flagged as a candidate within 5q14 based on published mouse body-mass biology. Suggestive linkage at chromosome 16p11.2 (LOD 2.7), a known general-population BMI locus. Proposed direction (conditional) for the CFTR PheWAS: replicate the BMI-z_5to10 phenotype in the contemporary CFGP-linked CFFPR cohort with WGS-era fine-mapping at 1p36.1 and 5q14.

Plain-English glossary

  • ARRDC3 (arrestin-domain-containing 3): a candidate gene within the chromosome 5q14 linkage region; published mouse work links it to body mass and energy expenditure regulation with a male-biased effect.
  • BMI (body mass index): weight in kg divided by height in m²; CF nutritional-status workhorse.
  • BMI z-score: standardized BMI relative to age- and sex-specific reference (here, CDC growth curves); 0 is population median.
  • CDC growth reference: standard pediatric growth curves published by the US Centers for Disease Control.
  • CF Twin-Sibling Study (NCT00037778): Hopkins family-based CF cohort recruiting families with two or more CF-affected family members.
  • CFFPR (CF Foundation Patient Registry): longitudinal US CF clinical-care registry; supplements Twin-Sibling Study clinical data here.
  • Dizygous (DZ) twins: fraternal twins, sharing on average 50% of DNA — same as full siblings.
  • Equal-environments assumption: the structural assumption in twin-design heritability that MZ and DZ twins share environment to the same degree; approximate at best.
  • F508del: the most common CF-causing CFTR variant; ~55% of the study population were F508del homozygotes.
  • Falconer heritability estimator: classical twin-design formula h² = 2(r_MZ − r_DZ); used here with bootstrap CIs.
  • FEV1 (forced expiratory volume in 1 second): workhorse CF lung-function measure.
  • Gastrostomy: surgically-placed feeding tube into the stomach, used as a nutritional intervention in CF children failing to gain weight.
  • Genome-wide significance (linkage): LOD ≥ 3.6, per Lander & Kruglyak 1995 conventions.
  • Heritability (h²): proportion of trait variation in the population attributable to genetic differences; same-sex twin-only estimates here 0.54–0.82.
  • Linkage analysis: family-based genome-scan discovery method tracking co-inheritance of chromosomal regions with trait values.
  • LOD score (logarithm of odds): linkage analysis test statistic; ≥3.6 genome-wide significant, ≥2.2 suggestive.
  • MERLIN / SOLAR: variance-components linkage-analysis software packages used here; gave essentially identical results.
  • Meconium ileus (MI): neonatal bowel obstruction occurring in ~15% of CF newborns; risk factor for poor BMI trajectory.
  • Monozygous (MZ) twins: identical twins, sharing ~100% of DNA.
  • Pancreatic insufficiency (PI): failure of the pancreas to produce sufficient digestive enzymes; ~90% of CF patients PI; required for malabsorption-driven malnutrition.
  • QTL (quantitative trait locus): chromosomal region contributing to a continuous trait.
  • Winner's curse: the upward bias in effect-size estimation at the discovery locus; corrected for here when reporting BMI variance explained.

ALEX For the final episode of today, we cross from biobank methods over to CF biology directly. The paper is Bradley, Blackman, Watson, Doshi, and Cutting 2012, in the American Journal of Clinical Nutrition, titled "Genetic modifiers of nutritional status in cystic fibrosis." First-author Gia Bradley, joint first with Scott Blackman, senior author Garry Cutting at Johns Hopkins.

JORDAN Why pick this one for the CFTR PheWAS reading project?

ALEX Because it's the foundational paper showing that nutritional status in pediatric CF — BMI specifically — is strongly heritable beyond what CFTR genotype explains, and pointing to two specific chromosomal regions that contain CF nutritional modifier genes. If we want BMI trajectory as a CF endpoint in any modifier-PheWAS, this paper is the anchor citation.

JORDAN I need terms. Let's start at the beginning.

ALEX Multiple terms today. BMI, body mass index, is weight in kilograms divided by height in meters squared — the most common quick measure of nutritional status. In children BMI is interpreted via z-scores against age-and-sex-specific reference curves, where zero is the population median, positive is above-median, negative is below-median. CDC stands for the US Centers for Disease Control, which publishes the reference growth curves used in this paper. The phenotype here is BMI-z-five-to-ten, meaning the average BMI z-score for each patient computed from clinic visits between ages five and ten years.

JORDAN Why that age range specifically?

ALEX Because it sits after the diagnostic period — most CF patients are diagnosed in infancy or before age five — and before the onset of CF-related diabetes, which usually begins in adolescence and confounds nutrition. It's the cleanest interpretable window of CF nutrition before adolescent complications kick in. The other clinical terms: pancreatic insufficiency, abbreviated PI, means the pancreas does not produce enough digestive enzymes; about ninety percent of CF patients are pancreatic-insufficient and need enzyme replacement to absorb fat and calories. Meconium ileus, abbreviated MI, is a neonatal bowel obstruction caused by abnormally thick meconium — the first stool — that occurs in about fifteen percent of newborns with CF. FEV1 stands for forced expiratory volume in one second, the workhorse measure of CF lung function.

JORDAN And the genetic terms?

ALEX Three more. Heritability, again, is the proportion of variation in a trait attributable to genetic differences in the population studied. A heritability of zero means all variation comes from environment; one means all from genes. Linkage analysis is the classic family-based discovery method — track which chromosomal regions are co-inherited with a trait across siblings and twins, and look for regions where co-inheritance is higher than expected by chance. LOD score, log of the odds ratio, is the linkage-analysis test statistic; a LOD above 3.6 is genome-wide significant, above 2.2 is suggestive. Quantitative trait locus, abbreviated QTL, is a chromosomal region that contributes to a continuous trait like BMI.

JORDAN One more — what's a CF twin and sibling study, and how is it different from a regular GWAS cohort?

ALEX A twin and sibling study deliberately recruits families with two or more CF-affected family members. Monozygous twins share one-hundred percent of their DNA. Dizygous twins and full siblings share, on average, fifty percent. By comparing how similar a trait is in monozygous twins versus in dizygous twins or siblings, you can estimate how much of the trait similarity comes from shared genes versus shared environment. This is the classical Falconer twin-design heritability estimator, and it was the gold standard in the pre-GWAS era. The CF Twin-Sibling Study is registered as NCT00037778, and the data overlap heavily with the CFF Patient Registry from 2000 to 2010.

JORDAN What was the question motivating the study?

ALEX Pediatric nutritional status in CF is a clinical outcome that strongly predicts later lung function and survival. The CFF nutrition guidelines were built on the observation that children with higher BMI percentiles have better lung function trajectories. But the variation in BMI among CF children is striking — some kids thrive with BMI z-scores near or above the general-population median, others fail to grow despite aggressive enzyme replacement, supplemental feeding, and even gastrostomy tubes. The clinical question: how much of that variation is genetic, and beyond CFTR itself, where are the genes?

JORDAN That's a tractable question.

ALEX Right. And by 2012 there were already candidate modifier genes for CF lung disease — Wright 2011 had reported chromosome 11p13 and 20q13.2 linkage hits for lung severity. But for nutrition specifically, no formal genome-wide modifier study had been published. This paper fills that gap, using the same Hopkins family-based cohort architecture.

JORDAN What did they actually do?

ALEX First the substrate. They drew longitudinal height and weight data from 2000 to 2010 for 1,618 twins and siblings with CF from 800 families, totaling 120,245 clinic visits and roughly 17,000 patient-years of follow-up. After applying inclusion criteria, the final analytic sample was 1,124 subjects — 130 monozygous twins, 42 dizygous twins, and 952 siblings. Family members all shared the same CFTR genotype, which is a critical design point: by restricting comparisons to within-family pairs, the CFTR genotype contribution to BMI variation is held constant, and any heritability that emerges must come from modifier loci outside CFTR.

JORDAN That's a clever design move.

ALEX It is. For each subject they computed BMI z-scores at every clinic visit using CDC reference equations, averaged per quarter to dampen visit-density bias, and then averaged from ages five through ten to get the BMI-z-five-to-ten phenotype. They then built adjusted versions of the phenotype residualizing out covariates that had univariate associations with BMI — female sex, birth cohort, pancreatic insufficiency, and meconium ileus history. They also built a version further residualized for FEV1 to separate lung-function effects from nutrition effects per se. Heritability was estimated by the classical Falconer twin design, with bootstrap confidence intervals from one million resamples. Linkage analysis used the MERLIN software package with variance-components methodology, and the SOLAR package as a sanity check; both gave essentially identical results.

JORDAN Push back on a design choice?

ALEX Honest pushback: heritability estimates from monozygous-versus-dizygous twin comparisons assume equal shared-environment effects in both twin types — the so-called equal-environments assumption. CF families with twin pairs often have shared treatments, shared diet, shared infections, and very similar care patterns; the equal-environments assumption is at best approximate. Their bootstrap confidence intervals capture sampling uncertainty but not violations of this structural assumption. So when they report heritability of 0.54 to 0.82, the upper end of that range should be read with caution.

JORDAN What did they find?

ALEX Three layers of findings. First, the BMI-z-five-to-ten phenotype itself was modest by general-population standards — median z-score of minus 0.07, which is the forty-seventh CDC percentile, slightly below the general-population median. As expected from CF clinical literature, BMI z-score correlated negatively with pancreatic insufficiency, with meconium ileus history, and with female sex; and positively with later birth cohorts — reflecting historical improvement in CF nutrition outcomes — and with lung function.

JORDAN And the heritability?

ALEX Substantial. In same-sex twin-only analyses, heritability estimates for adjusted BMI z-score ranged from 0.54 to 0.82. The Pearson correlation for BMI-z-five-to-ten was 0.80 for monozygous twin pairs, 0.59 for dizygous twin pairs, and 0.31 for same-sex sibling pairs less than three years apart in age — a clean genetic gradient. When monozygous twins were compared against the combined dizygous twin and sibling group, heritability estimates approached 1.0, which the authors flagged as upwardly biased because siblings have more nongenetic-source variation than dizygous twin pairs.

JORDAN And the linkage?

ALEX The headline finding. In 358 pancreatic-insufficient sibling pairs, they detected two genome-wide significant linkage peaks: chromosome 1p36.1 with a LOD score of 5.3, and chromosome 5q14 with a LOD score of 5.1. Both well above the 3.6 genome-wide-significance threshold. After winner's-curse correction, these loci were estimated to explain at least sixteen percent and at least fifteen percent of BMI variance respectively, in the pancreatic-insufficient subgroup. When they restricted to the 219 sibling pairs homozygous for F508del — the most common CFTR mutation — the chromosome 1p36.1 signal stayed strong at LOD 4.6 while the chromosome 5q14 signal weakened to LOD 3.4. When they adjusted for FEV1 to remove lung-function-mediated effects, the 1p36.1 signal weakened to LOD 3.2 while the 5q14 signal stayed strong at LOD 5.2.

JORDAN That's a meaningful contrast.

ALEX Yes — the authors' interpretation: 1p36.1 may harbor a modifier gene that influences both nutrition and lung function, while 5q14 harbors a nutrition modifier that's independent of lung function. They also noted a suggestive linkage at chromosome 16p11.2 with LOD 2.7, which is interesting because 16p11.2 is a known BMI-susceptibility region in the general population. And they highlighted one specific candidate gene within the 5q14 critical region — ARRDC3, the arrestin-domain-containing-3 gene — which had been recently implicated in regulating body mass and energy expenditure in mice with a sex-specific male-biased effect.

JORDAN Did they answer their question?

ALEX Yes. They answered both halves directly. Pediatric BMI variation in CF is substantially heritable beyond CFTR — heritability of 0.54 to 0.82 from twin-only analyses. And the genome contains at least two non-CFTR loci that contribute meaningfully to BMI in pancreatic-insufficient CF children: 1p36.1 with a lung-function-correlated effect, and 5q14 with a nutrition-specific effect possibly mediated by ARRDC3.

JORDAN What direction does this paper suggest for Runyu?

ALEX Plain English first. The BMI-z-five-to-ten phenotype Bradley 2012 defined is a clean pediatric-CF endpoint with documented heritability, documented linkage hits, and direct clinical relevance — lower BMI predicts worse lung function trajectory and worse survival. The CF Genome Project whole-genome sequencing substrate has substantially more genetic resolution than the linkage markers available in 2012; the same nutritional phenotype, computed on the larger contemporary CFFPR-linked cohort, could be subjected to a modern WGS-era GWAS or rare-variant burden analysis at the 1p36.1 and 5q14 loci to attempt fine-mapping. One specific direction this episode suggests: replicate the Bradley 2012 phenotype with a contemporary BMI trajectory analysis on CFGP-linked CFFPR data.

JORDAN Formally?

ALEX Formal version: replicate the Bradley 2012 BMI-z-five-to-ten heritability and linkage findings in the contemporary CFFPR-linked CF Genome Project cohort, using whole-genome sequencing to fine-map the chromosome 1p36.1 and chromosome 5q14 loci to candidate causal variants — explicitly testing ARRDC3 sex-stratified — and extending to a pediatric BMI trajectory phenotype rather than a static five-to-ten window. This is one possible direction the episode suggests; it is not a committed research design.

JORDAN What does the paper close, and what does it leave open?

ALEX The paper closes the question of whether pediatric CF nutritional status is heritable — yes, substantially so, beyond CFTR. It closes the broad location question for two large-effect modifier-gene regions — 1p36.1 and 5q14. It closes the lung-function-versus-nutrition independence question at the locus level — 5q14 is nutrition-specific, 1p36.1 is shared. It leaves wide open the causal-variant question at both loci — linkage gives megabase-level resolution at best, and the 5q14 region in particular contains many candidate genes besides ARRDC3. It leaves open whether the loci replicate at modern WGS-era genome-wide significance in the larger contemporary CFGP cohort. It leaves open whether the linkage signals reflect rare-variant burden or common-variant associations, since linkage analysis is blind to that distinction. And it leaves open modulator-era persistence — the Bradley cohort entirely predates Trikafta, and post-modulator CF nutrition patterns may have a different genetic architecture.

JORDAN Verification questions for that proposal?

ALEX What we'd need to check, framed as questions and not facts. Whether the CFGP-linked CFFPR cohort retains pediatric BMI trajectory data dense enough to construct a BMI-z-five-to-ten analog. Whether the chromosome 1p36.1 and 5q14 regions have been re-tested at genome-wide significance in any subsequent CF modifier GWAS — Corvol 2015, Zhou 2023 — and how those results compare to the 2012 linkage hits. Whether ARRDC3 is reliably callable from short-read whole-genome sequencing including its regulatory region. Whether the F508del-homozygote subgroup in CFGP is large enough to power the within-genotype subgroup analysis Bradley did, given that modulator-era cohort access patterns may have shifted the F508del-homozygote sampling. Whether the sex-stratified analysis ARRDC3 biology would suggest survives the power constraints. Whether modulator-era patients should be analyzed as a separate stratum or pooled, given the possibility that Trikafta-rescued residual CFTR function attenuates modifier-gene effects through downstream pancreatic preservation.

ALEX Tomorrow returns to biobank methods. We continue down the Batch 3 list — likely a 2023 plasma-lipidome GWAS paper, depending on the picker.

ALEX Pediatric CF BMI is fifty-to-eighty-percent heritable beyond CFTR, with two genome-wide-significant modifier-gene regions identified by linkage in 2012 — both regions remain candidate territory for any modern WGS-era modifier-PheWAS that uses nutritional status as a primary endpoint.
