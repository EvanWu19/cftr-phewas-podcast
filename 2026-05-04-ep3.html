Blurb

Aksit and colleagues 2022 — the closest published work in the CF-modifier literature to a phenome-wide scan, though formally a bivariate pleiotropy test rather than a true PheWAS. Using the CF Genome Project (CFGP) whole-genome sequencing dataset (the same Hopkins–UNC–Toronto–France–Canada consortium WGS that Zhou 2023 used), they ran two GWAS: one for cystic fibrosis–related diabetes (CFRD, time-to-event), one for meconium ileus (MI, neonatal binary). They identified 11 MI loci (6 novel) and 12 CFRD loci (5 novel). Three loci were pleiotropic: SLC26A9 and CEBPB increase risk for both; PRSS1 acts in opposite directions — the higher-CFRD-risk allele is the lower-MI-risk allele. The paper sets up the twenty-three-locus list against which a registry-anchored phenome-wide extension could be defined; the white space is the non-GI, non-pulmonary, longitudinal, and post-modulator pleiotropy that the bivariate design cannot detect.

Plain-English glossary

  • Allele — one of the alternative DNA letters at a variant position.
  • CEBPB — CCAAT/enhancer binding protein beta; a transcription factor with broad roles in inflammation and metabolism. Newly identified pleiotropic CF modifier.
  • CFGP — CF Genome Project; the Hopkins/UNC/Toronto/Paris/Canada WGS consortium that owns the whole-genome sequencing dataset.
  • CFRD — cystic fibrosis–related diabetes; a CF-specific form of diabetes with beta-cell failure but typically normal insulin sensitivity.
  • CFTR — Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane conductance Regulator gene; mutations cause CF.
  • CFFPR — Cystic Fibrosis Foundation Patient Registry; the longitudinal clinical database covering essentially all CF patients seen at CFF-accredited centers.
  • Common variant — minor allele frequency above 1% in the population.
  • Genome-wide significance — the conventional p<5×10⁻⁸ threshold for declaring an association real, accounting for genome-wide multiple testing.
  • GWAS — genome-wide association study; one trait, scan the whole genome.
  • Locus — a specific spot on the genome, typically a region around a gene.
  • MI (meconium ileus) — neonatal intestinal obstruction; affects ~15–20% of CF newborns.
  • Modifier gene — a non-CFTR gene that influences how CF presents.
  • PheWAS — phenome-wide association study; one variant, scan many traits. Aksit 2022 is not a PheWAS — it tests two pre-chosen phenotypes — but it tests the same pleiotropy idea a PheWAS would.
  • Pleiotropy — one gene affecting multiple traits.
  • PRSS1 — cationic trypsinogen, a pancreatic digestive-enzyme gene whose mutations cause hereditary pancreatitis. Pleiotropic CF modifier with opposite-direction effects on CFRD vs MI.
  • Rare variant — minor allele frequency below 1%.
  • SLC26A9 — solute carrier family 26 member 9; an epithelial chloride/bicarbonate channel that physically interacts with CFTR. Pleiotropic CF modifier — increases risk for both CFRD and MI.
  • WGS — whole-genome sequencing.

Methods to lift

  • Bivariate pleiotropy framing — running two single-trait GWAS in the same cohort, then overlaying significant loci to identify shared vs trait-specific signals; same-direction vs opposite-direction effect at pleiotropic loci is the scientific punchline. Useful template even if generalized to a phenome-wide design.
  • CF-restricted background design — testing pleiotropy within a CF-only cohort lets the analysis isolate CF-specific biology rather than general-population confounding (e.g. obesity-mediated overlap).
  • Time-to-event modeling for CFRD — age-of-onset rather than yes/no status as the outcome variable; preserves the gradual-onset structure of CFRD and increases power versus a binary lifetime-status outcome.
  • Common + rare variants both tested at SLC26A9 — single-variant tests at the lead common variant plus explicit reporting of rare-variant signal at the same locus; mixed signal warrants both single-variant and gene-burden testing in any extension.
  • Reference tools mentioned — same dataset and pipeline genealogy as Zhou 2023 (PMID 36921087), Sun 2022 (TOPMed-imputation, PMID 35128485), and the broader CFGP WGS infrastructure. Gives us a uniform substrate against which any extension can build.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: one direction this paper suggests is to take the twenty-three modifier loci identified in Aksit 2022 (11 MI-associated, 12 CFRD-associated, with 3 pleiotropic) and run a phenome-wide association scan of each locus against the full set of CFFPR-derived endpoints — extending beyond GI complications to lung function trajectory, chronic infection, BMI z-score trajectory, liver disease, transplant outcomes, mental health, mortality, and modulator response. SAIGE 2018 for binary phecodes; SAIGE-GENE+ for the rare-variant arm at SLC26A9 and any other locus where rare-variant signal is mixed with the common-variant lead. This is one of several directions opened by the paper, not a committed study design.

What to verify first

  • Variant-call presence — confirm the SLC26A9, PRSS1, and CEBPB lead variants from this paper (both common and rare arms) are present and well-typed in the actual CFGP WGS data we'd use.
  • CFFPR endpoint catalog inventory — which structured fields beyond CFRD and MI status are recorded with sufficient quality and longitudinal depth to support phenome-wide testing.
  • CFGP scope check — direct or proxy confirmation with Cutting (Hopkins), Knowles (UNC), Strug (Toronto), or Corvol (Paris) that a phenome-wide extension along these lines is not already in the consortium's pipeline. The risk-of-being-scooped is non-trivial here.
  • Modulator-era stratification — verify what fraction of registry follow-up post-dates 2019 modulator initiation and whether endpoints are recorded densely enough to support a pre/post-modulator stratified analysis.
  • WGS sub-cohort linkage to registry — what fraction of CFFPR registry patients have linked CFGP-WGS available, and whether that linkage is at the patient level (required) or only at the cohort level (insufficient).

Gap-map: closes

  • CFRD–MI pleiotropy in the CFGP WGS dataset — the bivariate question is now answered: 23 loci total, 3 pleiotropic, with PRSS1 directional split. Re-running this exact bivariate scan would be replication, not novelty.
  • Hypothesis-driven CFRD modifier discovery on full WGS — combined with Aksit 2020 (PMID 31697830, covered 4/30) the CFRD modifier-locus list at WGS depth is reasonably saturated for common-variant signals.

Gap-map: leaves open

  • Phenome-wide pleiotropy beyond GI complications. Aksit 2022 tests only CFRD and MI. Lung function trajectory, infection burden, transplant outcomes, modulator response, mental health, mortality, and cancer incidence are all outside the design. This is the largest white space — a phenome-wide extension of the same pleiotropy framework.
  • Longitudinal phenotype layer. The registry's repeated-measurement structure (quarterly FEV1, BMI z-score over time, exacerbation timestamps, microbiology cultures) is not used. Trajectory phenotypes are unmined.
  • Post-modulator era. The CFGP analysis window mostly pre-dates widespread Trikafta initiation; modulator-era pleiotropy is uncharacterized.
  • Pleiotropy with general-population disease loci. The CF-restricted design isolates CF-specific biology by construction but cannot detect cross-talk with non-CF disease genetics (e.g., do CF modifier loci show pleiotropy with general-population T2D or pancreatitis loci? Aksit 2020 partially addressed this for CFRD vs T2D; the MI side is open).
  • Effect-size estimates for pleiotropy with a third phenotype. Cannot be inferred from a two-phenotype overlap.

Numbers worth remembering

  • 11 MI loci, 6 novel; 12 CFRD loci, 5 novel; 3 pleiotropic (SLC26A9, CEBPB, PRSS1).
  • PRSS1 directionality split — same allele higher-risk for CFRD, lower-risk for MI.
  • SLC26A9 mixed common+rare — common variants and rare variants both contribute, with rare variants partially trait-specific.
  • CFGP cohort — Hopkins/UNC/Toronto/Paris/Canada WGS consortium; the same dataset Zhou 2023 used.

ALEX Good morning. Today's CF-cohort paper is Aksit and colleagues, 2022, "Pleiotropic modifiers of age-related diabetes and neonatal intestinal obstruction in cystic fibrosis," published in the American Journal of Human Genetics, volume 109, pages 1894 to 1908. First author is Melis Aksit at Johns Hopkins; senior author is Scott Blackman, also at Hopkins, the CFRD specialist. The consortium attribution is the CF Genome Project — the same dataset Zhou 2023 used for the lung-function modifier work we covered earlier. We're reading it for one specific reason. This paper is the closest published thing in the CF-modifier literature to a phenome-wide scan. It is not a true PheWAS — it tests two pre-chosen phenotypes — but it explicitly studies pleiotropy, which is the core question a PheWAS exists to answer. Reading it tells us how much of the CFTR-PheWAS design space has already been mined and where the white space actually is.

JORDAN Vocabulary first. There's a lot to set up.

ALEX Yes. Let me start broad. CF, cystic fibrosis, is a genetic disease caused by mutations in CFTR — that's the cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator gene, which codes for a chloride and bicarbonate channel on the surface of epithelial cells. CFTR doesn't work properly in CF; the downstream effects show up across multiple organ systems. The two "complications" this paper studies are CF-related diabetes and meconium ileus.

JORDAN Walk me through both.

ALEX CF-related diabetes — abbreviated CFRD — is a distinct form of diabetes that develops over time in CF patients. By age twenty about a fifth of CF patients have it, by age forty more than half. It's mechanistically different from the everyday type-2 diabetes most people think of: in CFRD the pancreas's beta cells stop making insulin properly, often because the same epithelial-transport failure that scars the lungs also damages the pancreas. Insulin sensitivity, the other half of type-2 diabetes biology, is mostly normal. So CFRD overlaps the beta-cell-failure half of T2D but not the insulin-resistance half. Meconium ileus, abbreviated MI, is at the other end of life — it's a neonatal intestinal blockage that affects roughly fifteen to twenty percent of CF newborns. The first stool, called meconium, is abnormally thick and obstructs the intestine within the first few days of life. It's a surgical emergency.

JORDAN Why study them together?

ALEX That's the whole point of the paper. They're both gastrointestinal complications of CF. They're both heritable beyond the CFTR genotype itself — meaning, two CF patients with identical CFTR mutations can differ on whether they develop CFRD or had MI, and the difference is partly explained by genes other than CFTR. And there's been earlier hint of overlap — they're slightly correlated within patients, and a few loci had been previously implicated in both. So Aksit and team asked the formal version of the question: do the CFRD and MI loci share genetic architecture, or are they independent?

JORDAN More vocabulary.

ALEX Pleiotropy is the term for one gene affecting multiple traits. A locus is a specific spot on the genome — typically a chromosomal region around a gene. WGS is whole-genome sequencing. GWAS, genome-wide association study, scans the entire genome for variants associated with one trait. A modifier gene, in CF, is a non-CFTR gene that influences the severity or specific manifestation of the disease. Genome-wide significance is the conventional p-value threshold for declaring a true association, set at five times ten to the minus eight to control false positives across the millions of variants tested. A common variant has minor allele frequency above one percent in the population; a rare variant is below that. SLC26A9, CEBPB, and PRSS1 are gene names; we'll define each when it comes up. CFGP is the CF Genome Project — the consortium that performed the whole-genome sequencing on the combined Hopkins, UNC, Toronto, French, and Canadian CF cohorts. CFFPR is the Cystic Fibrosis Foundation Patient Registry — the longitudinal clinical database the CFF maintains on essentially every CF patient seen at a CF Foundation–accredited care center. The CFGP genomic data is a subset of the CFFPR clinical population.

JORDAN Now the question. What did they actually do?

ALEX They took the CF Genome Project's whole-genome sequencing dataset and ran two separate genome-wide association scans. One scan with CFRD as the outcome — modeled as time-to-event, meaning age of CFRD onset rather than yes-or-no status. One scan with MI as the outcome — modeled as binary at birth. They did this within a CF cohort, not a general population, so every participant in the analysis carries two CFTR mutations and qualifies as having cystic fibrosis. Then they overlaid the two GWAS results. Loci significant in both scans are pleiotropic — modifiers that affect both diseases. Loci significant in only one are trait-specific. Within the pleiotropic group, they then asked whether the variant pushes both traits in the same direction or in opposite directions.

JORDAN I want to pause on one design point. Why does it matter that both diseases share the CFTR background?

ALEX It is the key design choice. In a general-population GWAS, if you found a locus associated with both type-2 diabetes and intestinal obstruction, you'd probably suspect confounding — maybe the locus drives obesity, which drives both. By restricting to a CF-only cohort, every patient already has CF, so any locus you find affects the CF version of the disease specifically — CFRD pleiotropy with MI in CF patients tells you something about CF biology, not about diabetes biology in general. It also drastically shrinks the cohort — five thousand CF patients is small relative to a UKBB or AoU cohort — but it lets you ask CF-specific pleiotropy questions that no biobank-scale design can answer.

JORDAN I want to push back on a specific design choice. They tested only two phenotypes. Why not run an actual PheWAS?

ALEX That's the right pushback. The design is bivariate, not phenome-wide. The two phenotypes were selected on biological plausibility and prior single-trait GWAS results, not chosen by being the top two of a phenome-wide screen. So this paper does test pleiotropy, but only between the two phenotypes the authors pre-committed to. If a locus is also pleiotropic with non-pulmonary, non-GI complications — depression, transplant rejection, kidney disease, certain cancers — this design will not detect that. The paper is a hypothesis-driven pleiotropy test, not a phenome-wide scan. That distinction matters for our gap analysis and we'll come back to it.

JORDAN Did they answer the question?

ALEX Yes. The headline numbers from the abstract are these. Eleven loci associated with meconium ileus, of which six were not previously identified. Twelve loci associated with CF-related diabetes, of which five were not previously identified. Three loci associated with both: SLC26A9, CEBPB, and PRSS1. SLC26A9 is the gene we've already met multiple times — a chloride and bicarbonate channel that physically interacts with CFTR. Variants near SLC26A9 increase risk for both MI and CFRD. CEBPB — CCAAT/enhancer binding protein beta — is a transcription factor with broad roles in inflammation and metabolism; variants there also increase risk for both. The interesting case is PRSS1, the gene that encodes cationic trypsinogen — a pancreatic digestive enzyme that, when mutated in non-CF settings, causes hereditary pancreatitis. At PRSS1 the higher-risk allele for CFRD is the lower-risk allele for MI. The same variant pushes the two diseases in opposite directions.

JORDAN Why is opposite direction biologically meaningful?

ALEX It tells you the underlying mechanism is not just generic GI-tract dysfunction. If both diseases were just downstream consequences of the same upstream defect, you'd expect every modifier to push both in the same direction. The fact that PRSS1 splits — pancreatic trypsinogen variants protective against MI but sensitizing for CFRD — implies the pancreatic exocrine biology is doing something specific that makes the gut at birth easier-or-harder while making the endocrine pancreas later-in-life vulnerable in a different way. SLC26A9 and CEBPB align across diseases; PRSS1 anti-aligns. That mixed pattern is the actual scientific finding.

JORDAN Is there anything in the abstract about rare versus common variants?

ALEX Yes. The abstract notes that at SLC26A9, both common and rare variants associated with the traits — and the rare variants partially split, with some carrying MI-only signal and others CFRD-only. So even within the same gene, the precise variant being tested matters; an SLC26A9 burden test is not the same as testing the common-variant lead SNP. That's a design subtlety that matters for any future scan that includes SLC26A9 in its candidate-gene list.

JORDAN Plain English first — what one direction does this paper suggest for the CFTR PheWAS?

ALEX Plain English. Aksit 2022 tested two CF complications against the same set of genes and found that some genes affect both, some only one, and some affect both in opposite directions. They had to pick the two complications in advance. The natural extension is: don't pick. Test every gene this paper found — the eleven MI loci, the twelve CFRD loci, and the three pleiotropic loci — against every CF complication tracked in the registry — not just CFRD and MI but lung function trajectory, chronic infection, pancreatic exocrine status, BMI trajectory, liver disease, transplant outcomes, mental health, mortality, response to modulator therapy. Some of those will recapitulate Aksit's findings, which is good as a positive control. Some will surface pleiotropy that the bivariate design could not see. That phenome-wide extension is what an actual CFTR PheWAS would look like.

JORDAN And the formal version?

ALEX One direction this paper suggests is to take the twenty-three modifier loci identified in Aksit 2022 — eleven MI-associated, twelve CFRD-associated, with three pleiotropic — and run a phenome-wide association scan of each locus against the full set of CFFPR-derived endpoints, not restricted to GI complications, using SAIGE 2018 for binary phecodes and SAIGE-GENE+ for the rare-variant arm at SLC26A9 and any other locus where rare-variant signal is mixed with the common-variant lead. To be clear, this is one of several directions the paper suggests, not a committed study design. We have not committed to a research design and we have not seen the CFF whole-genome variant calls or the registry endpoint catalog.

JORDAN Given that proposed direction, where's the gap?

ALEX Conditional on a phenome-wide extension of Aksit 2022's twenty-three-locus pleiotropy framework against CFFPR endpoints, this paper closes the GI-complication pleiotropy question for CFRD versus MI specifically — those two are now mapped against each other and against twenty-three loci. It leaves five things open. First, no non-pulmonary, non-GI pleiotropy is tested — depression, mortality, transplant outcomes, modulator response, cancer incidence, and chronic infection are all outside the bivariate design. Second, the CFFPR longitudinal phenotype layer — repeated FEV1, repeated BMI, exacerbation timestamps, modulator initiation dates — is not used here. Third, the post-modulator era is mostly outside this paper's data window; CF natural history changes after 2019 with widespread Trikafta initiation, and any locus calibrated on pre-modulator outcomes may behave differently going forward. Fourth, the design gives us point estimates for the new loci but not effect sizes against any phenotype this paper does not test — the magnitude of pleiotropy with a third phenotype cannot be inferred from a two-phenotype overlap. Fifth, the paper uses a CF-Genome-Project sub-cohort smaller than the full CFFPR; what fraction of CFFPR registry patients have linked WGS available is the operational constraint that determines whether a registry-anchored extension is even feasible.
