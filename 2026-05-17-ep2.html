Blurb

Ideozu et al. 2024 (Genome Medicine) analyzed CFTR variants in the full 454,727-participant UK Biobank whole-exome dataset, partitioned by PanUKBB six-way ancestry. They detected 4,193 distinct CFTR variants; 2,212 were European-unique. F508del was the most common CF-causing variant in every ancestry except East Asian, where V520F led (with 3600G>A and V456A also prominent). Sixteen participants carried two CF-causing variants — only two (both F508del homozygotes) had a CF diagnosis in UKBB; the remaining 14 did not. A further 154 participants carried one CF-causing variant plus a VCC (R117H most common). Phenotype enrichment in compound heterozygotes flagged the CF ICD-10 codes (E840/848/849), Pseudomonas colonization (B965) and multi-antibiotic resistance (U837) at Bonferroni significance; in the CF-causing-plus-VCC group, bronchiectasis (J47) and other airway codes were enriched. GEVA variant-age estimates indicated the East-Asian-common CFTR variants arose ~215–425 generations ago by neutral drift, not selection. The episode suggests one possible direction: a CFF-cohort-anchored mirror analysis on CFF Genome Project WGS using the same annotation stack, classifying every CFF-detected CFTR variant against this UKBB-WES reference. Anything in Ideozu's catalog is no longer claimable as a discovery; what remains is the WGS-only intronic landscape, the CFFPR clinical-grade phenotype depth, and the CFTR-by-modifier-gene interaction layer.

Methods to lift

  • PanUKBB six-way ancestry assignment over the full 454,727-participant UKB-WES release.
  • CFTR-locus variant extraction with full annotation against CFTR2 (CF-causing), CFTR-France (VCC), ClinVar, and AlphaMissense (uncharacterized high-impact triage).
  • Compound-heterozygote identification by allele-resolved phasing across all CF-causing variant pairs.
  • Hypergeometric phecode-enrichment of UKBB ICD-10 codes for compound-heterozygote and CF-causing+VCC subsets, with Bonferroni multiple-testing correction.
  • GEVA / AVA variant-age estimation for selected CF-causing variants; allele-frequency-vs-age comparison to age-matched neutral variants to test for selective pressure.
  • External reference cross-comparison: BRAVO TOPMed (56,448 CFTR variants reported) and All of Us (39,797 CFTR variants reported) as benchmarks for catalog completeness.

What to verify first

  • CFF Genome Project variant calls are already annotated with ClinVar, CFTR2 and CFTR-France classifications — or a re-annotation pipeline is established.
  • CFTR2 mutation-class (I–VI) assignments are available at variant resolution for the CFF cohort.
  • CFFPR phenotype variables (sweat chloride, pancreatic status, F-stool elastase, FEV1 trajectory, modulator response) map cleanly to the analytic layer.
  • The WGS-only CFTR variant subset (intronic, structural, TG/poly-T tract, deep-intronic splice variants like c.3718-2477C>T) is reliably callable in the CFF WGS pipeline.
  • A clear separation is maintained between "novel as a variant" (a high bar after Ideozu) and "novel as a phenotype association in CF patients" (the more accessible deliverable).

Gap-map

This is a biobank-methods paper, not a CFTR-WGS-cohort paper, so the gap-map structure is shifted from the usual CFTR-WGS template — but the substantive overlap with our project is high enough to be worth a closes/leaves-open pair.

  • Closes: The general-population CFTR-variant catalog up to the resolution of exome capture. Anything in Ideozu's 4,193-variant catalog with an Ideozu-reported population allele frequency is no longer claimable as a discovery in our work.
  • Closes: The basic phenotype-echo question for general-population CFTR compound heterozygotes — they do echo the CF airway phenotype, below diagnostic threshold but detectable at ICD-10 resolution.
  • Leaves open: The intronic and structural CFTR variation that WES misses (TG/poly-T tract, deep-intronic splice variants, large deletions). WGS-anchored work can address this directly.
  • Leaves open: The CF-clinical-care-grade phenotype panel (sweat chloride, F-stool elastase, FEV1 trajectory, modulator response) that UKB ICD-10 cannot reach.
  • Leaves open: CFTR-variant-by-modifier-gene interaction effects — Ideozu reports the CFTR catalog but does not test interactions with other loci.

ALEX Welcome to episode two. Today's paper is Ideozu, Liu, Riley-Gillis and colleagues, 2024, in Genome Medicine — "Diversity of CFTR variants across ancestries characterized using 454,727 UK biobank whole exome sequences." First author is Justin Ideozu at AbbVie, last author Aparna Vasanthakumar at AbbVie. This is, for our project, *the* methods paper of the week. It is the paper that defines the population-scale CFTR variant catalog we would have to position our CFF Patient Registry analysis against, and it is the paper most likely to be the reference cited the very first time a reviewer asks whether anything in our work is genuinely novel.

JORDAN Stop right there. Same drill as yesterday — every term in that intro spelled out. WES, exome, CFTR variant, ancestry, all of it.

ALEX All right. CFTR is the Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane conductance Regulator gene. It encodes a chloride and bicarbonate channel sitting on the surface of epithelial cells throughout the lung, pancreas, sweat glands, intestine and reproductive tract. When both copies of CFTR carry a CF-causing variant, the person has cystic fibrosis. WES stands for whole-exome sequencing. It is a technology where you only sequence the exons — the protein-coding chunks of DNA, roughly 1 to 2 percent of the genome — instead of the whole thing. It is cheaper than whole-genome sequencing, WGS, which sequences everything including the introns and intergenic regions. UKBB stands for UK Biobank, the half-million-person UK cohort we covered yesterday. The UKBB has been progressively releasing exome sequencing on its participants since 2018; by 2024 the count was at 454,727, which is what this paper analyzed. "Ancestry" in modern human-genetics work does not mean ethnicity in the social or self-report sense; it means similarity in patterns of common genetic variation to a reference population. The classification used in this paper is called PanUKBB — the Pan-UK Biobank pipeline — which sorts participants into six major continental groups: African (AFR), American or American Admixed (AMR), Central South Asian (CSA), East Asian (EAS), European (EUR), and Middle East (MID), with an unassigned mixed group called MIX. CF-causing variants are CFTR variants curated by an international clinical-genetics consortium as causing CF in patients homozygous or compound-heterozygous for them; the curating database is CFTR2. VCC stands for varying clinical consequences — these are CFTR variants where the same variant has produced CF in some patients and not in others, often because the second CFTR variant on the trans allele is mild. High-impact variants are sequence variants the variant-effect-predictor algorithms classify as likely to disable the protein — stop-gains, frameshifts, splice-site disruptors — regardless of whether anyone has yet matched the variant to a CF patient. AlphaMissense is DeepMind's 2023 missense-variant pathogenicity predictor; the authors use it to triage uncharacterized high-impact variants. CFTR-France is a French national CFTR-variant curation database. GEVA is a tool called the Genealogical Estimation of Variant Age — it estimates how many generations ago a given variant arose by looking at the lengths of the haplotypes it sits inside today. AVA is the Atlas of Variant Age, the precomputed table that pairs with GEVA. BRAVO is the public variant-frequency browser built on TOPMed; TOPMed is the Trans-Omics for Precision Medicine consortium, a large American WGS resource. AoU is All of Us, the NIH's diverse-population sequencing program. ICD-10 codes you've heard before — hospital-administrative disease codes. Bonferroni adjustment is the strictest multiple-testing correction — you divide your significance threshold by the number of tests. F508del is the most common CF-causing variant, a three-base-pair deletion that removes a phenylalanine at codon 508.

JORDAN Why is anyone reading this paper, beyond the obvious fact that it has the word CFTR in the title?

ALEX The motivating problem is that the CFTR2 database and its predecessor CFTR1 have been built largely from European clinic-ascertained CF patients. As a result, the catalog of "what counts as a CF-causing variant" is heavily Europe-anchored. The clinical consequence is real: a non-European person can carry a CFTR variant that would be classified as pathogenic if it were European-frequency, and either get missed on a carrier panel or end up with an uncertain-significance result. The authors wanted to know what the spectrum of CFTR variation actually looks like across ancestries when you sample without a CF-clinic ascertainment bias — i.e., from a general-population biobank. The unknown beforehand: how many CFTR variants are out there at the population level total, which are unique to which ancestry, how many UKB participants are walking around as compound heterozygotes for two CF-causing variants without a CF diagnosis, and whether the phenotype patterns of those compound-heterozygote carriers track classic CF or look milder. A secondary question — and one the methods primer for our project should note — was whether the CFTR variants common in non-European populations show signatures of having been under historical natural selection or whether they look like neutral drift.

JORDAN Methods?

ALEX Quite clean. They took all 454,727 UKBB WES samples, assigned each to one of six PanUKBB ancestries plus MIX, extracted all variants overlapping the CFTR locus, and counted them. They then annotated each variant against three references: the CFTR2 database for CF-causing status, the CFTR-France database for VCC and other clinically-relevant categories, and ClinVar plus AlphaMissense for the uncharacterized high-impact variants. They identified compound heterozygotes — defined as people carrying any two CF-causing variants — and pulled their UKBB phenotype records to ask whether they had a CF diagnosis and whether their ICD-10 code histories were enriched for classical CF features. Phenotype enrichment was tested with a hypergeometric test, with Bonferroni correction across phecodes. For variant age they used GEVA against the AVA reference and asked whether common CF-causing variants in non-European groups showed allele-frequency deviations from neutral expectation given their age. ALEX is going to push back on one design choice: this is WES, not WGS, which means almost the entire CFTR intronic landscape is invisible. The intron 9 TG-and-poly-T tract — a microsatellite that strongly modulates exon 9 splicing — is not captured. Deep-intronic variants like c.3718-2477C>T cannot be ascertained. So when the paper says "over 4,000 CFTR variants" it is conditional on what exome capture sees, and the headline number is necessarily an undercount of CFTR-locus diversity.

JORDAN And the headline numbers?

ALEX They detected 4,193 CFTR variants in total. Europeans (EUR) had the most unique CFTR variants at 2,212; the American group (AMR) had only 23. F508del was the most common CF-causing variant in every ancestry *except* East Asian, where V520F was the most common, with 3600G>A and V456A also prominent. Sixteen UKBB participants carried two CF-causing variants. Nine of those sixteen were in EUR, six in MIX, one in CSA. Only two of the sixteen had a CF diagnosis in UKBB — both were F508del homozygotes. The other fourteen had no diagnosis. A larger group, 154 participants, had one CF-causing variant plus a VCC. R117H was the most-reported VCC in this set; R117H plus F508del was the most common pair. Variant ages: 1717-1G>A was the youngest CF-causing variant the GEVA pipeline could date, originating roughly 425 generations or 10,600 years ago. V456A was the oldest at about 215 generations, around 5,300 years ago. None showed an allele-frequency-vs-age deviation that would suggest historical selection, so the authors concluded the common East Asian variants arose by neutral drift, not selective pressure. The phenotype enrichment was where this paper gets genuinely interesting for us. In participants with two CF-causing variants, the Bonferroni-significant ICD-10 codes included E840, E848 and E849 — the cystic-fibrosis codes themselves — plus B965 (colonization with Pseudomonas aeruginosa) and U837 (resistance to multiple antibiotics). Bronchiectasis was in the top 10 but didn't survive Bonferroni. In the participants with one CF-causing and one VCC variant — the *not-diagnosed-with-CF* group of 154 — pulmonary phenotypes including J47 (bronchiectasis), J998 and J440 (chronic-obstructive lung disease codes) were significantly enriched. So the answer to "do non-diagnosed compound heterozygotes look like they have CF?" is: many of them do, just below the diagnostic threshold, with the airway-pathology phenotype most visible. Did they answer the original question? Yes, on diversity. Yes, on phenotype enrichment. They sidestepped only one piece — they did not deeply integrate functional severity (CFTR2 mutation class I-VI) into the phenotype analysis, which is a missed lift for a future paper.

JORDAN One possible direction this suggests?

ALEX A CFF-cohort-anchored mirror analysis. Plain English: take the CFF Genome Project WGS, run the same variant-discovery and CFTR2/CFTR-France/AlphaMissense annotation pipeline that Ideozu ran, but on the CF-affected cohort. Then for every variant the CFF cohort carries that is not in the UKBB-WES catalog, ask: is it absent because it's truly absent at population frequency, is it absent because WES exome-capture misses it (intronic, structural), or is it absent because it's been generated by the CF-cohort-specific ascertainment process and is therefore a candidate for being functionally meaningful in a way the general-population reference can't see? Formal one-sentence version: "For each CFTR variant identified in CFF Genome Project WGS, classify against the Ideozu 2024 UKBB-WES population reference into one of {present-with-matching-frequency, present-with-CF-enriched-frequency, absent-in-WES-but-detectable-in-WGS-only-region, absent-in-both-by-low-frequency}, and within the CF-enriched-frequency stratum perform a phenotype-enrichment analysis using CFF Patient Registry phenotypes."

JORDAN What does it close and what does it leave open?

ALEX Given that proposal, this paper closes the question of what the population-level CFTR variant catalog looks like — the answer is "over 4,000 WES-visible variants, 2,212 unique to Europeans, with an East-Asian-specific common-variant set." It closes the question of whether CFTR compound heterozygotes in the general population echo the CF phenotype — they do, especially in airways. And it closes a major chunk of the "what variants can we plausibly claim as novel" question: any CFTR variant our CFF cohort detects that is already in Ideozu's catalog is non-novel as a discovery, even if its phenotype association in CF patients is novel. What it leaves open. First, intronic and structural CFTR variation is uncovered because this is WES — the TG/poly-T tract, deep-intronic splice variants like c.3718-2477C>T, large deletions and duplications. A CFF-WGS-anchored analysis could fill that gap directly. Second, the phenotype linkage in Ideozu is the UKB-ICD-10 hospital record. The CFF Patient Registry has CF-clinical-care-grade phenotypes — sweat chloride, pancreatic-sufficiency, F-stool elastase, FEV1 trajectory, modulator response — that are not in UKB. So an Ideozu-style ICD-enrichment analysis on CFF data would be richer and tighter. Third, the paper does not test interactions between CFTR variants and modifier-gene loci — which is exactly the next-step analysis a CF cohort with WGS plus clinical depth can support. Things to verify first. We'd need to check whether the CFF Genome Project variant calls have already been processed through ClinVar, CFTR2, and CFTR-France annotation pipelines or whether re-annotation is required. We'd need to confirm that CFTR2 mutation-class assignments are available at variant resolution. We'd need to verify that CFFPR phenotype variables map cleanly enough to support both an internal CF-versus-CF comparison and an external comparison against UKB ICD-10-derived phenotypes. None of these are blockers; they are pre-flight checks.
