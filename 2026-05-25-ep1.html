Blurb

Virolainen and colleagues' 2023 review in Genes and Immunity is the cleanest plain-English entry-point into the gene-environment (G×E) interaction literature, which is the methodological substrate of the next several biobank-papers in the reading queue. The review introduces the four Ottman-Kauffmann-Demenais models of G×E, then walks through five environmental categories (microbiome, pollutants and toxicants, viral infection, climate change, racism/stress/economic factors), with worked examples — FLG plus skin barrier irritants, AS3MT plus arsenic, DAPP1 plus diesel exhaust, IRF7 plus influenza or SARS-CoV-2, EBNA2 binding sites overlapping ~50% of lupus and MS risk variants. Methodological challenges are surveyed but not solved: interaction-term power penalty (~4× the main-effect sample size), exposure-measurement-quality bottleneck, multiple-testing burden under genome-wide G×E scans. The proposed CF-direction this episode invites is a pre-specified panel of CF-modifier-variant × CFFPR-captured-exposure interaction tests against lung-function and chronic-Pa endpoints — a generalization of the Collaco 2008 TGFB1-times-secondhand-smoke design template to a fuller modifier panel. The open question is whether CFFPR systematically captures the right exposures at the right granularity for the interaction terms to be testable at the CFGP-WGS sample size of ~5,000.

Plain-English glossary

  • CFTR (cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator) — a chloride and bicarbonate channel in epithelial cell membranes; loss-of-function mutations cause cystic fibrosis.
  • Complex disease — disease caused by many genes plus environment, typically with non-Mendelian inheritance (e.g., asthma, type-2 diabetes).
  • EBNA2 (Epstein-Barr nuclear antigen 2) — a viral protein expressed in latent EBV infection that hijacks human transcription factor machinery and reshapes human gene expression.
  • eQTL (expression quantitative trait locus) — a DNA variant that influences how much of a particular gene gets transcribed into RNA.
  • FLG (filaggrin) — a structural protein forming the outermost skin barrier; loss-of-function variants are the strongest single risk factor for atopic dermatitis.
  • G×E (gene-environment interaction) — when the effect of a genetic variant on disease risk depends on environmental exposure status (or vice versa); not additive but multiplicative.
  • GWAS (genome-wide association study) — a scan of hundreds of thousands to millions of common DNA variants across a cohort, testing each one's main-effect association with a phenotype.
  • Incomplete penetrance — when carriers of a disease-associated variant don't all develop the disease.
  • IRF7 (interferon regulatory factor 7) — a transcription factor that activates the type-I interferon antiviral response.
  • Monogenic disease — disease caused by a single-gene mutation following Mendelian inheritance (e.g., cystic fibrosis caused by CFTR mutations).
  • Pleiotropy — when one gene or variant affects multiple, often unrelated, traits.
  • PM2.5 — fine particulate matter ≤2.5 micrometers in diameter; WHO attributes 4–9 million annual global deaths to it.
  • QTL (quantitative trait locus) — a stretch of DNA that influences a measurable continuous trait.
  • vQTL (variance QTL) — a variant whose carriers show more dispersed phenotype values than expected, suggestive of a hidden interaction modulating the genotype effect.

Methods to lift

  • Ottman-Kauffmann-Demenais four-model G×E framework — canonical taxonomy of how a gene and an environment can jointly raise disease risk (additive, exacerbation in either direction, required-both, synergistic).
  • vQTL analysis (Wang et al. exemplar) — variance-QTL scan in ~300K UKBB individuals identifying 75 vQTLs enriched for G×E without requiring direct exposure measurement.
  • MARIO (Measurement of Allelic Ratios Informatics Operator) — bypasses QTL sample-size requirements by reading allelic imbalance in functional-genomics reads at heterozygous variants.
  • Induced pluripotent stem cell exposure panels — controlled in-dish exposure of genotyped iPSCs, reading out allele-specific gene expression; trades sample size for exposure precision.
  • EBNA2-binding-site enrichment scan — test whether viral transcription-factor binding sites cluster at human GWAS risk haplotypes for the disease the virus is implicated in.
  • Biologically guided hypothesis restriction — limit interaction-term tests to a pre-specified pathway or candidate-variant panel rather than genome-wide, trading discovery breadth for testable multiple-comparison correction.
  • Pollution-exposure geocoding from clinical addresses — convert clinic-reported addresses into census-tract-level pollution and median-income estimates (e.g., Auger/Beck Cincinnati asthma cohorts).

Use the Collaco 2008 TGFB1-by-smoke interaction design as the methodological template, extending it from a single candidate-by-single-exposure design to a small pre-specified panel-by-panel design with explicit interaction-term power calculation. Stratify by modulator era. Use Bonferroni correction across the (variant × exposure × outcome) cell grid.

What to verify first

  • Whether the CFFPR exposure schema systematically captures the candidate environmental fields at the granularity an interaction term needs (especially indoor mold, occupational, and geocoded ambient pollution beyond zip code).
  • Whether each published CF modifier sentinel SNP is reliably called in the CFGP-WGS variant set at modern QC, including imputation accuracy if any sentinel was originally array-genotyped.
  • Whether the interaction-term sample size is adequate at n≈5,000 — the standard rule of thumb says 4× the main-effect sample size for matched-power detection on the interaction; this is likely the binding constraint.
  • Whether modulator-era versus pre-modulator stratification is operationally feasible given current CFFPR longitudinal-treatment-status coverage.
  • Whether the published modifier sentinels were originally discovered in cohorts that substantially overlap the current CFGP-WGS sample — if so, the interaction follow-up is a refinement rather than a true replication, and must be described that way.
  • Whether differential exposure misclassification (e.g., smoke-status reporting bias correlating with genotype or with study site) threatens the interaction term — pre-specified bias-sensitivity analysis required.
  • Whether the chr20q13.2 modifier cluster sentinels survive at CFGP-genotyping-platform-call quality given their original Illumina 610-Quad provenance (Wright 2011) — may need imputation-quality auditing.

ALEX Welcome back to the CFTR PheWAS reading podcast. This is episode one of three today, dated May 25, 2026. The paper is Samuel Virolainen and colleagues from Cincinnati Children's, published December 2022 in Genes and Immunity, titled "Gene-environment interactions and their impact on human health." It's a review article — not a primary study — and we're reading it because the entire next batch of biobank papers on our reading list is focused on gene-environment and gene-gene interactions, and Virolainen is the cleanest plain-English entry-point to that whole literature.

JORDAN OK so before we go in. Gene-environment interaction. What does that actually mean? I've heard the phrase but I couldn't define it cleanly.

ALEX Good. Let's slow that down. A gene-environment interaction — written G times E in shorthand — is when the effect of a genetic variant on disease risk depends on whether you're exposed to some environmental factor, or vice versa. The two factors don't just add up — they multiply, or modify each other. In plain language: same genes, different environments, different outcomes.

JORDAN And what counts as "environment" here?

ALEX Anything outside the genome. Microbes you carry, air you breathe, foods you eat, viruses you've had, the temperature where you live, your job, your socioeconomic status, even things that happened before you were born. Virolainen organizes the review around five environmental categories — the microbiome, pollutants and toxicants, viral infections, climate change, and what they call racism, stress, and economic factors.

JORDAN And there's a formal model for how to think about a G-times-E interaction?

ALEX Yes. The paper cites a classic 1990 framework from Ruth Ottman, refined by Kauffmann and Demenais. Four canonical models. One — the risk genotype exacerbates the effect of the environmental exposure. Two — the exposure exacerbates the effect of the genotype. Three — both the gene and the environment are required to cause disease. Four — each one independently raises risk, but having them together raises risk more than the sum would predict. The fourth model — synergy — is the one the review focuses on.

JORDAN Got it. And new terms — let me list what I need defined before we go further. GWAS. eQTL. QTL. Monogenic versus complex. Penetrance. Pleiotropy. CFTR, since you keep mentioning it.

ALEX All right, primer round. GWAS — genome-wide association study — is a scan where you genotype hundreds of thousands or millions of single-letter DNA differences across the genome in a large group of people and ask, for each one, does carrying this letter associate with the disease. The output is a list of variants and their p-values. QTL stands for quantitative trait locus — a stretch of DNA that influences a measurable trait. An eQTL is a special case — a variant that influences how much a particular gene gets transcribed into RNA, that is, gene expression. So eQTLs are the bridge between "this DNA variant matters" and "here's what it actually does in the cell." Monogenic disease means caused by a mutation in a single gene, like classic cystic fibrosis caused by mutations in CFTR. Complex disease means caused by many genes plus environment, like asthma or type-2 diabetes. Penetrance is the probability that someone carrying a risk variant actually develops the disease — incomplete penetrance means many carriers stay healthy. Pleiotropy means one gene or one variant affects multiple, often unrelated, traits. CFTR is the cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator — it's a chloride and bicarbonate channel that sits in the membrane of epithelial cells lining the airways, the gut, the pancreas, and other surfaces. Broken CFTR is the cause of cystic fibrosis.

JORDAN And FLG, IRF7, EBNA2 — those come up a lot in this paper. Quick primers.

ALEX FLG is filaggrin — a protein that builds the outermost skin barrier; loss-of-function variants cause atopic dermatitis. IRF7 is interferon regulatory factor seven — a transcription factor that switches on the antiviral type-one interferon response. EBNA2 is Epstein-Barr nuclear antigen two — a viral protein made by the Epstein-Barr virus during latent infection that hijacks the human transcription machinery to change expression of human genes.

JORDAN OK. So what's the problem this review is trying to tackle?

ALEX The motivating problem is that for most complex diseases — autoimmune disease, asthma, cancer, inflammatory bowel disease, the things that account for the bulk of chronic illness in industrialized countries — neither pure genetics nor pure environment fully explains who gets sick. The genetic variants we know about typically explain only a few percent of disease variance. The environmental risk factors we know about explain another modest chunk. There's always a large unexplained gap. The hypothesis Virolainen defends is that a meaningful fraction of that gap lives inside gene-environment interactions, and we've underinvested in studying those because they're hard.

JORDAN Why are they hard?

ALEX Several reasons. First, you need very large cohorts to detect interaction effects — statisticians estimate roughly four times the sample size needed for a main effect of equivalent magnitude. Second, the environment is hard to measure consistently — the same person's pollution exposure, microbiome composition, viral load, and psychological stress change week to week. Third, environment is correlated with ancestry and socioeconomic status, which themselves correlate with genetic background, so untangling cause from confounder is genuinely difficult. Fourth, current GWAS infrastructure tests one variant at a time against a main effect — the multiple-testing penalty when you start testing variant-by-exposure terms across the genome becomes severe quickly.

ALEX Then the review walks through the environmental categories. The microbiome — the trillions of bacteria living on and in the body — both protects against allergic disease and, when disrupted, contributes to inflammatory bowel disease. The hygiene hypothesis sits here. Pollutants — fine particulate matter under 2.5 microns called PM2.5, the World Health Organization attributes between four and nine million annual deaths to it. Arsenic in groundwater exposes roughly 100 million people globally to unsafe concentrations. Heavy metals — cadmium, mercury, lead — chronic exposure interacts with metallothionein gene variants to determine who accumulates how much. Viral infection — Epstein-Barr virus is associated with multiple sclerosis, lupus, rheumatoid arthritis; respiratory syncytial virus is a risk factor for childhood asthma; hepatitis C and human papillomavirus cause cancer. Climate change — shifting pollen ranges, expanding vector-borne disease territory, food insecurity. And socioeconomic and structural factors — environmental toxin exposure tracks neighborhood, race, and income.

JORDAN I want to push back on the methods. You said interaction effects are hard to detect because you need huge sample sizes. Does the paper actually solve that?

ALEX No, it doesn't claim to. It catalogs methods people are trying. One is biologically guided hypothesis restriction — instead of testing every gene against every exposure, you restrict to a candidate pathway and test only those genes. Another is variance QTL analysis — vQTL — where you look for variants whose carriers show more dispersed phenotype values than expected. A vQTL signal is suggestive that something is modulating the genotype's effect, and that something is plausibly an environmental factor. The paper cites a study by Wang and colleagues that found 75 vQTLs in over 300,000 UK Biobank participants without ever needing to measure the environment directly. A third approach is machine-learning pipelines that ingest transcriptomic data plus environmental measurements together. Fourth — induced pluripotent stem cell models, where you take cells from genotyped donors and expose them in a dish to different environmental treatments, reading out allele-specific gene expression. Smaller sample size, more controlled exposure.

JORDAN All right, the headline findings. What does the review actually establish?

ALEX It's a review, so the findings are recapitulated from primary papers rather than new. But Virolainen highlights specific G-times-E examples that are well-supported. Filaggrin loss-of-function variants plus environmental factors — Staphylococcus colonization and irritant exposure — drive atopic dermatitis and the so-called atopic march into asthma and food allergy. AS3MT variants at the 10q24.32 locus plus arsenic exposure determine arsenic metabolism efficiency and skin-cancer risk. DAPP1 variants plus diesel exhaust drive airway hyperreactivity in a GWAS interaction study by Maazi and colleagues. IRF7 loss-of-function plus influenza or SARS-coronavirus-2 exposure produces life-threatening pneumonia in otherwise healthy people — about 3.5% of life-threatening COVID-19 pneumonia in one study, and about 10% of pediatric COVID-19 hospitalizations. EBNA2 binding sites in the human genome overlap with about half of all known lupus and multiple sclerosis risk variants, suggesting Epstein-Barr virus uses the human genome's autoimmune risk architecture as docking sites for its own transcriptional regulation. Two recent 2022 papers — Bjornevik and Lanz — argue causally that Epstein-Barr infection is required for multiple sclerosis.
