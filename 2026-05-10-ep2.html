Methods to lift

  • Cloud-passport data-access model — register through institutional ethics training to gain access to a Researcher Workbench rather than downloading data locally; outputs leave only after a re-identification review.
  • EHR-vs-survey phenotype concordance design — for any exposure or condition with both a registry/survey definition and an EHR-billing-code definition, run both, report effect-size concordance, treat divergence as a phenotype-definition sensitivity analysis rather than biological non-replication.
  • UBR-stratified primary analysis — race/ethnicity and other UBR-relevant strata reported as primary outcomes (forest plots), not as sensitivity-analysis afterthoughts.
  • Privacy-protection date shift — random one-to-365-day backward shift applied per participant to obscure absolute event timing; analyses that depend on calendar effects (seasonal, COVID-era, modulator-rollout-era) need to account for it.
  • Demonstration-project replication strategy — validate platform with canonical analyses (smoking-cancer, ACC/AHA risk score) before claiming readiness for novel discovery; report effect-size CIs versus published meta-analyses.

JORDAN Acronyms first. AoU is shorthand for the All of Us Research Program — the NIH-funded U.S. precision medicine cohort that aims to enroll at least one million diverse participants. UBR stands for underrepresented in biomedical research — an AoU program-defined category for participants who fall into one or more groups historically excluded from large genomic studies. ICD codes — International Classification of Diseases — are the standardized billing codes hospitals use; ICD-9 is the older version, ICD-10 is the current one in U.S. clinical practice. ALEX To put that all in plain English first — AoU is the U.S. NIH million-participant cohort, EHR is the medical-chart data, PPI is the survey-from-participant data, PM is the in-person height-and-weight kind of measurement, UBR is the program's underrepresented-in-biomedical-research label, PheWAS is the run-many-phecodes-at-once association design, ICD codes are billing codes, ASCVD is cardiovascular disease for risk-score purposes, and FAIR is the data-sharing principle stack.

ALEX Background and purpose. Before AoU launched in May 2018, the NIH precision-medicine roadmap had identified two specific gaps in U.S. cohort genomics. First, prior cohorts were demographically narrow — predominantly white, predominantly middle-class, predominantly recruited through academic medical centres in fewer than a dozen states. Second, the data-access model for those cohorts required investigators to download de-identified data dumps to local infrastructure, which restricted analysis to investigators with substantial computational resources. AoU sets out to invert both of those. On the demographics side, the explicit recruitment design over-samples groups defined as UBR by program criteria: race/ethnicity beyond non-Hispanic White, sexual and gender minorities, lower income levels, lower educational attainment, rural residence, age extremes, and people with disabilities. On the data-access side, AoU adopts what they call a passport model — researchers register through their institution, complete an ethics training module, and gain access to a cloud-based Researcher Workbench rather than downloading data locally. The slogan is "bring researchers to the data."

JORDAN What's the practical effect of "bring researchers to the data"?

ALEX It means the data never leaves the AoU cloud environment. Investigators run their analyses inside that environment, paying compute charges for their notebooks, and their outputs leave only after a review pipeline confirms that nothing in them re-identifies a participant. The advantage is that AoU can keep track of every analysis and audit access uniformly. The cost is that researchers without budgets for cloud compute are exposed to ongoing per-analysis costs that didn't exist under the old download-and-analyze-locally model. The paper estimates that the demonstration analyses in their own writeup cost between a few hundred and a few thousand dollars in compute charges — which is small for a well-funded lab and meaningful for a graduate student.

ALEX Methods. The paper describes the data substrate of the initial Researcher Workbench beta release as of June 2021, which is three hundred fifteen thousand seven enrolled participants. By design, every participant has data from "the Basics" survey, which covers demographics and is required before any other consent step. Second-tier surveys — Lifestyle, Overall Health, Personal Medical History, Healthcare Access and Utilization, Family Medical History — are completed by smaller subsets, in roughly the three hundred thousand to ninety thousand range depending on the survey. EHR data is available for two hundred three thousand eight hundred thirteen participants, and the intersection of "any survey response, any physical measurement, any EHR data" is one hundred ninety-six thousand seven hundred nine. A randomly applied backward date shift of one to three hundred sixty-five days protects participant privacy in date-sensitive analyses, which is why some survey responses appear in the data with timestamps that look like they predate the program's enrollment start — that's an artifact of the privacy-protection layer, not a data error. Eight thousand four hundred thirty-five participants have data from a Fitbit-style wearable device, sixty-two thousand six hundred sixty-four have completed the COVID-19 Participant Experience survey called COPE, and the planned trajectory adds biospecimen-derived genotyping and whole-genome sequencing in subsequent controlled-tier releases. To validate the dataset, the authors run four demonstration projects: a treatment-pathway visualization for diabetes and depression medications, a cancer phenome-wide association study using smoking as the exposure, a comparison of EHR-derived versus survey-derived smoking exposure, and a cardiovascular-risk-score calculation using the ACC/AHA Pooled Cohort Equations across self-reported race strata. The demonstration projects are explicitly described as not being designed to make new biological discoveries; they are designed to show that the dataset reproduces well-known associations and that the platform supports each of those analytical moves.

JORDAN Push back here — replicating things that are already known is good for confidence, but it doesn't tell us much about how AoU performs on questions where the right answer isn't already in the literature.

ALEX That's correct, and the paper itself acknowledges it. The validation strategy is a floor, not a ceiling. The unstated bet is that subsequent papers will probe the parts of AoU where novelty is genuinely possible — UBR-stratified discovery, EHR-billing-code-only versus survey-only phenotype definitions, biospecimen and wearable integration, and so on. This descriptor paper just establishes that the platform handles the canonical analyses correctly.

ALEX Results. Demographics first. Of the three hundred fifteen thousand participants in the beta release, forty-nine percent identify with a population other than non-Hispanic White alone, thirteen percent identify with a sexual or gender minority group, and seventy-eight percent meet at least one UBR criterion overall. That seventy-eight percent figure is the one that appears most often in subsequent AoU papers as the headline diversity claim. Second, the diabetes-and-depression treatment-pathway analysis covers nineteen thousand two hundred six T2D participants and twenty-nine thousand three hundred thirty-seven depression participants. The most common first-line medications are biguanides for T2D and SSRIs for depression in both White and non-White subgroups, but the rate at which patients are treated with that first-line agent differs significantly by race, with p less than 0.01, and the second and third medications also differ. Third, the cancer-smoking PheWAS recovers the canonical signals: respiratory cancers and cervical cancer associate strongly with ever-smoking, cutaneous neoplasms show the previously-reported protective association, and twelve out of fifteen phenotype confidence intervals overlap with the published meta-analysis confidence intervals from the literature. Twenty percent of the EHR-billing-code-derived smoking effect sizes are statistically significantly larger than the survey-derived ones, which the authors interpret as the EHR codes being less sensitive but more specific than self-report — patients who get the smoking ICD code attached to their chart are typically heavier smokers than the average self-reported "ever-smoker." Fourth, the ACC/AHA cardiovascular-risk calculation runs on forty-nine thousand nine hundred eighty-two participants who have all the model parameters in their EHR and replicates the well-known race-effect baseline differences in ASCVD risk. So the question they posed — does the platform produce sensible numbers on canonical analyses — is answered affirmatively.

ALEX Given that proposed direction, the gap that this paper closes for our purposes is the question of whether AoU is methodologically ready as a comparator. ALEX One last methodological note worth lifting before we close out — the AoU comparison of EHR-derived versus self-reported smoking exposure is genuinely useful evidence for any project that has to choose between a registry-curated phenotype definition and an EHR-derived phenotype definition. The AoU finding is that the two definitions agree on direction and approximately on rank-ordering of cancer-association magnitudes, but the EHR-derived definitions tend to produce somewhat larger effect sizes in roughly twenty percent of phenotypes, which the authors interpret as the EHR cohort being enriched for heavier smokers. The implication for our project is that wherever the CFFPR registry has a curated CFRD or chronic-Pa or transplant endpoint and AoU has only an EHR-billing-code analogue, we should not expect identical effect-size estimates from the two cohorts even when the underlying biology is the same.
