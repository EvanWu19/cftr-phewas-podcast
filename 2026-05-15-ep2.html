Blurb

Tanigawa, Wainberg, Karjalainen and colleagues (PLOS Genetics, 2020) ran a three-analysis rare-protein-altering-variant scan in UK Biobank (n=337,151) and FinnGen (n=176,899) for genes whose loss-of-function would lower intraocular pressure and protect against glaucoma. A single UK Biobank missense variant — rs28991009, p.Gln175His in ANGPTL7, MAF 0.81% — passed Bonferroni for both IOP measures (p=5.96×10⁻⁹ and 1.07×10⁻¹³; β=–0.53 to –3.40 mmHg) and conferred a 34% glaucoma risk reduction (OR=0.66, p=0.0062). FinnGen surfaced an independent Finnish-enriched missense (rs147660927, p.Arg220Cys, 50× population enrichment) with OR=0.71 across all glaucoma subtypes (p=1.9×10⁻¹²). A four-variant burden test on ANGPTL7, including one PTV, gave p~10⁻¹⁴, with concordant direction across the allelic series. Proposed direction for the CFF WGS cohort: mirror the three-analysis architecture — anchor in CFF WGS for rare-protein-altering-variant tests against a CFTR-modifier endpoint (CFRD, pancreatic exocrine insufficiency), augment via French and Canadian CF Gene Modifier Study cohorts. Open questions before committing: can French/Canadian cohorts replicate at comparable sequencing depth, what's the CFRD case count per cohort, and does any candidate gene have ANGPTL7-style tissue-coherent expression in a CFTR-relevant tissue?

Plain-English glossary

  • Allelic series: multiple distinct variants in the same gene, each with measurable effect, all pointing in the same phenotypic direction — strongest possible human-genetic evidence for a target.
  • ANGPTL7: five-exon protein-coding gene on chromosome 1p36; encodes Angiopoietin-related protein 7, highly expressed in trabecular meshwork.
  • Bonferroni correction: the most conservative multiple-testing correction — divide alpha by the number of tests. Here, 0.05 ÷ 41,590 = ~1.0 × 10⁻⁶.
  • Burden test: collapses several rare variants in one gene into a single "any carrier" indicator and asks if carriers differ from non-carriers; powerful when all variants act the same direction.
  • Dispersion test / SKAT: sum-of-squares variant-set test that doesn't require all variants to push the same direction; complements the burden test.
  • FinnGen: Finnish national biobank, ~200,000+ participants, valuable because the Finnish founder effect enriches certain otherwise-vanishingly-rare alleles 10–100×.
  • Founder effect: in a small founding population, variants present in even one founder rise to higher frequency than they would have in a large continuous population.
  • Glaucoma: group of optic nerve diseases that cause progressive irreversible vision loss; elevated IOP is the dominant modifiable risk factor.
  • Goldmann-correlated IOP: an IOP measurement standardized to the Goldmann applanation tonometer, the historical clinical gold standard.
  • Corneal-compensated IOP: an IOP measurement corrected for corneal biomechanics — relevant because corneal stiffness biases raw IOP readings.
  • IOP (intraocular pressure): fluid pressure inside the eyeball, measured in millimeters of mercury (mmHg).
  • MAF (minor allele frequency): frequency of the less common allele at a variant; here, "rare" = 0.01% to 1%.
  • MHC (major histocompatibility complex): chromosome 6p region of unusual variant density and linkage; commonly excluded from rare-variant scans because it breaks standard assumptions.
  • Missense variant: single-nucleotide change that swaps one amino acid for another in the encoded protein.
  • Multi-cohort design: anchor analysis in one cohort, replicate or amplify in a second; standard biobank-era architecture.
  • PLINK: open-source population-genetics analysis toolkit; the GWAS workhorse.
  • Principal components (genotype PCs): orthogonal axes summarizing the global genetic variation in the cohort; included as covariates to control for population stratification.
  • Protein-altering variant: umbrella term covering missense substitutions and protein-truncating variants — those most likely to change protein function.
  • Protein-truncating variant (PTV) / pLoF: premature stop, frameshift, or essential splice variant; predicted to abolish protein function.
  • Trabecular meshwork: the eye tissue that drains aqueous humor and controls IOP; ANGPTL7's main expression site.
  • rg (genetic correlation): the correlation between two traits' SNP-level genetic effects; reported as 0.71 between IOP and glaucoma.

Methods to lift

  • Three-analysis architecture — anchor cohort discovery, founder-enriched second-cohort replication, held-out same-cohort replication. The cleanest published rare-variant biobank template.
  • Variant frequency window — 0.01% < MAF < 1% for both the single-variant scan and burden test. Excludes ultra-rare (untestable in any single cohort) and common (better handled by GWAS).
  • MHC exclusion — drop chromosome 6p MHC region from any rare-variant scan; pre-register the exclusion before looking at results.
  • Burden + dispersion in parallel — report both tests for each candidate gene, with consistent direction-of-effect as a secondary check.
  • PTV-inclusion check — include at least one protein-truncating variant in the gene burden to anchor the loss-of-function direction of effect.
  • GWAS Atlas / open figshare deposition — summary statistics and gene-based test outputs deposited on figshare; pre-publication transparency standard.
  • Cross-subtype replication — for any glaucoma-style heterogeneous endpoint, test the variant across phenotype subtypes; cross-subtype consistency is a credibility multiplier.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: a rare-protein-altering-variant scan in the CFF WGS cohort (~5,000 patients) against a curated CFTR-modifier endpoint panel — CFRD, severe pancreatic exocrine insufficiency, FEV1 trajectory slope, BMI z-score — mirroring the Tanigawa three-analysis structure. Anchor analysis in CFF, founder-style augmentation via the French CF Gene Modifier Study and Canadian CF Gene Modifier Study cohorts, held-out replication on a CFF subset. Variant frequency window: 0.01% < MAF < 1% in the CFF cohort. Per-gene tests: single-variant under Bonferroni, plus SKAT-O burden+dispersion combined, plus a PTV-only sub-burden as an inhibition-direction sentinel.

What to verify first

  • Sequencing depth and platform parity between CFF WGS, French CFGM, and Canadian CFGM cohorts — burden tests assume comparable variant calling.
  • CFRD and pancreatic exocrine insufficiency case counts per cohort, with explicit power calculation against an ANGPTL7-equivalent OR (0.66) before any analysis is run.
  • Whether the candidate-gene shortlist has any genes with established tissue-coherent expression in CF-relevant tissues (airway epithelium, intestinal epithelium, exocrine pancreas, beta cells).
  • Pre-registration scope: which endpoints, which gene sets, which MHC handling, which Bonferroni denominator — all locked before unblinding.
  • Whether the French and Canadian cohorts permit the kind of multi-cohort data-sharing that the Tanigawa design relies on; this is a governance check, not a statistical one, and it usually takes longer than the analysis itself.

ALEX Welcome back. Episode two of today's podcast. I'm Alex, joined as always by Jordan. We're reading Tanigawa, Wainberg, Karjalainen, and colleagues, with senior authorship from Mark Daly and Manuel Rivas, published in PLOS Genetics in May 2020. The title: "Rare protein-altering variants in ANGPTL7 lower intraocular pressure and protect against glaucoma." This is a multi-cohort biobank study and one of the cleanest worked examples of how rare-variant discovery at biobank scale can point straight at a drug target.

JORDAN Alex, before we dive in, can you slow down on a few of those words? "Intraocular pressure," "glaucoma," "rare protein-altering variant" — pretend I haven't met any of these.

ALEX Sure. Let me lay it out plainly. Glaucoma is a group of eye diseases that progressively damage the optic nerve at the back of the eye. Left untreated it leads to permanent vision loss. It's one of the leading causes of irreversible blindness worldwide. Intraocular pressure — abbreviated IOP — is exactly what it sounds like: the fluid pressure inside the eyeball. Healthy eyes maintain pressure in a fairly narrow range. Elevated IOP is the single biggest modifiable risk factor for glaucoma, and almost every approved glaucoma drug works by lowering IOP. So if you can find a gene where naturally occurring loss-of-function variants lower IOP without causing other problems, that gene becomes an extremely attractive drug-target hypothesis.

JORDAN And "rare protein-altering variant"?

ALEX Protein-altering means the DNA change actually changes the amino acid sequence of the protein the gene encodes — so missense substitutions, where one amino acid swaps for another, and protein-truncating variants, also called PTVs or pLoF — predicted loss-of-function variants — where the change makes the protein incomplete. Both are the kinds of changes most likely to have a functional consequence, as opposed to a silent or synonymous change that doesn't alter the protein. Rare in this paper means minor allele frequency, MAF, between 0.01% and 1%. So uncommon, but common enough that a half-million-person biobank will see them in thousands of carriers.

JORDAN One more — they're using two different biobanks. Why two, and what are they?

ALEX UK Biobank we've talked about extensively — five hundred thousand UK adults recruited 2006 to 2010, deeply phenotyped, with genotyping arrays and now exome and whole-genome sequencing. The second cohort is FinnGen. FinnGen is a Finnish population biobank, around two hundred thousand participants at the time of this analysis, with the special feature that the Finnish population is a genetic isolate. A few thousand founders settled Finland a few hundred generations ago, the population then expanded fast, and many variants that are vanishingly rare elsewhere in Europe — frequency less than 0.01% — exist at thousand-fold-higher frequencies in Finland. That's the "Finnish founder effect," and FinnGen has been mined for exactly this — variants that are too rare to study elsewhere but tractable in Finland.

JORDAN Got it. So why this paper today?

ALEX For a CFTR PheWAS, this paper is methodologically a near-perfect template. They're not running an exome-wide scan. They're doing what Runyu would do with five thousand CFF participants: a targeted, hypothesis-anchored search for rare protein-altering variants tied to a specific endpoint, in a cohort augmented by a second-cohort replication that uses founder-effect enrichment to find variants the main cohort can't power. The CFF Twin and Sibling Study and the Gene Modifier Study are not founder populations, but the conceptual move — anchor on one cohort, replicate or amplify in a smaller, demographically distinct second cohort — transfers cleanly.

JORDAN Alright — what was the problem they set out to solve? What was unknown before this?

ALEX Glaucoma genetics had two pillars before this paper. The first was Mendelian glaucoma — single high-penetrance variants in genes like MYOC, CYP1B1, OPTN that cause juvenile-onset disease. The second was common-variant GWAS for adult-onset glaucoma and for IOP, which by 2018 had identified more than sixty-eight independent loci but explained a modest fraction of glaucoma heritability and pointed at biology that was, in many cases, hard to drug. What was missing was a third pillar: rare protein-altering variants in genes that GWAS had hinted at but couldn't pin to a specific gene. The unknown was, are there rare loss-of-function variants where carriers have lower IOP and lower glaucoma risk — the kind of natural-experiment finding that would say "this gene is druggable in this direction"?

JORDAN And why does the direction of effect matter so much?

ALEX Because drug development is much easier in the inhibition direction. Designing a small molecule to block a protein's function is a well-understood problem; designing one to enhance a protein's function is much harder. If you find a gene where loss of function lowers disease risk, you have a clean inhibition target. If loss of function raises risk, you have an enhancement target, which is a much steeper hill. So the search for protective loss-of-function alleles is one of the most strategically valuable things you can do with biobank-scale data.

JORDAN Okay — what did they actually do? What were the methods?

ALEX Three analyses. Analysis one: in UK Biobank, three hundred thirty-seven thousand one hundred and fifty-one unrelated White British participants, eighty-two thousand two hundred fifty-three of whom had measured IOP. They tested forty-one thousand five hundred ninety rare protein-altering variants — MAF between 0.01% and 1%, on the UK Biobank genotyping array, outside the major histocompatibility complex region — against two IOP measurements, the Goldmann-correlated and the corneal-compensated, using a generalized linear model in PLINK with age, sex, and the first four genotype principal components as covariates. They also ran burden and dispersion tests over multiple variants per gene.

JORDAN Hold on, "burden test" and "dispersion test" — what are those?

ALEX A burden test collapses several rare variants in a gene into a single carrier indicator and asks if carrying any of them is associated with the trait. It pools statistical power across variants that the authors believe should all act in the same direction. A dispersion test — SKAT and its descendants — asks the more general question of whether the variant set is over-represented in cases without assuming a single direction of effect. Burden tests are most sensitive when all variants in the set push the same way; dispersion tests are more robust when some variants in the set go opposite directions.

JORDAN Got it. Continue.

ALEX Analysis two: in FinnGen, they discovered a Finnish-enriched missense variant in ANGPTL7, rs147660927, encoding the amino acid change arginine-220 to cysteine, at MAF 4.3% in Finland — more than fifty-fold the frequency in non-Finnish Europeans — and they tested it against six thousand five hundred thirty-seven glaucoma cases and one hundred seventy thousand three hundred sixty-two controls, again using logistic regression with age, sex, and principal components. Analysis three: back in UK Biobank, in the set of participants not used in analysis one, they replicated the glaucoma association for the original missense variant.

JORDAN What's one design choice you'd push back on, Alex?

ALEX The MHC exclusion. They drop the major-histocompatibility-complex region from analysis one because that region has unusual variant density and population structure that makes rare-variant tests behave badly. It's a defensible choice, but it does mean that if a real ANGPTL7-equivalent target exists inside MHC, this study can't find it. For Runyu's purposes, the principle to lift: pre-register both your full-genome and your MHC-stripped analyses, so you have a clear answer before you look at results.

JORDAN Okay — results. Did they answer the question?

ALEX Yes, with unusual clarity. In analysis one, exactly one variant passed Bonferroni correction across the forty-one thousand variants tested: rs28991009 in ANGPTL7, the p.Gln175His missense substitution, at MAF 0.81%. The effect on IOP was minus 0.53 millimeters of mercury for heterozygotes and minus 3.40 millimeters of mercury for homozygotes on corneal-compensated IOP, and minus 0.67 and minus 2.37 for the Goldmann-correlated measure. P-values: 5.96 times ten to the minus nine for one IOP measure, 1.07 times ten to the minus thirteen for the other. Then in a replication of glaucoma case-control status, the same variant gave an odds ratio of 0.66, a thirty-four percent risk reduction, P 0.0062. The burden test on ANGPTL7 pooling four protein-altering variants, including one protein-truncating variant, p-values around ten to the minus fourteen.

JORDAN And FinnGen?

ALEX The Finnish-enriched p.Arg220Cys variant, odds ratio 0.71 for all glaucoma — a twenty-nine percent risk reduction — P 1.9 times ten to the minus twelve. Crucially, the protective effect held up across glaucoma subtypes: exfoliation glaucoma, primary open-angle glaucoma, and primary angle-closure glaucoma. That cross-subtype consistency is unusual; most glaucoma genes are subtype-specific.

JORDAN So they delivered an allelic series, plus replication, plus mechanism?

ALEX Mechanism circumstantially. ANGPTL7 encodes Angiopoietin-related protein 7. It's highly expressed in the trabecular meshwork — the eye tissue that drains aqueous humor and is therefore the main IOP-regulating structure — and also in cornea and retina. Earlier in vitro work had shown ANGPTL7 overexpression in trabecular meshwork cells alters extracellular-matrix proteins like fibronectin, collagen, MYOC, MMP1. The Tanigawa paper doesn't add new wet-lab mechanism. What it adds is the human-genetic evidence that you want loss of ANGPTL7 function in the right direction for glaucoma.

ALEX Cleanly. Three converging lines of evidence — Bonferroni-significant single variant in UKBB, Finnish-enriched independent variant in FinnGen, independent replication in held-out UKBB — all in the same direction, on a tissue-coherent biology.

The Tanigawa template is the most transferable thing on the table — anchor in one cohort, augment via a smaller demographically distinct second cohort, pre-specify the variant frequency window and the gene-collapsing rule, report both single-variant and burden p-values. The CFF WGS cohort is the main cohort. The augmentation cohort that maps cleanly onto FinnGen's role is the French CF Gene Modifier Study and the Canadian CF Gene Modifier Study — separate national CF registries, demographically distinct, with some founder-mutation enrichment at the CFTR locus itself. JORDAN Beat seven — given that proposal, what does this paper close, what does it leave open, what does Runyu need to verify?

ALEX What it closes: the question of whether the three-analysis multi-cohort design is the right architecture for finding a rare protective variant. The answer is yes, this is the cleanest published worked example. It also closes the question of whether burden plus dispersion plus single-variant in parallel beats any one of them alone. Yes, parallel testing is the right move. What it leaves open: this is a healthy-population study, not a disease cohort. ANGPTL7 has tissue-specific expression in the eye, and that's why a population study could see it. CFTR-modifier biology mostly lives in tissues that are already CFTR-dysfunctional in CF patients, and that interaction is invisible in healthy population data. So a finding in CFF data doesn't have to look like ANGPTL7 — modifiers may be modest-effect, may not survive Bonferroni at the n equals five thousand level, and may need a registry-cohort-specific design.

What we'd need to check before committing: first, whether French and Canadian CF Gene Modifier cohorts have whole-genome or whole-exome sequencing available at a comparable variant-calling depth to CFF WGS, because if they're array-only the burden tests won't transfer cleanly. Second, what the CFRD case count is in each cohort separately, because the Tanigawa numbers — four thousand cases, two hundred fifty thousand controls — are far beyond what any CF cohort will offer. Power calculations need to be done first, not retrofitted. Third, whether there's a CFTR-modifier-specific reason to expect ANGPTL7-style biology — that is, a tissue or pathway where a CFTR-related gene has constitutive expression and where loss of function would shift a CF phenotype.
