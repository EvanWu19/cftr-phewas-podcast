Blurb

Gudmundsson and colleagues — the team that built and maintains the Genome Aggregation Database (gnomAD) — wrote a 2022 user manual in Human Mutation explaining how clinicians and researchers should use the gnomAD browser to interpret a candidate disease-causing DNA variant. The paper walks through the browser feature-by-feature: ancestry-stratified allele frequency, per-base sequencing coverage, the LOEUF and MIS-Z constraint scores, the variant co-occurrence tool for in-cis vs in-trans estimation, and how those features combine into a defensible variant call. The proposed direction the episode suggests, conditional on later inspection of the CFF whole-genome data, is to apply a gnomAD-anchored, version-pinned, coverage-aware, ancestry-stratified variant curation pipeline to every candidate damaging variant in any modifier-gene PheWAS before any burden test is run. The open question is which gnomAD release to anchor to and whether the CFGP variant calls have already been filtered upstream — both unanswerable without inspecting the consortium pipeline.

Plain-English glossary

  • ACMG categories — five-level clinical classification of a variant: benign, likely benign, variant of uncertain significance (VUS), likely pathogenic, pathogenic. From the American College of Medical Genetics standard.
  • Allele — one of the two copies of DNA at a position; humans inherit one from each parent.
  • Allele frequency — the fraction of chromosomes in a reference population that carry a given variant.
  • ClinVar — a public NIH-curated database of clinical interpretations of genetic variants; updates frequently, can disagree with itself across submitters.
  • Constraint — population-level signal that natural selection has been removing damaging variants from a gene; heavily constrained genes resist damage and are good Mendelian-disease candidates.
  • Genome Aggregation Database (gnomAD) — the dominant public population-variation database; v2 aggregates 141,456 sequenced humans, v4 is larger.
  • Loss-of-function (LoF) — variants that prevent the gene from making its protein, including stop-gain, frameshift, and certain splice-site disruptions.
  • LOEUF — loss-of-function observed-over-expected upper bound fraction. Lower = more constrained. Bottom decile (LOEUF < 0.35) marks genes that strongly resist functional loss.
  • MIS-Z — analogous constraint metric for missense variants.
  • Penetrance — probability that a person carrying a pathogenic variant develops the disease.
  • Pleiotropy — one gene or variant affecting more than one trait.
  • Population database — a reference dataset of DNA variants from many ostensibly healthy individuals, used to filter out common variants when looking for rare-disease causes.
  • Recessive — disease pattern where two damaged copies of a gene are needed to cause the condition; CFTR is recessive.
  • Variant — neutral modern term for a DNA position differing from reference; replaces "mutation."
  • Variant co-occurrence tool — gnomAD feature that estimates whether two variants in the same gene are on the same chromosome (in cis) or on opposite copies (in trans), based on co-occurrence in single individuals.
  • VUS (Variant of Uncertain Significance) — variant where evidence is insufficient for a benign or pathogenic call; reducing the VUS rate is a core field goal.

Methods to lift

  • gnomAD browser feature set — public web tool at gnomad.broadinstitute.org for variant lookup, with population-stratified allele frequency, per-base coverage, predicted molecular consequence, ClinVar overlay, and the variant co-occurrence cis/trans estimator.
  • Coverage-aware presence/absence call — never treat absence of a variant from gnomAD as proof of true absence without first checking the per-base coverage track for adequate read depth at the position.
  • LOEUF (loss-of-function observed-over-expected upper bound fraction) — gene-level constraint metric; bottom decile (LOEUF < 0.35) identifies haploinsufficient or essential genes.
  • MIS-Z — missense constraint metric, complementary to LOEUF for missense interpretation.
  • Variant co-occurrence tool — for recessive disease workups, estimates trans-vs-cis for two candidate variants in the same gene without family data.
  • Ancestry-stratified allele frequency — gnomAD reports frequency per genetic ancestry group, not just pooled, allowing exclusion of variants common in any single ancestry from rare-disease candidate lists.
  • gnomAD release version pinning — the published version of the database used for a curation should be recorded so reanalysis can be reproduced.

ALEX Yes, that is exactly the right instinct. Let me walk through every term we are about to lean on. A variant is a position in a person's DNA where their letter — A, C, G, or T — differs from a chosen reference sequence; "variant" is the modern, neutral word for what older papers called a mutation. Variant interpretation is the clinical and scientific process of deciding whether a particular variant is benign, likely benign, of uncertain significance, likely pathogenic, or pathogenic — those five categories are formal labels from the American College of Medical Genetics, called the ACMG categories, used to report genetic test results to patients. A population database is a large collection of DNA sequences from many ostensibly healthy people, used as a frequency reference: if a variant shows up in five percent of the general population, it almost certainly is not the cause of a rare childhood-onset disease. gnomAD, short for Genome Aggregation Database, is the leading public population database — version 2 of the database aggregated 141,456 sequenced humans, version 4 has now grown larger. Allele frequency is simply how often a variant occurs in a reference population, expressed as a fraction. Allele just means one of the two copies of DNA at a position — humans have one copy from each parent. Loss-of-function, abbreviated LoF, refers to variants that prevent a gene from making its protein at all, including stop-gain mutations, frameshifts, and certain splice-site disruptions. ClinVar is a separate public database curated by the National Institutes of Health that catalogs the clinical interpretation of variants — its contents are constantly updated and sometimes inconsistent. Penetrance is the probability that a person who carries a pathogenic variant actually develops the disease. Constraint, in this context, is a measurement of how strongly natural selection has been removing damaging variants from a gene at the population level — heavily constrained genes are intolerant of damage and are good candidates for harboring disease-causing variants when damage does occur. Recessive means a person needs two damaged copies of a gene to get sick; CFTR variants follow a recessive pattern. Pleiotropy means a single gene or variant affects more than one apparent trait. Variant of uncertain significance, abbreviated VUS, is exactly what it sounds like — a position in DNA where the lab does not have enough evidence to call it benign or pathogenic, and the patient and clinician are stuck. Reducing the VUS rate is a major goal of the field. Now ask the paper what it teaches.

JORDAN Okay. What problem is this paper actually solving?

ALEX The problem is the everyday workflow of the clinical geneticist or rare-disease researcher. You have a patient with an undiagnosed condition. You sequence the patient. You get back a list of thousands of variants. The vast majority are benign — every healthy person carries roughly four to five million variants relative to the reference genome, including dozens to hundreds of loss-of-function variants, because most of them are in non-essential genes or do not actually break the protein. The job of the clinical geneticist is to filter that list down to the one or two variants most likely to explain the patient's disease. Population databases like gnomAD are the central filter, because if a variant is common in the general healthy population, it cannot plausibly be the cause of a rare severe disease. Before gnomAD existed in its current form, this filter was unreliable — small or unrepresentative reference cohorts gave wrong answers, especially for ancestries other than European. The 2022 review walks through the modern gnomAD browser, the data it shows, and how to use that data to make the variant interpretation call defensibly.

JORDAN And the paper is from the team that built the database?

ALEX Correct. The first author is Sanna Gudmundsson, and the senior authors are Heidi Rehm and Daniel MacArthur and Anne O'Donnell-Luria — these are the people who run gnomAD, the consortium that aggregates data from many sequencing studies, and the Broad Institute group that maintains the public browser. So this is not an outside review of gnomAD, it is the manual from the source. That matters because some of the practical guidance — what to do when ClinVar disagrees with allele frequency, when to trust constraint scores, when to use the variant co-occurrence tool — only really works if you know the database structure, and the authors do.

JORDAN What does the paper actually walk through? What are the tools the gnomAD browser exposes?

ALEX The paper goes through the browser features one by one. The first is allele frequency, broken down by genetic ancestry group, which is the core variant filter — a variant common in any population is unlikely to be the cause of a severe rare disease in most situations. The second is per-base expression — the gnomAD browser shows you, for any position in any gene, the read coverage from sequencing, so you can tell whether the position was actually well-covered enough for the absence of variants to mean something. This addresses an important subtle point: if a variant is missing from gnomAD, that absence can either mean "truly not seen in 141,000 people" or "we did not look there well enough" — and only the coverage data tells you which. The third feature is constraint scores. Constraint quantifies how much evolutionary pressure the gene has been under. The headline metric is LOEUF, short for loss-of-function observed-over-expected upper bound fraction — a number between zero and roughly two, where lower means more constrained. A LOEUF below 0.35, the bottom decile, means the gene strongly resists losing protein function. There is a related metric for missense variants called MIS-Z. The fourth feature is the variant co-occurrence tool, which estimates whether two variants in the same gene are on the same chromosome copy — in cis — or on opposite copies — in trans — based on whether they ever appear together in single individuals in gnomAD. For recessive disease, you need variants in trans for the patient to be affected. The browser also surfaces ClinVar interpretations and predicted molecular consequence side by side. ALEX pushes back here on one design choice: gnomAD's "non-cancer" subset and the choice of which sub-cohorts to count toward "general population" frequency are pragmatic but somewhat arbitrary, and there are known cases where a variant that looks reasonably common in gnomAD overall is actually concentrated in a single contributing study where the inclusion criteria were not strictly healthy.

JORDAN That is a lot of moving parts. Did the paper offer concrete worked examples or just describe the buttons?

ALEX It does both. The most useful sections are the worked examples where the authors walk through what changed when a clinical lab moved from older population data to the current gnomAD release. They give cases where allele-frequency thresholds reclassified a previously-reported pathogenic variant as benign because the variant turned out to be common in an under-represented ancestry — this is the founder-effect failure mode, where a candidate-gene-era paper based on a small European cohort declared a variant pathogenic and the variant turned out to be common in South Asians. They give cases where high constraint, meaning a low LOEUF score, supports calling a novel loss-of-function variant pathogenic in a patient with disease consistent with that gene. They highlight the variant co-occurrence tool's role in assigning recessive trans status. The numbers I find most striking are the average per-genome counts: a typical individual carries roughly four to five million variants relative to the reference, between 50 and 100 protein-truncating variants of which only a handful are in highly constrained Mendelian disease genes, and approximately 20,000 missense variants. The paper does not produce new disease-gene discoveries — that is not its job. It does answer the question it posed: it clarifies how to use gnomAD correctly, and what its limits are, with concrete browser-feature-by-browser-feature guidance and worked examples. It also frankly admits where gnomAD is underpowered — particularly for non-European ancestries and for very rare variants where even 141,000 genomes may not be enough.
