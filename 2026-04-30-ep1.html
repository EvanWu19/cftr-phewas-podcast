How did this episode land for you?

Bycroft and colleagues describe the genotyping, centralized sample and variant quality control, ancestry analysis, phasing, imputation and HLA inference for the full UK Biobank cohort: 488,377 samples retained after QC out of roughly half a million recruited, genotyped on the UK BiLEVE Axiom and UK Biobank Axiom arrays at 805,426 shared markers (~95% content overlap), imputed against the Haplotype Reference Consortium and UK10K+1000 Genomes panels to roughly 96 million variants per person, with two-field HLA alleles for 11 classical loci and a published positive-control height GWAS reproducing GIANT 2014. Duplicate concordance was 99.87% across 588 experimental duplicate pairs; sex-chromosome aneuploidies were detected in 652 individuals (0.134%); allele frequencies cross-checked against ExAC at 91,298 overlapping markers gave r^2 = 0.93. The paper is the canonical template for population-biobank genetics QC.

Plain-English glossary

  • Allele — one of the possible DNA letters at a variant position (e.g., A or G).
  • Ancestry — the populations someone descends from; matters because allele frequencies differ between populations.
  • Array (genotyping array) — a chip that asks about a pre-selected list of variant positions and returns each person's alleles at those positions.
  • Biobank — a large research collection that links biological samples to long-term health records.
  • Cohort — the group of participants in a study.
  • DNA — deoxyribonucleic acid, the four-letter (A/C/G/T) molecule that stores genetic information.
  • Gene — a stretch of DNA that codes for something the body uses, usually a protein.
  • GWAS — genome-wide association study; fix a trait, scan all variants for association.
  • Imputation — statistically inferring un-measured variant alleles from measured ones plus a reference panel of fully sequenced people.
  • LD pruning — dropping variants that are statistically redundant with neighbors (linkage disequilibrium).
  • Locus — a position in the genome (plural: loci).
  • MAF (minor allele frequency) — the fraction of chromosomes in the population carrying the rarer allele at a position. Common ≳ 1%; rare < 1%.
  • Mixed model — a regression with both fixed effects (age, sex) and random effects (e.g., a kinship matrix) so related individuals do not inflate results.
  • PC (principal component) — a compressed numeric summary of someone's ancestry derived from many variants.
  • Phasing — working out which alleles sit on which of the two parental chromosomes.
  • PheWAS — phenome-wide association study; fix a variant or gene, scan all phenotypes.
  • QC (quality control) — pre-analysis steps that flag or remove bad samples, bad variants, and processing artifacts.
  • Relatedness — how genetically close two people are; siblings ~50%, first cousins ~12.5%.
  • Variant — a position in DNA where individuals differ.
  • WGS (whole-genome sequencing) — reading essentially every letter of the genome, finding variants without a pre-selected list.

Methods to lift

  • KING — kinship-inference tool from the Chen / Abecasis group; estimates pairwise relatedness in a way robust to population structure. Use to identify related pairs prior to ancestry PCA and association testing.
  • fastPCA (EIGENSOFT v6.0.1) — scalable principal-components implementation from Alkes Price's lab; runs on hundreds of thousands of samples without full eigendecomposition. Compute PCs on an LD-pruned, unrelated subset, then project relatives onto those PCs.
  • BOLT-LMM — linear mixed-model association tool from Po-Ru Loh and Alkes Price; absorbs cryptic relatedness and population structure into a random-effect kinship term, scales to biobank N.
  • Per-batch marker QC across genotyping batches — distribution-of-batch-failures check; common-MAF markers pass nearly everywhere, only ~84% of MAF<1% markers pass in all batches. Adapt to per-sequencing-batch concordance for WGS calls.
  • Sample QC: missing rate + ancestry-residualized heterozygosity — flagged 968 samples (0.2%) in UKBB; the residualization step is what protects admixed participants from being thrown out for ancestry rather than data quality.
  • Sex-chromosome karyotype check via X/Y probe intensity — recovered 652 (0.134%) putative aneuploidies plus 378 self-report mismatches as a separate category; flag rather than drop.
  • Duplicate concordance + external allele-frequency cross-check (ExAC in UKBB; GnomAD as the modern analog) — 588 duplicate pairs at 99.87% mean concordance; 91,298 overlap markers at r^2 = 0.93 against ExAC.
  • SHAPEIT3 + IMPUTE4 against HRC and UK10K+1KG panels — phasing and imputation stack producing ~96M variants at 0.229% median switch error; historical baseline for the array era.
  • **HLA*IMP:02 for 11 classical HLA genes at two-field resolution** — validated by replicating known MS associations (HLA-DRB1*15:01, OR 3.16, p = 2.3e-85). Modern WGS analogs are HLA-LA and T1K; the positive-control logic is identical.
  • BGEN v1.2 + qctool v2 + SNPTEST — file format and tooling shipped alongside the data release.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: adapt the Bycroft 2018 sample-level quality-control pipeline — sex-chromosome aneuploidy detection, experimental-duplicate identification, KING-based relatedness inference, and fastPCA ancestry projection on an LD-pruned unrelated subset with relatives projected on after the fact — to the CFF cohort prior to any phenome-wide CFTR analysis, with mixed-model association testing carried forward to the analysis stage to absorb the elevated relatedness expected from the NCT00676169 twin-sibling sub-study and multiplex-family enrollment.

What to verify first

  • Variant call format — what file format the CFF WGS data is actually released in (joint-called multi-sample VCF, individual gVCFs, Hail matrix table, something else); this determines which tools in the Bycroft stack are usable and which must be substituted.
  • Pre-computed sample annotations — whether the CFF release ships sample-level relatedness, ancestry, and sex-aneuploidy annotations, or whether those need to be derived from scratch.
  • Site and batch composition — what fraction of samples come from each recruitment site and each sequencing batch / assay generation, since that determines whether per-batch QC at Bycroft's granularity is feasible and where multi-site batch effects might dominate.

ALEX Welcome in. Today we are reading one paper, end to end — Bycroft, Freeman, Petkova and colleagues, published in 2018 in Nature, volume 562, pages 203 to 209, titled "The UK Biobank resource with deep phenotyping and genomic data." This is the paper that defined what a modern population biobank looks like in genetics. Almost every methods reviewer in our field has read it, or has at least pretended to. We are reading it because the quality-control playbook it spells out is the one our CFTR PheWAS will eventually need to mirror, and because before we go anywhere near the CFF whole-genome data we should know what good looks like.

JORDAN Before we go in, what do I need to know? Pretend I have never opened a genetics paper. What are the words.

ALEX Fair, and important. Start with DNA — deoxyribonucleic acid, the molecule that stores your genetic information as a long sequence of four chemical letters, A, C, G and T. Three billion of those letters per copy, and you have two copies, one from each parent. A gene is a stretch of that sequence that codes for something the body uses, usually a protein. CFTR is a gene; cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator is what those letters spell out.

JORDAN And a variant?

ALEX A variant is a place in the DNA where people differ from each other. If at one position most people have an A and some people have a G, that position is a variant. The two possible letters at that spot are called alleles — so A is one allele, G is the other. The position itself is a locus, plural loci, just Latin for "place." Minor allele frequency, abbreviated MAF, is the fraction of chromosomes in the population that carry the rarer of the two alleles. If 5 percent of chromosomes carry G and 95 percent carry A, the MAF is 5 percent. Common variants are roughly above 1 percent MAF; rare variants are below.

JORDAN How do you actually measure variants in half a million people?

ALEX Two technologies. A genotyping array, which is the older and cheaper one, is a chip that asks about a pre-selected list of variant positions — say 800,000 of them — and tells you which alleles each person carries at each of those positions. Whole-genome sequencing reads essentially every letter of the genome and finds variants without a pre-selected list. UK Biobank in this paper used arrays. CFF has whole-genome sequencing. Imputation is the trick that bridges them — given the array calls and a reference panel of fully sequenced people, statistical software guesses, with a confidence score, what alleles each person probably has at the positions the array did not measure. UKBB imputed up from 800,000 measured positions to about 96 million.

JORDAN GWAS. PheWAS.

ALEX GWAS is a genome-wide association study — you fix one trait, say height or diabetes, and ask which variants across the genome are statistically associated with it. PheWAS is phenome-wide — you fix one variant or one gene, like CFTR, and ask which traits across an entire medical record are associated with it. We are doing the second one. A biobank is just a large research collection that links biological samples, like blood for DNA, to long-term health records, often hundreds of thousands of people. A cohort is the group of participants in a study. QC is quality control — the steps you take to throw out or flag bad samples, bad variants, and processing artifacts before you do real analysis. Relatedness is how genetically close two people are — siblings share roughly half their DNA, first cousins about an eighth. Ancestry is the populations someone descends from, which matters because allele frequencies differ between populations. Principal components, abbreviated PCs, are a math trick that takes a giant table of variants by people and compresses each person down to a handful of numbers that summarize their ancestry — the first PC often separates African from European from East Asian, for instance. A mixed model is a statistical regression that lets you include both fixed effects, like age and sex, and random effects, like a kinship matrix that says who is related to whom, so that related individuals do not falsely inflate your association results.

JORDAN That is what I needed. Now — why did UK Biobank exist in the first place? What problem was it solving?

ALEX Until the early 2000s, human genetics ran on small, carefully selected case-control studies. You would collect a few thousand people with disease X, a few thousand controls, and look for variants that differed between them. That works for strong, common signals, but it has three problems. First, sample size — most disease-associated variants have small effects, and to detect a small effect you need huge numbers. Second, phenotype depth — a typical case-control study only measures whether you have the disease, not your blood pressure, cholesterol, lung function, hospital admissions, prescriptions and labs across thirty years of follow-up. Third, generalizability — a study that recruited at one hospital cannot tell you about the broader population.

JORDAN So UKBB tried to fix all three at once.

ALEX Right. The pitch, going back to the original protocol around 2006, was a single prospective cohort of 500,000 UK adults aged 40 to 69 at recruitment, consented for open-ended research, with deep baseline phenotyping — a full lifestyle questionnaire, physical measurements, blood and urine assays — plus electronic linkage to the National Health Service for hospital episodes, cancer registry, death registry, and primary care records. Then later, on top of that backbone, you bolt on imaging, accelerometry, and genetics. The 2018 Bycroft paper is the deliverable for the genetics layer — they took blood from all 500,000, genotyped them on arrays, imputed up, applied a unified quality-control pipeline, and released the cleaned data to approved researchers worldwide.

JORDAN And the consequence is that any researcher with a question about a common disease and a candidate variant can now answer it in one cohort, with a uniform analysis, instead of meta-analyzing twenty small cohorts with different protocols. That is the bit that changed the field.

ALEX It also created a template. After UKBB, every other large biobank — All of Us in the United States, FinnGen in Finland, the Estonian Biobank, Biobank Japan, the Million Veteran Program through the Veterans Health Administration — copied the basic shape: large prospective cohort, broad consent, deep phenotype, electronic health record linkage, central genetics. The problem UKBB solved is the one we want CFF to solve at much smaller scale for cystic fibrosis specifically — though our patient registry has been collecting longitudinally since the 1960s, so the phenotype depth is, in some respects, deeper than UKBB's.

JORDAN One more thing on motivation. Why arrays first and not sequencing?

ALEX Cost and timing. In 2014, when the UKBB genotyping kicked off, whole-genome sequencing at population scale was still prohibitive — call it five hundred to a thousand US dollars per sample at research-grade depth, times 500,000 people. Arrays at that time were closer to fifty dollars per sample. Imputation off the array calls let them cover most common variation at array prices, defer the sequencing decision, and ship a usable resource years before WGS-at-scale was realistic. The trade-off is rare variants — anything below 1 percent MAF imputes poorly off arrays, and that is exactly the band where most CFTR-relevant biology lives. Halldorsson 2022, which we read tomorrow, is what fixed that trade-off.

JORDAN Methods. Walk me through the genotyping pipeline.

ALEX Two arrays. About 49,950 participants — the UK BiLEVE subset, where BiLEVE stands for Biobank Lung Exome Variant Evaluation, an early lung-function-focused sub-study — were genotyped on the UK BiLEVE Axiom array, which has 807,411 markers. The remaining 438,427 participants were genotyped on the UK Biobank Axiom array, with 825,927 markers. The two arrays share roughly 95 percent of their content, so you can analyze them jointly, but the 5 percent that is array-specific has to be tracked as a covariate to avoid confounding any disease that correlates with being in the BiLEVE sub-study, like smoking-related phenotypes.

JORDAN Sample QC.

ALEX Two main metrics. Per-sample missing rate — what fraction of the array calls failed for that person — and per-sample heterozygosity, adjusted for ancestry. Heterozygosity is the fraction of variant positions where someone carries two different alleles; it varies systematically with ancestry, so you have to residualize on principal components before flagging outliers, otherwise you preferentially throw out admixed individuals for the wrong reason. They flagged 968 samples, about 0.2 percent, as outliers on these metrics. They ran a sex check by comparing the relative intensity of probes on the X and Y chromosomes to self-reported sex and recovered 652 individuals — 0.134 percent — with putative sex chromosome aneuploidies like XXY or XYY, which they kept in the file with a flag rather than dropping.

JORDAN Relatedness and ancestry.

ALEX For relatedness they used KING, the kinship-inference tool from Wei-Min Chen and Gonçalo Abecasis's group, which estimates how related any two individuals are in a way that is robust to population structure. For ancestry they used fastPCA from EIGENSOFT version 6.0.1, the package out of Alkes Price's lab, which is a scalable principal components implementation that does not require a full eigendecomposition and so can handle hundreds of thousands of samples. The trick is to run PCA on an LD-pruned, unrelated subset first — LD pruning means dropping variants that are statistically redundant with their neighbors — and then project the related individuals onto those PCs after the fact, so the related cluster does not pull the axes toward themselves.

JORDAN Imputation.

ALEX They prephased — phasing means working out which alleles sit on which of your two parental chromosomes — using SHAPEIT3, then imputed with IMPUTE4 against the Haplotype Reference Consortium panel of 64,976 broadly European haplotypes, and additionally against the merged UK10K plus 1000 Genomes panel for better non-European and rare variant coverage. The output is roughly 96 million imputed variants per person. They also imputed two-field resolution alleles for 11 classical HLA genes — A, B, C, DRB1, DRB3, DRB4, DRB5, DQA1, DQB1, DPA1 and DPB1 — using HLA*IMP:02. For association testing in their height positive control they used BOLT-LMM, a linear mixed-model association tool from Po-Ru Loh and Alkes Price that scales to UKBB-size cohorts while absorbing relatedness and structure into a random-effect kinship term.

JORDAN Results — did the resource work?

ALEX Yes, and the numbers are the headline. After the full QC sweep they retained 488,377 samples genotyped at 805,426 markers passing all checks, and imputed up to approximately 96 million variants per person. Duplicate concordance — they had 588 pairs of experimental duplicates run independently through the genotyping pipeline — came in at 99.87 percent mean concordance, with the worst pair still at 99.39 percent, which is the headline number meaning "the array works as advertised." They cross-checked allele frequencies against ExAC, the Exome Aggregation Consortium reference, at 91,298 overlapping markers in European-ancestry participants and got an r-squared of 0.93, with about 300 markers showing wildly discordant frequencies that they flagged as probable probe failures, annotation errors, or mapping errors in repetitive regions of the genome. The sex-aneuploidy rate of 0.134 percent matches population estimates from older karyotyping studies, which is a sanity check that the assay reflects real biology rather than artifact. As a positive-control GWAS they ran height on chromosome 2 and reproduced the GIANT 2014 consortium's signals with a regression slope of 1.369 and r-squared of 0.965 — the slope above one is the interesting bit, because it means UKBB single-cohort power per locus actually exceeds the older twenty-cohort GIANT meta-analysis, partly because of cohort homogeneity, partly because the raw N is large enough on its own. So the answer to "did they build a usable resource" is unambiguously yes, and the paper has since been cited tens of thousands of times as the methodological template for this style of biobank. The released data — array calls, imputed dosages, HLA calls, principal components, kinship pairs — went out to approved researchers worldwide through the UKBB data portal, and within two years it was the engine behind hundreds of GWAS publications on traits ranging from body mass index to depression to atrial fibrillation.

JORDAN Now, what does this paper imply for our CFTR PheWAS — in plain English first?

ALEX In plain English: before we run any genetic analysis on the CFF whole-genome data, we would need to do the same QC sweep Bycroft describes — check for sex chromosome aneuploidies, find duplicate samples, infer who is related to whom, and project everyone into ancestry space using principal components — adapted from arrays to whole-genome calls. Then, when we eventually do association testing for CFTR variation against phenotypes from the registry, we would use a mixed-model approach so that related participants and ancestry differences do not inflate our results. More formally — conditional on inspection of the CFF whole-genome variant calls and registry phenotype linkage, a candidate direction is to adapt the Bycroft 2018 sample-level quality-control pipeline, including sex aneuploidy detection, duplicate identification, KING-based relatedness inference, and fastPCA ancestry projection, to the CFF cohort prior to any phenome-wide CFTR analysis.

JORDAN And the gap, given that direction.

ALEX Conditional on that "reproduce UKBB-style QC on CFF WGS" framing, Bycroft closes the methodology question — the per-batch QC distribution checks, the missing-rate plus residualized-heterozygosity sample filter, the karyotype-aware sex check, the KING-then-projected-PCA workflow, and the mixed-model association test are all spelled out in this paper and the supplement. The playbook is published. What this paper does not close is how that playbook adapts to two CFF-specific structural features. First, the CFF cohort almost certainly has a higher related-pair fraction than UKBB's 30.3 percent, because the registry includes participants recruited under the twin-sibling sub-study NCT00676169 and because multiplex CF families enroll together; the playbook assumes you can drop or down-weight relatives to a manageable fraction, and at higher relatedness you may need to rethink that. Second, multi-site, multi-year sequencing means batch effects across sequencing centers and assay generations, where Bycroft's batches were within one sequencing operation. What we would need to check, before committing to any of this, is, one, what variant call format the CFF data is actually delivered in — joint-called VCF, individual gVCFs, project-level Hail matrix table — because that determines which tools are even available; two, whether sample relatedness and ancestry annotations are pre-computed in the CFF release or whether we would need to derive them ourselves from scratch; and three, what the sample distribution looks like across recruitment sites and sequencing batches, because that determines whether per-batch QC is even feasible at the granularity Bycroft used.

JORDAN Tomorrow.

ALEX Tomorrow we read Halldorsson and colleagues 2022, the UKBB whole-genome sequencing rollout — the WGS sequel to this paper, where the same cohort gets re-sequenced and the rare-variant story actually starts. One-sentence takeaway for today: Bycroft 2018 is the published quality-control template for population-scale human genetics, and any defensible analysis of CFF whole-genome data starts by adapting it.
