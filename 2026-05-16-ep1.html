Blurb

Guindo-Martínez et al. 2021 (Nature Communications) describes GUIDANCE, an open-source pipeline that combines four reference panels for imputation, includes the X chromosome, and tests five inheritance models. Applied to the 62,281-person GERA cohort across 22 age-related diseases, it finds 94 genome-wide-significant loci — 26 of them previously unreported — and demonstrates that 13 of the new loci would have been missed under the standard single-panel, additive-only design. Three recessive associations stand out: rs77704739 near PELO with type 2 diabetes (OR-rec 4.32), rs201654520 in CACNB4 with cardiovascular disease (OR-rec 19.02), and rs557998486 near THUMPD2 with macular degeneration (OR-rec 10.5). The episode suggests one possible direction for the CFTR PheWAS: a non-additive, multi-panel, X-inclusive re-analysis of CF Genome Project WGS data against established CF modifier endpoints, conditional on a power audit for the smaller cohort.

Plain-English glossary

  • Additive model — risk goes up linearly with the number of risk-allele copies (0, 1, 2 copies → 0, 1, 2 units of risk).
  • CFGP — CF Genome Project, the WGS resource on roughly 5,000 CF patients.
  • Dominant model — one or two copies of the risk allele give the same effect.
  • GERA — Kaiser Permanente's Genetic Epidemiology Research on Adult Health and Aging, a 62,281-person northern-California cohort.
  • Genotypic test — full three-genotype model, no inheritance pattern assumed.
  • GWAS — Genome-Wide Association Study; tests one variant at a time against a single phenotype across the genome.
  • Haplotype — a stretch of DNA letters inherited together.
  • Heterodominant — heterozygotes look different from both homozygotes.
  • HRC — Haplotype Reference Consortium, the standard European-anchored imputation reference panel.
  • Imputation — statistical filling-in of un-genotyped variants using a reference panel.
  • PheWAS — Phenome-Wide Association Study; tests one variant against many phenotypes.
  • Recessive model — risk shows up only in homozygotes for the risk allele.
  • Reference panel (1000G, UK10K, GoNL, HRC) — fully-sequenced reference cohort whose haplotypes are used for imputation.
  • SNP — single-nucleotide polymorphism, a one-letter DNA variant.

Methods to lift

  • GUIDANCE pipeline (open source at cg.bsc.es/guidance) — orchestrates imputation across multiple reference panels and association testing across multiple inheritance models on the COMPSs distributed-computing framework.
  • IMPUTE2 — reference-panel-based genotype imputation tool used to fill in un-genotyped variants from HRC, 1000 Genomes Phase 3, UK10K, and GoNL.
  • SNPTEST — Oxford-developed variant-association test program supporting all five inheritance models.
  • METAL — meta-analysis tool used to combine association results across reference panels and external cohorts.
  • Per-variant best-panel selection — for each variant, pick the reference panel with the highest allelic dosage R² (imputation quality score) and carry that panel's call forward to association testing.
  • Five-model inheritance scan — test every variant under additive, dominant, recessive, heterodominant, and full genotypic models, then report the lowest-p-value model alongside the additive baseline.
  • X-chromosome inclusion — testing chromosome X under appropriate male-versus-female coding rather than dropping it from the analysis.
  • Two-cohort replication architecture — UK Biobank (younger, larger, looser phenotype definitions) plus FinnGen (older, smaller, richer phenotype ICD coding) used as complementary replication substrates.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage:

Re-analyze the CF Genome Project whole-genome sequencing substrate against the established CF modifier endpoints — CFRD, meconium ileus, FEV1 trajectory, BMI z-score, chronic Pseudomonas acquisition, CF liver disease — under the full five-inheritance-model panel (additive, dominant, recessive, heterodominant, genotypic), with the X chromosome included rather than excluded, using the GUIDANCE pipeline architecture as a published template, and reporting per-variant lowest-p-value-model alongside the additive baseline so the marginal contribution of non-additive testing is auditable.

What to verify first

  • Recessive-model power floor on a ~5,000-person CFGP substrate for variants at common (>5%), low-frequency (1–5%), and rare (<1%) MAF tiers, computed against the actual CFRD and MI case counts.
  • Whether multi-reference-panel imputation logic transfers to WGS (which directly observes variants rather than imputing them), or whether the analogous gain comes from joint variant calling against an external WGS reference such as TOPMed.
  • Coverage uniformity on chromosome X under the CFGP variant-calling pipeline in males versus females; X-call quality determines whether X-inclusion is methodologically defensible on this substrate.
  • CFGP ancestry composition versus the four-panel design — GERA was European-only; if CFGP includes meaningful non-European representation, an ancestry-stratified or trans-ethnic analytic stance is needed up front.
  • Phenotype harmonization between CFGP/CFFPR endpoints and what GUIDANCE assumes (age-related, ICD-coded, hospital-record-anchored) — CF endpoints are curated and date-resolved, not ICD-anchored.

Why we're reading it

Almost every CF modifier study uses the additive model and excludes chromosome X. Guindo-Martínez is the cleanest published case that this default leaves a large fraction of the genetic architecture undetectable, with effect sizes (OR 4 to 19 under recessive) that no plausible additive sample size could recover.

ALEX Welcome back. Today's first episode is Guindo-Martínez, Amela, Bonàs-Guarch and colleagues, 2021, in Nature Communications — "The impact of non-additive genetic associations on age-related complex diseases." Senior authors are Josep Mercader at the Broad and David Torrents at Barcelona Supercomputing Center, and the paper introduces a pipeline they call GUIDANCE. We're reading it because almost every modifier study you've heard us cover on this podcast — the SLC26A9 results, the EHF results, the MUC4/MUC20 results — has tested one inheritance model and excluded one chromosome, and this paper is the cleanest single defense of why doing the other thing matters.

JORDAN Hold up before we go any further. I want to clear out every term in that cold open. "GWAS," "inheritance model," "non-additive," "imputation," "reference panel," "X chromosome exclusion" — assume I have heard none of these.

ALEX Good. Let's lay it down in plain English. A GWAS, a genome-wide association study, is what you do when you genotype thousands of cases and controls and then march one variant at a time across the genome asking, "is this letter at this position more common in the people who have the disease?" The variant is called a SNP — a single-nucleotide polymorphism, a one-letter swap in the DNA. Most modern GWAS use SNP arrays plus something called imputation. Imputation means you only directly genotype a few hundred thousand or a million sites, and then you statistically fill in the other tens of millions of sites by leaning on a reference panel — a separate set of fully-sequenced people whose DNA tells you which letters travel together in stretches called haplotypes. The reference panel for most European studies has been HRC, the Haplotype Reference Consortium, which is the standard biomedical fill-in atlas. There are others — 1000 Genomes Phase 3, UK10K, and the Dutch GoNL panel. The "inheritance model" is the math you use to test the variant. The default model — the one almost everyone uses — is the additive model. It says risk goes up linearly with the number of risk-letter copies. Zero, one, two copies of the risk letter give you zero, one, two units of risk. The non-additive models are dominant, where one or two copies give you the same risk; recessive, where you only see the effect if both copies are the risk letter; and heterodominant, where heterozygotes — the people with one of each — actually look different from both homozygotes. And the X chromosome exclusion is just what it sounds like: most GWAS papers literally drop chromosome X from the analysis because it has tricky biology in females versus males and is annoying to handle. PheWAS is phenome-wide association study, the inverse of GWAS — one variant tested against many diseases.

JORDAN So this paper attacks all three at once.

ALEX Right. The motivating problem is missing heritability. Twin and family studies have always estimated, say, that 50 to 80 percent of the variation in a complex trait like type 2 diabetes or hypertension or macular degeneration is heritable. But the lead variants from GWAS, even now, explain a much smaller fraction of that variance than the heritability number says exists. People have argued about where the rest is hiding for over fifteen years. One candidate hiding place is rare variants that you couldn't see on old arrays. A second is variants with non-additive effects, especially recessive ones, where the effect only shows up in the homozygotes. If a recessive risk allele has a frequency of one percent, then only one in ten thousand people in your cohort is a homozygote — far too few to detect under the additive model, which dilutes the signal by counting heterozygotes as half. Imputation reference panels matter here because different panels are biased toward different populations and different variant types. HRC is dominated by SNPs and undercounts insertions and deletions. UK10K and 1000 Genomes carry more indels. None of them alone gives you the full spectrum of testable variants. The third candidate, the X chromosome, is just a chunk of the genome that GWAS has chronically left on the floor. So the question this paper poses is: if you build a pipeline that imputes from four reference panels jointly, includes the X chromosome, and tests five inheritance models — additive, dominant, recessive, heterodominant, and a full genotypic test — how much more do you find?

JORDAN And the cohort they applied it to is GERA, right? Tell me what that is.

ALEX GERA stands for the Kaiser Permanente Research Program on Genes, Environment and Health: A Genetic Epidemiology Research on Adult Health and Aging. It's a 62,281-person European-ancestry cohort from northern California, average age 63, which is the right age window for the diseases they care about. They focused on 22 age-related diseases — type 2 diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, asthma, cancers, hernias, macular degeneration, psychiatric conditions, and so on. After quality control they kept 56,637 individuals. They imputed those people independently using four reference panels — HRC, 1000 Genomes Phase 3, UK10K, and GoNL — and then combined the results by picking, for each variant, the panel with the best imputation quality score. They tested association under all five inheritance models. The tools they leaned on are SNPTEST, the standard variant-test program from Oxford; IMPUTE2 for the imputation; METAL for combining results across panels and cohorts; and PLINK for downstream variant handling. The whole thing runs on the COMPSs distributed-computing framework, which is what makes the all-reference-panels-all-models design computationally feasible. They report this entire pipeline as open-source software at cg.bsc.es/guidance.

JORDAN Push back on something for me.

ALEX My pushback is on the replication strategy. They used UK Biobank — half a million participants — and FinnGen, a 218,000-person Finnish biobank, to try to replicate their new hits. But the GERA phenotypes and the UK Biobank phenotypes are not defined the same way, and the average UK Biobank participant is younger than the average GERA participant. That means a recessive variant for an age-of-onset-dependent disease might genuinely exist in UK Biobank but not yet have shown up as cases. They acknowledge this — and they hedge by also pulling in FinnGen for cardiovascular disease specifically, because FinnGen has the older mean age. But the upshot is that their replication numbers are weaker than the original discovery numbers, which is awkward for a paper whose pitch is "we found things you would have missed."

JORDAN Okay. So what did they actually find?

ALEX 94 genome-wide significant loci across the 22 diseases, 26 of which they call "previously unreported." Of the 94, 27 — about a third — were only found by one reference panel; in particular HRC alone would have missed about 28 percent of the loci. 37 of the 94 had their strongest signal under a non-additive model, and 13 of the 26 new loci would not have been found at all under the additive model. The headline new recessive associations are three: rs77704739, a low-frequency SNP near PELO, associates with type 2 diabetes at a recessive odds ratio of 4.32, p=1.75×10⁻⁸. Under the additive model the same variant has an odds ratio of 1.15 and a p-value of 2.8×10⁻³ — totally invisible at genome-wide significance. The PELO signal replicated in UK Biobank for type 2 diabetes and for metformin use under the recessive model. Second, rs201654520 in CACNB4 associates with cardiovascular disease at a recessive odds ratio of 19.02, p=4.32×10⁻⁸; the additive estimate at the same variant is an odds ratio of 1.10 and a non-significant p-value of 0.11. Third, rs557998486 near THUMPD2 associates with age-related macular degeneration at a recessive odds ratio of 10.5, p=2.75×10⁻⁸; additively it's a p-value of 0.628. They calculate that to detect the THUMPD2 association under the additive model you would need an effective population sample of roughly 6.5 million people, versus about 476,000 under the recessive model. So yes — they answered their stated question. Non-additive modeling and multi-panel imputation are not just methodological hygiene; they pull out genuinely undetectable biology.

JORDAN Now — what's one specific concrete research question that this episode suggests for the CFTR PheWAS?

ALEX Plain English first. Inside a cystic fibrosis cohort, the canonical modifier signal hunts have all assumed additive effects. The Aksit, Blackman, Stonebraker, Faino papers we've covered — every one of them runs the standard additive test. If there are modifier variants where the heterozygote looks like the wild-type and only homozygotes carry the burden, those have been silently missed in fifteen years of CF modifier discovery. The Guindo-Martínez paper suggests one concrete extension. Take the CF Genome Project whole-genome sequencing data and re-run the canonical CF modifier endpoints — CFRD, meconium ileus, lung function trajectory, BMI z-score, chronic Pseudomonas acquisition, liver disease — under the full five-inheritance-model panel and a multi-panel imputation strategy, with the X chromosome included rather than dropped. Formally: one possible direction this episode suggests is a non-additive, multi-reference-panel, X-chromosome-inclusive re-analysis of the CF Genome Project WGS substrate against the established CF modifier endpoints, using the GUIDANCE pipeline architecture as a published template.

JORDAN Given that proposal, what does Guindo-Martínez close and what does it leave open?

ALEX It closes the credibility question. After this paper you cannot dismiss a non-additive scan as a fishing expedition — they demonstrated that under the recessive model you find variants with odds ratios of 4, 10, 19, that the additive test cannot see at any plausible sample size. It also closes the methodological-feasibility question by releasing the GUIDANCE pipeline as open source. What it leaves open, and what you'd need to verify before committing, is several things. First, the CF Genome Project cohort is around 5,000 individuals, while GERA is roughly 56,000. A recessive variant at one percent allele frequency yields one in ten thousand homozygotes — meaning the CFGP substrate may have zero or one or two homozygotes for a given low-frequency variant. We'd need to compute the recessive-model power floor before scaling expectations. Second, the CFGP cohort already has whole-genome sequencing rather than array-plus-imputation, which changes the multi-reference-panel argument — sequencing directly observes the variants instead of imputing them, so the four-panel combination logic doesn't transfer one-to-one. We'd need to check whether the equivalent gain comes from joint variant calling across CFGP plus an external reference, or from something else entirely. Third, the GERA pipeline excluded individuals failing standard QC and tested for European-ancestry stratification; we'd need to confirm the CFGP cohort's ancestry composition still supports the same QC posture without producing a non-European-ancestry blind spot. Fourth, the X chromosome inclusion has its own quality-control story under whole-genome sequencing in males versus females — we'd need to check whether the CFGP variant-call pipeline already handles X coverage uniformly. None of these are deal-breakers, but each is a "what we'd need to check" item before claiming this design is viable.
