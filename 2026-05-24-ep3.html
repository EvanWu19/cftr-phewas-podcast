Blurb

Kumar, Becker, Jansen, van Barneveld, Boztug, Wölfl, Tümmler, and Stanke (Genes & Immunity 2008) tested whether common variants in the FAS gene modulate cystic fibrosis severity. In 16 F508del/F508del patients with intestinal epithelial biopsies, FAS intron 2 SNP rs7901656 correlated with FAS expression (probeset 204781_s_at) under a dominant model at p=0.0009. In 37 nuclear families from the European CF Twin and Sibling Study, the rs2296603–rs7901656–rs1571019 haplotype reached p=0.0246 for differential frequency between severe and mild families. Comparative phylogenetic analysis revealed a conserved non-coding sequence around rs7901656; in silico binding-site prediction implicated four transcription factors, with the c-Rel binding site disrupted by the rs7901656 allele. The proposed direction this episode suggests is testing the same haplotype in the CFF WGS cohort as a candidate-region replication. The open question is whether the original finding has been independently replicated since 2008, whether intestinal epithelium is the right tissue context for CF lung severity, and whether the haplotype has already been tested in any of the 60+ existing CFTR-WGS papers (saturation risk).

Methods to lift

  • eQTL design on patient biopsies — pair genotypes at candidate SNPs with Affymetrix or RNA-seq expression on the same patients to localize regulatory effects.
  • Extreme-phenotype family case-control design — sample sibling pairs at severity tails of the cohort to amplify modifier signal.
  • Conserved non-coding element prioritization — phylogenetic conservation as a prior on functional importance of intronic SNPs.
  • In silico transcription-factor binding prediction — sequence-based scanning tools to nominate which TF is disrupted by a candidate regulatory SNP.
  • Haplotype-based association test — three-SNP haplotype frequency comparison between severity-stratified groups when single SNPs are individually under-powered.
  • Tissue-context flag — explicitly justify why expression was measured in the chosen tissue (intestinal epithelium here, because it was accessible — not because intestine drives CF lung disease).

Closes

ALEX Final episode of the day, Runyu — May 24th, 2026, episode three of three. Today's paper is "Expression levels of FAS are regulated through an evolutionary conserved element in intron 2, which modulates cystic fibrosis disease severity," first author Vinod Kumar, with senior authors Burkhard Tümmler and Frauke Stanke from Hannover Medical School, published in Genes and Immunity in December 2008. It is not open access, so we worked from the published abstract and the Europe PubMed Central metadata rather than the full text — we have to flag that on air. The paper is a candidate-gene modifier study, which means the authors pre-specified a gene they suspected — FAS — and went looking for variants inside it that correlate with how severe cystic fibrosis is.

JORDAN First things first. What is FAS, what is intron 2, what is rs7901656, and why is any of this in a cystic fibrosis paper? Because none of those words are familiar.

ALEX Let me lay down the vocabulary. FAS — the all-caps gene name — is short for Fas cell surface death receptor, also known as CD95. It is a protein on the surface of cells that, when bound by its partner Fas ligand, triggers a cellular suicide program called apoptosis. Apoptosis is controlled cell death — when a cell decides, on signal, to dismantle itself cleanly without spilling its contents and inflaming the surrounding tissue. FAS-driven apoptosis is one of the main routes the immune system uses to delete unwanted cells, including infected ones and self-reactive ones. An intron is a non-coding stretch inside a gene, between coding pieces called exons; introns get spliced out of the messenger RNA before the protein is made, but they are not silent — they often contain regulatory elements that decide how much of the gene gets transcribed and how. Intron 2 of FAS is simply the second intron in the FAS gene. SNP, as we covered in earlier episodes, is single nucleotide polymorphism, a single-letter DNA variant. The rs-numbers — rs7901656, rs2296603, rs1571019 — are stable identifiers in the dbSNP database that uniquely name specific SNPs. So when the paper says rs7901656 in FAS intron 2, it means "this particular single-letter change in the second intron of the FAS gene." A haplotype is a combination of alleles at multiple nearby SNPs that travel together on the same chromosome because they sit close enough that recombination rarely separates them. A dominant model in association testing means the rare allele acts dominantly: having one copy is enough to shift the trait. Affymetrix GeneChip U133 Plus 2.0 is a microarray product that measures expression of tens of thousands of human transcripts simultaneously, using small DNA probes printed on a chip — the "probeset 204781_s_at" referenced in the paper is the specific probe group that reads out the FAS transcript. F508del-CFTR — sometimes written p.Phe508del — is the most common cystic-fibrosis-causing CFTR variant, a three-letter deletion that removes the phenylalanine amino acid at position 508 of the CFTR protein, causing the misfolded protein to be degraded before it ever reaches the cell surface. F508del homozygotes are patients who carry F508del on both copies of CFTR. The European CF Twin and Sibling Study, anchored at Hannover Medical School under Burkhard Tümmler, is a long-running collection of CF-affected sibling pairs and twins specifically designed to identify CF disease modifiers by exploiting the fact that siblings share CFTR genotype but often differ in severity.

JORDAN Got it. Why this gene? Why was anyone looking at FAS for cystic fibrosis in 2008?

ALEX Two reasons converging. First, the airway pathology of cystic fibrosis is dominated by chronic inflammation that the body cannot resolve. Inflammatory cells — especially neutrophils, the most abundant white blood cells in human blood, named for the way their granules absorb neutral dye — pour into the airway, fail to clear the persistent infections, and themselves die in ways that damage surrounding tissue. The control of how and when immune cells die is the FAS pathway's bread and butter. So FAS sat on a short list of immunology-flavored candidate modifiers for CF lung disease. Second, the Hannover group had already built the European CF Twin and Sibling Study population — over 300 affected sibling pairs — explicitly to look for modifier signals. They had the right cohort for the question, and FAS was a defensible candidate. What was unknown going in: whether common variants in FAS itself, in real CF patients, are associated either with FAS expression or with clinical severity, and whether any such association replicates across independent cohorts.

JORDAN How did they tackle it?

ALEX Two-cohort design. Cohort one was 16 unrelated F508del homozygotes from whom intestinal epithelial biopsies had been collected. They measured FAS gene expression on these biopsies using the Affymetrix U133 Plus 2.0 expression microarray. They then genotyped these 16 patients at FAS intron 2 SNPs and asked whether genotype at any of those SNPs correlated with FAS expression level — that kind of analysis is called an expression quantitative trait locus or eQTL study. Cohort two was 37 nuclear families from the European CF Twin and Sibling Study, specifically selected because each family represented an extreme clinical phenotype — meaning either unusually severe or unusually mild CF disease despite the same CFTR genotypes. In those families they genotyped six informative SNPs spanning the FAS locus and tested for case-versus-control haplotype frequency differences. They then layered on bioinformatic work: comparative phylogenetic analysis of the FAS intron 2 sequence across multiple species to see whether the region around rs7901656 is conserved across evolution — conservation being a tell that a non-coding region matters functionally — and computational prediction of transcription factor binding sites in that region. ALEX pushes back on one design choice up front: the eQTL cohort of 16 patients is tiny by any modern standard, and the family cohort of 37 is small for a haplotype association test. The bioinformatic conservation and transcription-factor-binding work is doing a lot of the heavy lifting for biological plausibility.

JORDAN What did they actually find?

ALEX Three concrete results. First, in the 16-patient expression cohort, FAS intron 2 SNP rs7901656 genotype correlated with the Affymetrix probeset 204781_s_at signal — the FAS expression readout — consistent with a dominant model, at p equals 0.0009. So one allele of rs7901656 was associated with shifted FAS expression in CF intestinal epithelium. Second, in the 37-family case-control comparison, the three-SNP haplotype spanning intron 2 — rs2296603, rs7901656, rs1571019 — reached statistical significance for differential frequency between the severe-phenotype and mild-phenotype family sets at p equals 0.0246. Third, the bioinformatic work identified a non-coding sequence around rs7901656 that is evolutionarily conserved across vertebrates, and predicted four transcription factor binding sites in that region. Critically, one of those predicted binding sites — for the transcription factor c-Rel, an NF-kB family member that drives inflammatory gene expression — is altered by the rs7901656 allele. So the molecular story they propose is: rs7901656 sits inside a conserved regulatory element in FAS intron 2, the variant allele disrupts c-Rel binding, this changes how much FAS is expressed in the relevant tissue, and the resulting shift in apoptosis tipping point modulates CF disease severity. Did they answer the question? They produced a plausible, mechanism-grounded, internally consistent story across two cohorts. They did not produce — and could not produce, at those sample sizes — replicated, biobank-scale evidence.

JORDAN Concrete direction this suggests for what we are doing?

ALEX Plain English: in any modern CF whole-genome sequencing cohort, look up FAS intron 2 specifically, including rs7901656 and the conserved regulatory element around it, and ask whether the association with CF clinical severity that Kumar and colleagues reported in 2008 still holds. The formal one-sentence version: in the CFF whole-genome sequencing cohort, test the haplotype rs2296603-rs7901656-rs1571019 in FAS intron 2 for association with a composite CF severity phenotype, both as a candidate-region replication of Kumar et al. 2008 and as a localized fine-mapping analysis of the conserved non-coding element. That is one possible direction this episode suggests.

JORDAN Before we close, one more piece of vocabulary I want to make sure I have. You said NF-kB and c-Rel — what are those and why do they matter here?

ALEX NF-kB stands for nuclear factor kappa-light-chain-enhancer of activated B cells. It is a family of transcription factors — proteins that bind DNA at specific sequences and switch nearby genes on or off — that sit at the center of inflammatory signaling. When a cell senses an infection or a damage signal, NF-kB family members migrate into the nucleus and turn on hundreds of inflammatory and immune-response genes. c-Rel is one of the five mammalian NF-kB family members, and it is the one most strongly linked to lymphocyte function and to the regulation of cell-death programs. The fact that Kumar and colleagues' bioinformatic prediction places c-Rel binding right at rs7901656 is consequential because it links a candidate CF severity SNP to the inflammatory signaling axis that already dominates CF lung pathology. If the prediction is correct, you have a mechanistic chain: variant disrupts c-Rel binding, changes FAS transcription, changes apoptosis threshold in inflammatory cells, changes how the CF airway resolves or fails to resolve chronic infection. I want to be honest about a limit of this paper for us, though. The transcription-factor-binding prediction is in silico — done with a sequence-scanning algorithm rather than with a wet-lab assay that actually pulled down c-Rel protein bound to that region. So the c-Rel story is hypothesis, not demonstrated mechanism. A modern follow-up would use chromatin immunoprecipitation sequencing — ChIP-seq, which captures the actual DNA regions a specific transcription factor binds in living cells — to confirm or refute that c-Rel really does bind there in inflammatory cell types relevant to CF.
