Blurb

Collaco et al. (AJRCCM 2016) used nested mixed modeling on 2,639 sweat chloride measurements from 1,761 twins and siblings (CF Twin-Sibling Study, French CF Modifier Gene Study, Canadian Consortium for Genetic Studies — i.e. the same family base that became the CFGP WGS cohort) to partition sweat chloride variation: 56.1% CFTR genotype, 13.8% time-of-test, 13.5% environment, 9.9% residual, 6.8% unique-individual. Critically, modifier-gene heritability is negligible (H²=0; 95% CI 0–0.35). For our CFTR PheWAS, this paper rules sweat chloride out as a modifier-discovery phenotype and supplies a methods template — Collaco-style variance components — that should be applied to every continuous candidate phenotype before inclusion.

Methods to lift

  • Nested mixed-model variance components — random effects for patient, family, and time; fixed effects for age and sex; lme4 in R is the standard implementation. Apply per candidate continuous phenotype.
  • Twin-pair heritability decomposition — compare within-pair to between-pair variance to bound the additive modifier-gene heritability; identifies phenotypes worth a modifier-discovery PheWAS.
  • Within-vs-between subject decomposition — separate the population-level variance partition from the per-individual partition; phenotypes where within-individual time variation dominates are not productive trajectory phenotypes.

JORDAN Why are we reading a sweat chloride paper for a PheWAS proposal?

ALEX Because the answer to "is sweat chloride a usable phenotype for our PheWAS?" is a methodological question, and this is the paper that answers it. They run a nested mixed model, partition variance, and tell us where the signal comes from.

JORDAN Hit me with the variance components.

ALEX Across the population: CFTR genotype itself accounts for 56.1% of sweat chloride variation. Variation over time — meaning factors related to testing on different days — accounts for 13.8%. Environmental factors like climate and family diet are 13.5%. Other residual factors — test variability, lab-to-lab — are 9.9%. And unique individual factors, which is where modifier-gene effects would live, are 6.8%. Likelihood ratio test p less than 0.001 for the full model.

JORDAN And the modifier-gene heritability?

ALEX Negligible. H-squared equals 0, with a 95% confidence interval of 0 to 0.35. They use a twin design — comparing within-twin-pair variance to between-pair variance — which is the strongest possible test for additive genetic modifier effects beyond CFTR itself. The signal isn't there.

JORDAN That's a striking null. Walk me through the within-individual breakdown.

ALEX For an individual person with CF, variation in sweat chloride is 58.1% from variation over time and 41.9% from residual or random factors. So if you sweat-test the same patient twice, the difference is mostly day-to-day biology and lab measurement, not anything systematic about the person.

JORDAN OK. What does this mean for our gap-map?

ALEX It rules out one phenotype. Sweat chloride is dominated by CFTR genotype itself plus measurement noise, with effectively zero modifier signal. There is no point including sweat chloride as a PheWAS endpoint for modifier-locus discovery. We will hit CFTR by construction — that's a positive control, not a discovery — and we will not hit anything else.

JORDAN That's a useful exclusion. But this paper is older — 2016, premodulator. Does the conclusion still hold in the Trikafta era?

ALEX Good question and worth flagging. The 2016 paper's conclusions are about untreated or pre-modulator sweat chloride. Trikafta cuts sweat chloride substantially in responders, and the change in sweat chloride post-modulator is a different phenotype with potentially different variance partitioning. That's exactly the angle Eastman 2021 tried to chase with SLC26A9 and ivacaftor — and got a null. So even the modulator-response sweat chloride phenotype has a published null on its most-nominated modifier locus.

JORDAN So it's not just "sweat chloride is dominated by CFTR" — it's "sweat chloride doesn't have a usable modifier signal in either era."

ALEX That's the cleaner statement. There may still be a use for change in sweat chloride as a pharmacodynamic endpoint — a within-person difference rather than an absolute level — but for PheWAS-of-modifier-loci purposes, it's not a productive phenotype.
