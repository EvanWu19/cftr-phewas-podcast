Blurb

Collaco and colleagues pool 2,639 sweat chloride measurements from 1,761 CF twins/siblings across the CF Twin-Sibling Study (Johns Hopkins), French CF Modifier Gene Study (Trousseau), and Canadian Consortium for Genetic Studies (SickKids), then fit a nested mixed model that decomposes total sweat-chloride variance into named buckets. CFTR mutations themselves explain 56.1% of variance; within-subject longitudinal variation 13.8%; shared environment 13.5%; residual/test variability 9.9%; unique-individual factors 6.8%. Twin analysis returns negligible heritability (H^2 = 0; 95% CI 0.0-0.35) for the non-CFTR component — so modifier genes for sweat chloride are below detection. Within a single CF patient, day-to-day fluctuation (58.1%) plus residual noise (41.9%) means single-point measurements are unreliable for tracking response. The variance-decomposition framework is the lift, even though the answer for sweat chloride is "not a modifier-GWAS phenotype."

Plain-English glossary (alphabetical)

  • Biomarker — any measurable readout (blood, sweat, lung function, imaging) that reflects disease state.
  • BMI z-score — Body Mass Index standardized for a patient's age and sex; tracks CF nutritional status.
  • CF / Cystic Fibrosis — autosomal recessive disease caused by loss-of-function mutations in CFTR.
  • CFFPR — Cystic Fibrosis Foundation Patient Registry; longitudinal US clinical-data registry.
  • CFTR — Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane conductance Regulator; the chloride/bicarbonate channel mutated in CF.
  • Cross-sectional — one measurement per subject across many subjects.
  • F508del — three-base-pair deletion removing phenylalanine at residue 508 of CFTR; ~70% of CF chromosomes worldwide.
  • Fecal elastase — stool enzyme assay used to assess pancreatic exocrine function in CF.
  • FEV1 — Forced Expiratory Volume in one second; standard spirometric lung-function measure.
  • Fixed effect — a model term whose level is treated as known and estimated as a single coefficient.
  • GWAS — Genome-Wide Association Study; scan all common variants for association with one trait.
  • Heritability (H^2) — fraction of trait variance explained by additive genetic differences between individuals; 0 to 1.
  • Longitudinal — repeated measurements on the same subject over time.
  • Mixed model — regression with both fixed and random effects; random effects let you estimate variance components.
  • Modifier gene — a gene outside the main disease locus that nudges severity or sub-phenotypes.
  • Modulator — small-molecule drug (ivacaftor, lumacaftor, tezacaftor, elexacaftor) that partially restores CFTR function.
  • Monozygotic vs dizygotic twins — identical (~100% shared DNA) vs fraternal (~50% shared, like full siblings).
  • Nested model — random effects organized hierarchically (measurements within subjects within families within cohorts).
  • PheWAS — Phenome-Wide Association Study; fix a genotype, scan many phenotypes (mirror of GWAS).
  • Random effect — a model term whose levels are draws from a distribution; you estimate the variance of that distribution.
  • Residual CFTR function — partial channel activity left by mild CFTR mutations; correlates with lower sweat chloride.
  • Sweat chloride test — pilocarpine-iontophoresis assay measuring chloride concentration in sweat; >60 mmol/L diagnoses CF.
  • Trikafta — elexacaftor/tezacaftor/ivacaftor triple modulator; approved 2019, now standard of care for eligible genotypes.
  • Variance components analysis — statistical decomposition of total trait variance into named additive sources.
  • WGS — Whole Genome Sequencing.

Methods to lift

  • Pool measurements across multiple genotyped CF cohorts to get the sample size needed for variance decomposition.
  • Require repeat (longitudinal) measurements per subject so within-subject variance is identifiable.
  • Fit a nested mixed model with CFTR genotype as a fixed effect and named random effects for time-within-subject, family-shared environment, residual/test variability, and unique-individual factors.
  • Use twin/sibling structure (MZ vs DZ/full-sib) to estimate residual heritability after CFTR is regressed out.
  • Report variance partition both at the population level (between-subject view) and at the individual level (within-subject view) — they answer different questions.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Collaco's framework is the template; sweat chloride is the negative control showing the framework can also rule a phenotype out.

ALEX The sweat chloride test measures the concentration of chloride ions — chloride being the negatively charged form of the element chlorine — in sweat. In CF, the protein called CFTR — Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane conductance Regulator — is broken. CFTR is a channel that moves chloride and bicarbonate across cell membranes, including in the sweat duct. When it doesn't work, chloride doesn't get reabsorbed, so sweat ends up salty. A reading above sixty millimoles per liter is diagnostic for CF.

JORDAN And CFTR is the gene mutated in CF.

ALEX Right. The most common mutation is called F508del — a deletion of three DNA letters that removes phenylalanine at position 508 of the CFTR protein. About seventy percent of CF chromosomes worldwide carry it. Now — variance components analysis. That's a statistical technique that takes a measurement that wobbles around and decomposes the total wobble — the variance — into named buckets. Bucket one might be "differences between people." Bucket two might be "differences between days within the same person." Bucket three might be "differences between labs." The model gives you a percentage for each bucket.

JORDAN So it's like accounting for variance.

ALEX Exactly that. Two more terms. Heritability — written H-squared — is the fraction of total variance in a trait that's explained by genetic differences between individuals. It runs from zero to one. Heritability of one means purely genetic, zero means no genetic contribution detected. And longitudinal versus cross-sectional — longitudinal means repeated measurements on the same person over time; cross-sectional means one measurement per person across many people. Last term — modifier gene. In CF, CFTR is the disease-causing gene, but other genes elsewhere in the genome can nudge how severe the disease is. Those nudgers are called modifier genes. And residual CFTR function — some CFTR mutations leave a little bit of working channel; those genotypes give milder disease and lower sweat chloride than F508del homozygotes.

JORDAN And biomarker?

ALEX A biomarker is any measurement — blood, sweat, lung function, imaging — that reflects disease state. Sweat chloride is the classic CF biomarker.

JORDAN Okay. So what's the actual question?

ALEX The question is — sweat chloride is the diagnostic for CF, but among people who already have CF, sweat chloride values still vary. A given person can have one hundred millimoles per liter; another person can have eighty. And in the modulator era — drugs like ivacaftor and lumacaftor that partially fix CFTR — sweat chloride drops. So sweat chloride is being used as a treatment-response biomarker in clinical trials. But before you trust it as a biomarker, you need to know — when the number moves, what's moving it?

JORDAN Could be the genotype. Could be the lab. Could be the day.

ALEX Right. And those three sources have very different implications. If most of the variation between CF patients is driven by their CFTR genotype itself, then sweat chloride is mostly telling you which CFTR mutations they carry — useful but not new information. If a chunk is driven by other genes — modifier genes — then sweat chloride could be a phenotype for a modifier-gene GWAS — Genome-Wide Association Study, where you scan the whole genome for variants associated with a trait. If a big chunk is technical — lab to lab, day to day — then your biomarker is noisy and you'd need to fix the protocol before trusting drug-response readings.

JORDAN So this is upstream of any modifier-gene work.

ALEX Yes. You can't run a modifier GWAS on a phenotype you haven't characterized. The Collaco group asks — for sweat chloride specifically — what fraction of variance lives in each bucket?

JORDAN And why three cohorts?

ALEX Different visits, different days, sometimes years apart. That's the longitudinal piece.

ALEX They fit what's called a nested mixed model. "Nested" means the random effects have a hierarchy — measurements are nested within subjects, subjects are nested within families, families are nested within cohorts. The model partitions the total variance in sweat chloride into named random-effect buckets. CFTR genotype effect — they actually treat CFTR mutations as a fixed effect because they're known. ALEX Estimated separately within the twin-sibling structure. If identical twins are more similar than fraternal twins for a trait, that excess similarity is genetic.
