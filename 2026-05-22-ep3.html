One-paragraph blurb

This Nature Genetics paper from the North American CF Gene Modifier Consortium (Cutting/JHU, Knowles/UNC, Drumm/Case, Strug/Toronto) is the foundational GWAS-era CF lung-modifier paper. Three independently-ascertained CF cohorts — the GMS extremes-of-phenotype F508del-homozygous study, the CGS Canadian population-based pancreatic-insufficient cohort, and the TSS twin-and-sibling study — were combined to yield 3,467 patients, 2,494 association-substrate. Using a custom multi-year normalized lung-function quantitative phenotype (h²=0.51) and Illumina 610-Quad genotyping of 570,725 SNPs, they identified the chr11p13 EHF/APIP locus (sentinel rs12793173, F508del-homozygous P=3.34×10⁻⁸; joint-meta P=1.49×10⁻⁹) and a chr20q13.2 linkage signal (LOD=5.03, BMI-Z-adjusted LOD=5.72, 1.31-Mb support interval; 16-kb SNP cluster ~200kb from CBLN4 with rs6024460 P=1.34×10⁻⁴). Previously-nominated candidate modifiers TGFB1 and IFRD1 failed to replicate at genome-wide significance. One possible direction the episode suggests: PheWAS the chr11p13 and chr20q13.2 sentinel SNPs across the broad CFFPR phenome to test pleiotropy beyond lung function. Open: whether the original sentinel SNPs are well-genotyped in CFF WGS calls; whether non-pulmonary endpoints have adequate sample size.

Plain-English glossary

  • CF (cystic fibrosis): recessive multi-system disease caused by CFTR mutations.
  • CFTR (Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane conductance Regulator): the gene whose mutations cause CF.
  • CGS (Canadian Consortium for Genetic Studies): unselected population-based CF cohort, pancreatic-insufficient, used as an external-validity counterpart to the extremes-of-phenotype design.
  • eQTL (expression quantitative trait locus): SNP whose genotype predicts the expression level of a nearby gene; used to bridge from noncoding GWAS hits to candidate causal genes.
  • F508del (p.Phe508del): the most common pathogenic CFTR variant; ~70% of CFTR mutant alleles in northern European-ancestry CF populations.
  • F508del/F508del: patient is homozygous for F508del; useful for stratification because CFTR genotype is held constant.
  • FEV1 (forced expiratory volume in 1 second): the workhorse lung-function measure in CF; volume of air a person can forcefully exhale in 1 second.
  • GMS (Genetic Modifier Study): extremes-of-phenotype F508del-homozygous CF cohort, Cutting (JHU)-led.
  • GWAS: genome-wide association study.
  • h² (heritability): fraction of trait variance attributable to genetic differences.
  • Linkage analysis: family-based method that finds chromosomal regions co-inherited with a trait; sensitive to large-effect regions even when causal variant is rare or poorly tagged.
  • LOD score (logarithm of odds): linkage equivalent of a P-value; >3 conventionally suggestive, >5 strong.
  • Modifier gene: any gene outside CFTR itself whose variation affects CF severity.
  • ppFEV1 (percent-predicted FEV1): FEV1 normalized to expected for healthy person of same age, sex, height.
  • PI (pancreatic insufficient): CF phenotype where exocrine pancreas function is insufficient — most-severe-CFTR-genotype group.
  • Sentinel SNP: the most significant SNP at a locus, used as the locus-representative variant.
  • SFDR / WFDR (stratified / weighted false discovery rate): methods for integrating linkage and association evidence into a single significance framework.
  • TSS (CF Twin and Sibling Study): family-based CF cohort, Hopkins-led, supports linkage and family-based replication of association hits.

Methods to lift

  • Multi-cohort design combining extremes-of-phenotype (GMS), population-based (CGS), and family-based (TSS) substrates to enable both association and linkage on the same biological substrate.
  • Custom quantitative lung-function phenotype built from multiple years of FEV1, adjusted for age-related decline and mortality attrition — h²=0.51 demonstrating heritability is detectable.
  • Single-platform genotyping with platform-level QC using parent-child transmission consistency in TSS trios — the methodological backbone of the data-cleaning step.
  • Restriction to F508del/F508del as an analytic strategy to minimize CFTR allelic heterogeneity and concentrate the modifier signal — one of seven suggestive loci became genome-wide significant under this restriction.
  • Linkage scan in TSS using Merlin software for family-based testing; chr20q13.2 was found only via linkage and would have been missed by association alone.
  • WFDR/SFDR for combining association and linkage evidence into a unified genome-wide significance framework — chr20 cluster moved from rank 154 to top of genome under WFDR.
  • Imputation via MACH for fine-mapping the 11p13 region; did not substantially improve the sentinel-SNP association, suggesting the causal variant may not be on a denser haplotype panel.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: PheWAS the chr11p13 EHF/APIP sentinel SNPs (rs12793173, rs568529) and the chr20q13.2 cluster SNPs (rs6092179, rs6024437, rs8125625, rs6024454, rs6024460) across the CFFPR phenome, asking whether either locus exhibits pleiotropy with non-pulmonary CF endpoints — CFRD, CFLD, meconium ileus, pancreatic phenotypes, growth, mental health — beyond the lung-severity effect for which they were originally discovered. Test family-based / linkage-style analysis on the chr20q13.2 region if CFF cohort has sufficient sib-pair structure.

What to verify first

  • Whether sentinel SNPs from chr11p13 and chr20q13.2 are reliably genotyped or imputed at adequate quality in the CFF whole-genome sequencing variant call set.
  • Whether the CFFPR phenotype catalog supports each candidate non-pulmonary endpoint at sample sizes meaningful for stratified per-locus testing.
  • Whether the CFF cohort contains enough sib-pairs / family structures to support linkage analysis of the chr20q13.2 region.
  • Whether the F508del-homozygous-only restriction is operationally feasible given current modulator-era CF demographics — F508del-homozygous coverage in the registry may differ from the 2011-era distribution this paper relied on.
  • Whether the EHF/APIP signal retains its effect size in modulator-treated subjects, or whether modulator therapy partially "rescues" the residual-CFTR-expression mechanism the signal is thought to act through.

Gap-map

Closes:

  • Existence and genome-wide-significant replication of chr11p13 EHF/APIP as a CF lung-disease modifier.
  • Existence of a major-effect chr20q13.2 region detectable by linkage in CF sibships, not detectable by GWAS alone.
  • The TGFB1-and-IFRD1 candidate-gene era: TGFB1 fails to replicate at genome-wide stringency (best P ~10⁻³ to 10⁻⁴ in GMS only); IFRD1 not detected even at suggestive level.
  • Methodological demonstration that linkage and association integrated via WFDR/SFDR finds signal that either alone misses.

Leaves open:

  • Which gene at chr11p13 is the causal effector — EHF (transcription factor regulating CFTR expression) or APIP (Apaf-1-interacting anti-apoptotic protein, also implicated in methionine salvage), or both via independent mechanisms. Resolved partially by Corvol 2015 (eQTL anchors APIP) and Stanke 2014 (EHF haplotype anchors CFTR-chaperome regulation) — both downstream of this paper.
  • Which gene drives the chr20q13.2 signal: candidates include MC3R (melanocortin-3 receptor, energy balance and neutrophil inflammation), CASS4, AURKA, and CBLN4; none experimentally validated as the modifier effector in this paper.
  • Whether either locus is pleiotropic across non-pulmonary CF endpoints — the entire CFTR PheWAS-style question.
  • Whether modulator-era CF cohorts retain the signal at either locus, or whether pharmacologic CFTR rescue diminishes residual-expression-based modifier effects.
  • Whether trans-ancestry CF cohorts replicate the loci — all three consortium samples were predominantly European-ancestry.

ALEX Third episode of the day, and for this one we cross over to the CFTR side of the bookshelf. Today's paper is Wright, Strug, Doshi, and a long author list from the North American CF Gene Modifier Consortium, 2011, Nature Genetics, "Genome-wide association and linkage identify modifier loci of lung disease severity in cystic fibrosis at 11p13 and 20q13.2." This paper is the foundational discovery of the chr11p13 EHF/APIP CF lung modifier locus — the single most-replicated common-variant CF lung-severity hit in the literature — and it also reports a separate linkage signal on chr20q13.2. Almost every CF modifier paper after this one cites it.

JORDAN Before we dive in, walk me through the vocabulary again. I've heard most of these but I want them fresh.

ALEX Yes. Let me set the table. CF is cystic fibrosis, the recessive disease caused by mutations in CFTR — the Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane conductance Regulator gene. F508del, also written p.Phe508del, is the most common pathogenic CFTR variant — about 70 percent of CFTR mutant alleles in northern European-ancestry CF populations are F508del, and roughly 50 percent of CF patients are homozygous for it, meaning they carry F508del on both copies of chromosome seven. "F508del homozygous" or "F508del/F508del" therefore means the patient's CFTR genotype is the same as everyone else in that group — which is useful because it controls for one source of variation. A modifier gene is any gene outside CFTR itself whose variation makes CF lung disease milder or more severe.

JORDAN And the statistical terms?

ALEX GWAS, genome-wide association study, is what we covered yesterday — millions of common SNPs tested one at a time. Linkage analysis is the older method: instead of testing variants individually, you ask whether a chromosomal region is co-inherited with a trait inside families — typically using affected siblings — without needing to know the exact causal variant. Linkage is good at finding regions of large effect even when the underlying variant is rare or poorly tagged by common SNPs. LOD score is the linkage equivalent of a P-value — it's a log-base-ten ratio of the likelihood under linkage versus no linkage; a multipoint LOD above three is conventionally suggestive, above five is strong. FEV1 is forced expiratory volume in one second — the volume of air a person can forcefully exhale in one second after a maximal inhale, the workhorse lung-function measure in CF. ppFEV1 is percent-predicted FEV1, FEV1 normalized to what's expected for a healthy person of the same age, sex, and height. eQTL is expression quantitative trait locus — a SNP whose genotype predicts the expression level of a nearby gene; eQTLs are how you bridge from a noncoding GWAS hit to a candidate causal gene. h-squared is heritability, the fraction of trait variance attributable to genetic differences. WFDR and SFDR are weighted and stratified false discovery rate methods — ways of combining linkage and association evidence at the genome-wide scale rather than treating the two scans as independent.

ALEX Background and purpose. By 2011, the CF field knew two important things. First, lung disease severity varies enormously even among patients with identical CFTR genotypes — F508del homozygotes can have anything from severe early-onset disease to nearly normal lung function as adults. Second, twin and sibling studies had shown that around half of the residual variation in lung function in CF patients is heritable, with h-squared above point five — so there had to be modifier genes out there. Candidate-gene studies through the 2000s had nominated TGFB1 and IFRD1 as possible modifiers, with conflicting replication, and a small number of other hits with weaker support. Nobody had done a properly powered genome-wide modifier scan. The methodological challenge was real: CF is rare, recruiting thousands of patients with longitudinal lung-function data and DNA is hard, and an unselected CF cohort has the lung-function distribution skewed in ways that hurt GWAS power.

JORDAN How did they solve those problems?

ALEX They built a consortium. The North American CF Gene Modifier Consortium combined three independently-recruited samples with three different study designs. One: the Genetic Modifier Study, abbreviated GMS, ascertained unrelated F508del homozygotes from the extremes of the lung-function distribution — sick adults and adults with preserved lung function, deliberately oversampling the tails to boost power for a quantitative association scan. GMS was led by Garry Cutting at Johns Hopkins. Two: the Canadian Consortium for Genetic Studies, CGS, recruited an unselected population-based sample of pancreatic-insufficient CF patients across multiple Canadian centers, led by Lisa Strug and Peter Durie. CGS gave them an external-validity check against the extremes design. Three: the CF Twin and Sibling Study, TSS, recruited families where two or more children with CF had survived, supporting both linkage analysis and family-based replication of association hits. TSS was again Hopkins-led. Total assembled sample: 3,467 CF patients. After all QC, the association substrate was 2,494 unrelated GMS plus CGS participants, of whom 1,978 were F508del homozygotes.

ALEX They also designed a custom quantitative lung phenotype — not raw ppFEV1 at one time point, but a multi-year normalized lung-function score that adjusts for age-related decline and mortality attrition, computed from three years of FEV1 measurements per patient. That phenotype has h-squared of point five-one — so genetic effects are clearly detectable on it. Genotyping was done in one batch on the Illumina 610-Quad SNP array at Genome Quebec — five hundred and seventy thousand seven hundred and twenty-five autosomal and X-chromosome SNPs after QC. Doing everything on one platform let them clean the data using parent-child transmission consistency in the TSS trios and platform-level relatedness checks.

ALEX Now I want to push back on one design choice. The GMS extremes-of-phenotype design boosts power but does so at the cost of generalizability — patients in the population middle aren't in the discovery scan, and effect-size estimates from extremes designs are systematically inflated. The paper acknowledges this and uses the CGS population-based sample as a sanity check, which is the right move, but the published effect sizes still need to be read with that caveat.

ALEX Results. The headline finding is a single SNP, rs12793173, on chromosome 11p13, reaching genome-wide significance in the F508del-homozygous GMS-plus-CGS combined analysis at P equals 3.34 times ten to the minus eight. With CF-relevant covariates — sex, body mass index, and previously associated genes — included, the signal strengthens to P equals 9.42 times ten to the minus nine. In the joint analysis incorporating the family-based TSS sample, the locus reaches P equals 1.49 times ten to the minus nine in F508del homozygotes. The variant sits in an intergenic region just three-prime of two genes: EHF, the epithelial-restricted ETS-family transcription factor, and APIP, the Apaf-1-interacting protein.

ALEX A second locus came from the linkage scan, not from association. On chromosome 20q13.2, a multipoint LOD score of 5.03 at rs4811626 — genome-wide significant. Adding body-mass-index-z-score as a covariate bumped the LOD to 5.72. The one-LOD-unit support interval is 1.31 megabases wide. Within that interval, a sixteen-kilobase cluster of five SNPs in tight linkage disequilibrium — rs6092179, rs6024437, rs8125625, rs6024454, and rs6024460 — sits about 200 kilobases from CBLN4, with rs6024460 giving the lowest association P equals 1.34 times ten to the minus four. The other notable genes in the support interval include MC3R, CASS4, and AURKA. When the authors combined linkage and association into a single framework using weighted false-discovery-rate analysis, the chr20q13.2 cluster moved from rank 154 or worse on a pure-GWAS ranking to top of the genome — illustrating that linkage and association sometimes see different signal regimes and that integrating them is worth the methodological effort.

ALEX Importantly, the previously-nominated candidate-gene modifiers — TGFB1 and IFRD1 — did not achieve genome-wide significance in this scan. TGFB1 hit P-values in the ten-to-the-minus-three to ten-to-the-minus-four range in GMS depending on covariates — suggestive, not significant. IFRD1 was not reported as suggestive at all. This is one of the most cited "negative result" findings in CF modifier biology.

ALEX To translate the chr11p13 effect into something interpretable: 0.2 phenotype units of the consortium's normalized lung-function metric per allele, which corresponds to a difference of about 5.1 percent of predicted FEV1, or 254 milliliters of FEV1, in patients over 18 years of age. That's clinically meaningful at the population level — a five-percentage-point difference in ppFEV1 is on the order of what some modulator-drug effects show — but per-individual, the locus explains only one to two percent of the lung-function phenotype variance.

ALEX So did they answer their question? They answered "is there a common-variant modifier of CF lung disease that exceeds genome-wide significance" with yes — chr11p13. They answered "is there a region-of-major-effect that linkage can find when association cannot" with also yes — chr20q13.2. They answered the candidate-gene-era debate over TGFB1 with what's effectively a no-replication finding at genome-wide stringency. They did not establish causal genes at either locus — the chr11p13 sentinel sits intergenic, and the EHF-versus-APIP causal-gene question would not be resolved until follow-on work by Corvol 2015 and Stanke 2014.

ALEX One possible direction this paper suggests for the CFTR PheWAS work. In plain English: the chr11p13 EHF/APIP signal is real, replicated, and biologically interpretable. The chr20q13.2 signal is also real but mechanistically opaque. Both were discovered against the lung-severity phenotype only. If those loci are pleiotropic — meaning the same variants modulate non-lung CF outcomes — then there's a whole CFTR-PheWAS shape of question waiting at each locus. The formal one-sentence version: conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage, do the chr11p13 sentinel SNPs and the chr20q13.2 linkage-cluster SNPs show pleiotropic association with non-pulmonary CFFPR endpoints — CF-related diabetes onset, CF liver disease, meconium ileus, exocrine and endocrine pancreatic phenotypes, growth, mental health — beyond their established lung-severity effects, and does the chr20q13.2 signal index a region best tested via linkage-friendly family-based methods rather than association alone? One possible direction the paper suggests, not the direction we'll take.

ALEX This paper closes the question of whether genome-wide-significant common-variant CF modifier loci exist — they do. It closes the question of whether candidate-gene-era modifier nominations would survive a properly-powered scan — most did not.
