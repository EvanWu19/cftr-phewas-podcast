One-paragraph blurb

Three papers, two voices, one CFTR-PheWAS lens. Bycroft 2018 is the UK Biobank resource paper — the QC, ancestry, and relatedness playbook every CF cohort analysis silently borrows. Halldorsson 2022 is the 150K UKBB whole-genome rollout — GraphTyper, depletion-rank selection scoring, structural variants, and the haplotype reference panel pattern that frames how a biobank-scale rare-variant analysis is organized end-to-end. Aksit 2020 is the CFF-cohort GWAS for CF-related diabetes onset on n=5,740 — exactly the scale Runyu has been planning around — and it both (a) proves that 5K samples can find genome-wide signal when the phenotype is well-defined and (b) closes the door on framing a new proposal as "CFRD discovery." The three together produce a defensible proposal angle: a CFTR-anchored, registry-longitudinal, modulator-era PheWAS that asks what established modifier loci (PTMA, SLC26A9, TCF7L2) do across non-diabetes CF complications — a pleiotropy question prior phenotype-anchored work has not addressed.

Gap-map after today

  • Closed: UKBB-style QC and ancestry-stratification architecture is settled. KING + fastPCA-with-projection + batch-aware QC + BOLT-LMM/REGENIE + METAL is the canonical pipeline; no further methods reading needed on this layer.
  • Closed: Framing the proposal as "CFRD discovery in the CFF cohort" is a non-starter — Aksit 2020 already published the GWAS + 18-variant PRS at this sample size.
  • Open (priority): Modifier signals published pre-2020 have not been re-tested under modulator-era treatment. CFRD genetics in the post-Trikafta era is uncharacterized. Aligned with Patient Registry mandate.
  • Open: Pleiotropy of established CFRD modifiers (PTMA, SLC26A9, TCF7L2) across non-diabetes CF complications — exocrine pancreatic disease, infection, lung function trajectory — has not been systematically characterized. Inverse-PheWAS design fits naturally.
  • Open: Registry-derived longitudinal phenotypes (FEV1 trajectories, exacerbation counts, transplant timing) power survival-analysis PheWAS designs that snapshot UKBB-style PheWAS cannot replicate. Differentiator vs. UKBB-based work.

JORDAN Why does that matter for our CFTR work?

ALEX Because Runyu has roughly five thousand CFF WGS samples, and on day one of the analysis he has to answer the same questions Bycroft answered. How many sex aneuploidies does the CFF cohort have? JORDAN What about ancestry inference? The CFF cohort is mostly European but not entirely.

ALEX Bycroft uses fastPCA from the EIGENSOFT v6.0.1 package on a curated set of unrelated individuals, then projects everyone else into that PC space. The trick is doing PCA on a clean subset and then projecting — not running PCA on everyone, because relatives and ancestry outliers warp the principal components. This matters for us because the CFF cohort, even if it's predominantly European, almost certainly has Hispanic, African American, and admixed samples. If Runyu does a PheWAS without proper ancestry-stratified analyses, the reviewer's first comment is going to be about population structure inflation.

JORDAN There's an HLA piece in here too.

ALEX Eleven HLA genes, classical allele imputation using HLA*IMP02. They recover known HLA-disease associations, which is the validation that the imputation worked. So when we talk about a CFTR PheWAS, the phenotype harmonization problem is ours, not Bycroft's. JORDAN One thing I want to come back to — the array choice itself. They used two different arrays.

JORDAN Why does this matter for the CFF cohort?

ALEX Because the CFF cohort almost certainly has multi-batch sequencing too. The samples were collected over years across multiple sites. If the CFF VCFs were jointly called in batches and the batches map to recruitment site, batch effects can masquerade as ancestry effects or as phenotype associations. The Bycroft paper documents the kind of batch-aware QC sweep we should be doing — checking missingness by batch, allele frequency drift between batches, and dropping markers that fail per-batch tests. It's not glamorous and it's not in any methods primer, but skipping it is what reviewers find first.

JORDAN Anything in the Bycroft paper on relatedness specifically?

ALEX KING is their relatedness inference tool. They identify cryptic relatives at varying degrees and the released QC flags include kinship coefficient cutoffs. The number to remember is that even in UKBB — a population sample, not a family sample — there's a meaningful tail of related individuals that you have to either remove or model with a mixed model like BOLT-LMM. For the CFF cohort, where families participated in the twin and sibling studies that anchor NCT zero zero six seven six one six nine, the relatedness fraction is much higher and we cannot just drop relatives — we'd lose statistical power and bias the cohort. That means BOLT-LMM or a similar mixed-model approach is obligatory, not optional.

JORDAN One sentence — what's the single thing to take into our methods primer?

ALEX PCA on a curated unrelated subset and projection of everyone else, plus a batch-aware QC sweep before any association analysis. Those two design choices preempt the most common reviewer objections in a CF cohort PheWAS, and they're both in this paper.

JORDAN What about the depletion rank score they describe? That sounded interesting.

ALEX Depletion rank is their selection metric. They define windows along the genome and score each window by how depleted it is of variation relative to expectation. The provocative finding is that coding exons represent a small fraction of regions under strong sequence conservation. There's a lot of conserved non-coding sequence — regulatory regions, deeply constrained intronic blocks — that show selection signatures comparable to coding regions. For our purposes, the implication is that a CFTR-anchored analysis that only tests coding variants is throwing away a substantial fraction of the constrained variation that could plausibly modify CF phenotypes. JORDAN And the structural variants? Eight hundred ninety-five thousand is a lot.

ALEX Structural variants and microsatellites are the WGS-only contribution. If the CFF cohort's joint variant set was filtered to SNPs and indels only, then the SV catalog isn't there and we'd be answering a question downstream of "how many CF patients carry an unusual structural variant in CFTR or in a known modifier region." JORDAN Microsatellites too?

So microsatellite calls in a CFF WGS dataset would be a real differentiator.
