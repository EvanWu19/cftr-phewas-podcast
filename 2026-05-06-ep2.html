Blurb

Verma and colleagues report genome-wide associations for 2,068 traits across 635,969 participants in the VA Million Veteran Program (MVP) — the largest US-based, ancestrally diverse phenome-wide GWAS to date. They identify 13,672 genomic risk loci, 1,608 of which (~12%) only reach significance once non-European populations are included; statistical fine-mapping nominates causal variants at 6,318 distinct signals across 613 traits, with a third (n=2,069) coming from non-European participants. Broadly, the genetic architecture (effect sizes, polygenicity, allele-frequency spectrum) is similar across ancestries, but the specific causal alleles often differ — which is the operational takeaway for any CF PheWAS extension. The proposed direction this episode opens up is to ancestry-stratify any CFFPR-anchored CFTR-modifier PheWAS at the gene-and-pathway level rather than the SNP level, with non-European CF subgroups treated as primary analyses rather than sensitivity checks. Open questions: ancestry breakdown of CFFPR and CFGP participants, ancestry-related curation bias in CFFPR phenotyping, and whether per-ancestry sub-cohort sizes in CFGP can support stratified analysis at all.

Plain-English glossary

  • Ancestry inference (project-then-classify) — Two-step approach: project participants onto ancestry principal components from a labeled reference panel (gnomAD, 1000 Genomes), then assign ancestry labels with a classifier. Standard for biobank-scale ancestry calls.
  • Causal variant — The specific SNP at a GWAS locus that carries the actual biological effect, as opposed to nearby correlated SNPs that test as significant only because they are inherited together with the causal one.
  • Credible set — A small set of SNPs at a locus that together carry, e.g., 95% probability of containing the true causal variant. Output of statistical fine-mapping.
  • Fine-mapping — Statistical procedure that ranks SNPs at a GWAS-significant region by their probability of being the causal variant, given the LD pattern and per-SNP effect sizes. Tools: SuSiE, FINEMAP, CAVIAR.
  • Linkage disequilibrium (LD) — Statistical correlation between nearby SNPs caused by their tendency to be inherited as a block; means one true biological signal usually shows through many correlated proxy SNPs.
  • Locus — A region of the genome (commonly 1 Mb or LD-block-defined) containing at least one genome-wide-significant SNP for a trait. "13,672 loci" = 13,672 region-by-trait pairs.
  • MR-MEGA — Meta-Regression of Multi-Ethnic Genetic Association — a trans-ancestry meta-analysis method that allows per-ancestry effect-size heterogeneity by regressing on ancestry PCs.
  • MVP (Million Veteran Program) — VA-based research biobank of US military veterans; ~90% male, longitudinal VA-EHR phenotyping, ~25% non-European ancestry.
  • Phecode — A disease-classification rollup of ICD-9/ICD-10 codes (~1,800 categories) used as standard PheWAS units.
  • Polygenicity — Property of a trait whose genetic variation is contributed by many small-effect variants rather than a few large-effect ones.
  • Sex-dimorphic trait — A trait with substantially different distributions, prevalences, or genetic architectures between sexes.
  • Trans-ancestry meta-analysis — Combining GWAS results across ancestry groups using methods that allow per-ancestry effect heterogeneity.

Methods to lift

  • Ancestry-stratified per-trait GWAS (REGENIE/SAIGE) followed by trans-ancestry meta-analysis — Default modern pipeline; per-ancestry summary statistics published openly.
  • SuSiE-style genome-wide fine-mapping at every credible locus — Out of reach at our N for discovery, but usable for replicating fine-mapped signals from MVP/UKBB at CFGP-resident loci.
  • Open summary-statistics atlas — Trait-level summary statistics released, queryable; the most directly reusable artifact for any cross-cohort comparison.
  • Per-ancestry locus-novelty bookkeeping — Reporting the count of loci that only reached significance after non-European inclusion; should be adopted as a standard reporting field for any ancestry-stratified CF PheWAS.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: ancestry-stratified gene-burden and pathway-level PheWAS of CFTR-modifier candidate loci against pruned CFFPR phecodes, with non-European-ancestry CF subgroups powered as primary analyses rather than sensitivity checks, and with cross-ancestry concordance reported as the headline metric (rather than an appendix table).

What to verify first

  • Ancestry breakdown of CFFPR participants — actual non-European sample size, including the post-modulator-era recruitment cohorts; not yet personally inspected.
  • Whether CFFPR phenotyping protocols have been audited for ancestry-related curation bias (e.g., differential ICD-coding patterns across CF Care Centers serving different demographics).
  • Ancestry coverage of the CFGP WGS arm — whether non-European subgroups have enough sequenced patients to support stratified analysis at all, especially for rare-variant gene-burden tests.
  • Whether ancestry-PC computation in CFGP follows the Bycroft 2018 relatives-related PCA approach (covered Episode 1, 2026-04-30) — a CF cohort with siblings will produce kinship-confounded ancestry PCs unless the relatives-related approach is used.

ALEX Welcome back. This is Episode 2 of three for today, and we are reading "Diversity and scale: Genetic architecture of 2068 traits in the VA Million Veteran Program," published July 19, 2024 in Science, volume 385, issue 6706, article eadj1182. The first author is Anurag Verma; the senior authors are Scott Damrauer and Katherine Liao. We are reading it because at 635,969 participants and 2,068 traits, this is the closest published thing to a phenome-wide GWAS at the scale we wish we had — and the diversity story it tells is directly relevant to whether any CFFPR analysis would generalize.

JORDAN Pause. Yesterday we covered GBMI. We've covered FinnGen, AoU, UKBB. What makes the VA Million Veteran Program different?

ALEX A few things. The Million Veteran Program — call it MVP — recruits from US military veterans through Veterans Affairs hospitals. Compared to UK Biobank, MVP is older, mostly male, and substantially more ancestrally diverse — about a quarter of participants are non-European. Compared to All of Us, which intentionally recruits underrepresented groups, MVP is even older, and is driven primarily by veterans' lifelong VA medical record rather than a research enrollment encounter, which means the phenotyping is dense longitudinal clinical data, not survey-based. From a CF-modifier perspective, MVP itself contains very few CF patients — CF is largely pediatric in onset and CF patients historically have not been heavily represented among military veterans — so the value is not as a CF cohort but as a high-power, high-diversity reference for what genetic architecture actually looks like at population scale.

JORDAN Before we go further, define some terms for me. They use fine-mapping and causal variants. What do those mean?

ALEX Both of those come from the same problem. When a GWAS reports a hit at a chromosomal region, it usually reports many SNPs in that region as significant, because nearby SNPs are statistically correlated through what's called linkage disequilibrium — basically, neighboring DNA tends to be inherited as a block. So one true biological signal can show through forty or two hundred correlated proxy SNPs. Fine-mapping is the statistical process of asking, given the LD pattern in this region and the precise per-SNP effect sizes, which one or two SNPs are most likely to be the actual causal variant — the variant that is biologically doing the work, as opposed to just riding along correlated. The output is usually a credible set, a small list of SNPs that together carry, say, 95 percent probability of containing the causal one. Common tools are SuSiE, FINEMAP, and CAVIAR, the last of which the Zhou 2023 paper on the CFGP cohort applied with mixed success. Genome-wide fine-mapping at the scale Verma reports — 6,318 causal signals across 613 traits — is only possible because of the sheer N.

JORDAN Define loci too. They report 13,672 of those.

ALEX A locus is a region of the genome — usually defined as a one-megabase window or a tighter LD-block-defined window — that contains at least one genome-wide-significant SNP for a given trait. So 13,672 loci means 13,672 region-by-trait pairs where the GWAS crossed the significance threshold. Different traits often hit the same chromosomal region, so the number of unique chromosomal locations is smaller than 13,672, but the trait-loci pairs are what determines how many separate biological things you've discovered.

JORDAN Okay, now the background — why is the diversity question so central?

ALEX Because almost all of human genetics, until quite recently, has been done on people of European ancestry. Roughly 80 percent of GWAS participants historically have been European, even though Europeans are about 16 percent of the global population. There are two distinct problems this creates. The first is a power problem: variants that are common in African or East Asian populations and rare in Europeans simply will not reach significance in a European-only study. The second is a generalizability problem: polygenic risk scores trained on European data perform substantially worse when applied to African or Hispanic patients, because the LD structure differs and the effect-size estimates are biased by what was visible in the discovery cohort. Both problems compound any modifier-gene work in CF, because CF cohorts are even more European-skewed than general-population biobanks. Verma and colleagues come at this with the largest US-based diverse phenome-wide GWAS to date.

JORDAN How did they do it?

ALEX They genotyped 635,969 MVP participants, ran ancestry inference using a project-then-classify approach — standard in the field, similar to what we covered in the All of Us episode — and then ran ancestry-stratified GWAS for 2,068 traits derived from the VA electronic health record. The traits include phecodes, lab values, anthropometric measures, and disease endpoints. They then ran trans-ancestry meta-analyses combining the per-ancestry summary statistics. After identifying significant loci, they applied SuSiE-style fine-mapping to nominate causal variants. The pipeline is essentially the modern dosed version of the standard biobank GWAS recipe — REGENIE or SAIGE per ancestry, fixed-effects or MR-MEGA-style meta across ancestries, statistical fine-mapping at every credible locus. They publish the full GWAS atlas as summary statistics, openly, which is the most directly usable thing about the paper for any downstream work.

JORDAN Push back on something.

ALEX The sex composition. MVP is roughly 90 percent male. That is the nature of the US veteran population — but it means that for any sex-dimorphic trait — bone density, autoimmune diseases that skew female, reproductive endpoints — the MVP architecture is going to look different from a balanced-sex biobank. Anyone porting an MVP signal to a more balanced cohort like UK Biobank should account for that explicitly, and the paper does discuss it but doesn't fully resolve it.

JORDAN Results — what did they find?

ALEX Three numbers anchor the paper. First, they identified 13,672 genomic risk loci across the 2,068 traits — call that, as a coarse benchmark, about 6.6 risk loci per trait on average, dominated of course by traits like height and lipid levels that contribute hundreds of loci each. Second, 1,608 of those loci — about 12 percent — were only significant after including non-European populations in the meta-analysis. Those are loci that any European-only study, no matter how large, would have missed. Third, fine-mapping nominated causal variants at 6,318 distinct association signals across 613 traits, and a third of those — 2,069 fine-mapped causal signals — were identified in participants from non-European populations. They also report that the broad genetic architecture — the average effect sizes, the allele-frequency spectrum of contributing variants, the polygenicity — is broadly similar across ancestries, which is a hopeful empirical finding for the generalizability of any modifier framework, although the specific causal alleles often differ.

JORDAN That last part matters for us. If the architecture is similar across ancestries but the causal alleles differ, what does that mean for a CF cohort that's Northern-European-skewed?

ALEX It means we should expect that the modifier biology — the genes, the pathways, even the rough effect sizes — is likely transferable across CF ancestry groups, but the specific causal SNPs are probably not. So a CFGP-discovered SNP in a Northern European CF patient may not be the variant that carries the same biological effect in an African American or Hispanic CF patient — even if the underlying gene is the same. This argues for a gene-based and pathway-based reporting frame for any CF PheWAS we might propose, not a SNP-based one. SNP-based reporting risks looking ancestry-narrow even when the underlying biology is universal.

JORDAN One concrete research direction this paper opens up?

ALEX One direction would be to ancestry-stratify any future CFFPR-anchored PheWAS at the gene-and-pathway level rather than the SNP level, with explicit pre-registration of how non-European-ancestry CF patients are weighted in discovery versus replication. In plain English: when we test a candidate CF modifier gene against the registry phenome, we report the gene-level result separately for European, Hispanic, and African American CF subgroups, and we treat the comparison across those subgroups as the primary deliverable rather than treating non-European subgroups as an afterthought. Formal one-sentence version: ancestry-stratified gene-burden and pathway-level PheWAS of CFTR-modifier candidate loci against pruned CFFPR phecodes, with non-European-ancestry CF subgroups powered as primary analyses rather than sensitivity checks, and with cross-ancestry concordance reported as the headline metric.

JORDAN Conditional on that direction — what does Verma close, what stays open, what would we have to verify?

ALEX It closes the empirical question of whether ancestry-stratified architecture is broadly similar — yes, it appears to be, at population scale. It opens the question of whether that finding holds for CF specifically, which is a far more genetically homogeneous starting point because CFTR pathogenic variants are themselves ancestry-skewed. It also opens the operational question of whether CFFPR phenotype curation is consistent across ancestry strata; if Black CF patients are recruited into the registry through different clinical pathways than white patients, the phenotype labels themselves may carry an ancestry confounder, which would corrupt any cross-ancestry comparison. What we would need to verify: the ancestry breakdown of CFFPR participants — including the actual sample size in non-European strata, which we have not personally inspected; whether CFFPR phenotyping protocols have been audited for ancestry-related bias; and whether the CFGP WGS arm has comparable coverage in non-European participants — at small N, sub-stratifying may simply be statistically infeasible for the rarer ancestries. None of those three are answered yet.
