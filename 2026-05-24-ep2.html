Blurb

Varona, Legarra, Toro, and Vitezica (2018) review how to incorporate dominance and epistasis into genomic-selection models, working through the additive baseline, the biological-versus-statistical parameterizations, GBLUP and its dominance and epistasis extensions, the orthogonal NOIA framework, Bayesian and RKHS variants, and the empirical track record across cattle, pig, and poultry datasets. The empirical picture is mixed: non-additive variance components exist but are usually small, trait-specific, and rarely improve additive breeding-value accuracy by much. The proposed direction this episode suggests is fitting an orthogonal variance-component model partitioning CF severity variance into CFTR additive, CFTR within-locus dominance, genome-wide polygenic additive, and CFTR-by-genome epistatic components. The open question is whether a 5,000-patient cohort gives enough power to estimate dominance and epistasis components meaningfully, and whether the compound-heterozygous structure of CFTR genotypes is even compatible with the standard A/a allele coding the models assume.

Plain-English glossary

  • Additive effect — each copy of a variant shifts the trait by the same amount.
  • Allele — one of the DNA letter copies at a locus; humans carry two.
  • Bayes A/B/C — Bayesian shrinkage flavors for SNP-effect estimation, differing in their prior on the SNP-effect distribution.
  • Breeding value — the additive genetic value a parent transmits to offspring on average.
  • Compound heterozygote — an individual carrying two different variants at the same gene, one on each chromosome.
  • Dominance — deviation from additivity within a locus; whether the heterozygote behaves like one homozygote rather than midway.
  • EGBLUP — extended GBLUP, a relationship-matrix model that adds an epistasis term.
  • Epistasis — interaction between loci; effect of variant at locus 1 depends on alleles at locus 2.
  • GBLUP — genomic best linear unbiased prediction; uses a genomic relationship matrix to predict breeding values.
  • Genomic relationship matrix (GRM) — matrix capturing how genetically similar every pair of individuals is, computed from SNPs.
  • Genomic selection — using genotype-to-trait models to predict which unphenotyped animals will be best breeders.
  • Hadamard product — element-wise multiplication of matrices, used to build epistasis relationship matrices.
  • Heritability — fraction of trait variance due to genetic differences; narrow-sense is additive only, broad-sense includes non-additive.
  • Heterosis (hybrid vigor) — crossbred offspring outperforming either purebred parent.
  • Heterozygous — carrying two different alleles at a locus.
  • Kernel — mathematical object defining similarity between two items in some space.
  • Linkage disequilibrium (LD) — non-random co-occurrence of alleles at nearby loci.
  • Locus — a particular spot in the DNA.
  • NOIA — natural and orthogonal interactions parameterization (Alvarez-Castro and Carlborg) that produces orthogonal additive, dominance, and epistasis variance components.
  • Orthogonality (in variance components) — components do not share variance with each other, so each is independently interpretable.
  • RKHS — reproducing kernel Hilbert space; family of kernel-based regression methods generalizing GBLUP.
  • SNP — single nucleotide polymorphism, single-letter DNA variant common in the population.

Methods to lift

  • Biological vs. statistical parameterization — be explicit which one is being reported; the variance components are different, and the conversion formulas are spelled out in the paper.
  • NOIA orthogonal parameterization — Alvarez-Castro and Carlborg's framework for producing additive, dominance, and epistasis variance components that do not contaminate each other.
  • EGBLUP — Jiang and Reif, Martini et al. extended GBLUP for adding an epistasis kernel via the Hadamard product of additive GRMs.
  • Vitezica et al. 2017 full orthogonal model — formulation that lets you go to AAD, ADD, DDD higher-order interactions while preserving orthogonality.
  • Mate-allocation prediction (E(G_ij)) — formula for the expected genetic value of offspring of mating between two specific candidates under a dominance-aware model.
  • Bayesian shrinkage menu — Bayes A, B, C, plus de los Campos double-exponential and Erbe et al. mixture-of-distributions priors as alternatives to Gaussian SNP-effect priors.

What to verify first

  • Whether 5,000 patients gives meaningful power to estimate dominance and epistasis variance components — dominance estimation typically demands sample sizes much larger than additive estimation.
  • Whether the compound-heterozygous structure of CFTR (most patients carry two different variants) is compatible with the standard A/a allele coding the models assume, or whether a variant-class severity coding (minimal-function vs. residual-function) is required instead.
  • Whether existing CF twin/sibling studies (Tümmler cohort and related) have ever reported a formal additive plus non-additive variance decomposition — if not, that absence is itself a publishable gap.
  • Whether the statistical machinery, built for selection of future breeders, is interpretable when reframed for biological-modifier identification rather than prediction.
  • Whether the CFF SNP density and imputation quality place the data in linkage disequilibrium with the candidate causal modifier sites.

ALEX Welcome back. Episode two of three for May 24th, 2026. Today's paper is "Non-additive Effects in Genomic Selection" by Luis Varona, Andres Legarra, Miguel Toro, and Zulma Vitezica, published in Frontiers in Genetics in March 2018. It is a review written for the animal-breeding world, not the human-disease world. We are reading it because the math it describes — how to partition genetic variance into additive, dominance, and epistasis components — is the same math we would need if we ever wanted to ask whether a CFTR variant interacts with anything else in the genome rather than just adding to it.

JORDAN Before we touch a single equation, can you walk me through the words? I have heard "additive," "dominance," "epistasis," "GBLUP," "heterosis," "breeding value." None of them are in my working vocabulary.

ALEX Let me build them slowly. A locus is a particular spot in the DNA. At a locus you can have two copies — one from each parent — and each copy is called an allele. Suppose at one locus there is a common letter and a rarer letter, call them A and a. A person can be AA, Aa, or aa. Additive effect means each copy of the rarer allele shifts the trait by the same amount — so going from AA to Aa adds one unit, going from Aa to aa adds another unit. Dominance is the deviation from that clean additive pattern: it asks whether the heterozygote Aa looks more like AA, more like aa, or sits in between. If Aa looks identical to AA, the A allele is fully dominant. Epistasis is interaction between loci, not within one locus — it asks whether the effect of a variant at locus 1 depends on which alleles you carry at locus 2. Heritability is the fraction of trait variance in a population that is due to genetic differences. Within heritability, narrow-sense heritability counts only the additive part, and broad-sense heritability includes the dominance and epistasis parts too. Breeding value is a livestock-breeding term: it is the genetic value a parent will transmit on average to its offspring, and it is mathematically the additive piece. Heterosis, sometimes called hybrid vigor, is the phenomenon where crossing two purebred lines produces offspring that outperform either parent line — it is the practical evidence that non-additive effects exist. GBLUP stands for genomic best linear unbiased prediction, the workhorse statistical method that uses a genome-wide relationship matrix to predict breeding values. SNP again is a single-letter DNA variant. GRM is a genomic relationship matrix, a matrix that captures how genetically similar every pair of individuals is, computed from their SNPs. Kernel is a mathematical object that defines similarity between two objects in some space — when you use a GRM as a kernel, you are saying "people who share more SNPs are more similar." RKHS, reproducing kernel Hilbert space, is the family of methods that generalize this idea. Hold all of that and we will pull them out as we need them.

JORDAN Why was this paper written? What problem is it solving?

ALEX Livestock breeding moved to genomic selection roughly fifteen years before this paper. Genomic selection means: genotype your cattle or pigs across tens of thousands of SNPs, build a statistical model that maps SNP genotypes to a trait — milk yield, growth rate, disease resistance — and then use that model to predict which young, unphenotyped animals will be the best breeders. By 2018 the standard implementations of genomic selection were almost entirely additive. They assumed each SNP contributed an independent additive effect, summed them, and predicted breeding value. Varona and colleagues are arguing this leaves real signal on the table. Three reasons they list: first, modeling dominance and epistasis explicitly may increase the accuracy of breeding-value prediction. Second, modeling dominance lets you do mate allocation — figure out which specific dam-and-sire pair would produce the best offspring, not just rank parents independently. Third, modeling non-additive variance lets you design crossbreeding schemes that intentionally exploit heterosis. So the unknown they are tackling is: what statistical machinery exists, and what is missing, for routinely including non-additive effects in a genomic-selection pipeline? Before this paper, the literature was scattered across many different formulations using inconsistent notation — the review is partly an attempt to unify the bookkeeping.

JORDAN How do they actually go about it?

ALEX The structure is methodological synthesis rather than new experimentation. They start with the simplest additive SNP model, then build up. The baseline model writes a phenotype as the population mean plus a sum across SNPs of the SNP's additive effect times the individual's genotype code, plus residual noise. They then add a dominance term — for each SNP, a coefficient times an indicator for whether the individual is heterozygous. From there they explain the critical distinction between what they call the "biological" parameterization and the "statistical" parameterization. Biological effects are the genotypic values directly. Statistical effects are reframed as breeding values plus dominance deviations, which is the Fisher 1918 partition that defines heritability. The two parameterizations give the same predictions but split the variance differently, and they show how to convert between them. They then move from per-SNP effects to relationship-matrix formulations. Here is where GBLUP enters: instead of estimating millions of SNP coefficients, you compute a genomic relationship matrix from the SNPs, treat individual genetic values as a multivariate Gaussian whose covariance is the relationship matrix times the additive variance, and estimate one variance component. For dominance you build a second relationship matrix encoding heterozygote sharing, and for epistasis you build pairwise — and in principle higher-order — interaction matrices using Hadamard products of the additive and dominance matrices. They walk through several named approaches — EGBLUP, the NOIA (natural and orthogonal interactions) parameterization of Alvarez-Castro and Carlborg, the Vitezica et al. 2017 orthogonal model — and they explain that orthogonality matters because non-orthogonal partitions inflate variance estimates in ways that make the components uninterpretable. They also cover Bayesian shrinkage flavors — Bayes A, Bayes B, Bayes C — and reproducing-kernel-Hilbert-space regression as alternatives. ALEX pushes back on one design choice: the paper assumes throughout that the SNP set is dense enough to be in linkage disequilibrium with most of the causal variants. That assumption can break for rare variants and for any application where the markers are not capturing the truly causal sites, and the review does not dwell on what happens to non-additive variance estimates when that assumption fails.

JORDAN So what did they actually find when people tried these models on real data?

ALEX The empirical picture is mixed. They cite Jiang and colleagues 2017 finding a negligible percentage of variation explained by dominance for productive life in Holstein cattle. They cite Ertl and colleagues 2014 finding dominance accounted for up to 39 percent of total genetic variation for somatic cell score in Fleckvieh cattle. They cite Aliloo and colleagues 2016 as the rare case where including dominance meaningfully increased the accuracy of additive breeding-value prediction. For epistasis the picture is even more mixed — most cited studies found small or negligible epistatic variance once additive effects were properly accounted for. They cite Hu and colleagues 2016 finding no predictive improvement from including imprinting effects, while Guo and colleagues 2016 estimated imprinting at 1.3 to 1.4 percent of phenotypic variation in pigs, and Jiang 2017 reported imprinting effects above 20 percent for reproductive traits like pregnancy or conception rate in dairy cattle. So the pattern is: non-additive variance components exist, they are highly trait-specific, and across most production traits the additive part dominates. The honest summary the authors give is that the increase in additive breeding-value accuracy from adding dominance is usually scarce.

ALEX What Varona and colleagues close for us is the statistical recipe — the partition is well-defined, the relationship-matrix formulation makes it tractable at biobank scale, and the orthogonality conditions are documented. They give us a vocabulary and a reference list. What they leave open is everything specific to the cystic fibrosis application. We would need to check, first, whether the CFF cohort of roughly 5,000 patients gives any meaningful statistical power to detect dominance or epistasis variance — what we would need to check is the typical sample-size scaling for non-additive variance-component estimation, because the rule of thumb for dominance estimation is sample sizes much larger than for additive estimation. Second, we would need to check whether the CFTR locus structure even supports a clean within-locus dominance model — most CFTR patients are compound heterozygotes carrying two different variants, and the standard dominance code assumes two alleles A and a, not a continuous variant-effect spectrum. Fourth, the Varona paper is in livestock where the breeding-value estimation question dominates. For us the question is association, not selection of future breeders, and we would need to check whether the same statistical machinery is interpretable when the goal is identifying biological modifiers rather than predicting offspring performance.
