Blurb

Li, Strug, and the North American CF Modifier Gene Consortium published a 2011 Clinical Genetics paper running genome-wide PCA and STRUCTURE analyses on 3,076 unrelated North American CF patients drawn from the Hopkins, UNC, Case Western, and Toronto Sick Kids cohorts. The paper established that the North American CF research population is not homogeneously Caucasian — admixed African-Caucasian, Mexican-Caucasian, and Indian-Caucasian individuals are present at analytically consequential proportions, the substructure is comparable across the three consortium studies, and neither geography nor CFTR mutation type explains it. The operational consequence — every CF GWAS must include ancestry principal components as covariates — has been the default in the field for fifteen years and underwrites every modifier-genetics paper from Wright 2011 through Faino 2025. The proposed direction the episode suggests, conditional on later inspection of CFGP variant calls and CFFPR phenotype linkage, is a CFTR PheWAS that reports ancestry-stratified modifier-locus results as a primary outcome rather than a sensitivity check, anchored to a contemporary reference panel (gnomAD v4 or 1000 Genomes phase 3) rather than HapMap. The open question is whether the CFGP cohort's 2026 ancestry composition has shifted enough from the 2011 NACFM composition — through universal newborn screening expansion in the US and Canada — that the older substructure characterization is out of date.

Plain-English glossary

  • Admixture / admixed individual — genetic mixture from two previously separated ancestral populations; an admixed person carries DNA from multiple ancestral groups.
  • Ancestry-stratified analysis — running the same statistical test separately within each ancestry subgroup and reporting per-stratum effect sizes as primary results.
  • EIGENSTRAT — a specific software implementation of PCA designed for genetic data, named because it uses eigenvalue decomposition.
  • HapMap — early reference dataset of genotyped individuals from defined populations (Yoruba, Han Chinese, Japanese, Northern and Western Europeans), used as ancestry reference panel before the larger 1000 Genomes Project.
  • Identity-by-descent (IBD) — measurement of how much of two people's genomes is identical because of recent shared ancestry; pairwise IBD is used to remove cryptic relatives before population-structure analysis.
  • LD-pruning — removing variants in tight linkage disequilibrium with each other so that PCA reflects ancestry rather than redundancy at strong-LD regions.
  • Population structure / stratification / substructure — situation in which a study cohort is a mixture of subgroups with different genetic ancestries, which can confound a GWAS if disease prevalence also differs across the subgroups.
  • Principal Components Analysis (PCA) — statistical technique that reduces a high-dimensional dataset (here, hundreds of thousands of SNP genotypes per person) to a small number of summary axes capturing the largest sources of variation; on genome-wide SNPs, the top axes track genetic ancestry.
  • Reference panel — external dataset of genotyped individuals from defined ancestries used to anchor PCA axes or model-based ancestry inference.
  • SNP (Single Nucleotide Polymorphism) — single-letter DNA variant common in the population.
  • Spurious association — a statistical finding that looks real but is actually caused by confounding, not by the proposed mechanism.
  • STRUCTURE — model-based ancestry inference program that assumes each individual is a mixture of K underlying ancestral populations and estimates the mixing proportions.

Methods to lift

  • EIGENSTRAT PCA on LD-pruned genome-wide SNPs with HapMap or contemporary equivalent (gnomAD v4 / 1000 Genomes) reference panels — anchors principal-component axes to known continental ancestries and yields per-individual ancestry coordinates suitable as GWAS covariates.
  • STRUCTURE model-based ancestry inference as cross-check — run in parallel with PCA to confirm the substructure pattern is robust to method choice; disagreement between PCA and STRUCTURE flags edge-case admixed individuals worth manual review.
  • Pairwise IBD filtering before PCA — remove cryptic-relative pairs so the principal components reflect population ancestry rather than family relatedness.
  • Self-reported versus genotype-inferred ancestry comparison — document the disagreement rate and use genotype-inferred ancestry as the primary measure; self-report is supplementary.
  • Geography-and-genotype substructure decomposition — explicitly test whether substructure is driven by recruitment center (geographic confounding) or by CFTR-genotype-specific founder distributions before concluding it reflects general ancestry; in the Li 2011 cohort, neither was the driver, so the substructure was generic.
  • Cross-cohort substructure comparability check — compare per-cohort PCA distributions across the constituent studies of a multi-site consortium; comparable distributions justify pooled analysis with adjustment, divergent distributions block pooled analysis until covariate harmonization is fixed.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: a CFTR-modifier-locus PheWAS in the CFGP cohort that pre-specifies a contemporary genetic-ancestry partition (using gnomAD v4 ancestry classifier or 1000 Genomes phase 3 reference panel rather than HapMap), reports per-ancestry-stratum modifier-locus tests separately for each ancestry group with sufficient case counts, reports cross-ancestry concordance of effect sizes for each candidate modifier locus as a primary headline metric, and treats the ancestry-pooled analysis (with PC covariates) as a secondary integrative result rather than the primary finding.

What to verify first

  • The contemporary ancestry composition of the CFGP cohort using a modern reference panel — newborn screening has expanded enrollment of non-European-ancestry CF patients meaningfully since 2011 and the Li 2011 substructure characterization may understate current diversity.
  • Per-ancestry case counts at the candidate modifier loci (SLC26A9, TCF7L2, PTMA, EHF/APIP, CHP2, chr20q13.2 region) — within-stratum power may be insufficient for some loci even at the best-case pooled n ≈ 5,000.
  • The ancestry composition of the CFFPR phecode catalog — registries can over- or under-represent specific ancestries relative to the underlying CF population, and the phecode comparator base needs to be ancestry-aware.
  • Whether any newer CF-specific ancestry-inference work has been published on the WGS substrate (Polineni 2018 GMS analysis used the same Toronto-led ancestry pipeline; Zhou 2023 stratified by ancestry but did not publish a Li-2011-style standalone substructure characterization).
  • Whether the consortium has a preferred ancestry-classifier choice; replicating their pipeline is preferable to building a parallel one.
  • Whether reporting ancestry-stratified results as primary creates publication-review friction with consortium policy on cross-cohort harmonization.

Strug at the Hospital for Sick Children in Toronto, published in Clinical Genetics in February 2011, titled "Understanding the population structure of North American patients with cystic fibrosis." This is a foundational methods paper for the entire modern CF modifier genetics enterprise. It is the paper that established that a CF cohort cannot be treated as a homogeneous Caucasian population for genetic analysis. Every CF GWAS that came after — Wright 2011, Blackman 2013, Aksit 2020 and 2022, Zhou 2023, Faino 2025 — depends on the ancestry-adjustment infrastructure this paper put in place.

JORDAN Slow down. There were technical terms in there I will need defined before we go further.

ALEX Yes. Population structure, also called population stratification or substructure, is the situation in which a study cohort is not a single homogeneous population but is actually a mixture of subgroups with different genetic ancestries. This matters because the allele frequencies of common variants differ across ancestries — sometimes substantially — and if the disease being studied also differs in prevalence across those subgroups for non-genetic reasons, you can get a spurious genetic association that is really a marker of ancestry rather than a marker of disease. Spurious is the technical term — a finding that looks real but is not. Principal components analysis, abbreviated PCA, is a statistical technique that takes a high-dimensional dataset — here, hundreds of thousands of SNP genotypes per person — and reduces it to a small number of summary axes, called principal components, that capture the largest sources of variation. When you run PCA on genome-wide SNP genotypes, the top principal components track genetic ancestry. EIGENSTRAT is one specific software implementation of PCA designed for genetic data, named because it uses eigenvalue decomposition. STRUCTURE is a separate, model-based program that infers ancestry by assuming each individual is a mixture of K underlying ancestral populations and estimating the mixing proportions. Identity-by-descent, abbreviated IBD, is a measurement of how much of two people's genomes is identical because of recent shared ancestry; pairwise IBD is used to remove cryptic relatives from a study before population-structure analysis. SNP, single nucleotide polymorphism, is a single-letter DNA variant common in the population. Admixture is the genetic mixture that results when two previously separated populations interbreed; an admixed individual carries DNA from multiple ancestral groups. HapMap is an early reference dataset of genotyped individuals from defined populations — Yoruba from Nigeria, Han Chinese from Beijing, Japanese from Tokyo, Northern and Western Europeans — used as ancestry reference panels in older studies before the larger 1000 Genomes Project. North American CF Modifier Gene Consortium is the umbrella organization that combined three CF-research cohorts — the University of North Carolina cohort run by Knowles, the Case Western cohort run by Drumm, the Johns Hopkins cohort run by Cutting, plus a Canadian arm centered at Toronto Sick Kids run by Strug — into a unified modifier-genetics resource. NCT00037778 is the ClinicalTrials.gov identifier for that consortium. Now we can read the paper.

JORDAN Okay. What problem motivated the paper?

ALEX The dominant assumption among CF clinicians and researchers in the 2000s was that CF is essentially a disease of European-ancestry populations, and that CF research cohorts therefore inherit a homogeneous Caucasian genetic background by assembly. That assumption was operationally convenient — it meant CF GWAS studies could skip the ancestry-adjustment steps that other field GWAS were starting to take seriously after several embarrassing population-stratification-confounded findings in the late 2000s, where signals that looked like real disease loci turned out to be markers of ancestry being aliased into apparent disease association by an unbalanced case-control design. The Toronto group around Strug, who was simultaneously serving as the statistical lead for the CF Modifier Gene Consortium, started seeing ethnic complexity in the consortium's recruitment data that did not match the homogeneous-Caucasian assumption. Self-reported ancestry was suggestive of admixture in some patients, and the Hopkins Twin and Sibling Study and Toronto sibling samples included families with non-European-ancestry backgrounds. There was a second motivation that mattered for the consortium specifically. The North American CF Modifier Gene Consortium was actively running modifier-gene GWAS on its pooled samples — the same pooled samples that two months later would be reported in Wright 2011 as the chr11p13 EHF/APIP discovery — and the credibility of those modifier-gene findings depended on whether the consortium had quietly been ignoring a confounder. So the question the paper poses is empirical and binary: is there detectable population substructure in the North American CF research population at the level of genome-wide SNP genotypes? The follow-on question is whether the substructure, if present, is comparable across the three constituent cohorts of the consortium — because if it is not, then the consortium cannot pool data the way it has been pooling it. And the third question is whether the substructure is explained by something obvious — recruitment-center geography, CFTR-genotype-specific founder distribution — that would let the consortium adjust for it indirectly without needing genome-wide PCA.

JORDAN How did they actually do it?

ALEX They assembled genome-wide SNP array genotypes on 3,076 unrelated North American CF patients drawn from the three consortium cohorts — the Hopkins, UNC, and Case Western contingent of NACFM, plus the Toronto sibling sample. Standard QC was applied — variants were filtered for call rate, minor allele frequency, and Hardy-Weinberg deviation, and samples were filtered for genotyping quality and pairwise IBD to remove cryptic relatives so that PCA was not dominated by family relatedness rather than population structure. They LD-pruned the SNP set so that the principal components reflected ancestry rather than redundancy at strong-LD regions. They ran EIGENSTRAT PCA on the pruned LD-independent SNP set with HapMap reference populations included to anchor the principal-component axes to known continental ancestries — Yoruba, East Asian, and European. They ran STRUCTURE in parallel as a model-based ancestry-inference cross-check. They compared genotype-inferred ancestry to self-reported ancestry, and they tested whether population substructure within the cohort was explained by either geography (which clinic or center recruited the patient) or by CFTR mutation type (since some CFTR variants have ancestry-specific founder distributions). They then asked whether the population substructure was comparable across the three consortium studies. ALEX flags one design choice as worth pushing back on — the LD-pruning step and the choice of HapMap as reference panel were standard at the time but have been superseded by 1000 Genomes and now gnomAD ancestry frameworks; any contemporary repeat of this analysis on the CFGP whole-genome data would use a different reference panel and might give somewhat different ancestry assignments at admixed boundaries.

JORDAN What did they find — and did they answer their question?

ALEX They found that the North American CF research population is not, in fact, homogeneously Caucasian. The principal components analysis cleanly separated a majority European-ancestry cluster from smaller admixed groups — patients with admixed African-Caucasian ancestry, patients with admixed Mexican-Caucasian ancestry, and patients with admixed Indian-Caucasian ancestry — at proportions large enough to be analytically consequential. Self-reported ancestry agreed with genotype-inferred ancestry in most cases but disagreed often enough that genotype-inferred ancestry was the more reliable measure. The substructure was comparable across the three consortium studies — meaning no single study contributed a disproportionate share of the admixed cohort, and the consortium can pool data only if pooled analyses use ancestry adjustment. Geography did not explain the substructure — admixture was not concentrated at any single recruitment center. CFTR mutation type did not explain the substructure either — the ancestry-specific CFTR allele patterns that exist in larger populations were not strong enough in this cohort to drive the principal components. The headline conclusion is operational: any genetic association study using North American CF patients must detect, estimate, and adjust for population substructure to guard against spurious findings. Did they answer their question? Yes. The paper made the assumption of CF cohort homogeneity untenable from 2011 forward, and every CF GWAS published after this paper includes ancestry principal components as covariates by default. The headline number worth carrying forward is the 3,076-person size of the analyzable consortium cohort in 2011 — a baseline figure that helps us understand how much the field has scaled by 2026, when the CFGP cohort with whole-genome sequencing on roughly 5,000 patients is the contemporary substrate for the same kind of population-genetics question on a richer variant catalog.

ALEX Plainly: the days of pooled-analysis-with-PC-covariates as the only ancestry strategy are running out, and a 2026-and-later CFTR PheWAS should report ancestry-stratified results as a primary outcome rather than as a sensitivity check.
