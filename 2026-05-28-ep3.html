How did this episode land for you?

One-paragraph blurb

Collaco and colleagues used 1,535 subjects in 755 families from the U.S. Cystic Fibrosis Twin and Sibling Study (Johns Hopkins; PI Garry Cutting), linked to the CFF Patient Registry for treatment dates, venues, and respiratory cultures, to ask two practice-defining questions: (1) does the venue of IV antibiotic delivery (hospital vs. home) affect long-term FEV₁ trajectory after a pulmonary exacerbation, and (2) what is the optimal duration of therapy? Across 1,278 IV courses in 479 individuals, baseline FEV₁ change after treatment was −3.3 percentage points (95% CI −3.9 to −2.6) in the hospital group and −3.5 (95% CI −4.5 to −2.5) in the home group, t-test p=0.69 and adjusted regression p=0.91. Day-by-day FEV₁ recovery during therapy continued through day 8, plateaued at day 10, and did not improve further out to day 22 — suggesting standard 14-21-day courses provide no additional benefit over 8-10 days for most patients. A secondary finding: greater sick-decline (FEV₁ drop with exacerbation) predicted worse long-term decline even when acute recovery looked complete (p<0.001). The proposed direction for our work: a Collaco-style day-by-day improvement-curve analysis stratified by genotype at known CF lung-function modifier loci (SLC9A3, EHF, MUC4, MUC20, SLC6A14), to ask whether the population-average duration plateau hides genotype-dependent subgroups.

Plain-English glossary

  • Baseline change — Collaco's primary long-term outcome: (new baseline FEV₁ after exacerbation) − (baseline FEV₁ before exacerbation).
  • B. cepacia / Burkholderia cepacia complex — bacterial pathogen group particularly problematic in CF lungs; presence/absence used as a covariate in Collaco's regression.
  • CFF Patient Registry (CFFPR) — the U.S. Cystic Fibrosis Foundation's care-center registry; annual data submission from every U.S. CF care center, currently ~30k patients with longitudinal follow-up.
  • CFTR — the cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator gene; encodes an epithelial chloride channel.
  • F508del — the most common pathogenic CFTR variant; a 3-bp deletion removing phenylalanine at position 508 of the protein; ~50% of U.S. CF patients are homozygous.
  • FEV₁ (forced expiratory volume in 1 second) — volume of air a person can forcibly exhale in the first second of a forced expiratory maneuver; primary CF lung-disease outcome.
  • F508del homozygote — a patient carrying two copies of the F508del variant.
  • GEE (generalized estimating equations) — regression framework that adjusts standard errors for within-subject correlation when each subject contributes multiple observations.
  • Hospital vs. home IV — site of intravenous antibiotic delivery for a CF pulmonary exacerbation; home delivery typically via PICC line.
  • Immediate recovery — Collaco's short-term outcome: (post-therapy FEV₁) − (pre-therapy FEV₁).
  • Knudson percent predicted — the reference equation Collaco used to convert raw FEV₁ to percent of a healthy reference; superseded by GLI-2012 in modern practice.
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa — gram-negative bacterium chronically colonizing most adult CF lungs; status used as a covariate.
  • Pulmonary exacerbation (PEx) — acute worsening of CF respiratory symptoms prompting treatment intensification; in Collaco's dataset, defined by treating-clinician designation in the CFFPR.
  • Sick decline — Collaco's exacerbation-magnitude outcome: (pre-therapy FEV₁) − (baseline FEV₁).
  • Spirometry — pulmonary function test measuring exhaled volumes and flows.
  • U.S. CF Twin and Sibling Study — Johns Hopkins-based CF research cohort enrolling twin and sibling pairs in which both members have CF; designed for modifier-gene mapping; one of two foundational CF modifier-discovery cohorts (the other being the Canadian CF Gene Modifier Study).

Methods to lift

  • Twin/Sibling Study ↔ CFF Patient Registry linkage — pull individual-level treatment dates, venues, and respiratory cultures from the registry for subjects enrolled in the research cohort; this linkage is the methodological key.
  • Pulmonary-function-around-PEx index construction — define four FEV₁ measurements per exacerbation (baseline before, pre-therapy, post-therapy, new baseline after) with averaging windows, and derive sick decline, immediate recovery, and baseline change from these.
  • Day-by-day improvement curve — plot (FEV₁ on day-of-therapy − pre-therapy FEV₁) normalized to baseline FEV₁ and the population mean, against day of therapy, to recover the dose-response trajectory.
  • GEE regression clustered by individual — handles multiple courses per subject; also try clustering by family for sibship/twin structure as sensitivity.
  • Stepwise variable selection with p < 0.05 retention, except for age, sex, and total days of therapy held in by design in venue comparisons.
  • Paired hospital-vs-home within-subject analysis — for subjects with both course types at different times, use the most recent of each as a paired comparison.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage:

Repeat Collaco's day-by-day FEV₁-improvement-curve analysis on contemporary CFFPR-recorded IV antibiotic courses among CF patients in the CFF whole-genome-sequenced cohort (~5,000 patients), stratified by genotype at known CF lung-function modifier loci — primarily SLC9A3 (chr 5p15.33), EHF / APIP (chr 11p13), MUC4 / MUC20 (chr 3q29), and SLC6A14 (chr X q24). Primary readout: is the day-of-plateau (~day 10 in Collaco's analysis) the same across modifier genotypes, or is it genotype-dependent? Secondary readout: is the magnitude of the day-10 plateau improvement modifier-dependent? Modulator era must be controlled.

What to verify first

  • That the CFFPR of 2026 still captures venue and exact start/end dates with the granularity required to reproduce Collaco's day-by-day FEV₁ curves; treatment-data fields have changed structure across registry versions and the relevant fields need to be confirmed extant.
  • That the CFF WGS call set provides reliable genotypes at the modifier loci — particularly SLC9A3 chr5p15.33 (low-mappability region for short-read sequencing) and SLC6A14 on chrX (read-depth handling differs).
  • That enough exacerbation events with dense intra-therapy FEV₁ sampling exist in the WGS-sequenced subset specifically — the full CFFPR has plenty, but the 5k-patient WGS subset may not.
  • That modulator-era covariates (ivacaftor monotherapy, lumacaftor/ivacaftor, tezacaftor/ivacaftor, elexacaftor/tezacaftor/ivacaftor) can be coded at the time-of-therapy level — exacerbation phenotypes have changed materially since Collaco's 2003-2007 window.
  • That the published modifier-locus signals (SLC9A3, EHF, MUC4/MUC20, SLC6A14) have been replicated at GWAS-significance in independent CF lung-function GWAS; we should not start from disputed loci.
  • That sex confounding of venue (Collaco saw 66% female in home-only, 49% in hospital-only) still applies in the contemporary cohort — if home IV is now the dominant route in both sexes, the analysis structure may change.

Gap-map: closes / leaves open

  • Closes: that venue (hospital vs home) does not affect long-term FEV₁ change after a CF pulmonary exacerbation in the U.S. cohort; that FEV₁ recovery during IV therapy plateaus around day 8-10 and does not increase out to day 22; that the magnitude of sick decline predicts long-term decline independent of acute recovery; that registry+research-cohort linkage is operationally sufficient for day-resolution lung-function trajectory analysis.
  • Leaves open: whether the duration plateau is uniform across genotypes; whether modifier-locus variation interacts with venue or duration to produce genotype-dependent practice optima; whether sick-decline-as-prognostic-marker maps to identifiable genetic susceptibility; whether modulator-era treatment effects have shifted the day-of-plateau; whether the result generalizes outside the U.S. care system.

We're reading it because — even though this looks at first glance like a clinical-practice paper with no genetics — it is built on the U.S. Cystic Fibrosis Twin and Sibling Study cohort, which is one of the two foundational genetic-modifier discovery resources in CF, and it shows you what that cohort looks like when used for a non-genetic question. JORDAN I'm going to slow you down before we go in. What do I need to know to read this?

ALEX Lots. Let's start with the disease vocabulary. "Cystic fibrosis," CF, is the recessive genetic disease caused by loss-of-function mutations in the CFTR gene — that gene encodes a chloride channel in epithelial cells, and when it's broken, sticky mucus builds up in the lungs, the pancreas, and other organs. "F508del" is the single most common pathogenic CFTR variant — a three-base deletion that removes a phenylalanine at protein position 508. About half of U.S. CF patients carry two copies. "FEV one" — F, E, V, subscript one — is forced expiratory volume in one second, the volume of air you can blow out in the first second of a forced exhalation. It's measured by spirometry and it's the bread and butter outcome for CF lung disease — the lower the FEV one, the more obstructed your airways. It's usually reported as a percentage of what a healthy person of the same age, sex, and height would produce, called percent predicted. The reference equation Collaco uses here — Knudson — is from 1976, and you'll see in any modern CF paper that Knudson has been superseded by GLI-2012, but that doesn't matter for understanding this paper. "Pulmonary exacerbation" — PEx — is an acute worsening of respiratory symptoms in a CF patient that prompts intensification of treatment, usually intravenous antibiotics. The clinical definition is fuzzy and depends on the treating physician's judgment. "Intravenous antibiotic course" can be delivered in two settings — inpatient, meaning the patient is admitted to a hospital and receives the antibiotics there, or outpatient, meaning the patient receives the antibiotics at home, usually via a peripherally inserted central catheter, PICC line.

JORDAN And the cohort vocabulary?

ALEX "U.S. Cystic Fibrosis Twin and Sibling Study" — a research cohort founded at Johns Hopkins by Garry Cutting, designed to enroll twin pairs and sibling pairs in which both members have CF. The point of the design is gene-mapping — twins and siblings share controlled fractions of their genomes, so phenotypic differences within a pair point to non-CFTR modifying factors. The cohort eventually grew to many thousands of individuals. It is one of the two foundational genetic-modifier discovery cohorts in CF, along with the Canadian CF Gene Modifier Study. "CFFPR" or "CFF Patient Registry" is the U.S. Cystic Fibrosis Foundation's care-center registry — every U.S. CF care center submits annual data, and the registry currently holds about thirty thousand patients with longitudinal follow-up. The Twin and Sibling Study and the CFFPR are not the same database, but they are linked: this paper takes Twin-Sibling subjects and pulls their treatment dates, venues, and respiratory cultures from the CFFPR. That linkage is the methodological thing to notice.

JORDAN Anything else?

ALEX One more — "regression with generalized estimating equations," GEE. When you have multiple observations per person, ordinary regression assumes the observations are independent, which they aren't. GEE adjusts standard errors to account for within-person clustering. Collaco uses it because each subject contributes multiple courses of antibiotics.

JORDAN Now — why was this paper written, what was unknown?

ALEX Two unresolved practice questions in CF care. First, does it matter whether intravenous antibiotics for a pulmonary exacerbation are delivered in the hospital versus at home? Hospital delivery is the historical default — more controlled, easier compliance monitoring. Home delivery is cheaper, less disruptive to school and work, and patients prefer it. Several small studies — fewer than a hundred patients each, often single-center — had reported conflicting results on whether long-term lung function trajectory was different. Two studies, both European, had reported that home patients did worse on long-term FEV one one year after the exacerbation. Several other studies had reported no difference. The uncertainty was real and clinically consequential. Second question — what is the optimal duration of intravenous antibiotics? Standard U.S. practice was fourteen to twenty-one days. Was this evidence-based, or was it tradition? Data from ventilator-associated pneumonia in other populations suggested eight days might be as good as fifteen. CF had never been studied head-to-head, in part because randomized trials of antibiotic duration are operationally difficult in this population.

JORDAN How did they tackle it? Sample first.

ALEX Fifteen thirty-five U.S. Twin and Sibling Study subjects in seven hundred fifty-five families. Treatment dates, venue, and respiratory-culture data came from the CFFPR. Analysis was limited to intravenous-antibiotic courses of forty-two days or less clinically designated as pulmonary exacerbations. Starting dates ranged from January 2003 through November 2007. They derived three lung-function indices around each exacerbation — "sick decline," the drop in FEV one from baseline to immediately pre-treatment; "immediate recovery," the rise from pre-treatment to immediately post-treatment; and "baseline change," the difference between the FEV one baseline before the exacerbation and the new FEV one baseline after. Baseline change is the long-term outcome of interest. They classified courses into three groups — hospital-only, home-only, and combination — and ran t-tests, ANOVAs, and stepwise regression with GEE. Final dataset for the venue analysis was twelve hundred seventy-eight courses in four hundred seventy-nine individuals. For the duration analysis they used a separate dataset of twenty-four hundred twenty-six FEV one measurements obtained during therapy across thirteen hundred thirty-one exacerbations in four hundred ninety-two subjects, capped at the first twenty-two days.

JORDAN ALEX, push back on a design choice.

ALEX Sex confounded venue. Sixty-six percent of home-only patients were female; forty-nine percent of hospital-only patients were female. Age also confounded — home patients were older by about six years on average. They handled this with regression adjustment for age, sex, total days of therapy, P. aeruginosa status, B. cepacia status, CFTR genotype, baseline FEV one, and sick decline. The adjustment is reasonable but it cannot fix unmeasured selection — the clinical reasoning that decides "this patient gets home IV, that one gets hospital IV" is patient- and clinician-specific and not in the registry.

JORDAN Results — did they answer the question?

ALEX Yes, on both. Long-term baseline change after an exacerbation was negative three point three percentage points in the hospital group, ninety-five percent CI minus three point nine to minus two point six, six hundred two courses; and negative three point five percentage points in the home group, ninety-five percent CI minus four point five to minus two point five, two hundred thirty-two courses. The t-test p value comparing those two means was zero point six nine. The adjusted regression result was also non-significant — p equals zero point nine one — and the venue variable dropped out of the stepwise model entirely. The same conclusion held in a paired analysis on thirty-two subjects who had both a hospital course and a home course at separate times. So venue does not affect long-term outcome. On duration — they plotted mean improvement in FEV one against day of therapy, corrected for baseline FEV one, and observed that improvement continued through day eight, reached a plateau around day ten, and did not increase further out to day twenty-two. Shorter courses were also not associated with shorter intervals before the next exacerbation, meaning the longer courses were not buying any durability benefit. The headline conclusion — optimal duration may be seven to ten days, much shorter than the fourteen to twenty-one days that was standard practice. One additional important finding — patients with a greater initial drop in FEV one with their exacerbation had worse long-term decline even when their lung function appeared to recover, p less than zero point zero zero one. So the magnitude of sick decline predicts long-term trajectory independent of how well the patient bounces back acutely.

JORDAN One concrete direction for our CF work.

ALEX Plain English. The CFF Patient Registry already captures, for every exacerbation, the venue of treatment and the duration. Combined with longitudinal FEV one measurements and respiratory cultures, the registry is a natural place to ask whether the optimal duration of antibiotics — Collaco's seven-to-ten-day estimate — varies by genetic factors, especially modifier loci that already have replicated associations with CF lung-function severity, like SLC9A3, EHF, MUC4, MUC20, and the SLC6A14 X-linked variant. JORDAN Gap. What does Collaco close, leave open, and what would we need to verify?

ALEX It closes the conceptual question of whether duration-response curves are derivable from registry data, and whether the registry contains the relevant fields. It shows clearly that you can recover the day-by-day improvement trajectory from the dense FEV one measurements collected during inpatient and outpatient IV courses, and that the curve plateaus rather than rises linearly. It also establishes a baseline-FEV-one normalization procedure that the field has used since. What it leaves open — Collaco's analysis is genotype-blind. Subjects are classified by F508del homozygote status, but no other modifier variation is used.
