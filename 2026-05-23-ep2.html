Blurb

Rahit & Tarailo-Graovac (Genes 2020) review the state of genetic-modifier research in rare Mendelian disease — exactly the gap the CFTR PheWAS project lives in. Headline findings: only ~24% of modifier-genetics publications 1998–2019 focused on rare diseases despite rare diseases collectively affecting ~400M people worldwide; OMIM lists only 24 curated rare-Mendelian modifier entries (CFTR's modifier is recorded as TGFB1); and the NGS-era pipeline of variant filtering → pathogenicity-score prioritization (CADD/DANN) → functional-ontology annotation (Gene Ontology, PhenomeNET) → manual expert review is now standard for individual-genome modifier work, with tools like OligoPVP and VarCoPP doing automated multi-feature aggregation. Proposed direction for the CFTR PheWAS: apply a Rahit-style discordant-genotype-matched rare-variant prioritization pipeline to phenotypically discordant Phe508del-homozygote pairs within the CFF WGS cohort. Open question: whether any of the prioritization tools have been benchmarked on rare-Mendelian-modifier data at the scale we'd want to use them at.

Plain-English glossary

  • CADD: Combined Annotation Dependent Depletion — a widely-used variant pathogenicity scoring tool that integrates conservation and functional annotations.
  • CFF WGS cohort: ~5,000 CF patients with whole-genome sequencing data held by the CF Foundation Therapy department.
  • DANN: A deep-learning-based variant pathogenicity scorer; alternative to CADD.
  • Digenic disease: Disease caused jointly by mutations in two genes; classic example, Bardet-Biedl syndrome via BBS1 + BBS10.
  • Epistasis: Gene-gene interaction in which one variant changes the phenotypic effect of another.
  • Expressivity: How variably a disease manifests in different patients who all carry the same disease genotype.
  • Forward genetic screen: Mutate randomly, then look for phenotypic change — used in EMS/ENU mutagenesis in flies, worms, and mice.
  • Gene Ontology (GO): Curated vocabulary describing gene functions, processes, and cellular locations.
  • gnomAD: The Genome Aggregation Database — reference catalog of variants observed in ~141,000 ostensibly healthy people.
  • GWAS: Genome-Wide Association Study.
  • Mendelian disease: Disease caused by mutation in a single gene with a clean inheritance pattern (e.g., CF, sickle cell).
  • MTMR14: A myotubularin-family phosphatase; modifier of centronuclear myopathy.
  • MYH7 / MKL2: Two genes whose combined mutations cause left-ventricular noncompaction (digenic disease) — the NKX2-5-modifier case study in the review.
  • NKX2-5: A cardiac transcription factor; rare allele acts as a modifier that converts unaffected MYH7+MKL2 carriers into severely-affected patients (Gifford 2019 case).
  • NGS (next-generation sequencing): Umbrella term covering WGS, WES, and targeted exome sequencing.
  • OligoPVP / DeepPVP / VarCoPP: Computational tools that prioritize disease-causing variant pairs/triplets using pathogenicity scores plus phenotype-ontology and inheritance-mode features.
  • OMIM: Online Mendelian Inheritance in Man — curated catalog of Mendelian gene-disease links.
  • Orphanet: European rare-disease registry; used by the authors to restrict OMIM modifier list to "rare" entries.
  • PDZD7: Modifier gene for digenic Usher syndrome.
  • Penetrance: Probability a person carrying a known disease genotype actually develops the disease.
  • PhenoModifier: A 2019 manually-curated database of genetic modifiers, separate from OMIM.
  • PhenomeNET: A phenotype-ontology-based variant-prioritization framework.
  • PVP (PhenomeNET Variant Predictor): The base scoring engine on which OligoPVP and DeepPVP are built.
  • SMA (spinal muscular atrophy): Rare Mendelian disease caused by SMN1 loss; SMN2 copy-number acts as a natural dosage modifier.
  • SMN1 / SMN2: Survival motor neuron 1 and 2 — duplicated genes; SMN2 copy number suppresses SMA severity.
  • TGFB1: Transforming Growth Factor Beta 1 — listed in OMIM as the modifier gene for CFTR phenotype 602421 (bronchiectasis with or without elevated sweat chloride).
  • WES (whole-exome sequencing): Sequences only the protein-coding ~1% of the genome.
  • WGS (whole-genome sequencing): Sequences essentially every base of the genome.

Methods to lift

  • Discordant-genotype matched-pair design — find pairs/triplets of relatives or matched individuals with the same primary disease mutation but divergent phenotypes; compare their genome-wide variant profiles for candidate modifiers. (NKX2-5 LVNC case study template.)
  • Multi-feature pathogenicity-score aggregation — combine CADD + DANN + GWAVA + PhenomeNET + inheritance-mode + genotype features through a Random Forest or Deep Artificial Neural Network classifier (OligoPVP / DeepPVP / VarCoPP frameworks).
  • Functional ontology filtering — restrict candidate modifiers to genes within Gene Ontology terms or KEGG pathways related to the primary disease gene's function (e.g., apical-membrane transporters for CFTR).
  • PhenoModifier database cross-reference — query candidate modifiers against PhenoModifier and OMIM modifier entries to distinguish known from novel candidates.
  • Privacy-preserving aggregation methods — homomorphic-encryption tools like HEALER for cross-institution rare-disease cohort pooling when sample-size aggregation is required.

Apply a discordant-genotype-matched rare-variant prioritization pipeline to phenotypically discordant Phe508del-homozygote pairs and triplets within the CFF WGS cohort, using OligoPVP-style or VarCoPP-style multi-feature pathogenicity-score aggregation (CADD + DANN + Gene Ontology + PhenomeNET + inheritance-mode features). Cross-reference candidate modifier variants against the OMIM CFTR-modifier entry (TGFB1), the PhenoModifier database, and the prior CF GWAS modifier-locus list (Wright 2011 chr11p13/chr20q13, Corvol 2015 five-locus meta, Zhou 2023 WGS modifier scan).

What to verify first

  • Whether the CFF WGS cohort has been jointly variant-called and QC-passed at standards adequate for rare-variant prioritization (joint vs per-sample calling materially changes rare-variant counts).
  • Whether the Phe508del-homozygote subset is large enough (likely 2,000–3,000 patients) to define mild-vs-severe discordant pairs after matching on age, sex, ancestry, and treatment era.
  • Whether the prioritization tools (OligoPVP, VarCoPP, custom CADD+PhenomeNET aggregation) have been benchmarked on any rare-Mendelian-modifier dataset at all, and what their false-positive rates look like at the variant-filtering thresholds we'd need.
  • Whether the CFFPR phenotype linkage provides longitudinal depth (FEV1 trajectory, age at first chronic Pseudomonas, age at CFRD onset, BMI percentile trajectory) sufficient to define discordance beyond a single cross-sectional severity snapshot.
  • Whether the PhenoModifier database is well-maintained and currently usable (originally a 2019 publication; need to confirm it is still hosted and curated as of 2026).
  • Whether modulator-era (post-Trikafta, 2019+) phenotype data must be stratified out, because modulator response itself may absorb or alter modifier signal.

The genetics community has spent two decades pouring effort into common-disease GWAS — diabetes, hypertension, schizophrenia — because the sample sizes are gigantic and the statistical methods are well-developed. The rare-disease literature went a different direction: it focused on finding the *primary* gene for each disease, one disease at a time, using family-based linkage and now exome sequencing.
