One-paragraph blurb

Littlejohns and colleagues describe how UK Biobank's hundred-thousand-participant multi-modal imaging study was actually built — four purpose-built centres in Stockport, Newcastle, Reading, and Bristol; identical scanner hardware and frozen pulse sequences across sites; throughput of seventeen imaged participants per centre per day at ninety-five percent attendance; quality-control pass rates above ninety-six percent across brain, cardiac, abdominal, DXA, and carotid modalities; and a continuously-expanding catalog of imaging-derived phenotypes (IDPs) returned to researchers as ready-to-use columns. Roughly forty thousand participants imaged at time of writing, target completion in 2023. The episode reads this as a methods-feasibility paper, not a biological-discovery paper, and proposes a conditional CFTR PheWAS direction: a feasibility audit of CFFPR-linked imaging coverage and harmonization for chest, sinus, abdominal, and DXA modalities, asking whether any subset of CF-relevant imaging endpoints could plausibly serve as quantitative phenotypes for a modifier-locus PheWAS. The open question is whether CFFPR has linked imaging at the patient level at all, and at what coverage and harmonization quality.

Plain-English glossary

  • Carotid ultrasound — a non-invasive scan of the neck artery (the carotid) that measures the thickness of its inner two layers (intima-media thickness), used as a marker of early atherosclerosis.
  • DXA (dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry) — a low-dose X-ray scan that measures bone mineral density and body composition (fat mass and lean mass).
  • fMRI (functional MRI) — magnetic resonance imaging tuned to detect blood-oxygenation changes that track brain activity over time.
  • IDP (imaging-derived phenotype) — a single number extracted from an imaging scan by an automated pipeline (e.g., hippocampal volume in mm³, left-ventricle ejection fraction, liver fat fraction). Treated as a column of phenotype data alongside ICD codes.
  • MRI (magnetic resonance imaging) — a non-ionising imaging method using strong magnets and radio waves to produce three-dimensional pictures of soft tissue.
  • UKBB (UK Biobank) — a British prospective cohort of about half a million participants aged 40–69 at recruitment (2006–2010), with linked electronic health records, blood-sample assays, genotyping, and imaging.

Methods to lift

  • Frozen-protocol harmonization — at all imaging sites, identical scanner model, software version, coil hardware, and pulse sequences, with the explicit understanding that protocol stability beats per-site optimization at cohort scale.
  • Automated QC pipelines — image-level QC metrics (alignment to template, signal-to-noise, contrast-to-noise, head-motion estimates) reported per modality with pass-rate thresholds; IDP outputs blocked on QC pass.
  • IDP-as-phenotype-column — pre-computed imaging-derived phenotypes (regional brain volumes, ventricular ejection fraction, liver fat fraction, intima-media thickness, etc.) returned to the researcher catalog as ready-to-use phenotype columns.
  • Throughput operations — purpose-built centres with eighteen-participants-per-twelve-hour-day target, six radiographers, three healthcare assistants, lab specialist, healthcare-assistant team leader, centre manager per centre; six-week pre-opening training program; monthly MR-physicist refresh.
  • Recruitment-attendance funnel — explicit reporting of invited / interested / eligible / attended at each step, with response rates split by reminder timing (initial / 2-week / 4-week).

ALEX Welcome back to the CFTR PheWAS reading podcast. Today's first episode is "The UK Biobank imaging enhancement of 100,000 participants — rationale, data collection, management and future directions," by Thomas Littlejohns and colleagues, published in Nature Communications in 2020. The paper is essentially the operations manual and design rationale for the largest multi-modal medical imaging study ever attempted on a population cohort.

JORDAN So this is not a discovery paper at all. It's the paper that explains how the imaging data the rest of us use was actually collected.

ALEX Exactly. We're reading it because the imaging-derived phenotypes from this study now sit underneath a huge fraction of UK Biobank papers, and if we ever want to ask "what could a CFTR PheWAS do with imaging endpoints," we have to first understand what UK Biobank actually has, how it was harmonized, and what it cost in person-hours per participant to get there.

JORDAN Before we go in — there are a couple of acronyms I want to make sure we have on the table from the start. UKBB is shorthand for UK Biobank, the British half-million-person prospective cohort recruited between 2006 and 2010, ages forty to sixty-nine at recruitment, with linked electronic health records, blood-sample assays, genotyping, and now imaging. MRI is magnetic resonance imaging — a non-ionising scan that uses strong magnets and radio waves to produce three-dimensional pictures of soft tissue, very different from a CT scan, which uses X-rays. fMRI is functional MRI — same physics, but tuned to detect tiny changes in blood-oxygenation that track brain activity over time. DXA is dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry — a low-dose X-ray scan that measures bone mineral density and body composition, the same machine your radiologist uses to screen for osteoporosis. Carotid ultrasound is a non-invasive scan of the carotid artery in the neck, used to measure intima-media thickness, which is a marker of early atherosclerosis. And the term that's going to come up over and over in this paper is IDP — imaging-derived phenotype. An IDP is just a single number extracted from an imaging scan by an automated pipeline. The volume of your hippocampus, the ejection fraction of your left ventricle, the proton-density fat fraction of your liver — each of those is one IDP. UK Biobank publishes thousands of IDPs as ready-to-use columns next to your other phenotype data. That's the unit of analysis here.

ALEX Plain-English summary so far — UKBB is a huge British cohort, MRI is the magnet-and-radio-waves scanner, DXA is the low-dose X-ray bone-and-fat scan, carotid ultrasound is a neck-artery scan, and an IDP is a single pre-computed number derived from one of those scans, like "left ventricle wall thickness in millimeters."

ALEX Now to the background and purpose. Before this study, imaging research in epidemiology had two limitations. First, almost every population study that did include imaging only scanned one body region — the brain, or the heart, or sometimes the liver — but rarely all of them. Second, the cohorts were either small or selected, meaning thousands rather than tens of thousands, and often enriched for high-risk participants who already had the disease being studied. The Rotterdam Study and the German National Cohort were the closest comparators, but the German National Cohort was targeting around thirty thousand participants for its imaging arm. UKBB's planned scale of one hundred thousand participants, scanning every one of them on the same protocol across brain, heart, abdomen, bones, and carotid arteries, was unprecedented.

JORDAN Why does that scale matter?

ALEX Statistical power. The paper does an explicit calculation: at a hundred thousand participants you can detect odds ratios as small as 1.5 with eighty percent power for a relatively common exposure. That sounds like a modest improvement — but exposure-disease associations in adult chronic disease epidemiology often have effect sizes in that range, so going from "thirty thousand and we can't detect anything below 2.0" to "one hundred thousand and we can detect 1.5" is actually a regime change. The other motivation is mechanism. Imaging captures sub-clinical disease — fatty liver before it becomes cirrhosis, white-matter lesions before they become stroke, ventricular hypertrophy before it becomes heart failure. So even when you don't have enough symptomatic cases for a classical case-control study, you can have plenty of imaging-defined cases.

JORDAN And ideally those imaging-defined cases line up with the genetic data we already have.

ALEX Exactly. The animating idea is that you take a large number, you give every participant the same imaging protocol, you process it the same way, and then you ask which genetic variants associate with which imaging-derived phenotype, with statistical power that no clinical-imaging study has ever had.

ALEX The methods. The study runs out of four purpose-built imaging centres in Stockport, Newcastle, Reading, and Bristol, each chosen to minimize travel time for participants because driving distance turned out to be the single biggest predictor of whether someone showed up. Each centre runs four imaging stations: brain MRI, cardiac MRI plus abdominal MRI, DXA, and carotid ultrasound. The throughput target is eighteen participants per twelve-hour day, the centres are open every day except Christmas and Easter, and the actual achieved monthly average is seventeen per day with ninety-five percent attendance. To keep the imaging harmonized across the four sites, every centre uses the identical scanner model, identical software version, identical coil hardware, and identical pulse sequences. That's not a small thing. The brain MRI protocol alone takes about thirty-six minutes per participant and includes a structural scan called T1, a fluid-sensitive scan called T2 FLAIR, a magnetic-susceptibility scan called T2 star, a diffusion MRI for white-matter tract structure, and both resting-state and task-based fMRI for functional connectivity. The cardiac MRI captures cine views of the beating heart in short and long axes plus a T1-mapping sequence called ShMOLLI for tissue characterization. The abdominal MRI uses what's called Dixon imaging across six body stations to give whole-body fat and muscle composition plus dedicated liver scans for fat fraction and iron content. DXA scans bone density at total body, hips, spine, and thighs. Carotid ultrasound captures right and left carotid intima-media thickness.

JORDAN Push back for a moment — that's a lot of imaging per participant. Doesn't that mean the protocol freezes whatever was state-of-the-art in 2014 for the next decade?

ALEX That's the right question, and the paper is candid about it. They knew when they froze the protocol that some sequences would feel dated by the time imaging finished, but cross-site comparability beats per-site optimization at this scale — if Stockport upgrades its diffusion MRI in 2018 and Newcastle hasn't, the study has just stopped being a single dataset. So the protocol stays fixed, with the explicit understanding that future cohorts can build on top of it, not retrofit changes into it.

ALEX Results. As of early 2020, when the paper was written, around forty thousand participants had been imaged out of the hundred-thousand target, which is on track for completion by 2023. The pilot of about five thousand participants showed that the data collection was operationally feasible and that the quality-control metrics passed at ninety-six percent or higher across every modality. Brain MRI passes ninety-nine percent of the time on alignment, signal-to-noise, and head-motion checks. Cardiac MRI processing pipelines complete on ninety-nine to one hundred percent of scans. Abdominal Dixon stitching across six body stations works on ninety-three percent. DXA passes ninety-nine percent. Carotid ultrasound captures both arteries successfully on ninety-six percent. Then the paper inventories the IDPs that result. For brain MRI, hundreds of regional volume, cortical thickness, and white-matter integrity measures. For cardiac MRI, ventricular volumes, ejection fractions, myocardial wall thickness, and tissue-characterization metrics. For abdominal MRI, fat-mass distribution, lean-tissue mass, liver fat fraction, and pancreatic volume. For DXA, total and regional bone mineral density. For carotid ultrasound, intima-media thickness for left and right carotid. The headline operational claim is the IDP catalog itself — it grows continuously as new processing pipelines come online.

JORDAN Did they answer the question they posed?

ALEX They posed an operational question — can multi-modal imaging at population-cohort scale actually be done — and they answered yes, with the QC numbers and throughput numbers and IDP catalog to back it up. They did not test any biological hypothesis in this paper; that's by design.

ALEX One direction this episode suggests for Runyu's project is a careful inventory question — for any CF complication endpoint we might use as a phenotype, what imaging-derived analogue, if any, exists in the CFFPR registry, and at what coverage? Plain English: lung function in the registry is captured by spirometry, which is a breathing test. Imaging analogues that would carry richer mechanistic information — chest CT for bronchiectasis severity, abdominal MRI for pancreatic and hepatic involvement, sinus imaging for upper-airway disease — exist in clinical care but are not necessarily structured, harmonized, or extracted into IDPs the way UKBB does. ALEX Given that proposed direction, the gap that this paper closes for our purposes is mostly methodological — it tells us what the bar is for "imaging-as-phenotype-at-cohort-scale": purpose-built centres, identical hardware and software across sites, frozen protocols, automated QC pipelines, and pre-computed IDP outputs ready for genetic analysis. What it leaves open is everything specific to CF. UKBB participants are general population; their lung MRI is scarce because UKBB focuses on brain, heart, and abdomen, not chest; and their relevance to a CF-affected cohort is therefore indirect at best.
