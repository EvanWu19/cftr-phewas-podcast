Blurb

Khajouei, Ghisays, Karnes and colleagues (EBioMedicine, 2025) ran a single-locus phenome-wide association study (PheWAS) of APOE in 181,880 All of Us participants across seven ancestry groups — the diverse, social-determinant-rich counterpart to the European-only UK Biobank APOE PheWAS (Lumsden 2020). They read APOE diplotypes from rs429358 + rs7412 via PLINK, mapped EHR ICD codes to 2,318 phecodes, and ran logistic regression with the diplotypes ordered along the Alzheimer's risk axis (ε2/ε2 < ε2/ε3 < ε3/ε3 < ε2/ε4 < ε3/ε4 < ε4/ε4), adjusting for age, sex, EHR length, and 16 principal components. Seventeen phecodes cleared Bonferroni: strongest was hyperlipidaemia (OR 1.15/step, P=1.8×10⁻¹²⁹); largest effects were Alzheimer's (OR 1.55; ε4/ε4-vs-ε3/ε3 OR 7.4) and dementias; three protective signals (chronic liver disease, fatty liver disease, noninflammatory vulva/perineum disorders). Social-determinant adjustment barely moved APOE effects (signal is biological), but ancestry heterogeneity was real (hyperlipidaemia, mild cognitive impairment) with ancestry-unique hits (AV block in Admixed American/Latino). Proposed direction for the CFTR project: use APOE as a positive-control calibration locus to validate a phecodeX PheWAS pipeline before turning it on CFTR. Open: power at ~5,000 patients, the all-CF-cases phenome problem, and CFTR's hundreds-of-variants complexity versus APOE's two clean SNPs.

Plain-English glossary

  • All of Us (AoU) — NIH national research cohort (~413K participants, ~245K with WGS in v7), oversampling underrepresented US groups; DNA linked to EHR.
  • APOE — apolipoprotein E gene; lipid-transport protein; three common alleles ε2/ε3/ε4 defined by two SNPs (rs429358, rs7412).
  • Bonferroni correction — multiple-testing threshold = 0.05 ÷ number of tests (here 0.05/2318 ≈ 2.16×10⁻⁵).
  • Diplotype — the pair of alleles a person carries (e.g., ε3/ε4).
  • EHR — electronic health record; the digital clinical chart.
  • GWAS vs PheWAS — GWAS fixes one disease, scans the genome; PheWAS fixes one variant/gene, scans the phenome (all diseases).
  • ICD codes — International Classification of Diseases billing codes.
  • Logistic regression — statistical test for a yes/no outcome (disease present/absent) against predictors.
  • OMOP Common Data Model — standardised schema harmonising EHR data across ≥50 health systems.
  • Odds ratio (OR) — multiplicative change in odds of disease per unit of predictor.
  • Ordinal coding — treating the diplotypes as a single ranked dose along an a-priori risk axis.
  • Phecode / phecodeX — curated rollups of ICD codes into analyzable disease phenotypes (phecodeX 1.0 is the newer expanded map).
  • PheTK (PheWAS ToolKit) — Python package that extracts phenotypes/covariates and runs PheWAS inside the AoU Researcher Workbench.
  • PLINK 1.9 — genotype-QC and association toolkit; used here for genotype extraction and Hardy-Weinberg testing.
  • Principal components (PCs) — mathematical summary of genome-wide ancestry, included as covariates to control population stratification.
  • Social determinants of health (SDOH) — non-genetic factors (income, education, deprivation index) that track with both ancestry and disease.
  • srWGS — short-read whole-genome sequencing.

Methods to lift

  • PheTK — AoU-native PheWAS engine that pulls phenotypes, covariates (age, sex, EHR length, code/condition counts) from OMOP databases and runs the scan.
  • phecodeX 1.0 + phecode 1.2 — ICD→phenotype maps; 50-case/control floor per phecode; 18 disease categories.
  • APOE ordinal + decomposed models — primary risk-axis ordinal dose, plus separate ε2-dose, ε4-dose, and diplotype-vs-ε3/ε3 contrasts to avoid baking one risk ordering into all phenotypes.
  • 16 principal components — population-stratification control; ancestry inferred via random-forest classifier trained on 1000 Genomes + HGDP, projected with Rye (gnomAD ancestry labels: AFR/AMR/EAS/EUR/MID/OTH/SAS).
  • Bonferroni-by-test-count — separate thresholds recomputed for each subgroup (overall 2318, females 2096, males 1727).
  • SDOH sensitivity analysis — re-fit adding education, household income, social deprivation index; effect-size stability test.
  • Cross-group heterogeneity test — formal heterogeneity testing on any association appearing across sex/ancestry subgroups.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: stand up a phecodeX-based PheWAS pipeline (PheTK-equivalent logic, 50-case floor, ≥16 PCs, Bonferroni-by-phecode-count, ancestry- and sex-stratified models) and validate it against the established APOE association catalogue as a positive control — confirming recovery of hyperlipidaemia (OR ~1.15/step) and Alzheimer's (ε4/ε4-vs-ε3/ε3 OR in the single-digits-to-low-teens range) — before deploying the identical machinery on CFTR variants.

What to verify first

  • Whether CFF registry phenotype linkage is deep enough to construct phecodes at all (ICD-coded longitudinal records vs. registry-specific structured fields).
  • Realistic per-phecode case counts at ~5,000 patients — likely far below the ≥50-case floor for most non-CF phenotypes; quantify before assuming a phenome-wide scan is feasible.
  • Whether an all-CF cohort supports conventional case-control PheWAS or requires a within-CF quantitative-trait / severity-modifier framing instead (everyone is a "case" for CF; the relevant contrasts are CF-complication phenotypes).
  • Ancestry composition of the CFF cohort (heavily Northern-European-skewed) — whether ancestry-stratified analysis is even powered, and how PC adjustment behaves in a low-diversity cohort.
  • CFTR variant complexity — APOE is two SNPs; a CFTR scan must decide between single-variant, grouped-genotype (e.g., F508del-homozygous vs. other), or burden framings.

ALEX Episode one. Today's paper is Khajouei, Ghisays, Karnes and colleagues, 2025, in the journal EBioMedicine. The title is "Phenome-wide association of APOE alleles in the All of Us Research Program." We're reading it because it is the cleanest worked example I've found of a single-gene phenome-wide scan run inside All of Us — which is exactly the machine Runyu would have to drive if a CFTR scan were ever attempted in that cohort instead of the Cystic Fibrosis Foundation's own data.

JORDAN Before we go a step further, slow down and define the furniture. What is APOE, what is a phenome-wide scan, and what is All of Us? Treat me as if I've never opened a genetics paper.

ALEX Fair. APOE is the gene that codes apolipoprotein E, a protein that ferries fats — cholesterol and other lipids — around the bloodstream and the brain. Here's the wrinkle that makes APOE special. Most of the genome we talk about as single letters, single-nucleotide polymorphisms, "SNPs," one DNA letter swapped for another. APOE's famous variation is two SNPs, named rs429358 and rs7412, that travel together and, between them, produce three common versions of the protein called epsilon-2, epsilon-3, and epsilon-4 — written ε2, ε3, ε4. Everyone carries two copies, one from each parent, so your "diplotype" is a pair like ε3/ε3 or ε3/ε4. The ε4 version is the single strongest common genetic risk factor for late-onset Alzheimer's disease; ε2 is protective; ε3 is neutral. So APOE is the textbook example of a gene where which pair you carry shifts your risk for several diseases at once.

JORDAN And "phenome-wide"?

ALEX A phenome-wide association study — PheWAS — flips the usual genetics question around. A normal genome-wide study, a GWAS, fixes one disease and scans the whole genome asking which variants associate with it. A PheWAS fixes one variant, or one gene, and scans the whole "phenome" — every disease in the medical record — asking which conditions associate with it. The phenome here is built from electronic health records, the EHR, the digital chart from hospital visits. You take the billing codes — ICD codes, the International Classification of Diseases — and you roll them up into broader disease categories called phecodes, so that dozens of fiddly billing codes for, say, types of dementia collapse into one analyzable "dementia" phenotype. All of Us is the United States' big national research cohort: a National Institutes of Health programme that has enrolled hundreds of thousands of Americans, deliberately oversampling groups underrepresented in past genetics research, and linked their DNA to their health records.

JORDAN Why does APOE need re-studying if ε4-and-Alzheimer's is already textbook?

ALEX Because almost the entire APOE literature was built in people of European ancestry. The authors point out that the prior landmark PheWAS of APOE, by Lumsden in 2020, used UK Biobank but restricted itself to White British participants. So two big questions were genuinely open. First, do APOE's disease associations look the same across ancestries, or do the effect sizes differ? Second, how much of any apparent ancestry difference is biology versus social circumstance — what researchers call the social determinants of health, things like income, education, and neighbourhood deprivation that track with both ancestry and disease but aren't genetic? All of Us is unusually suited to both questions because it is large, ancestrally diverse, and it collects social-determinant survey data alongside the genomes. That combination is rare, and it is the gap this paper sets out to fill.

JORDAN So how did they actually do it?

ALEX They started from version 7 of the curated All of Us data, which holds about 413,000 participants, of whom roughly 245,000 have short-read whole-genome sequencing — reading essentially every letter of the genome. After requiring people to have both that sequence data and longitudinal health records, they landed on 181,880 participants across seven ancestry groups. They read off each person's APOE diplotype from the two defining SNPs using a genotyping toolkit called PLINK, then ran the phenome scan with a package called PheTK — the PheWAS Toolkit — inside the All of Us secure workbench. They mapped ICD codes to phecodes, kept only phecodes with at least 50 cases, and ended up testing 2,318 diseases. The statistical test was logistic regression — does carrying more "risk-ordered" APOE predict each disease — adjusting for age, sex assigned at birth, length of health record, and the first sixteen genetic principal components, which are a mathematical summary of ancestry used to stop population structure from creating fake associations.

JORDAN Push back on one thing for me. Is there a design choice here worth questioning?

ALEX Yes — the way they coded APOE. Their main model ordered the diplotypes along the Alzheimer's risk hierarchy, ε2/ε2 less than ε2/ε3 less than ε3/ε3 and so on up to ε4/ε4, and treated that as a single ordinal dose. That is elegant for Alzheimer's, where risk really does climb along that ladder. But it bakes the Alzheimer's risk ordering into every one of the 2,318 diseases, including ones where ε2 not ε4 might be the culprit. To their credit they noticed this and ran separate analyses — one tracking ε2 dose, one tracking ε4 dose, and one comparing each diplotype against ε3/ε3 directly. That's the right instinct, but it means the headline ordinal result has to be read as "associated with the APOE risk axis," not "ε4 specifically causes this."

JORDAN And what did they find?

ALEX In the overall cohort, 17 phecodes survived the phenome-wide significance threshold — which here was a Bonferroni correction, 0.05 divided by 2,318 tests, about 2 times ten-to-the-minus-five. The single strongest was hyperlipidaemia — high blood lipids — odds ratio 1.15 per step up the APOE axis, with a p-value of 1.8 times ten-to-the-minus-129, an astronomically strong signal. The associations with the largest effect sizes were neurological: Alzheimer's disease, odds ratio 1.55, then dementias, then cerebral degeneration. And then the interesting part — three conditions where the APOE risk axis was protective: chronic liver disease, fatty liver disease, and a genitourinary condition, noninflammatory disorders of the vulva and perineum. When they pushed harder, the ε4-versus-ε3/ε3 contrast for Alzheimer's gave an odds ratio of 7.4 in ε4 homozygotes — large, though smaller than the 12-to-15 seen in European-only studies, which they attribute to under-diagnosis and the relatively young, recently-enrolled cohort.

JORDAN Was there anything by sex worth flagging?

ALEX Yes, and it's a nice illustration of why you stratify. They re-ran the whole scan separately in females and in males — 2,096 testable phecodes in women, 1,727 in men, the difference partly because pregnancy-related codes only exist in one group. The top three were identical in both sexes — Alzheimer's, dementias, cerebral degeneration — so the headline neurological story is sex-stable. But the differences were real: men picked up extra cardiovascular associations, strongest for coronary atherosclerosis, and women showed a protective signal for that genitourinary phecode. They also caught a statistically significant APOE-by-age interaction for dementia — the association was clear only in people diagnosed at age 60 or above and vanished below 60 — which is exactly the kind of effect a pooled, unstratified analysis would smear out and hide. That's the transferable lesson more than the specific hits: stratification surfaces structure that pooling conceals.

JORDAN Did the ancestry and social-determinant questions get answered?

ALEX Partly, and honestly. Adding the social-determinant adjustment barely moved the APOE effect sizes — the same 17 associations came back in nearly the same order — which argues the APOE signal is biological, not an artefact of who is poor or educated. But the social factors themselves were independently associated with the diseases, so they matter, just not by confounding APOE. On ancestry, they found genuine heterogeneity — the effect sizes for hyperlipidaemia and mild cognitive impairment differed significantly across ancestry groups — and a couple of ancestry-unique hits, like first-degree AV heart block in the Admixed American/Latino group. So they answered the question they posed: APOE's catalogue largely replicates across a diverse cohort, but with real, measurable differences in magnitude by ancestry and some new sex-specific signals. They did not fully resolve why those differences exist — that's left open.

JORDAN So what's the one concrete direction this suggests for the CFTR project?

ALEX Here it is in plain English first. APOE is the rare gene where a single locus produces a clean, replicable spray of associations across many diseases — so it's the perfect calibration target. Before trusting any CFTR phenome scan, you'd want to first reproduce a known APOE-style result on your own platform, to prove the pipeline detects what it should. The concrete direction: stand up exactly this PheTK-plus-phecodeX pipeline, run it on a positive-control locus like APOE in whatever cohort is available, confirm you recover hyperlipidaemia and Alzheimer's at the expected effect sizes, and only then turn the same machinery on CFTR variants. The formal one-sentence version: one possible direction this episode suggests is to benchmark a phecodeX-based PheWAS pipeline against the established APOE association catalogue as a positive control before deploying it for a CFTR phenome-wide scan, with ancestry- and sex-stratified models built in from the start.

JORDAN And conditional on going that way — what does this paper close, and what would still be open?

ALEX Conditional on that benchmarking direction, the paper closes several things. It gives a concrete, citable parameter set: phecodeX with a 50-case floor, the first sixteen principal components, a Bonferroni threshold tied to the number of phecodes tested, and the PheTK toolkit as the engine. It demonstrates that social-determinant adjustment can be added without destabilising a strong genetic signal, and it shows the exact form an ancestry-heterogeneity test takes. What it leaves open is the harder half. It does not tell us whether 5,000 patients — the size of the CFF whole-genome cohort — is anywhere near enough power; APOE's signals here ride on 181,000 people and effects this clean. It does not address the all-cases problem: in a cohort where everyone has cystic fibrosis, the phenome is dominated by CF complications, so the phecode landscape and the case-control logic both change. And it does not touch CFTR variant complexity — APOE is two tidy SNPs, CFTR is hundreds of mutations of wildly different frequency. What Runyu would need to check, before committing, is whether the CFF registry's phenotype linkage is deep enough to build phecodes at all, what the realistic case counts per phecode would be at 5,000 patients, and whether an all-CF cohort even supports a conventional case-control PheWAS or needs a within-CF quantitative-trait framing instead. None of that is decided, and none of it assumes anyone has yet looked at the CFF sequence data.
