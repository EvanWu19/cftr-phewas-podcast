How did this episode land for you?

Blurb

Linda Ottensmann and colleagues ran a genome-wide association study of 179 individual plasma lipid species in 7174 Finnish adults from the GeneRISK cohort, then ran a phenome-wide follow-up across 953 disease endpoints in 377,277 FinnGen participants. Their headline numbers: 495 variant-trait associations, 56 lipid-associated loci, 8 of them novel (DTL, STK39, CDS1, AGPAT2, SGPL1, YPEL2, KCNJ12, AGPAT3), 50 fine-mapped variants with posterior inclusion probability above 0.9, and 2937 variant-disease associations spanning 46 of the loci. The proposed direction this episode suggests is a multivariate joint GWAS across canonically defined clusters of correlated cystic fibrosis complication traits, followed by colocalization-based PheWAS against external biobanks. The open question — and a non-trivial one — is whether a 5000-patient CF cohort with ~20 correlated complication traits yields enough multivariate boost to justify the methodological complexity over parallel univariate tests.

Plain-English glossary

  • Bonferroni-corrected significance (BFS): p-value threshold after dividing by the number of independent tests; here 7.35e-10 for univariate.
  • Canonical correlation analysis (CCA): statistical method that finds the linear combination of multiple correlated traits most associated with a variant; returns one p-value per cluster.
  • Ceramide (Cer): a class of sphingolipid; among the most heritable lipid classes in this study.
  • CLPP (colocalization posterior probability): Bayesian probability that a GWAS signal and a disease signal at the same locus share the same causal variant.
  • Credible set: a small set of variants that together carry, e.g., 95% of the probability that the causal variant is among them.
  • Fine-mapping: statistical post-processing that estimates, for each variant in a GWAS-significant region, the probability it is the true causal driver.
  • GeneRISK: prospective Finnish cohort of 7292 individuals; the 7174 with complete lipidome + genotype data were used here.
  • Genome-wide significance (GWS): the standard p-value cutoff of 5e-8 across the whole genome.
  • GWAS (genome-wide association study): statistical scan testing every common variant against a trait one variant at a time.
  • HDL-C, LDL-C, TC, TG: standard four cholesterol-panel lipids — high-density lipoprotein cholesterol, low-density lipoprotein cholesterol, total cholesterol, triglycerides.
  • Imputation: statistical inference of un-genotyped variants by matching to a fully-sequenced reference panel.
  • Linkage disequilibrium (LD): correlation in genotype between nearby variants caused by their tendency to be inherited together.
  • Linear mixed model (LMM): regression that accounts for relatedness among samples.
  • Lipidome: the full set of distinct lipid molecules in a biological sample.
  • MAF (minor allele frequency): the frequency in the population of the rarer allele at a variant site.
  • PheWAS (phenome-wide association study): scan testing one variant against many disease/trait endpoints.
  • PIP (posterior inclusion probability): probability that a specific variant is the causal driver in a fine-mapping credible set.
  • Population isolate: a population (here Finland) with a founder-bottleneck history where some rare variants are enriched.
  • SISu v3: Sequencing Initiative Suomi reference panel built from ~3700 high-coverage Finnish whole-genome sequences.

Methods to lift

  • MMM v1.01 — linear-mixed-model GWAS engine, accounts for relatedness in the sample.
  • Canonical correlation analysis (CCA) — multivariate GWAS engine for clusters of correlated traits.
  • Hierarchical clustering on absolute pairwise correlations — defines trait clusters before multivariate testing.
  • Variance inflation factor (VIF) pruning — removes redundant cluster members; threshold here was VIF > 5.
  • FINEMAP — Bayesian fine-mapping tool; produces credible sets and per-variant PIPs.
  • FOCUS — transcriptome-based fine-mapping that prioritizes causal genes by predicted expression.
  • FUMA — annotation platform for tissue expression enrichment and gene-set lookups.
  • VEP — Variant Effect Predictor; classifies variants as missense / splice / intronic / etc.
  • CADD score — variant deleteriousness score; >10 = top 10% most deleterious, >20 = top 1%.
  • Bonferroni correction — for 68 PCs explaining 90% of phenotype variance, BFS = 5e-8 / 68 = 7.35e-10.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage:

Run a multivariate joint GWAS using canonical correlation analysis (or related multivariate framework) across canonically defined clusters of correlated CF complication traits drawn from the CFF Patient Registry — candidate trait set: FEV1 percent predicted trajectory, BMI percentile, age at CFRD onset, liver disease severity score, sweat chloride, pancreatic exocrine status, meconium ileus history, age at first chronic Pseudomonas colonization. Fine-map detected loci with FINEMAP, prioritize genes via functional-variant filtering and FOCUS, then run a phenome-wide colocalization (CLPP > 0.01) against an external biobank's non-CF disease endpoints to characterize pleiotropy of CF modifier loci.

What to verify first

  • CFF whole-genome sequencing (~5000 patients) is in a joint-called, QC-passed state with patient-level linkage to CFFPR phenotype tables.
  • Candidate complication phenotypes are recorded densely enough across the cohort — many are sparse longitudinally.
  • Pairwise correlation structure among the candidate traits is high enough that a multivariate test gains meaningful power over parallel univariate tests; if traits are near-orthogonal, multivariate adds little.
  • An external biobank with appropriate non-CF endpoints (cardiometabolic, hepatic, neurologic, oncologic) is accessible for colocalization replication, and its LD reference panel is compatible with the CFF cohort's ancestry composition.
  • Sample size for a multivariate test with high-dimensional outcome may be more sensitive to relatedness structure than univariate analyses; the CFF cohort includes multiplex families and twin/sib pairs from NCT00676169 that need to be modeled, not dropped.
  • Whether software for multivariate GWAS at this scale (MMM, MTAG, MultiABEL) supports unrelated-plus-related mixed samples without rejecting too many participants.

ALEX Good morning. Today's paper is Ottensmann and colleagues, "Genome-wide association analysis of plasma lipidome identifies 495 genetic associations," published in Nature Communications in 2023. First author Linda Ottensmann at the Institute for Molecular Medicine Finland, senior author Matti Pirinen.

JORDAN Why are we reading a paper about lipids when we are supposed to be building toward a CFTR PheWAS?

ALEX Two reasons. First, this is one of the most worked-through multivariate GWAS designs I have seen in print, and the trick they use is the trick we would need if we ever tried to combine correlated cystic fibrosis complication traits into a single test. Second, they wire the GWAS to a phenome-wide analysis on the back end, which is exactly the shape of the analysis we want to imitate. Lipids are not the point — the architecture is.

JORDAN Before we go in, I want to slow down. What is a lipidome?

ALEX Fair. Let me lay out vocabulary so nothing later sneaks past. A lipid is a fat or fat-like molecule in your blood. The cholesterol number your doctor quotes you is a lipid. The lipidome is the full inventory of every distinct lipid species circulating in your plasma. There are hundreds of them, and modern mass spectrometry can measure them in a single shot. A lipid species is one specific molecule — a phosphatidylcholine with two specific fatty-acid tails, for example, not just "phosphatidylcholine in general." Classes like CE for cholesterol esters, TAG for triacylglycerols, SM for sphingomyelins, Cer for ceramides — those are families of related lipids.

JORDAN And GWAS — I have heard it but should hear it again.

ALEX GWAS is genome-wide association study. You measure a trait in lots of people, you genotype them at millions of common variants across the genome, and at each variant you ask: do people who carry it have a higher or lower value of the trait? Each test gives you a p-value, the probability of seeing this much effect by chance alone. The cutoff for taking a hit seriously across the whole genome is p less than five times ten to the minus eight, conventionally written 5e-8. That is called genome-wide significance, or GWS.

JORDAN And PheWAS?

ALEX A PheWAS, phenome-wide association study, flips the question. Instead of one trait against all variants, you take one variant against all traits. The phenome is the catalog of measurable diseases and traits in a cohort.

JORDAN One more. Fine-mapping?

ALEX After GWAS finds a region of the genome that matters, you usually cannot tell which one variant in that region is the actual causal one, because nearby variants travel together — they are in linkage disequilibrium, often called LD. Two variants in high LD have correlated genotypes across the population, so they will both light up in a GWAS even if only one of them is doing the work. Fine-mapping is the statistical post-processing that estimates, for each variant in the region, the probability it is the true driver. That probability is called the posterior inclusion probability, PIP. PIP above 0.9 is a strong bet. The output is also a credible set — a small set of variants that together carry, say, 95 percent of the probability that the causal variant is somewhere in there. A small credible set means you have narrowed it down; a credible set of 200 variants means LD is still hiding the answer and your gene targeting is no better than the region was before fine-mapping.

JORDAN And one acronym I keep seeing in their tables — MAF.

ALEX Minor allele frequency. At any given position in the genome where a variant exists, you have a more common allele and a rarer one. MAF is just the frequency of the rarer one in the population you sampled. MAF of 0.5 means a true coin flip, MAF of 0.01 is one percent — that is rare, and rare variants need either huge sample sizes or population enrichment to be detected. The Ottensmann paper exploits the second route.

JORDAN Okay. So what problem were the authors trying to solve?

ALEX The big lipid GWAS that exist — the Million Veteran Program with over six hundred thousand people, the Graham consortium with one and a half million — those measure the standard four lipids: HDL cholesterol, LDL cholesterol, total cholesterol, triglycerides. Doctors order those numbers because they predict cardiovascular disease. But those four numbers are a summary. The actual lipidome has roughly two hundred distinct species, and the species correlate with each other. Some lipid species predict cardiovascular events better than the standard four do. So the unanswered question is: what variants influence individual lipid species that the standard four cholesterol numbers miss, and do those variants explain disease in ways the standard lipids cannot?

JORDAN Why Finland?

ALEX Population isolate. The Finnish population went through a historical bottleneck — small founding population, geographic isolation — which means rare deleterious variants that exist in mainland Europe are sometimes enriched or sometimes absent in Finland. That makes Finnish cohorts unusually good at finding rare coding variants that have visible effects. The authors quote a sixty-nine-fold Finnish enrichment for one of their novel hits, the CDS1 locus. You would never see that variant frequently enough in UK Biobank to detect it.

JORDAN How did they actually do the study?

ALEX GeneRISK cohort, 7174 Finnish adults with both genotype data and lipidome data. They measured 179 lipid species across 13 classes using a shotgun lipidomics platform from a company called Lipotype. Genotyping was a HumanCoreExome BeadChip, then imputation against the SISu version 3 reference panel, which is itself built from high-coverage whole-genome sequencing of about 3700 Finns. Imputation is the trick where you genotype someone at, say, 500 thousand variants on a chip, and then infer their genotypes at 10 or 20 million more variants by matching them to a reference panel of fully sequenced people. After QC they had 11.3 million testable variants.

JORDAN And the statistical engine?

ALEX Two parallel analyses. The univariate side is one variant, one lipid species at a time, using a linear mixed model. A linear mixed model, LMM, is a regression that explicitly accounts for the fact that some people in your sample are related to each other, which would otherwise inflate your p-values. They used MMM version 1.01 for that. The multivariate side groups correlated lipid species into clusters — they got 11 clusters by hierarchical clustering on absolute pairwise correlations — and then tests each variant against the cluster as a whole using canonical correlation analysis. Canonical correlation is a statistical method that asks: of all the linear combinations of the lipid species in this cluster, which one is most associated with this variant? You get a single p-value per cluster instead of one per species. ALEX pushes back here: I think the cluster definitions are doing a lot of hidden work — they chose hierarchical clustering, they removed members with variance inflation factors above five, and they ended up with eleven clusters. A different clustering choice would have given a different answer. The paper does not explore that sensitivity directly, though they do compare univariate against multivariate side by side.

JORDAN And then fine-mapping and prioritization.

ALEX They ran FINEMAP, a Bayesian fine-mapping tool, on each GWAS region. They prioritized genes two ways: by picking functional variants — missense, splice, that sort of thing — with PIP above 0.5, and by running FOCUS, which is a transcriptome-based fine-mapping tool that asks which gene's predicted expression best explains the signal. They used FUMA to look at which tissues those genes were most expressed in.

JORDAN What did they find?

ALEX 495 variant-trait associations at the Bonferroni-corrected significance threshold. That maps to 56 lipid-associated loci. Eight of the fifty-six are entirely new — DTL on chromosome 1, STK39 on 2, CDS1 on 4, AGPAT2 on 9, SGPL1 on 10, YPEL2 and KCNJ12 on 17, and AGPAT3 on 21. AGPAT2 and AGPAT3 are particularly clean — they encode enzymes in the same family as the already-known AGPAT1, which catalyze a specific step in the phospholipid biosynthesis pathway. That is a satisfying mechanistic story: three paralogous genes, three associated loci, one shared enzymatic step. The CDS1 lead variant, rs191460656, has minor allele frequency 0.07 in this Finnish cohort and is sixty-nine times more common in Finland than in non-Finnish Europeans — the kind of signal a population isolate uniquely supplies. Heritability estimates ranged from zero to 0.45 across the 179 lipid species, with sphingomyelins and ceramides among the most heritable classes — those lipids are under tighter genetic control than the triglycerides that dominate routine cholesterol panels. Among the fine-mapped variants, 50 had PIP above 0.9, and many of those were missense variants in known lipid genes — APOE, PCSK9, LIPC, LCAT, GCKR. Did they answer their question? Yes, partially. They added eight new loci to the lipidome map and they showed that multivariate analysis recovered 36 percent more associations than the univariate approach on the same data. The 953-endpoint FinnGen PheWAS produced 2937 variant-disease associations across 46 of the loci, including expected hits — coronary artery disease, type 2 diabetes, cholelithiasis, nonalcoholic fatty liver disease — plus less expected ones like the novel YPEL2 intronic variant associated with hypertension at p equals 2e-7.

JORDAN What does this paper suggest for a CFTR PheWAS?

ALEX Here is one specific direction this episode suggests. CF complication phenotypes — lung function decline measured by FEV1, body mass index, CF-related diabetes, sweat chloride, pancreatic exocrine insufficiency severity, meconium ileus history, liver disease severity — those traits correlate with each other inside a single patient. Right now in the CF modifier literature each trait gets its own univariate GWAS, and people pay a Bonferroni penalty paper by paper. A multivariate test along the lines of what Ottensmann did, treating correlated CF complication traits as a cluster and testing each variant against the cluster as a whole using canonical correlation, could potentially recover modifier signals that no individual univariate test reaches. Formally: a multivariate joint GWAS across canonically defined clusters of correlated CF complication traits, fine-mapped to candidate causal variants, followed by a phenome-wide colocalization against external biobanks to test whether CF modifier loci have non-CF disease associations.

JORDAN What does this paper close and what does it leave open for that direction?

ALEX What it closes — the multivariate-plus-fine-map-plus-PheWAS pipeline now has a published, working blueprint in the lipid space, including tool names — MMM, FINEMAP, FOCUS, FUMA — and a defensible cluster construction procedure. What it leaves open — Ottensmann had 7174 people and 179 traits, and the multivariate boost was substantial. A CF cohort of around 5000 with maybe twenty correlated complication traits is in the same order of magnitude on sample size but with far fewer trait dimensions, so the expected power gain from multivariate analysis is unknown. What we would need to check before committing to that direction — first, that the CFF whole-genome sequencing data is in a joint-called, QC-passed state that the CFF Patient Registry phenotype tables actually link to at the patient level. Second, that the registry phenotypes Runyu would want to combine — FEV1 trajectory, BMI percentile, CFRD onset age, liver disease severity score — are recorded densely enough across the cohort to support multivariate analysis. Third, that the correlation structure among those CF complication traits is high enough that multivariate adds power — if traits are nearly orthogonal, the multivariate gain collapses. Fourth, that an external biobank with non-CF disease endpoints can serve as the PheWAS replication target, since the CF cohort itself does not give you the cardiometabolic, neurologic, and cancer endpoints that FinnGen provides. None of these have been verified by Runyu personally, and none should be assumed.

JORDAN One more verification piece I want to name.

ALEX Right — colocalization. They reported a Bayesian quantity called CLPP, colocalization posterior probability, above 0.01 as their threshold for declaring that a lipid signal and a disease signal at the same locus share a causal variant. The reason colocalization matters is that two independent signals at the same locus do not actually imply shared biology — it could just be two different causal variants that happen to live in the same neighborhood. Colocalization gives you a probabilistic answer to "is it the same variant driving both?" rather than just "are both signals here?" We would need to verify the colocalization software they used works with our cohort-derived summary statistics and the external biobank's, because mismatched LD reference panels can wreck colocalization analyses. That is the kind of methodological landmine that only surfaces when you actually try to run the pipeline, not when you outline it on a whiteboard.

JORDAN Tomorrow's tee-up.

ALEX Episode two today is the All of Us diversity scoping review, which is the policy-and-design counterpart to the methods paper we just covered. Where Ottensmann is about statistical machinery, Mapes is about who is in the cohort the machinery runs on, and the two questions intersect more directly than you might think — population-isolate cohorts like FinnGen win on rare-variant discovery precisely because of their non-representative ancestry, and that is the same lever and the same limitation we will discuss tomorrow. One-line takeaway from Ottensmann: when correlated traits cluster naturally, multivariate GWAS can outperform parallel univariate tests, and a phenome-wide back-end turns lipid hits into disease hits.
