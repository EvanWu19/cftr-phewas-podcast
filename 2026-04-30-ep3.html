Aksit et al. 2020 — CFRD genetic modifiers and overlap with T2D

Single-paper re-digest. Aksit and colleagues conducted a GWAS for CF-related diabetes onset in 5,740 individuals with CF drawn from the North American and French CF Modifier Consortia, using age at CFRD diagnosis as a survival-style phenotype. They identified three genome-wide significant loci — PTMA (novel), TCF7L2 (also a canonical T2D locus), and SLC26A9 (a known CF complication modifier) — and showed via polygenic risk scores that CFRD overlaps T2D along the beta-cell function axis but not along insulin sensitivity, and shares essentially nothing with T1D's autoimmune axis. They built and replicated an 18-variant CFRD PRS in an independent cohort. The discovery cohort here is the same CFF-anchored resource Runyu plans to use for the CFTR PheWAS, which makes this paper a load-bearing reference for both the saturation and the gap-map sections of the concept note.

Gap-map: closes

  • CFRD GWAS in the CFF-anchored modifier cohort at n ~5,740 — definitively done.
  • Question of whether CFRD shares genetic architecture with T2D — answered yes, along the beta-cell function axis.
  • Question of whether CFRD is autoimmune-like (T1D-like) — answered essentially no; islet autoimmunity PRS unassociated.
  • Existence of a validated, replicated CFRD polygenic risk score — done (18-variant score, replicated in an independent cohort).
  • Whether PTMA is a novel CF modifier — established.

Gap-map: leaves open

  • Phenome-wide scans of the established CF modifier loci (PTMA, SLC26A9, TCF7L2) across non-diabetes CF complications in the same cohort — not done.
  • Modulator-era stratification: Aksit's CFRD onsets are largely pre-elexacaftor; how modulator exposure modifies the genetic architecture of CFRD and other complications is open.
  • Cross-complication pleiotropy of PTMA and SLC26A9 — explicitly acknowledged as needing follow-up.
  • Rare-variant burden tests on the established loci using WGS rather than imputed common variants — out of scope for this paper.
  • PRS construction with modern methods (LDpred2, PRS-CS) — Aksit used an FDR < 0.1 variant-selection approach, which is permissive and a candidate for methodological improvement.
  • Replication cohort exact n — flag to confirm from the PDF before citation.

ALEX Three loci hit genome-wide significance. PTMA, which is novel. TCF7L2, which is the canonical type 2 diabetes locus, possibly the most replicated T2D variant in human genetics. JORDAN And the polygenic risk score work — that is the second half of the paper, right?

ALEX That is the conceptually interesting half. They built weighted PRSs for type 1 diabetes, type 2 diabetes, insulin secretion, postchallenge glucose, fasting plasma glucose, insulin sensitivity, and islet autoimmunity, using published GWAS summary statistics from the general population. Then they tested each PRS for association with CFRD onset in their CF cohort.

JORDAN That is a slick design. You are using the rest of the world's diabetes genetics to triangulate which physiological axis of CFRD lines up with which other diabetes.

ALEX Exactly. And the answer is clean. CFRD lights up the T2D PRS strongly. It lights up the PRSs for insulin secretion, postchallenge glucose, and fasting plasma glucose. It lights up T1D weakly. It does not light up insulin sensitivity consistently, and it does not light up islet autoimmunity at all.

JORDAN So CFRD is mechanistically a beta-cell-failure disease, not an insulin-resistance disease, and not an autoimmune disease.

ALEX Yes. And the framing they end on is the right framing — CFRD overlaps T2D along the beta-cell axis, but it is not T2D, and it is not T1D either. It is a third thing that shares part of its genetic architecture with T2D and shares almost nothing with T1D.

JORDAN I want to come back to the two CF-specific loci. PTMA and SLC26A9 — neither shows up in T2D scans?

ALEX Correct. They explicitly checked. The PTMA and SLC26A9 signals do not appear in the population T2D summary statistics. Which is interesting because it means CFRD has a private genetic architecture on top of the shared T2D-like architecture. Roughly speaking, you can think of CFRD risk as the T2D-like polygenic background plus a CF-specific layer where CFTR genotype, PTMA, and SLC26A9 contribute risk that does not exist in the general population.

JORDAN SLC26A9 is the chloride channel modifier locus, right? It keeps showing up in CF complications.

ALEX It is an anion transporter, often described as a CFTR modifier in epithelial tissues. It has previously been associated with meconium ileus, with lung function trajectory in some analyses, and now with CFRD. This is the kind of locus that should be a phenome-wide red flag for us. If we ever get to running a CFTR PheWAS, SLC26A9 is one of the first variants we would scan across every available phecode.
