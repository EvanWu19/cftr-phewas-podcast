Show notes — Ghoussaini 2021, Open Targets Genetics

Blurb

Ghoussaini and colleagues describe Open Targets Genetics, an open-access integrative portal at https://genetics.opentargets.org that aggregates GWAS summary statistics from the NHGRI-EBI GWAS Catalog, the UK Biobank SAIGE and Neale Lab scans, plasma pQTLs on 2,994 proteins, eQTLs from GTEx and the eQTL Catalogue across 92 tissues, and chromatin-interaction data from promoter capture Hi-C, FANTOM5 CAGE, and DNase-hypersensitivity-site mapping. On top of that it runs a four-step pipeline: fine-mapping (GCTA-COJO conditional analysis plus Approximate Bayes Factor for studies with full summary statistics; PICS for studies without), cross-trait and disease-molecular-trait colocalisation across 3,621 studies, a Variant-to-Gene aggregated score, and a Locus-to-Gene machine-learning score trained on >400 gold-standard loci. The proposed direction this episode suggests: use the portal to enumerate general-population pleiotropy for CFTR and for every catalogued CF-modifier gene, then use that pleiotropy map to pre-register a focused phenome subset for the eventual CFTR PheWAS — rather than running brute-force across all phecodes. The open question: whether the underlying coverage is deep enough at non-European ancestry and for rare-variant burden signals, both of which are weak points the authors flag.

Plain-English glossary

  • CAGE (cap-analysis-of-gene-expression): Experimental method that maps where transcription actually starts in a genome.
  • Chromatin interaction / Hi-C / promoter capture Hi-C: Experiment that captures which pieces of DNA are physically close in three-dimensional space inside the cell nucleus, with promoter capture Hi-C focusing on gene promoters.
  • Colocalisation: Statistical test asking whether two GWAS signals at the same locus (e.g. a disease and an eQTL) are driven by the same underlying causal variant.
  • Credible set: The small list of variants — typically one to twenty — that fine-mapping identifies as collectively containing the causal one with high probability.
  • DNase-hypersensitivity site (DHS): A region of the genome where the DNA is unwound enough for regulatory proteins to bind — a signature of active regulatory elements.
  • EFO (Experimental Factor Ontology): A controlled vocabulary that lets a computer treat "type 2 diabetes" and "T2DM" and "diabetes mellitus type II" as the same trait.
  • eQTL (expression quantitative trait locus): A DNA variant that changes how much messenger-RNA a nearby gene makes.
  • Fine-mapping: Statistical procedure that narrows a GWAS hit down to its plausible causal variants.
  • GCTA-COJO: Tool that performs conditional analysis on GWAS summary statistics — testing whether a second independent signal exists after accounting for the top hit.
  • GRCh38: The current human reference-genome coordinate system.
  • GWAS (genome-wide association study): Scan of roughly ten million common DNA variants across the genome, testing each for association with a trait.
  • L2G (Locus-to-Gene) score: Open Targets' supervised machine-learning prediction of the causal gene at each GWAS locus.
  • LD (linkage disequilibrium): Tendency for nearby DNA variants to travel together on the same chromosomal chunk.
  • Locus: A region of the genome containing a signal.
  • NHGRI-EBI GWAS Catalog: Public database of published GWAS results.
  • PheWAS (phenome-wide association study): Inverse of GWAS — fix one variant or gene, scan it against thousands of diseases at once.
  • PICS (probabilistic identification of causal SNPs): Fine-mapping method that uses LD structure alone, for studies without full summary statistics.
  • pQTL (protein quantitative trait locus): A variant that changes how much of a protein circulates in blood.
  • Trait: A disease or measurement.
  • V2G (Variant-to-Gene) score: Open Targets' single aggregated score per variant-gene pair combining distance, VEP, QTL, and chromatin-interaction evidence.
  • VEP (Variant Effect Predictor): Ensembl tool predicting the functional consequence of a variant.

Methods to lift

  • Fine-mapping via GCTA-COJO + Approximate Bayes Factor — conditional analysis to identify independent signals followed by per-signal causal-variant probabilities.
  • Fine-mapping via PICS — alternative when only top hits and an LD reference are available (relevant for any CFGP GWAS modifier reanalysis where summary statistics are not available).
  • Colocalisation (coloc-style methodology referenced) — disease GWAS vs. eQTL/pQTL at the same locus to ask whether they share the causal variant.
  • L2G machine-learning gene prioritisation — supervised classifier trained on >400 gold-standard loci, combining fine-mapping credible sets with functional-genomics features.
  • V2G aggregated score — single variant-to-gene score combining distance, VEP, QTL evidence, and chromatin interaction.
  • Programmatic access via GraphQL API at https://genetics-api.opentargets.io and bulk download from the EMBL-EBI FTP.

What to verify first

  • That CFTR's full pleiotropy map in Open Targets is plausibly complete — confirm presence of mild non-CF CFTR phenotypes (CBAVD, chronic pancreatitis, chronic rhinosinusitis) on the gene page.
  • That the underlying eQTL evidence behind any colocalisation hit comes from a CF-relevant tissue (airway epithelium, intestinal epithelium, pancreatic ductal cells) and not exclusively from blood.
  • That linkage-disequilibrium block resolution at each modifier-gene query doesn't blur the signal across mechanistically unrelated neighboring genes.
  • A fallback plan for non-European-ancestry CF patients — Open Targets fine-mapping pipelines are predominantly European-ancestry as of the 2021 paper.
  • Whether the rare-variant burden architecture at each modifier locus (where it exists) is captured at all — Open Targets is built around common-variant GWAS substrate.

Notes

Over ninety percent of drug candidates that enter clinical trials fail, mostly because they don't actually work — they target a biology that isn't driving the disease. Earlier work, particularly Nelson and colleagues in 2015 and updated by King and colleagues in 2019, showed that drugs whose target gene has prior human-genetics evidence linking it to the disease are roughly twice as likely to make it through clinical development as drugs without that evidence. JORDAN Why is that hard? GWAS finds variants. Don't we know what gene they're near?

ALEX Most GWAS hits are not in coding regions — they're in non-coding DNA, meaning they're not changing the protein sequence of a gene. They're regulatory: they change when, where, or how much a nearby gene is made. And "nearby" can mean a hundred thousand base pairs away, or sometimes even further, through a three-dimensional folding contact in the chromosome. So a variant on chromosome two might actually be acting on a gene that's not its closest neighbor. To resolve which gene is the right answer you need at least four kinds of evidence layered on top of the GWAS signal: which variants are statistically the most likely causal ones — fine-mapping — which genes those variants actually contact in three-dimensional chromosome conformation experiments, which genes' expression levels change with the variant — eQTL data — and what the variant is predicted to do functionally if it falls inside coding sequence, using a tool called the Variant Effect Predictor or VEP. The whole point of Open Targets Genetics is to do this layering at scale, for every published GWAS signal at once, in a reproducible pipeline anyone can query.
