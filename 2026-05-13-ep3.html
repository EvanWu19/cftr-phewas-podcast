Show notes — McDougal 2010, MBL2 modifier framework

Blurb

McDougal and colleagues from the Cutting laboratory at Johns Hopkins used a structural modeling framework — incorporating CFTR genotype, age, Pseudomonas aeruginosa infection status, and lung function — on 788 patients from the US CF Twin and Sibling Study to disentangle confounding in candidate-gene tests of MBL2. The result: low-MBL2 genotypes associate with Pa infection 1.94 years earlier (P=0.0034) and with mucoid Pa conversion 2.72 years earlier (P=0.0003) than high-MBL2 genotypes, but MBL2 does not associate with the gap between infection and mucoid conversion, nor with FEV1 directly. The proposed direction this episode suggests: define Pa-acquisition-age and mucoid-Pa-conversion-age as two distinct time-to-event endpoints in any CFTR PheWAS infection module rather than collapsing them into "chronic Pa colonization." Open question: whether the MBL2 effect persists in the post-2019 modulator era, and whether the high-versus-low genotype dichotomy hides effect-size heterogeneity that diplotype-aware analysis would surface. Plain-English glossary

  • Biofilm: A self-protective matrix that bacteria secrete around themselves once they convert from planktonic (free-swimming) growth to attached, community-dwelling growth.
  • Cox proportional-hazards model: Standard survival-analysis regression for time-to-event endpoints, modeling the hazard rate (instantaneous risk of the event) as a multiplicative function of covariates.
  • Candidate-gene study: A study that tests a specific pre-specified gene for association with a trait, in contrast to a genome-wide scan that tests every variant in the genome.
  • CF Twin and Sibling Study: A Johns Hopkins-led longitudinal CF-genetics cohort designed to use identical twins, fraternal twins, and sibling pairs to disentangle genetic versus environmental drivers of CF variability.
  • CFTR / F508del: Cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator gene. F508del is the most common CF-causing mutation — a three-nucleotide deletion that removes the phenylalanine at position 508 and causes the protein to misfold.
  • Complement cascade: A series of plasma proteins that, once activated by recognition signals such as MBL binding to bacterial sugars, opsonize and lyse microbes as part of innate immunity.
  • Confounding: A statistical situation where an apparent association between A and B is actually driven by a third variable C that influences both.
  • Diplotype: The specific pair of haplotypes a person carries at a locus — finer-grained than a "high/low" producer category.
  • FEV1 (forced expiratory volume in 1 second): The volume of air a person can forcefully exhale in the first second of a spirometry test. Usually expressed as % of age-sex-height predicted.
  • Innate immunity: The non-adaptive arm of the immune system — pattern-recognition receptors, complement, phagocytes — that responds rapidly without requiring prior antigen exposure.
  • MBL / MBL2 / mannose-binding lectin: Plasma protein that binds sugar patterns on bacteria and fungi and activates the complement immune cascade. Encoded by the MBL2 gene on chromosome 10, with three common structural variants (B, C, D) plus promoter variants (H/L, X/Y).
  • Modifier gene: A gene that is not the disease-causing gene but that nonetheless alters disease outcomes.
  • Mucoid Pa / mucoid conversion: Conversion of Pseudomonas aeruginosa from a planktonic non-mucoid form to a biofilm-forming mucoid form, typically irreversible and associated with accelerated CF lung-function decline.
  • **Pa / Pseudomonas aeruginosa:** Gram-negative opportunistic bacterium; the single most important infectious driver of CF lung disease progression.
  • PFT (pulmonary function test): Standard spirometric measurement of lung health; FEV1 is the headline metric.
  • Time-to-event endpoint: An outcome measured as the time elapsed from a defined start point to a defined event (e.g. "age at first Pa-positive culture"), analyzed with survival methods that handle censoring.

Methods to lift

  • Two-stage time-to-event design — Pa-acquisition age as one endpoint, mucoid-Pa-conversion age as a second, gap between them as a third. Each is modeled separately so the gene's mode of action can be localized.
  • Confounder-identification framework — build a structural model of how CF disease variables (CFTR genotype, age, Pa status, FEV1) confound each other, then identify which confounders to adjust for in each MBL2-CF-trait test before running the regression. This is the methodological ancestor of KNoRMA (Taylor et al., the lung-function-mortality-adjusted phenotype).
  • Reporting all phenotypes tested — McDougal's discipline of reporting both positive and negative findings (no effect on lung function, no effect on infection-to-mucoid-conversion gap) is what makes this paper hold up against the candidate-gene-era replication problem.
  • Twin/sibling cohort substrate — the family-pair design controls for shared environment and shared CFTR genotype in a way that population-based cohorts cannot.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: define Pa-acquisition-age and mucoid-Pa-conversion-age as two distinct time-to-event endpoints in any CFTR PheWAS infection-phenotype module, and explicitly model the two-stage structure (acquisition then conversion) using the McDougal 2010 confounding-adjusted framework rather than treating chronic-Pa-colonization as a single binary outcome. Test candidate modifier loci (CHP2, MBL2, plus newer signals as they emerge) against each stage independently and against the gap between stages, then interpret the three-way pattern as evidence about mechanism.

JORDAN OK before we go in — slow down. MBL2. Pa. CFTR. Twin study. What do I need to know?

ALEX Let's walk through every one. CFTR is the cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator gene on chromosome seven. Mutations in CFTR cause cystic fibrosis. The most common CF-causing mutation is F508del, which deletes one phenylalanine amino acid from the CFTR protein, prevents the protein from folding correctly, and leaves the airway epithelium unable to properly hydrate its mucus surface — that's the proximate cause of the CF lung phenotype. MBL2 is the gene encoding mannose-binding lectin, a protein abundant in blood that recognizes sugars on the surface of bacteria and fungi and tags them for destruction by the complement immune cascade. MBL2 is on chromosome ten and has three common amino-acid-changing variants in exon one — designated alleles B, C, and D — plus two regulatory promoter variants designated H slash L and X slash Y. People homozygous for the major-allele variants have high circulating MBL protein; people carrying the variants have intermediate to very low circulating MBL. JORDAN, the convention in the MBL2 literature is to group genotypes into "high-producing" and "low-producing" categories. Pa stands for Pseudomonas aeruginosa, an opportunistic gram-negative bacterium that colonizes the cystic fibrosis lung typically during childhood and is the single most important infectious driver of CF lung disease progression. Pa starts out as a "non-mucoid" planktonic form and over years converts to a "mucoid" biofilm-forming form — that mucoid conversion is clinically important because mucoid Pa is essentially impossible to clear and accelerates lung function decline. PFTs are pulmonary function tests, the standard measurement of CF lung health, usually summarized as FEV1 percent predicted — the volume a person can forcefully exhale in one second, normalized to age-sex-height expectation. A modifier gene is a gene that's not CFTR but that nonetheless changes CF outcomes. The Twin and Sibling Study is a Johns Hopkins-led longitudinal cohort designed by Cutting and Beaty to use genetically informative pairs — identical twins, fraternal twins, sibs — to disentangle genetic versus environmental drivers of CF variability.

JORDAN Got it. Background?

ALEX From the late 1990s through the late 2000s, MBL2 was repeatedly tested as a candidate CF modifier gene. Some studies found that low-producing MBL2 genotypes associated with worse CF lung function, faster decline, earlier Pa infection, or shorter survival. Other studies found nothing. The literature was contradictory — and the leading hypothesis from the Cutting group was that the contradiction arose because the previous papers had not adequately accounted for the dense confounding structure among CF disease variables. CFTR genotype affects everything downstream. Age confounds lung function. Pa infection both causes lung-function decline and is more likely once lung function declines — so Pa and FEV1 confound each other in a way that depends on time. The proposition was: if you build a structural model of how CF disease variables relate to each other, and only then test the effect of MBL2, you will get a cleaner answer about whether MBL2 actually matters and on which phenotype it acts. That's the setup. The motivation matters because it tells you what kind of evidence the paper is providing — not a brand-new variant discovery, but a methodological correction to a contradictory prior literature on an already-known candidate gene.

JORDAN Methods?

ALEX This is the abstract-derived part — I want to be careful. The cohort is 788 patients from the US CF Twin and Sibling Study. They derived a modeling framework incorporating four key variables: CFTR genotype, age, Pseudomonas aeruginosa infection status, and lung function. That framework was then used to identify which disease variables confound each MBL2-CF-trait test. They tested MBL2 — categorized as "high-MBL" versus "low-MBL" genotype groups — against multiple CF phenotypes, principally age at first Pa infection, age at conversion to mucoid Pa, time from infection to mucoid conversion, and lung function. The abstract does not give us the specific statistical test by name, but the era and the Cutting-group convention strongly suggest Cox proportional-hazards survival analysis for the time-to-event endpoints and linear regression for lung function, with confounders identified through the modeling framework. One design choice worth pushing back on, JORDAN — the high-versus-low MBL2 genotype dichotomy. That dichotomy is the standard in the MBL literature but it collapses many possible diplotypes — combinations of A-A, A-B, A-C, A-D, B-B, B-C, B-D, C-C, C-D, D-D at the structural locus, plus the H/L and X/Y promoter genotypes — into two categories. Zygosity-preserving designs of the kind Lumsden 2020 used for APOE were not standard practice yet at the candidate-modifier-gene era of 2010. That's the design fragility to flag, and it's a recurring fragility across the entire candidate-gene era of CF modifier research.

JORDAN Results — specific numbers, please.

ALEX From the abstract. Low-MBL2 genotypes associated with Pa infection acquired 1.94 years earlier than high-MBL2 genotypes, P equals 0.0034. Among Pa-infected patients, low-MBL2 genotypes associated with mucoid Pa conversion 2.72 years earlier than high-MBL2 genotypes, P equals 0.0003. MBL2 did not associate with the time between Pa infection and mucoid conversion — meaning the speed of progression from non-mucoid to mucoid given that infection has happened is not MBL2-dependent. MBL2 did not associate with lung function directly. So the punchline from this paper is that MBL2 affects timing of acquisition of Pa and timing of mucoid conversion but not the progression rate or the FEV1 trajectory. JORDAN, those are abstract-level effect sizes — roughly two-to-three years of earlier acquisition for the low-MBL group, at p-values that pass conventional thresholds but would not survive a strict multiple-testing correction across the broader CF modifier-gene landscape. The authors answered their motivating question — yes, MBL2 has a real, replicable effect, and the previous literature's inconsistency reflected confounding rather than absence of effect.

JORDAN Hold on — I want to make sure I understand the two-stage structure. Help me?

ALEX Sure. Think of chronic Pa colonization as two distinct events strung together in time. Event one is the first time Pseudomonas aeruginosa shows up in a respiratory culture — that's Pa acquisition, usually in childhood, with a population average around age six in CF patients of the McDougal era. Event two is the first time the cultured Pa shows the mucoid biofilm phenotype, which typically happens a few years later. Between the two events, patients are "Pa-infected, non-mucoid." After event two, they are "Pa-infected, mucoid" and they very rarely revert. McDougal asked three sub-questions. Is MBL2 associated with how soon event one happens? Yes — 1.94 years earlier in low-MBL. Is MBL2 associated with how soon event two happens (counting from birth)? Yes — 2.72 years earlier in low-MBL. Is MBL2 associated with the gap between event one and event two — given you've already been infected, do you progress faster to mucoid? No. So MBL2 acts on timing of arrival at both events, but not on transition speed between them. The same gene moves both arrival times in lock-step, suggesting an innate-immune-surveillance hurdle that gates both colonization and the biofilm transition, rather than two separate biological processes the gene happens to influence.

JORDAN One more thing — what's the broader pattern? Why do candidate-gene papers from the 2000s replicate so unevenly? I want a mental model of how skeptical to be of any given finding.

ALEX Good question to carry around — your skepticism dial should be set higher for candidate-gene papers from the 2000s than for biobank papers from the 2020s. Two reasons converge. First, the cohorts were small — McDougal's 788 patients was a respectable CF-genetics cohort for 2010 but tiny by modern biobank standards where N is hundreds of thousands. Small cohorts produce noisy effect-size estimates, and noisy estimates from one cohort don't replicate cleanly in another. Second, the candidate-gene era pre-dated genome-wide multiple-testing discipline. A paper that tested MBL2 against ten CF phenotypes and reported only the smallest p-value would not necessarily flag that it had done ten tests. The McDougal 2010 paper is notable for being more disciplined than the average of its era — it explicitly tested four phenotypes and reported on all of them, including the negative results for lung function and for infection-to-mucoid-conversion timing. That's part of why it has held up.

JORDAN And the gap conditional on that direction?

ALEX What this paper closes — the question of whether MBL2 is a real CF modifier on Pa-related phenotypes is essentially settled in the affirmative for the high-versus-low dichotomy.
