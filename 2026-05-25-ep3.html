How did this episode land for you?

Blurb

Polineni and colleagues (Marsico Lung Institute / UNC + Strug + Cutting + Drumm + Corvol consortium) used nasal mucosal RNA-seq on 134 CF patients (122 Phe508del homozygotes, all pancreatic-insufficient) cross-referenced with CF Gene Modifier Consortium GWAS data from n=5,659 patients to identify host-defense pathways modulating CF lung disease severity. Three convergent analyses: (1) differential gene expression vs KNoRMA lung phenotype identified seven host-defense pathway domains at q<0.15 — viral infection, inflammatory signaling, lipid metabolism, macrophage function, innate immunity including HLA, apoptosis, ion transport — almost all "detrimental" (increased expression = worse disease); (2) GeneSetScan GWAS pathway analysis independently identified airway mucosal host-defense pathways including goblet-cell biology, ciliary trafficking, mucin biology, EHF, and CFTR interactome; (3) 18 specific genes overlapped between the two analyses at gene-level p<0.10, overlap p=3.6×10⁻⁶ — including ICAM1, IL8, HCFC2, C3, HLA-B, HLA-DRB1, TLR2, TLR3. The chr11p12-p13 (EHF/APIP) and chr6p21.3 (HLA) GWAS risk alleles were associated with increased expression of CFTR interactome pathways — the first reported association of non-CFTR genomic variation with CFTR-processing machinery itself. The CF-direction this episode invites is a CFGP-WGS-era replication of the Polineni transcriptomics-plus-GWAS pathway-integration design, pre-specifying the seven Polineni pathways and 18-gene convergent set as the replication targets, with mandatory modulator-era stratification.

Plain-English glossary

  • APIP (APAF1-Interacting Protein) — also known as MtnB; the first committed enzyme of the methionine salvage pathway; candidate effector at chr11p13 EHF/APIP locus.
  • CFTR interactome — the set of proteins that physically or functionally interact with CFTR during folding, trafficking, processing, and channel regulation.
  • EHF (ETS Homologous Factor) — epithelial-restricted transcription factor that regulates CFTR expression and other epithelial-barrier genes; candidate effector at chr11p13 EHF/APIP locus.
  • eQTL (expression quantitative trait locus) — DNA variant that influences expression level of a particular gene.
  • GeneSetScan (v.0.021) — SNP-to-pathway gene-set analysis tool that maps genotyped SNPs to genes (50 kb upstream/downstream) and pathways via ENSEMBL.
  • GO (Gene Ontology) — structured vocabulary annotating each gene with biological-process, molecular-function, and cellular-component terms.
  • GWAS (genome-wide association study) — scan of common DNA variants for main-effect association with a phenotype.
  • HLA (human leukocyte antigen) region — chr6p21.3 region encoding antigen-presenting molecules for adaptive immunity.
  • HNE (human nasal epithelia) — primary airway epithelial cells obtained by nasal curettage; surrogate for lower-airway biology.
  • KEGG (Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes) — widely used curated biological pathway database.
  • KNoRMA (Kulich Normal Residual Mortality Adjusted) — CF Gene Modifier Consortium standardized lung-disease phenotype based on 3-year FEV1 normalized to CF reference population, mortality-attrition adjusted.
  • Methionine salvage pathway — biochemical pathway recycling methylthioadenosine to methionine; involves APIP/MTAP/AMD1; produces polyamines (proinflammatory) and methylthioadenosine (antiinflammatory).
  • MTAP (Methylthioadenosine Phosphorylase) — methionine salvage pathway enzyme.
  • Phe508del — most common CF-causing CFTR mutation; single amino-acid deletion at codon 508; present in ~66% of CF chromosomes worldwide.
  • PEER factor — probabilistic estimate of expression residuals; standard covariate for eQTL/transcriptomic analysis.
  • RNA-seq — RNA-sequencing; counts RNA copies per gene per sample.
  • SAFE (Significance Analysis of Function and Expression) — pathway-analysis tool using 10,000 phenotype-label permutations to compute per-pathway p-values.

Methods to lift

  • Nasal mucosal curettage RNA-seq pipeline — 3-mm sterile cytology brush at inferior turbinate; nine curettages per visit with one stained for differential cell counts, others pooled for RNA isolation; nasal-lavage cytokines (IL-8, IFN-γ-IP-10, IL-1Ra) measured concurrently to quantify mucosal inflammation at sampling.
  • Illumina HiSeq 2000 25M-reads-per-sample target — minimum sequencing depth for the differential-expression substrate.
  • FPKM-1 expression threshold based on Y-chromosome female-background 95th percentile — empirical floor for "expressed" genes, calibrated to a biologically-impossible background.
  • SAFE (Significance Analysis of Function and Expression) with 10,000 permutations — phenotype-label permutation pathway analysis; Benjamini-Hochberg q<0.15 reporting threshold.
  • GeneSetScan v.0.021 SNP-to-pathway mapping — SNPs mapped to 50 kb up/downstream of protein-coding genes via ENSEMBL v.82.
  • Pathway-size filter 10–200 genes — excludes both noise from tiny pathways and dominance by huge ones.
  • Custom CF-relevant pathway set — pre-specified pathway library extending KEGG/GO with CF-specific gene groupings (EHF positive/negative correlation pathways, goblet-cell pathway, ciliary trafficking, CFTR interactome subsets at multiple Phe508del-treatment timepoints).
  • 9-PC + 2-surrogate-variable secondary expression-confounder adjustment — pathway-result robustness check across two distinct unmeasured-confounder correction frameworks.
  • 39-paired-sample repeatability test — assess intra-subject stability of nasal-epithelial gene expression across two study visits.
  • 18-gene convergent intersection test — count genes appearing at p<0.10 in both differential-expression and GWAS-pathway analyses; compare against random-overlap expectation.
  • Per-locus SNP-to-pathway association via SAFE on top 5 GWAS loci — tests whether the GWAS-significant SNP's risk allele predicts expression of any pre-specified pathway.

The paper is Deepika Polineni, Hong Dang, and 18 co-authors led from the Marsico Lung Institute at UNC Chapel Hill in collaboration with the Strug lab at Toronto, the Cutting lab at Hopkins, the Drumm lab at Case Western, and the Corvol group in Paris — published January 2018 in the American Journal of Respiratory and Critical Care Medicine, titled "Airway Mucosal Host Defense Is Key to Genomic Regulation of Cystic Fibrosis Lung Disease Severity." This is a foundational paper in the modern CF-modifier literature and the dataset substrate for several methods papers we've covered, including yesterday's Wang 2022 SS2 colocalization application.

JORDAN Before we go in. Quick term primer. RNA-seq. Transcriptomics. Pathway analysis. SAFE. KEGG. GO. Differential expression. Heritability. KNoRMA. GWAS. eQTL. CFTR. Phe508del. HLA. EHF. APIP. nasal epithelium.

ALEX All right, all of those, plain language. RNA sequencing — RNA-seq — is a lab technique that takes a tissue sample, extracts the RNA, converts it to a sequence-able form, and counts how many RNA copies exist for each gene. The output is gene-by-sample expression counts. Transcriptomics is the umbrella for studies that use those counts as the primary data. Pathway analysis is when you don't ask "which individual gene is different" but rather "which group of biologically related genes is collectively different" — you sum or otherwise aggregate signals across all genes in a pre-defined pathway like the Toll-like receptor signaling pathway. SAFE — Significance Analysis of Function and Expression — is the specific pathway-analysis tool this paper uses; it permutes phenotype labels 10,000 times to compute a null distribution and reports per-pathway p-values and false-discovery-corrected q-values. KEGG — Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes — is the most widely used curated pathway database. GO — Gene Ontology — is a structured vocabulary that annotates each gene with biological-process, molecular-function, and cellular-component terms. Differential expression means a gene's expression level differs systematically between two groups — in this paper, between patients with mild and severe CF lung disease. Heritability is the fraction of variance in a trait attributable to genetic factors as opposed to environment. KNoRMA — Kulich Normal Residual Mortality Adjusted — is the consensus quantitative phenotype the CF Gene Modifier Consortium uses for lung disease severity; it normalizes FEV1 to a CF reference population and adjusts for survival attrition. GWAS we've covered — genome-wide scan of variant-trait associations. eQTL — DNA variant that influences gene expression. CFTR is the cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator gene; mutations cause CF. Phe508del is by far the most common CF-causing mutation — a single amino-acid deletion at codon 508 of CFTR — present in about two-thirds of CF chromosomes worldwide. HLA — human leukocyte antigen — is the chr6p21.3 region encoding the molecules that present antigens to T cells, the central command of adaptive immunity. EHF — ETS Homologous Factor — is an epithelial transcription factor that regulates CFTR expression. APIP — APAF1-Interacting Protein — is the methylthioribulose-1-phosphate dehydratase enzyme in the methionine salvage pathway. Nasal epithelium — the lining of the inside of the nose — is the most-accessible airway tissue and is treated as a surrogate for lower-airway epithelial biology.

JORDAN OK background. What problem does this paper tackle?

ALEX The motivating gap is the heritability-versus-GWAS-yield gap. Twin and sibling studies in CF have estimated that more than 50% of the variation in CF lung disease severity is heritable — that is, controlled by genetic variation outside CFTR itself. That's a huge fraction. But the GWAS literature available in 2018 — primarily the Corvol 2015 5-locus meta-analysis on 6,365 patients — had only identified five genome-wide-significant loci, and those five loci together explain only a few percent of phenotypic variance. So there's a massive gap between "the heritability is 50%-plus" and "the GWAS-identified variants explain 2-to-3%." The Polineni hypothesis is that part of that gap can be closed by changing what tissue you measure. Earlier CF transcriptomic work had used lymphoblastoid cell lines — easy to obtain, but not airway. Polineni reasoned that doing RNA-sequencing directly on CF airway tissue and integrating with the existing GWAS would identify modifier pathways that the GWAS plus lymphoblastoid combo had missed.

JORDAN Push back. Why not use lower airway tissue directly?

ALEX Because you can't biopsy distal airway in living CF patients except surgically. The compromise is nasal mucosal curettage — a 3-millimeter sterile cytology brush scraped along the inferior turbinate. The paper cites prior work establishing nasal epithelium as a reasonable surrogate for lower-airway epithelium for CFTR-relevant gene expression. It's the most-accessible airway tissue in a living human, and it's CF-relevant because the CFTR protein is expressed across the airway epithelium uniformly.

JORDAN What did they actually do?

ALEX A multi-center study across four sites — Case Western, Johns Hopkins, University of Toronto, UNC — with 134 CF patients, almost all of them pancreatic-insufficient, 122 Phe508del homozygotes. Nasal-mucosal RNA was sequenced on Illumina HiSeq 2000 to a minimum of 25 million reads per sample. They cross-referenced with the CF GWAS data from the same patients — n equals 5,659 in the broader GWAS analysis. Three analyses. One — relate gene expression to KNoRMA lung phenotype, with SAFE pathway analysis at false-discovery-rate threshold 0.15. Two — relate GWAS-derived genetic associations to pathway memberships using GeneSetScan. Three — for each of the five Corvol-2015 genome-wide-significant loci, test whether the locus's risk allele associates with expression of any pathway. Standard covariates — age, gender, ancestry, study site, expression principal components, infection markers including nasal-lavage cytokine levels.

JORDAN Pathway analysis seems easy to abuse. How did they guard against false positives?

ALEX SAFE uses 10,000 permutations of phenotype labels to build a null distribution. Pathway p-values are then Benjamini-Hochberg corrected within each pathway set; only pathways at q<0.15 are reported. They also constrain pathway size — only pathways with between 10 and 200 genes are tested, to avoid both noise from tiny pathways and dominance by huge pathways. The custom CF-relevant pathway set was pre-specified from prior CF literature, not data-mined.

JORDAN Results — what did they find?

ALEX Three clean findings. First, the differential expression arm identified pathways significantly associated with CF lung-disease severity in seven biological domains — viral infection, inflammatory signaling, lipid metabolism, macrophage function, innate immunity including HLA genes, apoptosis, and ion transport. Top KEGG pathways included Hepatitis C virus pathway at p=0.0004 q=0.048, Herpes simplex virus infection at p=0.0005 q=0.048, p53 signaling at p=0.0017 q=0.096, Toll-like receptor signaling at p=0.0068 q=0.149, Influenza A virus at p=0.0074 q=0.149. Critically, almost all of these were "detrimental" — increased pathway expression associated with worse lung disease, consistent with the hyperinflammatory-response hypothesis in CF.

ALEX Second, the GWAS pathway arm — using GeneSetScan on the 5,659-patient GWAS dataset — independently identified pathways related to airway mucosal host defense including N-glycan biosynthesis at p=0.019, Herpes simplex virus infection at p=0.030, asthma at p=0.102, goblet-cell-relevant pathway at p=0.001, ciliary trafficking at p=0.006, mucin Calu3 pathway at p=0.024, EHF positive correlation pathway at p=0.092, and the COPD pathway at p=0.099.

ALEX Third — and this is the integration headline — when they intersected the gene lists driving the differential expression result with the gene lists driving the GWAS result, 18 specific genes showed up in both at gene-level p<0.10. The chance of that overlap is p=3.6 × 10⁻⁶ — far beyond random. The 18 overlapping genes include ICAM1, IL8, HCFC2, C3, HLA-B, HLA-DRB1, TLR2, TLR3 — all canonical innate-immune and HLA-region players. The biological coherence of the overlap, not just its statistical significance, is the substantive finding.

ALEX Plus a fourth result — the five Corvol-2015 GWAS loci tested for SNP-pathway associations. Four of the five — chr11, chr5, chr6, and chrX — showed significant pathway associations. The chr11p12-p13 EHF/APIP locus risk allele was associated with decreased expression of CFTR-dependent ion-channel regulation pathway and with increased expression of CFTR interactome pathways. The chr6p21.3 HLA-region risk allele was also associated with increased expression of CFTR interactome pathways. This is the first reported association of non-CFTR genomic variation with CFTR-protein processing and function — variants near HLA and near EHF/APIP appear to modulate the CFTR molecular machinery itself, not just immune response.

JORDAN Did they answer the question they posed?

ALEX Partially. They demonstrated that CF airway transcriptomics plus GWAS jointly identify heritable host-defense pathways modulating CF lung disease — yes, the convergence story holds. They identified specific pathways and gene sets enriched for both heritability and disease-association — yes. But no individual gene reached genome-wide significance in isolation. The Hepatitis C virus pathway at q=0.048 is the strongest claim and it is a *pathway* not a *gene*. They did not validate any pathway in a replication cohort or with functional experiments. They highlighted the methionine salvage pathway — AMD1, MTAP, APIP — as a therapeutic-relevant candidate because of polyamine biology and a referenced bronchoalveolar-lavage metabolomics study, but the mechanism remains hypothesis-generation rather than mechanism-demonstration.
