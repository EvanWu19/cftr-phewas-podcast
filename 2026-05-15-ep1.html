Blurb

Li, Glessner, Coe and colleagues (Nature Communications, 2020) mapped rare copy number variants in 100,028 European-ancestry individuals from Children's Hospital of Philadelphia and the University of Washington — the largest rare-CNV catalogue at the time, pooling SNP-array and array-CGH genotyping under the ParseCNV pipeline. They identified 11,314 deletion, 5,625 duplication, and 2,746 homozygous-deletion CNV regions, with 14% previously uncatalogued. CNV regions overlap OMIM genes 2.94-fold, GWAS loci 1.52-fold, and ncRNAs 1.44-fold above random. Across four disease categories — autoimmune, cancer, cardio-metabolic, neuro-psychiatric — they pin down headline structural-variant hits (ITGB8 chr7p15.3 deletion for autoimmunity, p<6.87×10⁻¹⁹; SGCZ chr8p22 deletion for cancer, p<7.5×10⁻²⁸; HCN2/POLRMT chr19p13.3 duplication for neuro, p<6.5×10⁻⁴⁷) and surface drug-repurposing candidates. Proposed direction for the CFF WGS cohort: a rare-CNV-burden phenome-wide scan against a CF-adjacent phenotype panel, in parallel with the SNV-burden arm. Open questions before committing: is CFF WGS depth-uniform enough for stable CNV calling, are CF-complication case counts adequate for power, and is the cohort jointly called?

Plain-English glossary

  • aCGH (array comparative genomic hybridization): an array that directly compares fluorescence intensity of patient DNA against reference DNA across probe positions; reads out dosage rather than allele-specific signal.
  • B-allele frequency: the relative intensity of the two alleles at a heterozygous SNP position; shifts predictably when a chromosome is deleted or duplicated.
  • CNV (copy number variant): a structural change where a stretch of DNA from ~hundreds to millions of base pairs is deleted from or duplicated on a chromosome.
  • CNV burden: total kilobases of CNV detected in one person's genome.
  • CNVR (CNV region): a contiguous stretch of the genome where multiple unrelated people independently have CNV calls; the unit of analysis.
  • Dosage sensitivity / haploinsufficiency: a gene's phenotypic response to losing one copy; haploinsufficient genes produce disease at half-dose.
  • GWAS (genome-wide association study): systematic single-variant test for association between common SNPs and a trait.
  • hdCNVR (homozygous deletion CNV region): a deletion present on both chromosomal copies — no functional gene product.
  • Log-R-ratio: the total normalized fluorescence intensity at an array probe; shifts down for deletions, up for duplications.
  • MAF (minor allele frequency): the frequency of the rarer allele or variant at a locus.
  • OMIM: Online Mendelian Inheritance in Man — curated catalogue of disease genes.
  • ParseCNV: the CNV-calling pipeline used here; built and validated by the senior author's group on prior cohorts.
  • PheWAS (phenome-wide association study): systematic test of one or many variants against a wide panel of phenotypes.
  • Pleiotropy: one variant affecting multiple distinct phenotypes.
  • pLoF / PTV (predicted loss-of-function / protein-truncating variant): a single-letter variant predicted to abolish protein function.
  • qPCR (quantitative PCR): a wet-lab assay to confirm a called CNV exists at a specific locus in a specific sample.
  • Recombination hotspot: a genomic interval where meiotic crossovers occur much more frequently than the genome average; many recurrent CNV breakpoints sit in hotspots.
  • SNV (single-nucleotide variant): a one-letter DNA change.
  • WGS (whole-genome sequencing): sequencing the entire genome at base-pair resolution, in contrast to arrays which only sample fixed positions.

Methods to lift

  • ParseCNV — CNV-calling pipeline; consumes SNP-array B-allele frequencies and log-R-ratios (and aCGH log-R-ratios) and outputs CNV regions per sample.
  • Sample QC bundle — call rate >98%, LRR SD <0.25, GC-content correction, <100 CNV calls per sample, monozygotic-twin / duplicate removal.
  • Permutation-based enrichment test — 10,000 random shuffles of CNV regions against OMIM, GWAS, and ncRNA annotation tracks, with empirical p-value calibration.
  • qPCR confirmation — independent validation in ~2,127 randomly selected samples; >95% confirmation rate; the credibility floor for CNV findings.
  • Disease-category bucketing — broad-net design that pools related ICD codes into four buckets to let underpowered single-disease analyses share strength.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: a rare-CNV-burden phenome-wide scan in the CFF WGS cohort (~5,000 patients) testing whether structural variants below the SNV-array resolution floor contribute residual variance to a pre-registered CF-adjacent phenotype panel — pancreatic exocrine insufficiency, CFRD, meconium ileus, FEV1 trajectory, BMI z-score, CF liver disease — in parallel with (not in place of) the SNV-burden arm. Caller candidate: Manta + GATK gCNV joint-called against a CFF-internal reference panel, with CNV regions filtered to MAF <1% and validated by qPCR on a 100-sample sentinel set before the main scan.

What to verify first

  • Is CFF WGS jointly called across all ~5,000 samples, or sample-by-sample? Joint calling is required for stable CNV-region definitions.
  • What is the coverage uniformity across CFF WGS samples? CNV calling from short-read WGS is sensitive to depth variation; a coefficient of variation across samples above ~0.3 will inflate false positives.
  • Phenotype case counts in the WGS-linked subset for CFRD, meconium ileus, severe pancreatic exocrine insufficiency, severe liver disease — under ~200 cases per endpoint and a CNV-burden test is underpowered for anything but the largest effects.
  • Is the CFF WGS cohort ancestry-stratifiable, or is it predominantly European? CNV calling and association testing both work better within one ancestry stratum.
  • What is the current CFGP/CFGS CNV-calling stance on this cohort? Some consortium analyses have already excluded CNVs at the QC stage; we'd inherit their decision unless we re-call.

ALEX Great place to start. Let me lay out the vocabulary, because almost none of this is obvious. Until now, almost every paper we've covered has been about single-nucleotide variants — abbreviated SNVs — which are one-letter changes in the DNA sequence. Like, the letter at position one-million-and-something on chromosome seven goes from a G to an A. That's what gnomAD catalogues. That's what UK Biobank exome studies have been looking at. A copy number variant — abbreviated CNV — is a structural change. Instead of one letter swapping for another, an entire stretch of DNA, anywhere from a few hundred to several million base pairs long, is either deleted from one or both chromosomes, or duplicated so you have extra copies. It's the difference between a typo and a missing paragraph.

JORDAN Why does it matter whether you have two copies versus one versus three?

ALEX Two reasons. The first is dosage. Most genes are tuned to work at the level they're expressed when you have two functioning copies, one from each parent. If you delete a copy you have half the dose, and for some genes that's catastrophic — these are called haploinsufficient genes — and for others it's silent. If you duplicate, you have one and a half times the dose, which for certain genes also drives disease. The second reason is that CNVs are typically much larger than single-letter changes, so a single CNV can wipe out or duplicate dozens of genes at once. That's why CNVs are responsible for a huge fraction of the recognized genomic-disorder syndromes — 22q11 deletion syndrome, Williams syndrome, the 16p11.2 deletions associated with autism — these are all CNVs.

JORDAN Okay, so why is this paper called rare CNVs, and what counts as rare?

ALEX Rare in this paper means a CNV that appears at a frequency above roughly thirty samples out of a hundred thousand — about 0.03% — but below the common-variant threshold. The reason that matters: common CNVs tend to be benign because purifying selection has had time to weed out the harmful ones. Rare CNVs, especially deletions and homozygous deletions, are enriched for ones that actually do something to phenotype.

JORDAN And remind me — we've heard the phrase "European ancestry" a lot. Why does the cohort being European matter for what they can detect?

ALEX For two reasons. First, the CNV reference catalogues that the field uses for calling were built mostly on European samples, so calling accuracy is highest there. Second, what you can detect statistically depends on the variant being present often enough in your sample to make the association stable. A CNV at one in a thousand frequency in Europeans might be vanishingly rare or completely absent in other ancestry groups, so restricting to one ancestry is partly a tradeoff for statistical power and partly an acknowledgement that mixing ancestries with different CNV catalogues introduces noise that swamps signal.

JORDAN Got it. So why this paper today, and why for the CFTR PheWAS project?

ALEX Two reasons. One: scale. The largest published rare-CNV map before this paper was an order of magnitude smaller. Bigger means more rare CNVs become statistically tractable. Two: the disease-category framework. The authors don't go after single diseases. They lump their participants into four big buckets — autoimmune, cancer, cardio-metabolic, and neuro-psychiatric — and ask which CNVs are enriched in each bucket. That kind of broad-net design is conceptually close to what we'd want for a phenome-wide CFTR modifier study, where Runyu doesn't have a single endpoint in mind but rather a curated panel of CF-adjacent phenotypes.

JORDAN Alright, so what was the actual problem the authors set out to solve? What was unknown before they did this?

ALEX The state of the field in roughly 2018 — when this analysis was assembled — was that everyone knew CNVs mattered for some specific conditions. Recurrent large deletions had been mapped for autism, schizophrenia, congenital heart disease, intellectual disability. But for common adult-onset diseases — autoimmunity, the common cancers, hypertension, type 2 diabetes — the role of rare CNVs was murky. The existing CNV maps had been built on cohorts of a few thousand or, at most, a few tens of thousands. The Wellcome Trust did sixteen thousand cases across eight common diseases in 2010 and that was considered ambitious at the time. So the contested questions were: do common adult diseases have a meaningful rare-CNV contribution, or is the action really at the single-nucleotide level? And if CNVs do contribute, are the same CNVs hitting multiple diseases, or are they disease-specific?

ALEX Because the answer determines where genetics money should go for the next phase. If CNVs contribute meaningfully to autoimmune disease, you want array data — which detects CNVs well — and you want it at scale, and you want it in disease registries. If they don't, you spend your money on exome sequencing instead. Genetics has finite resources and this is a real allocation question.

JORDAN What's the second thing they wanted to answer?

ALEX Drug repurposing. If a rare CNV that deletes a particular gene is protective against an autoimmune disease, that gene becomes a candidate drug target — because the deletion is essentially a natural human experiment showing what happens when you lose that gene's function. ALEX Two cohorts pooled to a hundred thousand and twenty-eight unrelated European-ancestry individuals. JORDAN Hold on. SNP array and array CGH — what's the difference?

ALEX SNP array measures intensity at known single-letter positions and uses the pattern of intensities and allele frequencies to infer "there's only one copy of DNA here" or "there are three copies." Array CGH — comparative genomic hybridization — directly compares fluorescence intensity of patient DNA versus reference DNA across probe positions, so it measures dosage more directly. Both can call CNVs, but they have different blind spots. Mixing platforms is a known headache; the authors burn a lot of supplementary methods on showing that the platforms agree.

JORDAN How do they actually go from raw array data to a list of CNVs?

ALEX They use a pipeline called ParseCNV — a CNV-calling tool the senior author's group built and validated in earlier papers. For SNP arrays it uses two signals: the B-allele frequency, which is the relative intensity of the two alleles at heterozygous positions, and the log-R-ratio, which is the total intensity normalized to expected. Both shift in characteristic ways when copy number changes. For array CGH it just uses the log-R-ratio. They impose strict quality control — sample call rate above ninety-eight percent, log-R-ratio standard deviation below 0.25, fewer than a hundred CNV calls per sample, GC-content correction, removal of duplicates and monozygotic twins. After QC they have around a hundred thousand high-quality samples and they validated calls in twenty-one hundred samples with quantitative PCR, getting better than ninety-five percent confirmation.
