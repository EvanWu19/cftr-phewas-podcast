Blurb

Cavazos and Witte 2021 (HGG Advances) is a paired simulation-plus-UKBB-real-data study showing that polygenic risk scores built in European-ancestry GWAS lose 30–40% of their accuracy when applied to African-ancestry individuals, with linear-degradation between admixture extremes. They simulate 200,000 Europeans and 200,000 Africans per replicate via msprime, generate admixed individuals via RFMix with 8-generation random mating, and test 12 PRS variant-selection × weighting strategies. Key result: including variants discovered in an African-ancestry GWAS with fixed-effects meta-analytic weighting recovers African-ancestry PRS accuracy from r=0.45 (EUR-only) up to r=0.75, essentially matching the European-in-European baseline of r=0.77. Local-ancestry weighting underperforms simpler meta-analytic mixtures. Confirmed in UK Biobank for HbA1c, asthma, and prostate cancer. Proposed direction (conditional) for the CFTR PheWAS: pre-register ancestry-aware PRS construction — fixed-effects meta-analytic weighting across multi-ancestry sumstats where available, plus per-ancestry stratified PRS application — as a design choice from the start rather than as a post-hoc rescue.

Plain-English glossary

  • Admixture: recent ancestral mixing across population groups (e.g., African Americans are typically ~20% European / ~80% African).
  • Allele frequency: the proportion of chromosomes in a population that carry a particular variant; varies across ancestries.
  • Ancestry (population genetic): statistical clustering of individuals based on shared ancestral genetic background; common labels (EUR/AFR/EAS/SAS/AMR/MID/MIX) are convenience groupings, not biological categories.
  • Coalescent simulation (msprime): a population-genetics simulation framework that generates synthetic genomes consistent with a specified demographic history.
  • Fixed-effects meta-analysis: a meta-analytic method that pools effect-size estimates across studies under the assumption of a single true underlying effect; here used to combine European and African GWAS sumstats.
  • Genetic drift: random allele-frequency change across generations, strongest in populations that went through bottlenecks (e.g., out-of-Africa migration).
  • GWAS (genome-wide association study): variant-by-variant association testing across the genome in a discovery cohort.
  • HapMap3: a curated set of ~1.1 million well-imputed common SNPs used by many PRS pipelines as a default variant universe.
  • HbA1c: glycated hemoglobin, a long-term blood-glucose marker used as a quantitative trait in the real-data application.
  • LD (linkage disequilibrium): statistical co-inheritance of nearby variants on the same chromosome; LD patterns differ across populations.
  • LD score: a measure of how strongly a variant tags causal variation in a population's LD pattern.
  • Local ancestry: ancestry assignment at a single genomic location for an admixed individual; the genome alternates between segments inherited from each ancestral population.
  • PAGE / H3Africa / Million Veterans Program / All of Us: large GWAS consortia explicitly recruiting diverse populations.
  • PLINK / RFMix / METASOFT: software for variant clumping, local-ancestry inference, and fixed-effects meta-analysis respectively.
  • PRS (polygenic risk score): weighted sum of many small-effect variant contributions, predicting genetic predisposition for a trait.
  • Tag variant: a marker SNP in LD with the true causal variant; most GWAS hits are tag variants rather than causal variants themselves.

Methods to lift

  • Coalescent simulation (msprime) for population-genetic ground-truth: build 200,000-EUR + 200,000-AFR synthetic genomes per replicate, with controlled true causal variants and effect sizes.
  • RFMix admixed-individual simulation with 8-generation two-way random mating; 5,000 admixed test individuals per replicate.
  • 12-cell PRS design grid (3 variant-discovery populations × 4 weighting schemes including local-ancestry-weighted) for systematic PRS-construction-strategy comparison.
  • Fixed-effects meta-analysis via METASOFT to pool EUR and AFR sumstats with consistent LD reference.
  • Linear-combination multi-ancestry PRS (α₁·PRS_EUR + α₂·PRS_AFR) with mixing coefficients fit by 5-fold cross-validation; stratified for binary phenotypes.
  • LD-score-based diagnostic to attribute portability loss to LD vs. allele-frequency vs. effect-size mechanisms.
  • Real-data validation in UKBB across HbA1c, asthma, and prostate cancer with 96 PRS variants (16 p-value thresholds × 2 ancestral discovery populations × 3 weighting schemes), partial-R² as the comparison metric.
  • Stratification by global ancestry deciles for portability-vs-admixture-slope estimation.

Biobank #2

Inclusion of variants discovered from diverse populations improves polygenic risk score transferability

Cavazos TB · HGG Advances · 2021

PMC full text PDF DOI PubMed
Read full transcript (39 turns · ~1991 words)

ALEX Welcome back. Today's paper is Cavazos and Witte 2021, in HGG Advances, titled "Inclusion of variants discovered from diverse populations improves polygenic risk score transferability." Two-author paper from the University of California, San Francisco. It's the direct counterpoint to yesterday's Sinnott-Armstrong paper, which showed how to build polygenic risk scores at UK Biobank scale but also showed those scores transferring poorly across ancestries.

JORDAN So today is the "why does that fail and how do we fix it" episode?

ALEX Exactly. It's a simulation study plus a UK Biobank real-data follow-up, asking what happens when you build a polygenic risk score in one ancestry group and apply it to another, and what mix of approaches gives you a score that works across all groups.

JORDAN Before we start, I'm going to need definitions again. Where do we begin?

ALEX Six terms. First, ancestry. In population genetics, ancestry refers to the geographic origin of a person's ancestors as reflected in their DNA. Common cluster labels in modern studies are European, African, East Asian, South Asian, Hispanic or Latino, and admixed African American. These are statistical clusters, not biological categories, and the labels themselves are continuously debated. Second, admixture. An admixed individual has recent ancestors from multiple ancestry groups. The most-studied admixed group genetically is African Americans, whose typical genome is roughly twenty percent European ancestry and eighty percent African ancestry. Third, linkage disequilibrium, abbreviated LD. This is the statistical association between two nearby DNA variants because they tend to be inherited together. LD patterns differ between ancestry groups, which matters because most GWAS hits are "tag" variants — markers in LD with the true causal variant — rather than the causal variant itself.

JORDAN That's a critical concept. Keep going.

ALEX Fourth, allele frequency. The proportion of chromosomes in a population that carry a particular variant. A variant common in Europeans may be rare or absent in Africans, and vice versa. Fifth, genetic drift. The random change in allele frequencies over generations, especially strong in populations that went through bottlenecks — small founder populations whose descendants inherit only a fraction of the original genetic diversity. Europeans went through a major bottleneck during the out-of-Africa migration, which is why African populations carry more genetic diversity than non-African populations. Sixth, genome-wide association study, abbreviated GWAS. The basic discovery design — test millions of common variants across the genome for association with a trait or disease, in a sample of thousands or hundreds of thousands of people.

JORDAN And local ancestry?

ALEX Local ancestry is the ancestry assignment at a single point in the genome for an admixed person. The genome of an African American typically alternates between segments inherited from African ancestors and segments inherited from European ancestors. Local ancestry inference tools — RFMix being the canonical one — assign each chromosomal segment to its ancestry of origin. Local-ancestry-weighted PRS uses this to mix population-specific weights segment by segment.

JORDAN What was the core problem they were trying to solve?

ALEX As of 2021, when this paper came out, the field had a serious ethics-and-utility problem. Over ninety percent of GWAS participants were of European ancestry — about four-and-a-half times higher than the European share of the world's population. The PRS literature had inherited this skew: nearly every published PRS for a complex trait was discovered and optimized in Europeans. When those scores were applied to people of African ancestry, they explained on average only about a quarter to a half of the variance they explained in Europeans. For East Asians and South Asians, the gap was smaller but still substantial. Two questions had been mostly unresolved. First, why does this gap exist — is it because the GWAS power was lower, or because the LD and allele frequencies are different, or because the underlying biology has different causal variants across populations? Second, what's the best way to fix it given the GWAS data we actually have today?

JORDAN What's the design they used to attack it?

ALEX They used a simulation framework, msprime, to generate genomes for two-hundred-thousand Europeans and two-hundred-thousand Africans per simulation, fifty independent simulations total. They modeled the demographic history of the out-of-Africa migration. Then for each simulation, they generated five-thousand admixed individuals via RFMix, two-way European-African admixture with random mating across eight generations. That setup gives them complete control: they know which variants are truly causal, what the true heritability is, and what the true effect sizes are. Then they run GWAS in their simulated Europeans and Africans, build polygenic risk scores using various combinations of variant-selection populations and effect-size-weighting populations, and measure accuracy in the held-out simulated test cohorts.

JORDAN What did they vary?

ALEX Three main knobs. Where the PRS variants were discovered — European GWAS, African GWAS, or a fixed-effects meta-analysis of both. How the effect-size weights were estimated — European, African, fixed-effects meta-analysis, or a local-ancestry-weighted approach where each segment of an admixed person's genome gets weights matched to its inferred local ancestry. And what disease genetic architecture was assumed — varying number of causal variants from 200 to 1,000, and varying heritability from 0.33 to 0.67. The main text shows results for 1,000 causal variants and heritability of 0.5; supplementary figures sweep the rest.

JORDAN Push back on a design choice for me.

ALEX One honest pushback: they assumed complete sharing of effect sizes between European and African populations. In other words, if a variant is causal in Europeans with a particular effect size, the same variant is causal in Africans with the same effect size. This makes the simulation more tractable but pre-emptively rules out one of the three hypothesized mechanisms for portability loss — population-specific causal variants or different effect sizes. So when they conclude that the portability gap is driven by LD and allele frequency differences, that conclusion is partly baked in by the assumption. The real-data UK Biobank analysis is what pressure-tests this against actual biology.

JORDAN What did they find?

ALEX Several layered findings. First, applying a European-built PRS to simulated Europeans gave a correlation of 0.77 between the PRS and the true genetic liability. Applying it to simulated Africans dropped that correlation to 0.45 — a forty-one percent decrease. For admixed individuals, accuracy decreased linearly with increasing African ancestry: people with eighty to one-hundred percent European ancestry had correlation 0.73, intermediate twenty-to-eighty percent European ancestry had 0.61, and low-European-ancestry admixed individuals — under twenty percent European — had correlation 0.53. The drop was monotonic and stark.

JORDAN That's the failure mode quantified.

ALEX Yes. Second finding: when they used European-discovered variants but reweighted them with African effect-size estimates or with a fixed-effects meta-analysis, accuracy improved for high-African-ancestry individuals — from 0.45 up to 0.65 in fully-African individuals. So partial recovery is possible even before changing variant selection. Third, and this is the big result: when they used African-discovered variants with fixed-effects meta-analysis weighting, accuracy for fully-African individuals reached 0.75 — essentially matching the European-in-European baseline of 0.77. Fourth, a linear combination of a European PRS and an African PRS — the so-called multi-ancestry approach — produced the most consistent accuracy across the full admixture spectrum.

JORDAN And the local-ancestry approach?

ALEX That one underperformed. Local-ancestry-weighted PRSs were typically not as accurate as the simpler fixed-effects-meta or African-weighted alternatives. The reasons are technical — local-ancestry inference adds noise, and the local-ancestry-specific GWAS sample sizes within an admixed individual are limited. The authors' interpretation is that under complete cross-population effect-size sharing, the local-ancestry layer adds complexity without enough payoff.

JORDAN What about the real UK Biobank application?

ALEX They tested HbA1c, asthma, and prostate cancer in self-reported European and African UK Biobank participants. Same pattern. European-derived PRSs explained one-and-a-half percent of HbA1c variance in Europeans but only zero-point-zero-seven percent in Africans — a twenty-fold gap. Asthma and prostate cancer showed similar gaps. And consistent with the simulations, including African-discovered variants improved African-ancestry accuracy in real data across all three traits.

JORDAN Did they answer the question they posed?

ALEX Yes, and the answer is layered. The portability gap exists primarily because European-discovered variants are tag variants for causal sites in patterns of LD specific to Europeans, plus the allele frequencies of those tags differ across populations. The fix is to build GWAS in non-European populations of equivalent power and combine the resulting PRSs through linear mixture. Local-ancestry weighting is not as helpful as the field had hoped. And — the bigger point — the real progress will come from substantially larger non-European GWAS, which is a recruitment problem more than a methods problem.

JORDAN What direction does this paper suggest for Runyu?

ALEX Plain English first. The CF Genome Project cohort is not all of one ancestry. The CFF Patient Registry includes Hispanic, Black, and Asian CF patients, even if Europeans dominate. Any modifier-gene PRS or biomarker PRS we build risks repeating the very ancestry-portability failure mode this paper describes — except in a smaller cohort where the failure is harder to detect because the non-European subgroups have low sample size. One specific direction the episode suggests is to make ancestry-aware PRS construction a pre-registered design choice from the start.

JORDAN Formally?

ALEX Formal version: in any future CFTR PheWAS using polygenic risk scores — whether for CF modifier endpoints or imported biomarker PRSs — the analysis would pre-register an ancestry-aware approach using either (a) a fixed-effects meta-analytic weighting across PRSs derived from multiple ancestry-stratified GWAS, where available; or (b) ancestry-stratified PRS application with separate effect estimates per ancestry stratum in the CF Genome Project cohort; and would report per-ancestry PRS-endpoint association estimates rather than pooled estimates only. This is one possible direction the episode suggests; it is not a committed research design.

JORDAN What does the paper close, and what does it leave open?

ALEX The paper closes the methodological question of how to combine multi-ancestry GWAS sumstats into an ancestry-portable PRS — fixed-effects meta-analysis weighting plus inclusion of non-European discovery variants is the practical recipe. It closes the question of whether local-ancestry weighting is uniformly superior — it isn't, under the modeled assumptions. It leaves open the question of effect-size heterogeneity, since their simulation assumed full sharing. It leaves open whether non-European GWAS at smaller-than-European sample sizes provide enough power for the meta-analytic combination to actually help. And it leaves open whether the conclusions generalize to non-European-and-African ancestry comparisons — South Asian, East Asian, Native American admixture patterns differ in ways that two-way European-African simulation cannot capture.

JORDAN Verification questions for the CF setting?

ALEX What we'd need to check, framed as questions and not facts. Whether the CFF Patient Registry has consistent self-reported race and ethnicity capture across enrollment eras, sufficient for ancestry stratification. Whether genetic-ancestry principal components derived from CF Genome Project whole-genome sequencing align with self-reported ancestry, or reveal substantial admixture that needs local-ancestry inference. Whether per-ancestry sample sizes in the CF cohort are adequate for ancestry-stratified PRS application — fully-African CF patients may number in the low hundreds, and the Cavazos analyses had cohort sizes of tens of thousands. Whether external-weight biomarker PRSs from yesterday's plan exist in non-European GWAS form, or only in European-derived form — if only European, the portability fix would require importing non-European GWAS for the same biomarkers, which may not exist. Whether modulator-era inequity — disparities in Trikafta access by race in the CFFPR cohort — would confound ancestry-stratified comparisons.

JORDAN Tomorrow's tee-up?

ALEX Tomorrow we shift back to CF biology with Bradley and colleagues 2012 in the American Journal of Clinical Nutrition, on genetic modifiers of nutritional status in cystic fibrosis. The first formal twin-and-sibling-based heritability and linkage analysis of BMI in pediatric CF.

JORDAN One-line takeaway for today?

ALEX A polygenic risk score built in one ancestry can fail by a factor of two or more in another ancestry, and the field-tested fix is to include non-European-discovered variants and combine PRSs across ancestries by linear mixture — ancestry-aware design has to be pre-registered, not bolted on.
