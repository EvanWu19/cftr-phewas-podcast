Blurb

Zheng, Haberland, Baird and colleagues (Nature Genetics, October 2020) built the first phenome-wide Mendelian-randomization screen of the plasma proteome, testing 1,002 plasma proteins against 225 disease phenotypes using 2,113 pQTL instruments from five published proteome GWAS plus colocalization filtering. Of 413 MR-supported protein-phenotype pairs, only 283 (68.5%) survived colocalization — i.e., roughly a third of naive proteome-PheWAS hits are LD-confounding artifacts. Their PWCoCo extension (pairwise conditional + colocalization) rescued multiple legitimate hits that standard coloc missed in multi-signal regions, including IL23R→Crohn's disease (posterior 99.3% after PWCoCo vs 0% before). Target-indication pairs with both MR and colocalization support were significantly enriched in approved drugs versus failures. Proposed direction (conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage): a phenome-wide MR scan in a UKBB-scale general-population substrate restricted to the subset of 1,002 plasma proteins prioritized by colocalization with CF-relevant outcome traits (chronic Pseudomonas colonization, lung function decline, CFRD onset, CFLD), using cis-only tier 1 instruments and PWCoCo, with downstream cross-reference to plasma proteomic measurements available in CF-cohort datasets. Open question: whether CF-relevant outcome GWAS at biobank scale have effective sample size adequate for cis-only MR detection.

Plain-English glossary

  • APS — absolute proportion of singletons; selection metric for SVs analogous to LOEUF for SNVs.
  • cis pQTL — protein quantitative trait locus within 500kb of the gene encoding the protein.
  • coloc / colocalization — statistical test of whether two GWAS signals at the same locus share a single causal variant; outputs posterior probability 0–1.
  • druggable genome — curated list of ~2,000 genes whose protein products are plausibly modulable by a drug; stratified into tiers 1 (approved-drug targets), 2 (closely related), 3 (secreted/extracellular).
  • GWAS — genome-wide association study; tests every common variant in the genome against a trait.
  • instrumental variable — a variable that proxies an unmeasured exposure under randomization-like conditions; in MR, the inherited DNA variant is the instrument.
  • LD / linkage disequilibrium — population-scale correlation between nearby variants on the same chromosome; the central source of false positives in naive PheWAS.
  • MR / Mendelian randomization — causal-inference method using inherited variants as instrumental variables for an exposure (here, a protein level).
  • MR-Base / OpenGWAS — public database of GWAS summary statistics used as the outcome side of two-sample MR.
  • PheWAS — phenome-wide association study; sweep across many phenotypes for one variant or gene.
  • pQTL — protein quantitative trait locus; SNP that changes blood protein level.
  • PWCoCo — pairwise conditional and colocalization analysis; iterative coloc that handles multi-signal regions correctly.
  • SNP / SNV — single-nucleotide variant; one-letter DNA difference.
  • tier 1 / 2 / 3 instruments — Zheng's reliability sorting of pQTLs: tier 1 passes specificity + consistency, tier 3 is non-specific (dropped from analysis).
  • trans pQTL — pQTL outside the cis window; indirect, more likely pleiotropic.
  • two-sample MR — version of MR where the exposure-variant and outcome-variant associations come from two separate GWAS samples.

ALEX Welcome back to the CFTR PheWAS reading podcast. Today we're going through "Phenome-wide Mendelian randomization mapping the influence of the plasma proteome on complex diseases" by Jie Zheng, Valeriia Haberland, Denis Baird and colleagues, published in Nature Genetics in October 2020. The team is a Bristol-GSK-Biogen collaboration and the paper has been cited over 600 times in just five years.

JORDAN Why are we reading a proteome paper on a podcast about CF genetics?

ALEX Because it is one of the cleanest worked examples of how to combine two ideas — phenome-wide screening and causal inference — into a single pipeline. If you ever want to ask not just "does this protein associate with disease" but "would lowering this protein actually change the disease," this is the methodological template.

JORDAN Before we go into the paper, I need a vocabulary primer. Walk me through the acronyms.

ALEX Sure. The plasma proteome is the full catalog of proteins floating in your blood plasma. There are about a thousand of them that you can measure cheaply with current assays, and a few thousand more with newer technology. GWAS — genome-wide association study — is the standard sweep where you test every common variant in the genome against a trait. PheWAS — phenome-wide association study — flips that around: you fix one variant or one gene and you sweep across every disease and trait you can measure. SNPs are single-nucleotide polymorphisms — single-letter differences in the DNA. A pQTL is a protein quantitative trait locus: a SNP that changes the blood level of a specific protein. A cis pQTL sits near the gene that encodes the protein, within five hundred kilobases. A trans pQTL sits somewhere else in the genome and changes the protein indirectly.

JORDAN And Mendelian randomization?

ALEX Mendelian randomization is the central trick of the paper. Imagine you want to know whether high blood cholesterol causes heart attacks. You can't randomize people to have high cholesterol — that's the kind of experiment you can't run on humans. But nature has already done a sort of randomization for you. At conception, the genetic variants that nudge your cholesterol up or down are shuffled randomly. So if you find that people who inherited a cholesterol-raising variant also have more heart attacks, you can use that genetic variant as a stand-in for a randomized exposure. That is Mendelian randomization. It uses inherited DNA variants as instrumental variables — pre-randomized proxies — for a biological exposure you can't manipulate directly. The two-sample version, which is what Zheng et al use, means the protein-variant link and the disease-variant link come from two separate GWAS published in different population samples, which is what makes the method scalable across a thousand proteins and two hundred diseases at once.

JORDAN What is the druggable genome, and what does colocalization actually output as a number?

ALEX The druggable genome is a curated list of about two thousand genes whose protein products can plausibly be modulated by a drug. Tier 1 is the genes already targeted by approved drugs or late-stage clinical candidates — about fourteen hundred genes. Tier 2 is closely related to those targets. Tier 3 is secreted or extracellular proteins with weaker drug-like-binding evidence. If your MR hit lands in a tier 1 gene, you are essentially saying the human genetic evidence supports a target that is already known to be drug-tractable. Colocalization outputs a posterior probability — a number between zero and one — that the two GWAS signals at the locus share a single causal variant. The conventional threshold for "colocalized" in this paper is a posterior above 0.8, which is a fairly stringent bar.

JORDAN Linkage disequilibrium keeps showing up too. What is it?

ALEX LD. When two variants sit close together on the same chromosome they tend to be inherited together, so they correlate in the population. That means a variant you measured might be hitchhiking on a neighbor that is the real causal variant. Colocalization is the test that checks whether two GWAS signals at the same location share one underlying causal variant or whether they merely overlap by accident of LD between distinct causal variants.

JORDAN Now, what was the problem they wanted to solve?

ALEX The motivation is brutally practical. The pharmaceutical industry's success rate has been falling. Only one in ten drug targets that enters clinical trials reaches approval. About half fail for lack of efficacy and a quarter for safety. So anything that lets you triage targets earlier — before you spend hundreds of millions of dollars — has enormous value. Previous work showed that drug targets that have human genetic support for the disease they're being developed against are roughly twice as likely to make it through trials.

JORDAN So genetic evidence picks the winners.

ALEX Yes, but most of that prior work was about which gene's variants associate with disease. The proteome is a more direct level — proteins are what drugs actually bind to. If you can show that the genetic variant changes the protein level, and that that change in protein level then changes disease risk, you have a much more interpretable causal chain. Before Zheng et al. nobody had run that whole chain phenome-wide across a thousand proteins at once with both MR and colocalization safeguards.

JORDAN What did they actually do?

ALEX They pulled pQTLs from five published GWAS of plasma proteins. After QC they retained 2,113 pQTLs covering 1,699 proteins. They sorted those pQTLs into three tiers of reliability. Tier 1 instruments passed both a specificity test — meaning the variant did not also strongly influence other proteins, which would create pleiotropy problems — and a consistency test across studies. Tier 2 had some heterogeneity. Tier 3 was non-specific instruments that influenced more than five proteins each; those were dropped because they violate the core MR assumption that your instrument acts on the outcome only through the exposure of interest. They then ran two-sample MR for 1,002 plasma proteins against 225 phenotypes — 153 diseases plus 72 disease-relevant risk factors — using outcome GWAS summary statistics from a public database called MR-Base.

JORDAN Where does colocalization come in?

ALEX Right after the MR. MR alone is vulnerable to LD confounding. If your pQTL is in LD with the real causal variant for the disease but is not itself causal for the protein, you'll get a false positive MR signal. So for every MR hit they ran a colocalization analysis. They got 413 protein-phenotype pairs supported by MR — but only 283 of those, about 69 percent, also survived colocalization. That immediately told them that nearly a third of naive proteome PheWAS hits would be genetic-confounding artifacts.

JORDAN Push back on something they did.

ALEX The big one is the choice of colocalization. Standard colocalization assumes there is at most one causal variant at the locus in each trait. In the cis region for a protein you often have two or three independent association signals, and the same on the disease side. If you ignore that, you get colocalization failures that are really just the algorithm choking on multiple signals. So Zheng et al built and applied PWCoCo — pairwise conditional and colocalization analysis — which iteratively conditions each independent signal on the others before running coloc. It is a sensible extension, but it is a methodological choice the rest of the field has not fully adopted, and the proportion of their final hits that depended on PWCoCo rather than baseline coloc is non-trivial. Anyone replicating their pipeline has to commit to PWCoCo or accept a different denominator of hits.

JORDAN Did the pipeline answer the original question?

ALEX Largely yes. In the cis-only analysis — the cleanest, lowest-pleiotropy version of MR — they identified 111 putatively causal effects of 65 proteins on 52 phenotypes, with MR p-values below 3.5 times ten to the minus seven and colocalization posterior probabilities above 80 percent. Across cis and trans combined the total was 283. The IL23R interleukin-23 receptor case study is the textbook illustration of why PWCoCo matters: standard colocalization between IL23R and Crohn's disease showed zero probability of a shared causal variant, but after PWCoCo iteratively conditioned on the four independent Crohn's signals in the region, the IL23R primary instrument colocalized at posterior probability 99.3 percent. The same conditional fix rescued a haptoglobin signal for LDL-cholesterol. They then linked each protein to the druggable genome and to historic drug-development records. Their headline finding: target-indication pairs that had both MR and colocalization support were significantly enriched in approved drugs versus those that failed, evidencing the value of the pipeline as a triage filter.

JORDAN Where did the hits cluster across disease areas?

ALEX Across a range of categories. They reported colocalization-supported causal effects on anthropometric phenotypes such as height and body-mass index, cardiovascular and autoimmune diseases, and inflammatory bowel disease as the IL23R example illustrates. Several findings replicated previously reported associations — for instance proteins long suspected of CETP-like or IL6R-like causal contributions — which served as positive controls. Disease categories where they found very little were not enumerated explicitly in the main text but the implication was that signal density tracks GWAS power: outcome traits with large published GWAS gave more hits, outcome traits with small GWAS gave fewer. That maps directly onto the CF problem — CF complications are exactly the case where outcome-side power is low, which is one reason we read the paper today.

JORDAN One concrete direction this episode suggests for the CFTR PheWAS?

ALEX Here is one possible direction. The CFTR gene encodes the CFTR protein, which is itself a chloride channel — not a soluble plasma protein and probably not in the Zheng pQTL catalog as a direct target. But CFTR sits in a network of plasma-measurable epithelial and inflammatory proteins, and one possible direction is to use the Zheng pipeline in reverse: identify which of the 1,002 plasma proteins is most causally linked, by their MR-plus-colocalization criterion, to CF-relevant phenotypes — chronic Pseudomonas colonization, lung function decline, CFRD onset, CFLD — in general-population biobank scans, and then ask which of those proteins is itself differentially expressed or differentially measurable in CF cohorts. Formally: a phenome-wide Mendelian-randomization scan in a UKBB-scale general-population substrate for the subset of 1,002 plasma proteins prioritized by their colocalization with CF-relevant outcome traits, using cis-only tier 1 instruments and PWCoCo, with downstream cross-reference of the surviving proteins to plasma proteomic measurements available in CF-cohort datasets.

JORDAN What does the paper close, and what is left open under that direction?

ALEX What the paper closes: the pipeline itself is mature and the inferential pitfalls — LD confounding, multi-signal regions, pleiotropy of trans instruments — are documented and have published solutions in their code base. So the analytic substrate is reusable. What is left open: nearly all of it on the CF side. We do not know which plasma proteins are robustly measurable in CF patients, particularly in adults where chronic infection and CFTR-modulator therapy may shift baseline plasma proteomics in ways that bias both the exposure and outcome. We do not know whether enough CF-relevant phenotypes have well-powered general-population proxies for two-sample MR. Lung function decline does, broadly. Pancreatic insufficiency does not — there is no equivalent biobank phenotype that maps cleanly onto CF pancreatic destruction. And we do not know whether the CFFPR has the longitudinal protein-level data to layer a CF-side replication step on top of the biobank MR. Things we would need to check before committing: whether any of the 65 proteins Zheng identified map to CF-relevant pathways at face value; what plasma proteomic data the CFF Therapy department holds alongside the WGS, if any; whether MR-Base or its successor OpenGWAS has phenotype summary statistics that proxy for CF complications adequately; what the smallest defensible discovery sample size is for cis-only MR on a CF-relevant outcome given the underlying outcome GWAS effective sample size; and whether the proteome-PheWAS direction would deprioritize the genome-wide variant scan that has been the field's standard CF-modifier analytic design.

JORDAN One more thing — the paper claims drug targets with MR-plus-colocalization support are more likely to be approved. How robust is that signal?

ALEX They used historical drug-development data: target-indication pairs with both MR and colocalization support were enriched in approved drugs relative to the background rate of approvals across the druggable genome, and the enrichment was statistically significant in their evaluation against Supplementary Table 20. They are careful not to overclaim — they frame the pipeline as a prioritization filter, not as a guarantee — but the size of the effect is large enough that GSK and Biogen co-authoring the paper is consistent with their internal target-triage practice. That is itself a signal worth noting: when industry geneticists publish a method that they are also using internally, the publication is selecting for methods that have already paid off in private validation.

JORDAN Tomorrow's tee-up?

ALEX Tomorrow's second episode brings us to gnomAD-SV — the structural variation reference for medical and population genetics by Collins, Brand and colleagues, Nature 2020. From causal-protein inference back to the variant catalog itself. One-line takeaway today: MR plus colocalization is the cleanest way to turn a proteome PheWAS hit into something that survives causal scrutiny, and the field-grade implementation of that pipeline is now publicly available and reusable.
