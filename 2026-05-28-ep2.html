One-paragraph blurb

The All of Us Research Program Investigators (lead corresponding: Joshua C. Denny) describe the U.S. National Institutes of Health flagship million-person prospective cohort, intentionally designed to redress UK Biobank's healthy-volunteer and majority-white skew. By July 2019 the program had enrolled more than 175,000 core participants and more than 230,000 total participants across 340+ recruitment sites — 51% non-white and 80% from underrepresented populations, both exceeding the program's stated diversity targets (>45% racial/ethnic minorities and >75% underrepresented among biospecimen contributors). Data sources are EHR linkage under a common data model (OMOP), surveys at fifth-grade reading level in English and Spanish, physical measurements, Fitbit data, and — pilots starting early 2020 — genotyping and whole-genome sequencing performed at designated genome centers. Researchers access the data through a cloud-based Researcher Workbench under a "passport" approval model that grants topic-agnostic access; participants receive ACMG-59 actionable variants and CPIC pharmacogenomic results. The proposed direction for CF: use All of Us as a transferability cohort for CFF-cohort modifier signals, contingent on the actual CF case count in the workbench (load-bearing unknown) and on whether short-read WGS in All of Us reliably calls the harder CFTR variant classes.

Plain-English glossary

  • ACMG-59 genes — list maintained by the American College of Medical Genetics and Genomics of genes where pathogenic findings are considered medically actionable and worth returning to participants.
  • Biobank — a biorepository (frozen blood/saliva) plus paired participant-level data (surveys, EHR, measurements).
  • Cohort study — observational design that recruits a group of people and follows them over time, measuring exposures and outcomes.
  • Common data model — standardized schema for representing EHR data so records from many systems can be queried together; in All of Us, the OMOP Common Data Model maintained by OHDSI.
  • CPIC — Clinical Pharmacogenetics Implementation Consortium, publisher of guidelines linking genetic variants to drug-response recommendations.
  • Direct-volunteer enrollment — All of Us recruitment route for participants not affiliated with a Health Care Provider Organization, who enroll online and visit a participating clinic or lab.
  • EHR (electronic health record) — the structured and narrative clinical data held by a health system on a patient.
  • Genome center — facility designated to produce genomic data from program biospecimens (e.g., All of Us has dedicated genome centers funded through the program).
  • HPO (Health Care Provider Organization) — All of Us partner site type — regional medical centers, federally qualified health centers, the Veterans Health Administration.
  • OHDSI — Observational Health Data Sciences and Informatics consortium, maintainer of the OMOP Common Data Model.
  • OMOP — Common Data Model adopted by All of Us for EHR data representation.
  • Passport model — data access pattern where approved researchers can study any topic that meets the program's allowable-use criterion, without per-project re-approval.
  • Pharmacogenomics — DNA variation that predicts response to or toxicity from particular drugs.
  • Prospective cohort — cohort design that measures exposures first and outcomes later.
  • Researcher Workbench — All of Us cloud-based analysis environment where data is queried in place rather than downloaded.
  • Sync for Science — protocol developed by the Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology to let participants share their EHR data directly with research programs.
  • Underrepresented in biomedical research (UBR) — All of Us umbrella term covering racial/ethnic minorities plus additional populations historically not recruited at meaningful rates.
  • WES (whole exome sequencing) — sequencing only the protein-coding ~2% of the genome.
  • WGS (whole genome sequencing) — sequencing essentially every base in the genome.

Methods to lift

  • OMOP Common Data Model EHR harmonization — the OHDSI-maintained schema that lets multi-site EHR data be queried under a single set of concept IDs.
  • Researcher Workbench — Google-Cloud-hosted JupyterLab + RStudio environment where data analyses are run in place; replaces the project-by-project download paradigm used by UK Biobank.
  • "Passport model" data access governance — single approval covers topic-agnostic future research use; an analyst-friendly governance model that may be portable to other large U.S. cohorts.
  • Electronic consent with embedded video and comprehension-check questions — design pattern usable for any cohort requiring informed consent from non-experts; targeted to a fifth-grade reading level.
  • Genome center model — distributed sequencing across multiple designated centers with central quality control, distinct from UKB's single-vendor sequencing pipeline.
  • Public researcher registry — All of Us publishes approved researchers' names and brief project descriptions, an unusual U.S. transparency posture.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage:

Use All of Us as a transferability and replication cohort for CF modifier signals identified in the CFF cohort. The concrete first step is to identify the CF case set in the All of Us Researcher Workbench by OMOP concept IDs corresponding to ICD-10 E84 (cystic fibrosis) and the relevant pediatric ICD-9 mappings, then characterize: (1) ancestry distribution of the CF subset, (2) availability of WGS for the CF subset, (3) CFTR variant distribution in the WGS calls, and (4) phenotype-data depth for CF-relevant outcomes (lung function, BMI, CFRD, hepatobiliary disease). The transferability question is whether modifier effects discovered in the CFF cohort (predominantly white, predominantly U.S.) generalize to the All of Us CF subset (intentionally diversity-enriched).

What to verify first

  • The actual count of CF cases currently in the All of Us cohort, retrieved via the workbench's data browser, before any analysis design is committed. This is the single load-bearing number.
  • Within-disease diversity: program-wide diversity statistics do not guarantee diversity inside the CF subset specifically; check the CF subset's racial/ethnic distribution separately.
  • CFTR variant-calling reliability in the All of Us short-read WGS pipeline for harder CF-relevant variant classes — large deletions/duplications, intron-9 polyT tract, the TG-T repeat — none of which standard short-read pipelines call out of the box without specialized callers.
  • Phenotype depth of the EHR linkage for CF-specific outcomes (PEx counts, modulator use, F-Best/year FEV₁): the CFF Patient Registry has CF-tuned phenotyping that the All of Us OMOP EHR mapping does not.
  • Governance fit: the passport model lets us analyze without per-project IRB but does not give us a clean path to return CF-relevant findings to participants; assess whether that asymmetry is acceptable for the planned publication.
  • The OMOP concept ID set for CF and its complications: some CFF-Registry-native concepts (e.g., specific pulmonary exacerbation definitions, sweat chloride values) may not map cleanly to OMOP standard concepts.
