Blurb (one paragraph)

Bessonova, Volkova, Higgins and colleagues 2018 in Thorax is the Vertex-sponsored post-marketing safety study that paired the US Cystic Fibrosis Foundation Patient Registry (CFFPR) with the UK CF Registry to ask whether ivacaftor — the first CFTR-channel-potentiator drug, approved 2012 for G551D and other gating mutations — modifies the natural history of CF over multi-year follow-up. 1256 US and 411 UK ivacaftor-treated patients were matched five-to-one against untreated comparators on age, sex, center, and CFTR mutation severity. The headline: significantly lower risks of death, organ transplantation, hospitalization, pulmonary exacerbation, Pseudomonas and Aspergillus prevalence, CF-related diabetes, hepatobiliary disease, bone/joint disease, and depression in the ivacaftor group, plus a ppFEV1 between-group difference of 6.7 percentage points in the US and 8.1 in the UK over the 2011/2012 → 2014 window. Proposed direction (conditional on CFGP WGS variant-calls and registry linkage): CFFPR-anchored post-Trikafta FEV1-trajectory analysis stratified by validated CF lung-modifier-gene score (SLC26A9, EHF/APIP, MUC4/MUC20, AGTR2/SLC6A14, CHP2) with five-to-one nearest-neighbor matching à la Bessonova. Open: the modifier-by-modulator interaction is untouched; survivor bias in 2014 ivacaftor-cohort definition is not formally quantified.

Plain-English glossary

  • CFFPR — US Cystic Fibrosis Foundation Patient Registry, ~115 accredited centers, ~84% consent rate.
  • CFTR — Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane conductance Regulator gene; codes for an epithelial chloride/bicarbonate channel.
  • CFTR modulator — small-molecule drug that fixes CFTR function rather than treating downstream symptoms. Ivacaftor is the first; lumacaftor, tezacaftor, elexacaftor are the others.
  • CFTR mutation class — I (no protein), II (folding defect, e.g. F508del), III (gating defect, e.g. G551D), IV–VI (residual function). Severe = I/II/III.
  • F508del — three-letter deletion, class II, the most common CF-causing variant worldwide (~70% of CF chromosomes).
  • G551D — class III gating mutation, ivacaftor's original FDA indication.
  • Ivacaftor (Kalydeco) — Vertex Pharmaceuticals CFTR potentiator drug, FDA-approved 2012.
  • ppFEV1 — percent-predicted forced expiratory volume in 1 second, the standard CF lung-function measure.
  • PEx — pulmonary exacerbation, a flare requiring antibiotic treatment.
  • UK CFR — UK Cystic Fibrosis Registry, ~32 pediatric + 28 adult centers.
  • Comparator — matched untreated control patient.

Methods to lift

  • Paired-registry observational substrate (US CFFPR + UK CFR analyzed separately, no pooling).
  • 1:5 nearest-neighbor matching on age, sex, CF center, and CFTR mutation class.
  • Pre-treatment historical balance check (2010 CFFPR mortality/transplant comparison between G551D carriers and F508del homozygotes) to defend against unmeasured genotype-severity confounding.
  • Endpoint stack: death, organ transplantation, hospitalization, pulmonary exacerbation, CF complication prevalence by organ system, respiratory microbiology prevalence by organism, ppFEV1 trajectory in a sub-cohort.
  • Stratified analyses by age, sex, baseline ppFEV1 as sensitivity checks.
  • Chi-square or Fisher's exact for categorical endpoints with small cells; p-values reported in lieu of CIs where matching was used.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFGP WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: a CFFPR-anchored, observational, matched-comparator analysis of post-Trikafta FEV1 trajectory, stratified by validated CF lung-modifier genotype score (built from SLC26A9, EHF/APIP, MUC4/MUC20, AGTR2/SLC6A14, CHP2 lead variants), with Bessonova-style 1:5 nearest-neighbor matching on age, sex, baseline ppFEV1, CFTR mutation class, and CF center, testing a between-group FEV1-slope-by-modifier-score interaction term as the primary test.

What to verify first

  • CFFPR ↔ CFGP WGS linkage is in place and current enough for the Trikafta-era patient population (~2019 onward).
  • The five-modifier-gene score is internally validated in a CFFPR-style registry cohort, not just in the original GWAS discovery samples.
  • Matched-comparator design survives sample-size attrition once you stratify by modifier-score deciles, given that Trikafta-eligible patients are now the majority of CFFPR.
  • The registry replatform priority Alex Elbert flagged doesn't actively block the CFGP-linkage work this analysis depends on.
  • Survivor-bias quantification: a sensitivity analysis on patients who died before modulator availability is necessary to defend the matched-cohort baseline.
  • Modulator-era stratification: Kalydeco-only-eligible (~2012–2019) vs Trikafta-eligible (~2019+) patients are different cohorts and need separate handling.

ALEX Welcome back. Today's first paper is Bessonova, Volkova, Higgins and colleagues, 2018, in *Thorax*, the British thoracic-medicine journal. The title is "Data from the US and UK cystic fibrosis registries support disease modification by CFTR modulation with ivacaftor." We're reading it for two reasons. One: it is the cleanest published worked-example of using both the US Cystic Fibrosis Foundation Patient Registry — the CFFPR — and the UK CF Registry as paired observational substrates for a CFTR-genetic-stratification clinical-outcomes question, which is exactly the substrate Runyu's day job at the CFFPR sits on top of. Two: the senior author list includes Alexander Elbert and Bruce Marshall from the US CF Foundation. Alex Elbert is the manager who told Runyu earlier this year that the registry-replatform priority makes a CFTR PheWAS publication unlikely to land in his department this year — so the paper is also a useful window into what a paper with Alex's name on it actually looks like and what the registry can deliver when it leads a clinical-outcomes study.

JORDAN Stop. Spell out every term in that cold open as if I have never heard it. Cystic fibrosis registry, ivacaftor, CFTR modulator, all of it.

ALEX Good. Cystic fibrosis is an autosomal recessive disease — meaning you need a damaging mutation on both copies of the gene — caused by mutations in the CFTR gene. CFTR stands for Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane conductance Regulator and it codes for a channel in the cell membrane that moves chloride and bicarbonate ions out of epithelial cells. When CFTR is broken, the surface fluid layer over airway epithelium gets too thick and salty, the airways clog with mucus, the pancreas scars, sweat is very salty, and patients suffer chronic lung infection ending in respiratory failure. There are over two thousand known CFTR mutations. The most common, F508del, is a three-letter deletion that prevents the protein from folding correctly. F508del is class II — the protein is made but degraded before it ever reaches the cell surface. G551D, a different mutation, is class III — the protein gets to the surface but the channel gate fails to open in response to its normal stimulus. CFTR mutations are grouped into classes I through VI; classes I, II, and III are the "severe" classes, IV through VI are residual-function. Ivacaftor is the trade name Kalydeco from Vertex Pharmaceuticals, a small-molecule drug approved by the FDA in 2012 for CF patients who carry at least one G551D mutation. It is called a CFTR potentiator — it binds the CFTR channel at the cell surface and forces the gate open. Because it acts at the surface, it works for class III mutations like G551D where the channel reaches the membrane but won't open; it doesn't help class II mutations like F508del where the channel never gets there. CFTR modulator is the umbrella term for drugs like ivacaftor and the later ones — lumacaftor, tezacaftor, elexacaftor, sold as Orkambi, Symdeko, Trikafta — that fix CFTR function rather than treat downstream symptoms. CFFPR is the US Cystic Fibrosis Foundation Patient Registry — over 115 accredited CF care centers, about 84 percent of the US CF population consents and contributes annual data on lung function, hospitalizations, microbiology cultures, transplant status, and complications. The UK CFR — Cystic Fibrosis Registry — is the equivalent for England, Wales, Scotland, Northern Ireland: thirty-two pediatric and twenty-eight adult CF centers, over 99 percent data entry rate. ppFEV1 is percent-predicted forced expiratory volume in one second — how much air you can blow out in the first second, expressed as a percentage of the volume a healthy person of your age, sex, height, and ancestry would blow out. It is the standard CF lung-function measure. PEx is pulmonary exacerbation — a flare of respiratory symptoms that requires antibiotic treatment. Hazard ratio, relative risk, p-value — standard. Comparator, in this paper, means a matched untreated control patient.

JORDAN What's the question they're trying to answer?

ALEX The clinical-trial data on ivacaftor was a few hundred patients at a time over a few years. Vertex is the sponsor of this paper, which is a post-marketing safety study — required as a condition of approval. The marketing question is straightforward: are there long-term safety surprises now that real patients have been on the drug for years outside the controlled clinical-trial setting. But the scientific question that justifies publishing it in *Thorax* is bigger: does ivacaftor modify the natural history of CF — meaning does it slow or reverse the disease over years, not just improve symptoms over weeks — when you look at it through the lens of two of the largest CF registries in the world? Survival, transplantation, infection acquisition, hospitalization, exacerbation frequency — these are the disease-modification endpoints. If ivacaftor moves them in the right direction in real-world observational data, that is the strongest evidence short of running a thirty-year placebo-controlled trial that the drug actually changes what it means to have CF. The motivation matters because, before ivacaftor, every CF drug treated downstream consequences — antibiotics for infections, pancreatic enzymes for digestion, dornase alfa for mucus. Ivacaftor was the first drug that fixed the broken channel itself. The proof-of-concept question was whether fixing the channel changes the trajectory.

ALEX Their substrate is the 2014 annual data extracts from the US CFFPR and UK CFR. In the US they identified 1256 ivacaftor-treated patients — patients who had at least one record of receiving the drug in 2014 — and 6200 untreated comparator patients matched five-to-one. In the UK, 411 ivacaftor-treated and 2069 comparators, also five-to-one. Matching was on age, sex, CF center, and mutation severity. Average length of ivacaftor exposure ascertained from the registries was two years in the US and 1.3 years in the UK — the drug came to commercial availability later in the UK. The big confound the matching couldn't fully fix: ivacaftor in 2014 was only indicated for class III gating mutations, primarily G551D — 74 percent of the US ivacaftor group had at least one G551D allele and one hundred percent of the UK ivacaftor group did. The comparator pool was dominated by class II F508del homozygotes. So the comparison is mostly G551D-on-ivacaftor versus F508del-homo-untreated. To handle that, the authors went back to 2010 CFFPR data — pre-ivacaftor — and showed that mortality and transplantation rates were essentially identical between G551D carriers and F508del homozygotes at that time. That is the historical-balance check that lets them attribute later differences to ivacaftor rather than to the underlying genotype. Endpoints: death, organ transplantation, hospitalization, pulmonary exacerbation, prevalence of CF complications, prevalence of pathogenic organisms in respiratory cultures, and lung function — ppFEV1 — measured over time in a subset who started ivacaftor in the first year of commercial availability. Analyses were done within each registry separately; no pooling.

JORDAN I'd push back on one design choice on-air. The matching is supposed to balance the cohorts, but the very thing the drug is supposed to do — improve lung function and stabilize the patient — is also exactly what would make a patient *able* to stay on a chronic medication versus drop off it. There is a survivor bias here that the historical-balance check doesn't address: a sicker patient who would have died on no treatment may also be a patient who, on ivacaftor, dies before the 2014 snapshot and is therefore not in the ivacaftor group. The cohort is defined by "received ivacaftor in 2014," which conditions on having lived to be on the drug in 2014.

ALEX That's a legitimate concern. The authors don't address it directly. They do exclusion-test the patients who never had a lung-function assessment, but they can't bring back the people who died before getting on the drug. Hold that for later.

The headline they wanted: ivacaftor does modify the disease — better survival, fewer hospital visits, fewer exacerbations, lower CFRD, lower infection prevalence, sustained lung function over a multi-year window.

JORDAN One specific concrete research direction that builds on this paper, for Runyu?

ALEX Plain English first. Bessonova establishes that the CFFPR + UK CFR pair is a credible substrate for a multi-year, modifier-stratified, CFTR-treatment-effect study. The natural follow-up that has not yet been done is to ask whether the disease-modification effect of CFTR modulators varies by *modifier-gene background*. That is, does a patient with a "good" SLC26A9 or EHF genotype, who would have done relatively well anyway, gain a different number of FEV1 percentage points on Trikafta than a patient with a "bad" modifier-gene background, who started in a worse place? JORDAN And the gap and verification, given that proposal?

ALEX Bessonova closes the question of whether CFTR modulators in general modify CF natural history — yes — and closes the question of whether the CFFPR + UK CFR substrate can support a multi-year disease-modification analysis — yes. It also closes the historical-balance question between G551D carriers and F508del homozygotes pre-ivacaftor — they were comparable, so the post-2012 divergence is plausibly drug-attributable. What it leaves open: no modifier-gene analysis is attempted, so the modifier-by-modulator interaction question is wide open. The survivor-bias concern I raised is not addressed.
