How did this episode land for you?

Blurb

Taliun and colleagues describe the first 53,831 whole genomes from the NHLBI TOPMed (Trans-Omics for Precision Medicine) Program — a deliberately ancestry-diverse, deep-coverage (~38x mean) WGS resource pooled from 32 heart-lung-blood-and-sleep cohorts. Centralized BWA-MEM alignment to hs38DH and joint variant calling with the GotCloud pipeline (with an SVM-based site filter in place of GATK VQSR) produced 410,323,831 SNVs and indels — 97% with MAF below 1% and 46% singletons. PC-AiR identified 40,722 unrelated samples (third-degree threshold), Eagle 2.4 phased the haplotypes, and RFMix performed local-ancestry inference within a seven-way ancestry framework. The TOPMed-based public imputation reference panel (97,256 individuals, 308M variants by paper date) lifts imputation r^2 in African-ancestry samples at MAF ~0.001 from ~0.17 (1000 Genomes / HRC era) to ~0.96 — making rare-variant imputation in non-European cohorts genuinely tractable. Caveat for CFTR PheWAS work: the panel ascertainment is healthy / heart-lung-blood-and-sleep, so CFTR pathogenic alleles are unlikely to be present at imputable frequencies.

Plain-English glossary

  • Alignment — Mapping each short DNA read produced by a sequencer onto its correct location in the reference genome.
  • Ancestry inference — Estimating a person's genetic ancestral background from their variant data, often as a mixture of reference populations.
  • Ascertainment bias — Distortion in what variants you can see, driven by how participants were recruited (e.g., a healthy-only cohort lacks disease alleles).
  • BRAVO — TOPMed's public variant browser (bravo.sph.umich.edu).
  • BWA-MEM — Burrows-Wheeler Aligner with maximal exact match algorithm; standard short-read aligner.
  • dbGaP — Database of Genotypes and Phenotypes, NIH's controlled-access repository.
  • dbSNP — NCBI's public catalogue of short genetic variants.
  • Decoy sequences — Extra sequences appended to the reference (the "DH" in hs38DH) that absorb reads from regions otherwise prone to mismapping.
  • Eagle 2.4 — Statistical phasing software used by TOPMed.
  • F508del — Most common pathogenic CFTR variant in cystic fibrosis (a 3-bp deletion in CFTR).
  • gnomAD — Genome Aggregation Database; large diverse variant catalogue, predominantly exome-derived.
  • GotCloud — Cloud-friendly joint variant calling pipeline developed at the University of Michigan.
  • Haplotype — The set of variants sitting on the same physical chromosome copy.
  • hs38DH — Human reference genome build 38 (GRCh38) with decoy sequences added.
  • Imputation — Statistically filling in unobserved genotypes by matching observed array genotypes against reference-panel haplotypes.
  • Joint calling — Calling variants across all samples together rather than one at a time, improving rare-variant accuracy.
  • MAF (Minor Allele Frequency) — Frequency of the rarer allele at a variant site in a population.
  • NHLBI — National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute (NIH).
  • PC-AiR — Principal Components Analysis in Related samples; computes ancestry PCs that aren't distorted by family structure.
  • Phasing — Determining which variants lie on the same chromosome copy (maternal vs. paternal).
  • Principal components (PCs) — Top axes of genetic variation; in human cohorts they typically align with continental ancestry.
  • Reference panel — Curated collection of fully sequenced, fully phased genomes used as the basis for imputation.
  • RFMix — Software for local-ancestry inference; assigns each chromosomal segment to an ancestral source population.
  • r^2 (imputation) — Squared correlation between imputed and true genotypes; the standard imputation accuracy metric.
  • Singleton — A variant observed in exactly one individual in the cohort.
  • SVM site filter — Support Vector Machine classifier used as TOPMed's variant quality filter, replacing GATK VQSR.
  • Third-degree relatedness — Less closely related than first cousins (e.g., first cousins once removed); TOPMed's threshold for "unrelated."
  • TOPMed — Trans-Omics for Precision Medicine; NHLBI's diverse WGS programme.
  • UKBB — UK Biobank; ~500K-participant cohort, predominantly European-ancestry.
  • VCF — Variant Call Format; the standard text file format for variant calls.
  • VQSR — Variant Quality Score Recalibration; GATK's standard variant filter (replaced here by SVM).
  • WES — Whole Exome Sequencing; protein-coding ~1-2% of the genome.
  • WGS — Whole Genome Sequencing; essentially all ~3 billion base pairs.

Methods to lift

  • BWA-MEM alignment to hs38DH (build 38 + decoy) as the canonical short-read alignment target.
  • GotCloud joint variant calling for multi-study cohorts where a single coherent call set is required.
  • SVM-based site filter (TOPMed-style) as an alternative to GATK VQSR; useful when training data for true/false sites are available.
  • PC-AiR with a third-degree relatedness threshold to derive an unrelated subset (53,831 -> 40,722 here) for analyses requiring sample independence.
  • Eagle 2.4 for population-scale haplotype phasing.
  • RFMix on a seven-way ancestry reference for local-ancestry inference in admixed individuals.
  • TOPMed Imputation Server (NHLBI BioData Catalyst) as the diverse-WGS imputation backbone for array-genotyped or low-coverage cohorts.
  • BRAVO variant browser for quick allele-frequency lookups in 53,831 diverse genomes.

Proposed direction (conditional)

If the eventual CFF WGS cohort contains a non-European subset too small to analyze in isolation, TOPMed-based imputation could be used to expand variant coverage genome-wide for that subset. Strict caveat: TOPMed's panel is drawn from heart-lung-blood-and-sleep / generally-healthy ascertainment, so CFTR pathogenic alleles are likely absent or below imputable frequency. TOPMed should therefore be treated as an off-CFTR-locus imputation backbone only, with direct sequencing or a CF-specific reference covering the CFTR locus itself.

What to verify first

  • Ancestry composition of the CFF WGS cohort — the value of TOPMed imputation scales with the non-European fraction.
  • Whether existing CFF VCFs already carry multi-ancestry inferences (global ancestry, PC coordinates, RFMix-style local ancestry); if not, this is infrastructure work, not just analysis.
  • What fraction of known pathogenic CFTR alleles are present in the TOPMed imputation panel at frequencies sufficient to impute reliably (BRAVO browser lookup against CFTR2 / ClinVar pathogenic list).
  • Whether downstream PheWAS use cases require array-imputed coverage at all, or whether direct WGS variant calls from the CFF cohort already suffice.

JORDAN NHLBI is the National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute — one of the institutes inside the U.S. National Institutes of Health.

ALEX Right. And TOPMed stands for Trans-Omics for Precision Medicine. So this is the heart-lung-blood institute's big sequencing programme. The reason it matters for us is straightforward: this is the paper that made rare-variant imputation in non-European ancestries a real thing. If you've ever heard the phrase "TOPMed imputation server," this paper built it.

JORDAN Before we go any further I have to slow you down, because half of what you just said is jargon. Can we start from zero? What is whole genome sequencing?

ALEX Sure. Whole genome sequencing — WGS — means reading essentially all three billion base pairs of a person's DNA, not just the protein-coding part. The protein-coding-only version is called whole exome sequencing, WES, and it's about one or two percent of the genome.

JORDAN And alignment?

ALEX When a sequencer reads DNA, it doesn't read the whole chromosome end to end. It reads millions of short fragments, each about a hundred and fifty base pairs long. Alignment means taking each of those short reads and figuring out which spot on the standard reference genome it came from. Mapping reads to the reference. The reference genome is a consensus assembled human genome that everybody uses as a coordinate system.

JORDAN Variant calling?

ALEX Once reads are aligned, you look at each position and ask, does this person differ from the reference here? If yes, that's a variant. Variant calling is the pipeline that turns aligned reads into a list of variants per person.

JORDAN Joint calling versus single-sample calling?

ALEX Joint calling means you call variants across all the samples together rather than one person at a time. It's more accurate, especially for rare variants, because evidence from one person at a position helps interpret weak evidence in another.

JORDAN Phasing?

ALEX You inherit two copies of every chromosome — one from each parent. Phasing means working out which variants sit on the same physical chromosome copy versus the other. The combination of variants on one chromosome is called a haplotype.

JORDAN Minor allele frequency?

ALEX MAF — minor allele frequency — is just how common the rarer version of a variant is in a population. A MAF of one percent means one out of every hundred copies in the population carries that allele.

JORDAN Reference panel and imputation?

ALEX A reference panel is a big collection of fully sequenced, fully phased genomes. Imputation means: somebody else has cheap genotyping array data — maybe half a million sites measured directly — and you statistically fill in the rest of the genome by matching their measured sites to haplotype patterns in the reference panel. The bigger and more diverse the panel, the better the fill-in.

JORDAN Principal components and ancestry inference?

ALEX If you take everyone's genome-wide variants and run principal component analysis, the top components tend to line up with continental ancestry. Ancestry inference is using genetic data to estimate someone's genetic background, usually as a mixture of reference populations.

JORDAN Last one — ascertainment bias.

ALEX Ascertainment bias means: who got recruited into the study shapes what you can see. If a cohort enrolled only healthy people, you won't see disease alleles. If it enrolled only Europeans, you won't see African-specific variants. Hold onto that one — it comes back in beat seven.

ALEX OK, with that loaded, why did TOPMed need to exist? Two big resources came before it. The UK Biobank — UKBB — half a million participants, deeply phenotyped, but the genomes are roughly ninety-four percent European-ancestry, and at the time of this paper most of UKBB had only array genotyping plus exomes, not whole genomes for everyone. So UKBB is big but ancestry-skewed and historically not WGS-first.

JORDAN And gnomAD?

ALEX gnomAD — the Genome Aggregation Database — is genuinely diverse and very large, but the bulk of it is whole exome sequencing, not whole genome. It's brilliant for protein-coding constraint analysis, and the WGS slice exists, but it's smaller, and the noncoding ninety-eight percent of the genome was undercovered for diverse populations.

JORDAN So the gap was specifically diverse, deep, whole-genome data.

ALEX Exactly. NHLBI saw that gap and assembled TOPMed by leveraging more than eighty existing heart-lung-blood-and-sleep cohorts — studies that had already enrolled people with deep clinical phenotyping, including African American, Hispanic and Latino, East Asian, Amish, Samoan, and European American participants. The first freeze, which is what this paper describes, is fifty-three thousand eight hundred and thirty-one of those samples sequenced at roughly thirty-eight-fold mean coverage on Illumina platforms, jointly called through a single centralised pipeline. The point of central joint calling is that even though the samples came from thirty-two different studies, you treat the resulting catalogue as one coherent dataset rather than thirty-two separate ones.

JORDAN And the deliverables for the outside world are what?

ALEX Three things. A variant browser called BRAVO, an imputation server hosted at NHLBI's BioData Catalyst, and the underlying genotype and phenotype data through dbGaP — the Database of Genotypes and Phenotypes — under controlled access.

JORDAN Walk me through methods. What pipeline did they actually run?

ALEX Reads were aligned with BWA-MEM — the Burrows-Wheeler Aligner, maximal exact match algorithm — to the hs38DH reference, which is human reference build thirty-eight with decoy sequences added so that off-target reads have somewhere to go instead of mismapping. Variants were jointly called for every freeze using a pipeline called GotCloud, developed at Michigan, which is a cloud-friendly framework for genotype likelihood calculation and joint calling.

JORDAN And quality filtering?

ALEX Here's a TOPMed-specific choice. The conventional GATK pipeline filters variants with a method called Variant Quality Score Recalibration, VQSR. TOPMed instead used a Support Vector Machine — SVM — site filter trained on known true and false variants. A support vector machine is a machine learning classifier that learns a boundary between two categories. So they trained it to separate likely-real variants from likely-artefacts using site-level features.

JORDAN Phasing?

ALEX Eagle version 2.4 — a haplotype phasing program by Po-Ru Loh — applied across all fifty-three thousand samples.

JORDAN Now the ancestry piece. PC-AiR?

ALEX PC-AiR stands for Principal Components Analysis in Related samples. The problem is, classical PCA gets distorted when your sample contains a lot of relatives, because relatedness pulls components toward family structure rather than ancestry. PC-AiR identifies a maximally unrelated subset, runs PCA on that, and projects everyone else on. TOPMed used PC-AiR with a third-degree relatedness threshold — meaning less closely related than first cousins — and that's how they went from fifty-three thousand eight hundred and thirty-one total samples down to forty thousand seven hundred and twenty-two unrelated samples for analyses where independence matters.

JORDAN Local ancestry?

ALEX For admixed individuals, ancestry isn't one label, it's a mosaic — different chromosomal segments come from different ancestral populations. RFMix is a software tool that infers, segment by segment, which ancestral population each piece of chromosome came from. TOPMed used RFMix on top of a seven-way ancestry framework — seven reference population groups — to do that local-ancestry painting.

JORDAN Headline results.

ALEX Four hundred ten million single-nucleotide and small insertion-deletion variants across fifty-three thousand eight hundred and thirty-one people. Ninety-seven percent of those have MAF under one percent. Forty-six percent are singletons — seen in exactly one person across the whole cohort. Seventy-nine percent of variants weren't in dbSNP at the time, meaning TOPMed roughly doubled the public catalogue of known human variation.

JORDAN And ancestry composition?

ALEX A mix dominated by European American and African American participants, with substantial Hispanic and Latino, plus East Asian, Amish, and Samoan groups. African American and Caribbean groups had the highest heterozygosity, which is consistent with the recent African origin of modern humans — more genetic diversity in the source population than in the descendant migrating populations.

JORDAN Now the imputation result, because that's the one we actually care about.

ALEX They built a TOPMed-based imputation reference panel — by the paper's date it's ninety-seven thousand individuals and three hundred and eight million variants — and then asked, if you take an array-genotyped person and impute them against this panel, how accurate are the imputed genotypes at low frequency? That is a step change. That is rare-variant imputation in African-ancestry samples becoming usable for the first time.

JORDAN So did they answer their question?

ALEX The question was: can we build a diverse, deep, whole-genome resource that the field can use as a reference panel? The answer is yes, they did, and it's now publicly accessible through the imputation server.
