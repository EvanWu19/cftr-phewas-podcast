Blurb

Backman and colleagues at the Regeneron Genetics Center released the exome catalog for 454,787 UK Biobank participants and ran a roughly two-billion-test rare-variant association sweep across 3,994 health traits using REGENIE, identifying 564 genes with associations at P less than or equal to 2.18 x 10^-11. Ninety-one percent of the rare-variant signals were independent of nearby GWAS common-variant signals, and 81 percent of the well-powered associations replicated in the 133,370-person DiscovEHR cohort. For our purposes this is the canonical rare-variant biobank methods paper — REGENIE step-1 plus step-2 with leave-one-chromosome-out, pLOF and pLOF-plus-damaging-missense burden masks at MAF cuts of 1 percent, 0.1 percent and singletons, and conditional re-fitting on the local GWAS sentinel. The honest read-across to a 5,000-sample CF cohort is that we are not powered for unbiased exome-wide discovery and should run this pipeline on a pre-registered panel of CFTR-modifier candidate genes with a panel-scale significance threshold.

Plain-English glossary

  • Burden test (gene-burden test) — a test that collapses all rare qualifying variants in a gene into one per-person score and tests that score against the trait, instead of testing each rare variant individually.
  • Conditional analysis — re-running a rare-variant association test while statistically holding fixed the nearby common-variant GWAS signal, to ask whether the rare-variant result is independent biology or just a rediscovery.
  • DiscovEHR — the Geisinger Health System exome cohort (~133,370 participants) used as the independent replication dataset in this paper.
  • Exome — the roughly 2 percent of the genome that codes for proteins.
  • Genome-wide significance threshold — a Bonferroni-style P-value cutoff that accounts for the total number of tests run; here, P less than or equal to 2.18 x 10^-11.
  • GWAS (genome-wide association study) — a scan of common genetic variants (typically MAF above 1-5 percent) for association with a trait, usually using genotyping arrays plus imputation.
  • LMM (linear mixed model) — a regression that includes a random-effect term for genetic relatedness so related participants do not generate false positives.
  • LOCO (leave-one-chromosome-out) — when building the polygenic background term for a test on a given chromosome, exclude that chromosome from the background, so nearby linked variants cannot inflate the test statistic.
  • MAF (minor allele frequency) — the population frequency of the rarer allele at a variant site.
  • Mask (variant mask) — the rule that decides which variants count toward a gene-burden test; e.g., "pLOF only at MAF less than 1 percent" or "pLOF plus damaging missense at MAF less than 0.1 percent."
  • Missense variant — a single-nucleotide change that swaps one amino acid for another in the protein; the protein is still made but may be altered in function.
  • pLOF (predicted loss-of-function) — a variant predicted by annotation to break the protein, including stop-gained, frameshift, and canonical splice-site variants.
  • REGENIE — open-source regression library from the Regeneron Genetics Center, designed for biobank-scale rare-variant association testing using a two-step ridge-then-test structure.
  • Replication — confirming a discovery in an independent cohort before counting it as established.
  • Singleton — a variant observed in exactly one participant in the dataset.
  • Step-1 / Step-2 (REGENIE) — step-1 fits a stacked ridge regression over a sparse genome-wide SNP set to produce a per-person polygenic background prediction; step-2 tests each variant or gene-burden mask against the trait using the step-1 prediction as a covariate.
  • WES (whole-exome sequencing) — sequencing of the protein-coding portion of the genome only, rather than the full ~3 billion base pairs.

Methods to lift

  • REGENIE two-step pipeline: step-1 stacked ridge regression on a sparse genome-wide variant set to estimate polygenic background, relatedness and population structure; step-2 per-variant and per-gene burden tests conditioning on the step-1 predictor.
  • Leave-one-chromosome-out (LOCO) construction of the step-1 predictor to prevent proximal-LD contamination of test statistics.
  • Approximate Firth logistic regression for binary outcomes to keep test statistics calibrated for low case counts and ultra-rare carriers.
  • Two burden masks per gene: pLOF only, and pLOF plus deleterious missense (5-algorithm consensus); evaluated at MAF cutoffs of 1 percent, 0.1 percent and singletons.
  • Bonferroni significance threshold P less than or equal to 0.05 / total tests, which yielded 2.18 x 10^-11 for their full grid; recompute for our smaller test grid.
  • Conditional re-fit on the nearest GWAS sentinel common variant to demonstrate independence (their 91 percent figure).
  • Replication restricted to gene-trait associations with at least 80 percent power for replication in the independent cohort.
  • Ancestry-stratified follow-up: discover in the largest ancestry, then test directional concordance in smaller ancestry strata as a sensitivity check rather than a discovery mode.
  • Carrier-count floor for reported burden hits; no relaxation of the Bonferroni threshold for primary discovery.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Adopt Backman's REGENIE pLOF and pLOF-plus-damaging-missense burden pipeline, restricted to a curated panel of approximately 20 known CFTR-modifier candidate genes, with a hypothesis-prioritized significance threshold (panel-scale Bonferroni) rather than the exome-wide P less than or equal to 2.18 x 10^-11 cutoff. This is the regime where a roughly 5,000-sample CF cohort can plausibly detect high-effect rare-variant gene-burden signals, analogous to Backman's positive controls; it is not a population-scale exome-wide scan and should not be presented as one.

This direction is conditional on the verification items below. Backman closes the methods question (which pipeline to run); it does not close the power question (whether n equals 5,000 is sufficient for any specific gene-burden test we care about) or the multiple-testing question (what Bonferroni correction applies to a hypothesis-prioritized panel rather than an exome-wide scan).

What to verify first

  • Inspect the CFF WGS variant call files (VCF / BGEN / PGEN) for compatibility with REGENIE's expected input format, and confirm the available functional annotations (VEP / SnpEff) support pLOF and 5-algorithm-consensus damaging-missense masking.
  • Compute power tables for our specific candidate genes and CF-relevant phenotypes — given plausible carrier frequencies and odds ratios, what sample size achieves 80 percent power, and where does n equals 5,000 sit on those curves.
  • Pre-register the candidate gene list, the phenotype list, the variant masks, the MAF cuts and the panel-scale significance threshold before unblinding any analysis on the CFF cohort.

The exome is the roughly two percent of the genome that codes for proteins, so WES means we read only the protein-coding portion, not the whole three billion base pairs. Next, pLOF — that stands for predicted loss-of-function. A pLOF variant is one that the annotation pipeline predicts will break the protein — a stop-gain, a frameshift, a splice-site disruption. Missense is a different category — a missense variant changes one amino acid in the protein, but the protein still gets made; whether it works depends on which amino acid and where.

JORDAN So pLOF is "the protein is gone," missense is "the protein is altered."

ALEX Roughly, yes. Now, gene-burden test. The idea is that any single rare variant on its own is too rare to test, so we collapse all the rare variants in a gene into one score per person — usually "does this person carry any qualifying rare variant in this gene, yes or no" — and then we test that score against the trait. That gives us power we would never have on a single variant. MAF is minor allele frequency — how common the rarer of the two alleles is in the population. A singleton is the extreme case, a variant seen in exactly one person in the dataset. A variant mask is the rule that decides which variants count toward the burden — for example, "pLOF only, MAF below one percent." LMM stands for linear mixed model — a regression that handles relatedness between participants, because in a biobank cousins and siblings show up and naive regression will give you false positives. LOCO stands for leave-one-chromosome-out — when you build the genetic background term, you leave out the chromosome you are testing, so the background does not contaminate the test through nearby variants in linkage. Genome-wide significance threshold is the Bonferroni-style P-value cutoff that accounts for the millions of tests. And replication is the rule that a finding only counts once it shows up in an independent cohort.

JORDAN Got it. Loud and clear.

ALEX Now the background. Why does this paper exist. For about fifteen years the field has been running GWAS — genome-wide association studies — on common variants, the ones that show up in five percent or more of the population. GWAS works because you can genotype common variants cheaply on an array, and the UK Biobank had array data on roughly five hundred thousand people from day one.

JORDAN But common variants are mostly small effects, right.

ALEX Right. Most common-variant hits nudge risk by a few percent. The variants with bigger biological effects tend to be rarer, because evolution removes them. To find rare protein-altering variants, you cannot rely on an array — the array does not even ask about most of them. You have to actually sequence. And exome sequencing is the cost-effective compromise — you sequence the two percent of the genome that codes for proteins, where rare loss-of-function and damaging missense variants are concentrated, instead of paying for whole-genome on every participant.

JORDAN And UKBB did not have exomes on day one.

ALEX Correct. UKBB launched with arrays. Then Regeneron's Genetics Center entered a partnership with UKBB to actually sequence the exomes — first a fifty-thousand-person tranche in 2020, which is the Van Hout paper from earlier this week, and then this paper, the four hundred fifty-thousand expansion. So the goal stated in the abstract is direct — use natural protein-altering variation in nearly half a million people to find rare-variant gene-trait associations at biobank scale, and to ask how those rare-variant signals relate to the existing common-variant GWAS catalog.

JORDAN One paper, one resource, four thousand traits.

ALEX Three thousand nine hundred ninety-four traits, to be exact, but yes — they ran the whole phenome.

ALEX The engine is REGENIE. One-clause description — REGENIE is a regression library released by the Regeneron Genetics Center, designed specifically for biobank-scale data where you have hundreds of thousands of related individuals and millions of variants and millions of tests. It is built around the linear-mixed-model logic but it is fast enough to run in a reasonable wall-clock time on UKBB, which previous tools were not.

JORDAN And the two-step structure.

ALEX Step one of REGENIE fits a stacked ridge regression — a regularized regression — over a sparse set of common variants spread across the genome. That gives you, per person, a polygenic background prediction for the trait that absorbs relatedness and population structure. Step two then takes each variant or each gene-burden mask and tests it for association with the trait, using the step-one prediction as a covariate so the test statistic stays calibrated. The leave-one-chromosome-out construction means that when they test a gene on chromosome seven, the background prediction was built without chromosome seven, so nearby linked variants cannot artificially inflate the test.

JORDAN And the masks.

ALEX Two masks per gene. Mask one is pLOF only — only variants annotated as predicted loss-of-function. Each mask is then tested at three minor-allele-frequency cutoffs — one percent, point one percent, and singletons. So per gene you end up with roughly six burden tests, plus per-variant tests. Across about twenty thousand genes and four thousand traits, that is on the order of two billion tests, and the Bonferroni-corrected genome-wide threshold lands at P less than or equal to two point one eight times ten to the minus eleven.
