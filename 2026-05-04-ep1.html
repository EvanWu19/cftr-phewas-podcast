Blurb

SAIGE-GENE+ is the corrected successor to SAIGE-GENE for set-based rare-variant association tests. The 2020 SAIGE-GENE worked at MAF less than or equal to 1%, but its variance-component test was inflated when restricted to MAF less than or equal to 0.1% or 0.01% — exactly the ultra-rare regime where most pLOF biology lives. The 2022 plus version fixes the type-I-error inflation at those tighter MAF cutoffs and adds a built-in mechanism for combining evidence across multiple MAF strata and multiple functional-annotation strata in a single per-gene p-value. Demonstrated on UKBB whole-exome sequencing across 30 quantitative and 141 binary traits, yielding 551 gene-phenotype associations. For a future CFTR PheWAS, SAIGE-GENE+ is the natural gene-burden engine for the candidate-modifier-gene scan we'd run on the ~5,000-person CFF whole-genome cohort — but its calibration at small N and its annotation-file dependencies need explicit verification before adoption.

Plain-English glossary

  • Allele — one of the alternative DNA letters at a variant position.
  • DNA — the molecule that stores genetic information; a string of about three billion A/C/G/T letters per person.
  • Functional annotation — a computational label predicting what a variant does (pLOF, damaging missense, synonymous).
  • Gene-burden test — see "Set-based test." Output is one p-value per gene rather than one p-value per variant.
  • MAC (minor allele count) — raw integer count of copies of the rarer allele in the sample.
  • MAF (minor allele frequency) — proportion of chromosomes in the population carrying the rarer allele.
  • pLOF (predicted loss-of-function) — a variant computationally predicted to break the gene completely (premature stop, frameshift, splice-site disruption).
  • Saddlepoint approximation (SPA) — a tail-accurate statistical approximation, the trick that saved single-variant calibration in 2018 SAIGE; degrades at very low MAF in set-based tests, motivating SAIGE-GENE+.
  • Set-based / rare-variant aggregation test — instead of testing one variant at a time, pool all rare variants in a gene and test the aggregate.
  • Type I error / false-positive rate — the rate at which a method declares a discovery under the null hypothesis. "Well-controlled" means a p-value of 0.05 actually corresponds to 5% under the null.
  • UKBB — UK Biobank, the half-million-person UK cohort with linked health records.
  • Variance component test — a set-based test that asks whether variants explain more trait variance than null, sensitive when variants in a gene push in opposite directions.
  • Variant — a position in the genome where people differ.
  • WES (whole-exome sequencing) — sequencing of just the protein-coding fraction of the genome (~1–2%).
  • WGS (whole-genome sequencing) — sequencing of the entire genome.

Methods to lift

  • Multi-MAF combination per gene — SAIGE-GENE+ produces a single combined p-value per gene by aggregating sub-tests at multiple MAF cutoffs (canonically 1%, 0.1%, 0.01%). Replaces having to pre-commit to one MAF cutoff or run several Bonferroni-corrected.
  • Multi-annotation stratification — variants partitioned into pLOF-high-confidence, pLOF-plus-damaging-missense, and synonymous-as-empirical-null strata; combined within the same gene-level test.
  • Variance-component tail re-calibration — improved approximation of the variance-component test statistic's tail at MAF ≤ 0.1% or 0.01%, addressing the SAIGE-GENE 2020 inflation in that regime.
  • Synonymous-as-empirical-null — using synonymous variants in the same gene as a calibration check that the burden test is calibrated under the null. This is a standard rare-variant practice that SAIGE-GENE+ supports natively.
  • Reference tools mentioned — SAIGE 2018 (single-variant binary GWAS, PMID 30104761, covered yesterday); SAIGE-GENE 2020 (the immediate predecessor — same machinery without the recalibration); REGENIE-burden (Backman 2021, PMID 34662886, covered 5/1) as the dominant alternative gene-burden engine.

What to verify first

  • Per-gene rare-variant carrier counts at our N — for each candidate modifier gene, count the variants we'd expect in each (MAF cutoff × annotation) stratum at n≈5,000. Empty strata dilute power rather than recover it.
  • SAIGE-GENE+ calibration at small N — this paper's calibration evidence is at UKBB scale (~400K). Verify on simulations or via the synonymous-as-empirical-null stratum on the actual CFF data that calibration holds at n≈5,000.
  • Annotation file construction — the per-variant annotation file (LOFTEE-high-confidence vs damaging-missense vs synonymous) depends on the variant-effect-predictor version, gnomAD constraint table version, and damaging-missense ensemble used. Lock these to reproducible versions before any analysis run.
  • Power realism — Backman 2021's exome-wide rule of thumb says we are ~90× underpowered for an unrestricted exome scan. SAIGE-GENE+ does not fix the underlying power floor; it controls type-I error. Power planning must precede gene-list scoping.
  • WGS-specific extension — SAIGE-GENE+ is demonstrated on WES coding regions. Non-coding rare-variant aggregation (promoter, enhancer variants) requires separate annotation infrastructure not covered by this paper.

JORDAN Before we go in, give me the vocabulary again. Some of it carries over from yesterday but I want it fresh.

ALEX Yes. We'll re-prime the carry-over terms and add the new ones. DNA is the molecule that stores genetic information. A variant is a position in the genome where people differ. The minor allele frequency, abbreviated MAF, is the proportion of chromosomes in the population that carry the rarer of the two letters at a variant — if one in a thousand people carry it, MAF is 0.001, or 0.1 percent. Minor allele count, MAC, is the raw integer count of the rare allele in your sample. WGS, whole-genome sequencing, reads every letter; whole-exome sequencing, abbreviated WES, reads only the protein-coding parts, which is about one to two percent of the genome. UKBB is the UK Biobank, a half-million-person research cohort with linked health records and now exome and genome data.

JORDAN And the new vocabulary specific to today?

ALEX Five terms. Set-based test, also called gene-burden test or rare-variant aggregation test. The idea is simple. Single-variant tests are the natural unit when a variant is common — many people carry it, you can compare carriers to non-carriers and get a clean signal. When a variant is rare — say, one in ten thousand people — no single position has enough carriers to power a test. So instead of testing one position at a time, you collect all the rare variants in a gene, sum or weight them, and test the aggregate. That collection is the "set" in set-based test. Second term: type I error, also called the false-positive rate. If a method has well-controlled type I error, the p-values it reports are calibrated — a p-value of 0.05 actually corresponds to a five-percent chance under the null hypothesis. If type I error is inflated, the method produces too many small p-values, and your discoveries are mostly noise. Third: variance component test. A specific kind of statistical test where you don't ask "do these variants on average push the trait up or down" — that would be a burden test in the strict sense — but rather "do these variants explain more variance in the trait than chance would predict." It's more sensitive when variants in a gene push in different directions, some up, some down. Fourth: pLOF, predicted loss-of-function. A computational annotation that flags variants likely to break a gene completely — premature stop codons, splice-site disruptions, frameshift insertions and deletions. The opposite of damaging-but-still-functional. Fifth: functional annotation. Any computational label that predicts what a variant does — pLOF is one example, predicted-damaging missense is another, synonymous-and-likely-neutral is a third. Modern set-based tests stratify variants by these labels.

JORDAN Good. So what was the specific problem these authors were staring at?

ALEX Set this up against where we left off yesterday. In 2018 Zhou published SAIGE — it solved single-variant testing for binary traits at biobank scale, and it had a hard floor at minor allele count twenty. Below MAC twenty, single-variant testing breaks. The natural next move is to run gene-burden tests, where you aggregate the sub-MAC-twenty variants in a gene and test the aggregate. The 2020 sequel, SAIGE-GENE — without the plus — did exactly that, extending the SAIGE machinery to gene burden using a variance-component framework. It worked well at one threshold: include all variants with MAF less than or equal to one percent. But people quickly noticed that one-percent is too loose for ultra-rare effects. The biology of pLOF rare-variant burden lives mostly below MAF 0.1 percent and a lot of it lives below 0.01 percent. So researchers re-ran SAIGE-GENE at the tighter MAF cutoffs, and they found a problem. The variance-component test became inflated. P-values that should have been calibrated to the null were systematically too small. That's exactly the type-I-error failure I just defined. So you have a tool that works at MAF less than 1 percent, fails at MAF less than or equal to 0.1 or 0.01 percent, and the regime where it fails is precisely the regime that contains most of the biologically interesting rare-variant signal.

JORDAN What was breaking?

ALEX The SAIGE-GENE variance-component test was using a saddlepoint approximation for the test-statistic distribution. We talked about saddlepoint approximation yesterday — it was the SAIGE 2018 trick that saved single-variant calibration on imbalanced binary traits. When you push the same machinery from single variants down into a set-based variance component test on ultra-rare variants, the assumptions the saddlepoint relies on degrade. Specifically, when you have very few non-zero entries in your variant matrix — which is what happens at MAF 0.01 percent in any cohort — the test statistic's true distribution is dominated by a small number of carriers, and the saddlepoint formula no longer matches the true tail. Result: p-values too small, discoveries that don't replicate.

JORDAN And SAIGE-GENE+ fixes this how?

ALEX The plus does two things. First, it changes how the variance-component test's tail is approximated to be accurate at very low MAF. The authors don't only use saddlepoint at the ultra-rare end; they use a more careful tail calibration appropriate for the regime where you have a handful of carriers spread across cases and controls. The result, according to their abstract, is "greatly improved type I error control and computational efficiency to facilitate rare variant tests in large-scale data." Second, and this is the more practically important change for us, the plus version explicitly supports running multiple MAF cutoffs and multiple functional annotation strata in one analysis and combining their evidence. So you can simultaneously test pLOF-only at MAF less than or equal to 0.01 percent, pLOF-plus-damaging-missense at MAF less than or equal to 0.1 percent, and the full aggregate at MAF less than or equal to 1 percent — and SAIGE-GENE+ produces a single combined p-value per gene. That combination logic recovers power that you'd lose by running each stratum separately and Bonferroni-correcting. The abstract reports that incorporating multiple MAF cutoffs and functional annotations improves power and uncovers new gene-phenotype associations.

JORDAN How did they show it works?

ALEX They ran the new tool on UK Biobank whole-exome sequencing data. The application is, in their words, "the analysis of UKBB whole exome sequencing data for 30 quantitative and 141 binary traits." The headline result: 551 gene-phenotype associations identified. That's the deliverable number. The methodological calibration evidence, where they show that the type-I-error problem at MAF 0.1 percent and 0.01 percent is now actually controlled, lives in the supplement — same pattern as the 2018 SAIGE paper. The structure of the empirical demonstration is: a calibration grid showing that the new method's reported p-values match the expected uniform-null distribution across MAF cutoffs and case-control imbalances; then a discovery list showing that the calibrated method finds gene-trait associations that the older, miscalibrated method either missed or reported as false positives.

JORDAN I want to push back on something. Five hundred and fifty-one gene-phenotype associations across 171 traits — that's an average of about three discoveries per trait at biobank scale. Is that a lot or a little?

ALEX Honest answer: it's a lot relative to what the un-calibrated tools would credibly report, because most of their discoveries would not replicate. Set-based rare-variant biology is a structurally smaller signal than common-variant biology — most genes don't have an enriched rare-variant burden for any one trait, and the genes that do tend to be the ones where loss-of-function is biologically catastrophic — APOB for cholesterol, BRCA for cancer, that flavor of result. Five hundred fifty-one well-calibrated gene-trait pairs across the entire UKBB is consistent with the rare-variant gene-burden signal being real but sparse, which is what every other large rare-variant analysis has shown.
