Blurb

Vlasschaert and colleagues, from the Bick group at Vanderbilt, define a five-layer workflow for identifying clonal hematopoiesis of indeterminate potential (CHIP) in the UK Biobank exome cohort (454,787 people) and the All of Us whole-genome initial release (98,560 people). The layers combine sequencing-depth thresholds, population-scale recurrence testing against age and the TERT promoter SNP rs7705526, binomial-test germ line discrimination in TET2 and CBL, gene-list pruning (74 → 58 driver genes), and tuned minimum alternate-allele depth. The pipeline rescues the ASXL1 G646Wfs∗12 frameshift as a real CHIP variant (HR 18.2 for incident myeloid malignancy at VAF ≥10%) while removing the neighboring G645Vfs∗58 as an artifact. One concrete podcast direction: build an analogous five-layer pipeline for CFTR and CF-modifier-locus variant curation in the ~5,000-person CFF whole-genome cohort, anchored on a CF-clean trait analogue rather than age, with explicit homopolymer and reference-assembly checks. Open question: whether population-scale recurrence filters work at 1/90th the sample size and whether structural variants need a parallel pipeline.

Plain-English glossary

  • All of Us (AoU): NIH-funded U.S. research program enrolling a million+ participants with diverse ancestry; first WGS tranche of 98,560 used in this paper.
  • ANNOVAR: Variant annotation software that attaches gene, transcript, and protein-consequence labels to a list of raw variant calls.
  • Biobank: Large research cohort with linked genetic data and longitudinal health records.
  • CCUS (clonal cytopenia of undetermined significance): CHIP plus an unexplained low blood count.
  • CHIP (clonal hematopoiesis of indeterminate potential): Somatic mutation in a myeloid driver gene at VAF ≥2% in someone without a hematologic diagnosis.
  • COSMIC: Catalogue of Somatic Mutations in Cancer; curated database of cancer-associated mutations.
  • Frameshift: Insertion or deletion of a number of bases not divisible by three, shifting the reading frame and usually truncating the protein.
  • Germ line variant: Inherited variant present in every cell, contrasted with a somatic variant acquired during life.
  • GRCh38 / hg38: Current human reference genome assembly; contains a known erroneous duplication of U2AF1 on chromosome 21.
  • Hematopoietic stem cell: Bone-marrow stem cell that gives rise to all blood cell lineages.
  • Homopolymer: Run of the same base (e.g., GGGGG) that causes systematic indel errors during PCR-based sequencing.
  • Hotspot: Specific gene position where mutations cluster across patients.
  • ICC / WHO: Consensus committees that defined the current diagnostic criteria for CHIP and myeloid neoplasms.
  • Indel: Small insertion or deletion mutation.
  • Mutect2: Somatic variant caller within the GATK toolkit; uses local haplotype assembly and Bayesian modeling.
  • PCR (polymerase chain reaction): DNA amplification step in some library-prep protocols; introduces indel errors at homopolymer runs.
  • PCR-free: Library prep without the PCR amplification step, used by All of Us; avoids the homopolymer indel artifact.
  • Somatic mosaicism: Accumulation of mutations in subsets of body cells over time.
  • TERT promoter rs7705526: Common germ line variant near the telomerase gene; one of the strongest known correlates of CHIP, used in this paper as a true-CHIP anchor signal.
  • UK Biobank (UKB): 500,000-person UK biobank with linked EHR; first 50k exomes on NovaSeq S2, remainder on S4.
  • VAF (variant allele fraction): Percent of sequencing reads at a position showing the mutant base.
  • WES (whole-exome sequencing): Sequencing of the ~2% of the genome that codes for protein.
  • WGS (whole-genome sequencing): Sequencing of essentially the entire genome.

Methods to lift

  • Mutect2 (GATK) — local-haplotype Bayesian somatic variant caller; the default starting point for biobank CHIP calling.
  • ANNOVAR — variant annotation tool; attaches gene, transcript, protein-consequence labels.
  • U2AF1 custom pileup script — handles the hg38 chromosome 21 duplication that defeats standard variant callers.
  • Population-scale recurrence test — for any variant seen in ≥20 (UKB) or ≥15 (AoU) individuals, test association with a true-positive anchor signal (here: age and TERT rs7705526) at P<0.10; flag as artifact if not associated.
  • Binomial test for germ line discrimination — in TET2 and CBL, test if read counts at a site look statistically like 50/50 heterozygote; flag at P<0.01 with manual rescue of sites where adding borderline calls improves the anchor-signal association.
  • Sample-level QC — flag samples with unusually high variant counts (e.g., ≥4 mutations) and visually verify the call list.
  • minAD optimization — tune the minimum alternate-allele depth threshold (here: 3 → 5 for UKB) to maximize anchor-signal association.
  • Cox proportional-hazards modeling on incident outcomes — adjusted for age, age-squared, sex, smoking status, and 10 ancestry PCs.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: build a five-layer variant-curation pipeline for CFTR and CF-modifier-locus variants in the ~5,000-person CFF whole-genome cohort, mirroring Vlasschaert's framework — (1) sequencing-depth and VAF basic filters; (2) population-scale recurrence checks against gnomAD and within-cohort frequency; (3) gene-specific anchor-trait association tests using a CF-clean trait (sweat chloride, KNoRMA lung severity, PI/PS status) in place of age and TERT; (4) germ line vs technical-artifact discrimination by binomial test plus homopolymer and reference-assembly inspection; (5) optimization of caller thresholds. Output a released, reproducible call set with explicit per-variant flags for the CF research community.

What to verify first

  • Whether the CFF WGS library prep was PCR or PCR-free (determines whether ASXL1-style homopolymer artifacts are even possible).
  • Whether any CF-modifier or CFTR-region locus has an analogous hg38-assembly issue (paralog, duplication, or unresolved repeat) requiring custom pileup handling.
  • Whether a clean anchor trait exists for the CFF cohort with sample size sufficient to detect a P<0.10 association at variants seen in ~20 individuals (5,000 / 90 of the UKB substrate).
  • Whether the CFF call set already includes a structural-variant layer; the Vlasschaert pipeline only handles SNVs and small indels.
  • Whether CFTR allele class assignments (Class I–VI) and existing CF-modifier sentinels in the cohort have been deduplicated against gnomAD-SV and against any prior multi-allelic calling errors.
  • Whether the Cox proportional-hazards framework transfers cleanly to CFF endpoints (CFRD onset, exacerbation frequency, CFLD onset, transplant) given competing-risks structure and modulator-era stratification.

Today's paper is Caitlyn Vlasschaert, Taralynn Mack, and colleagues, with senior author Alexander Bick at Vanderbilt, published in Blood in 2023, titled "A practical approach to curate clonal hematopoiesis of indeterminate potential in human genetic data sets." The team analyzed about five hundred and fifty thousand people across the UK Biobank exome cohort and the All of Us initial whole-genome cohort.

JORDAN And we are reading it for the methods, right? Not because cystic fibrosis is in it.

ALEX Exactly. This is a paper about how to take messy biobank sequencing data and decide which calls are real. The substance is hematology, but the discipline of filtering is exactly the discipline you would need on a CF whole-genome cohort.

JORDAN Okay, before we dive in I want to ask the boring question. What is clonal hematopoiesis, and what are the data sets they are using? I want to start at zero.

ALEX Fair. Let me lay down the terms one at a time. Clonal hematopoiesis of indeterminate potential, abbreviated CHIP, is a condition where one blood stem cell picks up a mutation as you age, and its descendants grow into a measurable fraction of all your blood cells. The mutation is somatic, meaning acquired during life in one cell, not inherited from your parents. That stands in contrast to a germ line variant, which you were born with and which sits in every cell of your body.

JORDAN So CHIP is age-related and only lives in blood.

ALEX Right. The other acronyms we will need. WGS is whole-genome sequencing, reading every base of the genome. WES is whole-exome sequencing, only the protein-coding parts, about two percent of the genome but cheaper. VAF, variant allele fraction, is the percent of sequencing reads at a position that show the mutant base; for a germ line heterozygote it is around fifty percent, for a CHIP clone it is anywhere from two percent up to twenty or thirty percent. A biobank is a large research cohort with linked genetic data and health records. UK Biobank, or UKB, has half a million people with linked health records, recruited two thousand eight to two thousand ten. All of Us, AoU, is the United States equivalent, more diverse, with whole-genome data on around one hundred thousand at the time this paper was written. ICC and WHO are the consensus committees that wrote the current CHIP diagnostic guidelines. CCUS, clonal cytopenia of undetermined significance, is CHIP plus a low blood count of unknown cause. Mutect2 is a variant caller, software that reads the aligned sequencing files and outputs a list of candidate mutations. GRCh38, also called hg38, is the current human reference genome. A hotspot is a specific position in a gene where mutations cluster.

JORDAN Okay, primer down. So what is the actual problem they are solving?

ALEX The problem is that the same biobank can give you very different CHIP prevalence numbers depending on who is doing the calling. Different groups using different filters on the same UK Biobank exomes have published wildly different CHIP rates and disease associations. The Vlasschaert team set out to write down a reproducible workflow that anyone could follow. They also wanted to use the sheer scale of the data to spot which variants are sequencing artifacts versus real mutations, and which apparent CHIP variants are actually germ line variants leaking through.

JORDAN Why is that hard? I would have thought a mutation is a mutation.

ALEX A few reasons. First, the gold-standard way to confirm a somatic mutation is to sequence a non-blood tissue from the same person and show the mutation is not there. Biobanks do not have that. Second, sequencing introduces errors at certain positions and at certain repetitive sequence motifs called homopolymers, runs of the same base, where the chemistry slips. Those errors look like real mutations. Third, the reference genome has actual mistakes in it. The U2AF1 gene on chromosome twenty-one is duplicated in GRCh38, so reads from one true copy split across two fake copies and the variant caller cannot call it without a custom workaround. Fourth, the originally published list of seventy-four CHIP driver genes is too generous; some of those genes never actually carry age-associated CHIP clones in big cohorts.

JORDAN And what is the motivation outside the paper? Why do epidemiologists care?

ALEX CHIP is associated with cardiovascular disease, all-cause mortality, and progression to myeloid cancers like myelodysplastic syndrome and acute myeloid leukemia. If your CHIP calls are noisy, your effect estimates collapse toward zero. The authors actually demonstrate this: small changes in filtering parameters substantially change the effect size on incident outcomes. So the question they are answering is, what does a reproducible, low-noise pipeline for CHIP calling look like at biobank scale.

JORDAN Now methods. How did they actually do it?

ALEX They start with the two raw substrates. UKB whole-exome, four hundred fifty-four thousand seven hundred eighty-seven people, median sequencing depth forty-fold, meaning each base of each exon is read on average forty times. All of Us whole-genome, ninety-eight thousand five hundred sixty people, also forty-fold, PCR-free library prep. PCR-free matters because polymerase chain reaction introduces in-vitro indel errors at homopolymer runs; the AoU PCR-free approach avoids that whole artifact class. Next, they run Mutect2, a Bayesian local-haplotype variant caller within the GATK toolkit, against a list of seventy-four canonical CHIP driver genes such as DNMT3A, TET2, ASXL1, JAK2, TP53. Mutect2 outputs about six hundred thousand putative variants in UKB and one hundred forty thousand in AoU. They then run ANNOVAR, an annotation tool, to attach gene names, protein consequences, and functional impact tags to each variant. For U2AF1, they bypass Mutect2 and use a custom pileup script that counts reads at known hotspot positions across both duplicated loci.

JORDAN Then filtering.

ALEX Then filtering, in layers. Layer one is sequencing depth: minimum total read depth twenty, minimum reads supporting the alternate allele three, support in both forward and reverse reads, minimum VAF two percent. That drops them to about one hundred thousand UKB variants. Layer two uses the population scale itself: any variant seen in twenty or more people is checked for association with two strong correlates of CHIP, age and a TERT promoter variant rs7705526. If a recurrent variant correlates with neither, it gets flagged as an artifact. Layer three is a binomial test for germ line contamination in TET2 and CBL: if the read counts look statistically like fifty-fifty, the variant is probably germ line. Layer four prunes the gene list itself; sixteen genes never showed an age association and were dropped. Layer five tunes the minimum alternate allele depth from three up to five in UKB. The pushback I want to flag is the binomial test cutoff at P less than 0.01, which can be too strict in the other direction; very large CHIP clones at VAF near fifty percent get thrown out as if they were germ line. The authors handle this by manually rescuing three TET2 sites where adding back the borderline calls actually improved the age signal.

JORDAN Walk me through what they found.

ALEX After all five layers, they end up with thirty thousand one hundred forty-six CHIP variants in UKB and five thousand six hundred sixty-nine in AoU. The first big result is on ASXL1. A frameshift variant called G646Wfs star twelve, which a lot of groups had been throwing out because it sits in a homopolymer, turns out to be real if you require VAF above ten percent. It is one of the three most common CHIP variants in both cohorts, six hundred thirteen calls in UKB and one hundred nineteen in AoU, with a 2.4-fold increased hazard of death and an eighteen-fold increased hazard of incident myeloid cancer in Cox models adjusted for age, smoking, sex, and ten ancestry principal components. The neighboring variant G645Vfs star fifty-eight is the opposite: it has no age association at any VAF threshold in UKB and is completely absent from PCR-free AoU. It is an artifact. The second result is on incident myeloid malignancy, by CHIP clone size. People with small clones, VAF two to ten percent, progress to malignancy at 0.03 percent per year. People with very large clones, above thirty percent, progress at 0.5 percent per year. The third result is ancestry: self-reported Latino or Hispanic individuals in All of Us had significantly lower CHIP prevalence than other groups, which they argue means that biobanks built on people of European ancestry only have been giving us biased epidemiology.

JORDAN Did they answer the question they posed?

ALEX They answered it for CHIP. They produced a workflow, a released call set, and concrete demonstrations of where the workflow rescues real signal and where it drops noise. What they did not do, and acknowledge, is solve the underlying problem that there is no matched non-blood tissue to confirm somatic status. The pipeline is a probabilistic best-effort, not a gold standard.

ALEX It closes the question of whether population-scale recurrence filters are useful. They demonstrably are.
