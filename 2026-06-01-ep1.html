Blurb

Chiles et al. (2026) report the first whole-genome sequencing analysis of weight loss in COPD, pooling 16,972 participants from the TOPMed Initiative and the All of Us Research Program. Using single-variant tests (GENESIS in TOPMed, SAIGE in All of Us), gene-based collapsing (SAIGE-GENE+), cross-cohort meta-analysis (METAL), and transcriptome prediction (MetaXcan), they identify seven novel loci — headlined by common variants in DRAIC (chr15) and RFX3 (chr9), plus rare large-effect signals in HCN1 and an intergenic PPP4R2/PDZRN3 region. The proposed direction for this project: build a parallel longitudinal nutritional-failure analysis in the CFF WGS cohort and ask whether CF weight loss shares genetic architecture with these COPD loci or with general-population BMI loci. The central open question is power and phenotype definition — this study needed 17,000 participants and still showed Q-Q deflation, and it mixed spirometry- and billing-code-defined COPD.

Plain-English glossary (alphabetical)

  • BMI (body mass index): weight-for-height number used to define the weight-loss outcome (final BMI < 20, or ≥5% loss).
  • Cachexia: dangerous illness-driven wasting of muscle and fat; weight loss is its core criterion.
  • COPD (chronic obstructive pulmonary disease): smoking-related disease of persistently narrowed airways.
  • Cosmopolitan / ancestry-blended analysis: analysis run across all ancestry groups together rather than within one.
  • Gene-based (collapsing) test: pools all rare variants in a gene to gain power that single rare variants lack.
  • Intronic / intergenic: inside a gene's non-coding part / between two genes.
  • Locus (pl. loci): a genomic location — a neighborhood, not necessarily one named gene.
  • Meta-analysis: statistically merging results from separate cohorts.
  • Mixed model: statistical method that corrects for relatedness and population structure.
  • Odds ratio (OR): factor by which a variant changes the odds of the outcome; >1 risk, <1 protective.
  • p-value / 5×10⁻⁸: surprise meter; the genome-wide-significance bar after testing millions of variants.
  • Q-Q deflation: observed signals weaker than chance expectation — a sign of low power or conservative calibration.
  • Single-variant test: tests one DNA position at a time for association.
  • TOPMed: NIH program aggregating WGS across many older heart/lung cohorts.
  • WGS (whole genome sequencing): reading essentially the whole genome, not just selected slices.

A locus, plural loci, is just a location on the genome — a neighborhood, not necessarily a single named gene. A variant is a spot where people's DNA letters differ. When they say a variant is intronic, it sits inside a gene but in a part that doesn't directly code for protein; intergenic means it sits between genes. An odds ratio, OR, tells you how much a variant changes the odds of the outcome — above one means more risk, below one means protection. And a p-value is the long-shot meter: how surprised you'd be to see this result if the variant truly did nothing. In genome-wide work the magic threshold is five times ten-to-the-minus-eight, written 5×10⁻⁸, because they're testing millions of spots and need to guard against flukes.

JORDAN That five-times-ten-to-the-minus-eight number — that's the "we tested everything so the bar is brutal" line?

ALEX Exactly. Cross it and people believe you.

JORDAN So what was actually unknown going in?

ALEX We knew weight loss kills in COPD — it's the core criterion for cachexia and it raises mortality. But the risk factors were murky, and nobody had looked at the whole genome for it. Earlier work by some of these same authors used older, sparser genotyping and turned up a couple of hints that didn't hold up. The motivating question here: if we read the entire genome in a big, diverse sample, can we find DNA variants that predispose someone with COPD to lose weight? And can we do it without the usual problem in American genetics — that most discoveries come from people of European ancestry and don't transfer to anyone else?

JORDAN Why is cachexia even a genetic question, though? Isn't wasting just "you're sick, you eat less"?

ALEX That's the intuition, and it's partly true — but it doesn't explain why two people with equally bad lung function can have completely different body trajectories. One holds weight, one wastes away. When you see that kind of person-to-person spread on top of the same disease, geneticists smell a modifier — some inherited difference in metabolism, muscle biology, or appetite signaling that sets how hard the wasting hits. That's the same logic that drives our whole CF modifier project: same primary disease, different outcomes, look for the genes that tune the difference. So this paper is a clean worked example of that reasoning applied to a different lung disease.

ALEX Because a finding that only holds in white Europeans isn't real biology, it's a sampling artifact. They deliberately ran ancestry-specific analyses and a blended one, and we'll see that some of their strongest hits showed up specifically in Black and African-American participants — signals you'd miss entirely in a Europe-only study.

The other is the All of Us Research Program, the NIH's flagship million-person American cohort built to be deliberately diverse. Here's a wrinkle ALEX wants to flag: they defined COPD differently in the two. In TOPMed they used spirometry — an actual breathing test where you blow into a machine. On chromosome 9, inside RFX3, a signal in the cosmopolitan analysis. Separately, in All of Us alone, two rare large-effect variants: one intronic in HCN1 in Black/African-American participants, odds ratio 2.43, and an intergenic one between two genes called PPP4R2 and PDZRN3 that was strongly protective — odds ratio around 0.18 to 0.21, meaning carriers were much less likely to lose weight.

JORDAN Those rare-variant odds ratios are huge compared to the common ones. Why?

ALEX That's a general rule worth internalizing. Common variants — the ones most people carry — tend to have small effects, odds ratios near 1.2 or 1.4, because if a common variant had a giant effect, evolution would usually have pushed it out. Rare variants can carry big effects precisely because they're rare. So in genetics you trade off: common variants are easy to detect but each does little; rare variants can do a lot but you need a mountain of samples to even see them. This paper catches both kinds, which is part of why they wanted whole-genome data rather than a chip.
