How did this episode land for you?

Blurb

Kar, Quiros, Vassiliou and colleagues at Cambridge published a 2022 Nature Genetics paper combining somatic mutation calling on 200,453 UK Biobank exomes with germline GWAS to map the inherited architecture of clonal hematopoiesis (CH). The paper identifies 14 germline loci associated with CH, up from 4 prior, and demonstrates two methodologically important findings: subtype-specific architecture (TCL1A and CD164 have opposite-direction effects on DNMT3A-CH versus TET2-CH, both genome-wide-significant and replicated) and Mendelian Randomization-based causality assignment (smoking and longer telomeres are causal CH risk factors; CH causally raises atrial fibrillation but not coronary artery disease after age adjustment). The proposed direction the episode suggests, conditional on later inspection of the CFGP variant calls and CFFPR phenotype linkage, is a CFTR-genotype-class-stratified PheWAS — modifier-locus tests run separately within each major CFTR-genotype subgroup so that opposite-direction subtype-specific effects can be detected if they exist. The open question is whether the genotype-class subgroups in CFGP are large enough to power within-class scans, and how to handle multi-stratum testing without exhausting the cohort's effective degrees of freedom.

Plain-English glossary

  • Atrial fibrillation — irregular fast heartbeat originating in the upper heart chambers; common, increases stroke risk.
  • Clonal hematopoiesis (CH) — situation in which one bone-marrow stem cell with a damaging mutation comes to produce a disproportionate fraction of circulating blood cells; very common with age and raises blood-cancer risk.
  • CHEK2 1100delC — a well-known truncating variant in the CHEK2 cell-cycle-checkpoint gene; raises risk of breast cancer and DNMT3A-CH.
  • Conditional analysis — re-running a GWAS at a locus while controlling for the lead variant, to ask whether more than one independent signal is present.
  • DNMT3A / TET2 — the two most common CH driver genes; both regulate DNA methylation, but in opposite directions (DNMT3A adds methyl, TET2 removes).
  • Driver mutation — damaging variant that gives the cell carrying it a fitness advantage over its neighbors.
  • Genome-wide significance — p-value < 5 × 10⁻⁸; the conventional threshold for declaring a real GWAS hit.
  • Hematopoietic stem cell (HSC) — rare bone-marrow cell from which all blood lineages descend.
  • LDSC (linkage disequilibrium score regression) — method that uses GWAS summary statistics to estimate trait heritability and detect population-stratification inflation.
  • Linkage disequilibrium (LD) — the non-random co-occurrence of nearby variants on the same chromosome; r² is the standard measure.
  • Mendelian Randomization (MR) — statistical method using inherited genetic variants as natural-experiment instruments to test whether an exposure causally affects an outcome.
  • PheWAS — Phenome-Wide Association Study; fix one variant or gene and scan many phenotypes.
  • Somatic mutation — DNA change acquired during life in a body cell, not inherited from parents; CH driver mutations are somatic.
  • Variant allele fraction (VAF) — fraction of a person's blood cells carrying a particular somatic mutation; high VAF is a large clone, low VAF is a small clone.
  • Whole-exome sequencing (WES) — sequencing of only the protein-coding 1.5 percent of the genome.

Methods to lift

  • Subtype-stratified GWAS — run the same germline GWAS pipeline separately within each major molecular subtype of the disease (here, DNMT3A-CH versus TET2-CH versus large- versus small-clone); compare effect sizes across subtypes with forest plots and report opposite-direction signals as a primary finding rather than a footnote.
  • Held-out replication cohort within the same biobank — reserve a later UKBB exome release (here 221,285 individuals not in the discovery 200K) for replication of the 20 unique discovery lead variants; report replication at p < 0.05 with explicit accounting for multiple testing across the variant set (here Bonferroni gives p < 0.0025).
  • LDSC heritability with cell-type partitioning — estimate trait heritability from GWAS summary statistics and partition across histone-mark and ATAC-seq annotations to identify which cell types harbor most of the heritability signal (here, hematopoietic stem cells and progenitors).
  • Mendelian Randomization for causality assignment — use established external genetic instruments for candidate exposures (smoking initiation, telomere length, alcohol, BMI, apolipoprotein levels) to test whether observational associations between the exposure and the outcome reflect causation; report directionality and effect sizes.
  • Forward PheWAS from CH status as exposure — once CH cases are identified, run a separate PheWAS asking which downstream diseases are more common in CH carriers, controlling for age and other covariates; useful for converting a molecular finding into a clinical-relevance story.
  • Rare-variant focused scan at MAF 0.2–1% — separately test the same cohort for low-frequency-variant associations with relaxed significance thresholds, to catch effects too rare for the standard common-variant GWAS but too common for gene-burden tests.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: a CFTR-genotype-class-stratified modifier-locus PheWAS in the CFGP cohort, with the stratification partition pre-registered (F508del-homozygotes versus F508del-compound-heterozygotes with severe second allele versus F508del-compound-heterozygotes with mild second allele versus rare non-F508del genotypes), the candidate modifier-locus list pre-registered (anchored to prior CFGP modifier publications: SLC26A9, TCF7L2, PTMA, EHF/APIP, CHP2, and the chr20q13.2 linkage region), per-stratum effect sizes reported with forest plots as a primary outcome, and explicit power calculations per stratum acknowledging that compound-heterozygote subgroups may number only a few hundred each in CFGP.

What to verify first

  • Which CFTR-genotype-class partition is standard in published CFGP modifier work, so we use the same partition rather than inventing a new one.
  • The actual case count per class in the CFGP cohort once the variant calls are inspected — F508del-homozygotes will be the bulk, but compound-heterozygote class counts determine whether the per-stratum tests are powered at all.
  • Whether prior CFGP modifier papers report genotype-class-stratified results for any locus, which would tell us empirically whether opposite-direction subtype effects are biologically plausible in CF or merely a methodological possibility.
  • Whether the consortium has a preferred multi-stratum analytic framework (Bonferroni across strata × loci × phecodes is conservative; hierarchical or shrinkage-based testing is more powerful but also requires consortium buy-in).
  • Whether the Mendelian Randomization framework transfers to within-CF causal questions — the Kar paper uses external biobank instruments and the CF-population analogue would need a within-CF or population-matched instrument set, which may not exist for relevant exposures (smoking, BMI, modulator initiation timing).
  • Whether registering the genotype-class partition in advance creates a tension with the consortium's own publication queue — replication-only versus discovery framing has different consortium-level review implications.

Vassiliou at the University of Cambridge, published in Nature Genetics in August 2022, titled "Genome-wide analyses of 200,453 individuals yield new insights into the causes and consequences of clonal hematopoiesis." This is a big-cohort genome-wide association study of an unusual phenotype — not a disease the patient walks in with, but a slowly accumulating molecular event detected in their blood. We are reading it because the methodology — combining somatic mutation calls inside the UK Biobank exomes with germline GWAS on top, then stratifying the GWAS by molecular subtype of the somatic event — is exactly the kind of subtype-stratified analysis we will want to think about for CF complications.

JORDAN Slow down. There were several technical terms in there I cannot let pass. Can we do a glossary first?

ALEX Yes. Clonal hematopoiesis, abbreviated CH, is the situation in which a single blood-forming stem cell in your bone marrow acquires a damaging mutation that gives it a slight competitive advantage over its neighbors, and over years it produces an outsized fraction of the white blood cells circulating in your blood. The mutations are somatic — meaning acquired during life, not inherited — and they sit in a small set of well-known driver genes. The most common CH driver gene is DNMT3A, an enzyme that adds methyl groups to DNA; the second most common is TET2, which removes them. Other CH driver genes include ASXL1, JAK2, TP53, and PPM1D. CH gets more common with age — over a third of people in their seventies show detectable CH — and it raises the risk of blood cancers and some non-blood diseases. A blood stem cell, formally a hematopoietic stem cell or HSC, is the rare bone-marrow cell from which all blood cell lineages descend. A driver mutation is a damaging variant that gives the cell carrying it a fitness advantage. Whole-exome sequencing, abbreviated WES, sequences only the protein-coding portions of the genome — about 1.5 percent of the total — and is the substrate UKBB used to detect both germline variants in the patient and somatic mutations in the patient's blood. GWAS, the Genome-Wide Association Study, scans common DNA variants across a cohort to find which ones associate with a trait. PheWAS, the Phenome-Wide Association Study, flips it — fix the variant, scan many phenotypes. Mendelian Randomization, abbreviated MR, is a clever statistical trick that uses inherited genetic variants as natural-experiment instruments to test whether an exposure causally affects an outcome rather than just correlating with it. Variant allele fraction, abbreviated VAF, is the fraction of a person's blood cells carrying a particular somatic mutation; high VAF means a large clone, low VAF means a small clone. Telomere length is the length of the protective DNA caps at chromosome ends, which shortens with age. Linkage disequilibrium score regression, abbreviated LDSC, is a method that uses GWAS summary statistics to estimate how heritable a trait is and how much of the GWAS test statistic is real signal versus population structure inflation. Conditional analysis is a re-fit of the GWAS at a locus controlling for the lead variant, used to ask whether more than one independent signal exists in the same region. With those defined, we can read the paper.

JORDAN Okay. What problem is this paper actually trying to solve?

ALEX The clinical world has known for about a decade that CH exists, that it gets more common with age, and that it raises the risk of acute myeloid leukemia and other blood cancers. What was much less clear, until this paper, was the inherited-genetic landscape behind CH — what germline variants make a person more or less likely to develop a clonal expansion in the first place. Before Kar 2022, only four germline associations with CH had been identified in European-ancestry populations, all from a smaller earlier UKBB analysis at roughly 50,000 exomes. The biology was thin and the field could not yet say with confidence whether the germline architecture of DNMT3A-driven CH and TET2-driven CH was the same architecture or different architectures. There was also a separate causality question — observational studies linked CH to smoking, to short or long telomeres, to atrial fibrillation, to coronary disease — and observational studies cannot distinguish causation from confounding. The Kar paper aimed to do four things in one analysis: scale up the germline GWAS by quadrupling the sample to 200,453 UKBB participants, stratify the GWAS by CH subtype to test whether DNMT3A-CH and TET2-CH have shared or distinct germline architectures, run Mendelian Randomization to ask which of the observational risk factor associations are causal, and run a forward PheWAS asking which downstream diseases CH itself raises the risk of.

JORDAN How did they do it, mechanically?

ALEX They started with whole-exome sequencing on the 200,453 UKBB participants — the entire 200K initial exome release. They ran somatic mutation calling against a curated list of 43 CH driver genes, filtered against a predefined list of CH driver variants, and identified 11,697 mutations in 10,924 individuals — UKBB CH prevalence of 5.45 percent. They split CH by major driver gene — 5,185 with DNMT3A mutations, 2,041 with TET2, plus smaller groups for ASXL1, JAK2, PPM1D, and others — and by clone size using VAF, with VAF below 0.1 defined as small-clone CH and at or above 0.1 as large-clone CH. They then ran a germline GWAS on 184,121 individuals of genetically inferred European ancestry, comparing 10,203 CH cases to 173,918 controls without CH. Heritability was estimated using LDSC at 3.57 percent — modest but real. Cell-type heritability partitioning showed enrichment in hematopoietic stem cell and progenitor open chromatin, confirming that CH is a stem-cell-intrinsic phenomenon. Then they ran four additional stratified GWAS — DNMT3A-CH, TET2-CH, large-clone CH, small-clone CH — to look for subtype-specific architecture. Replication used an independent UKBB exome subset of 221,285 European-ancestry individuals released after the discovery analysis. Mendelian Randomization used established external genetic instruments for smoking initiation, telomere length, alcohol use, adiposity, and apolipoprotein levels as exposures, with CH as the outcome. ALEX flags one design choice as worth pushing back on — the GWAS was European-only because the non-European UKBB subset had only 505 CH cases against 11,893 controls, which is genuinely underpowered, but the consequence is that the entire architecture they describe applies only to European-ancestry CH and the transferability is left as an extended-data figure rather than a primary analysis.

JORDAN What did they find — and did they answer their question?

ALEX They identified seven genome-wide-significant loci for overall CH. Three were previously reported in European-ancestry populations — two distinct signals at 5p15.33 around the TERT gene, and one at 3q25.33 near SMC4. Three were new — 4q35.1 at ENPP6 lead variant rs13130545, 6q21 at CD164 rs35452836, and 11q22.3 at ATM rs11212666. The DNMT3A-stratified analysis added two more — 1q42.12 at PARP1 lead variant rs138994074, which is in tight linkage with a missense variant in the PARP1 catalytic domain, and 18q12.3 at SETBP1 rs8088824. The TET2-stratified analysis added 7q32.2 at TMEM209 lead variant rs79633204. The single most striking subtype-specific finding is at 14q32.13 near TCL1A — the same allele has odds ratio 1.28 with TET2-CH and odds ratio 0.87 with DNMT3A-CH, opposite directions, both genome-wide-significant, both replicated in the held-out 221K replication cohort. A similar opposite-direction pattern showed at 6q21 near CD164. A rare-variant focused scan — minor allele frequency 0.2 to 1 percent — identified a CHEK2 variant at 22q12.1, lead variant rs62237617, that is in perfect linkage with the well-known 1100delC truncating allele and confers an odds ratio of 4.1 specifically for DNMT3A-CH. The Mendelian Randomization analyses showed that smoking initiation and longer leukocyte telomere length are causal risk factors for CH, and that genetic predisposition to CH causally raises the risk of myeloproliferative neoplasia, non-hematological malignancies, atrial fibrillation, and blood epigenetic ageing — but does not, after adjustment, causally raise coronary artery disease risk, contrary to earlier observational reports. So yes, they answered their question: the germline architecture of CH is partly shared and partly subtype-specific, and the most clinically loaded downstream consequence — coronary artery disease — does not survive Mendelian Randomization after age adjustment.

JORDAN What concrete CFTR PheWAS direction does this paper suggest, in plain English first?

ALEX Plainly: when a single locus has opposite effects on two molecular subtypes of a disease, you only see it if you stratify. The Kar paper would have missed the TCL1A and CD164 subtype-specific signals if it had pooled all CH together. CF has analogous subtypes — F508del-homozygotes, F508del-compound-heterozygotes with a Class I or II variant on the second allele, F508del-compound-heterozygotes with a Class III to V variant on the second allele, and rare non-F508del genotypes. These are not just labels; they correspond to different residual CFTR function and different modulator-responsiveness. Formally: a CFTR-genotype-stratified PheWAS — the same modifier-locus list tested separately within each CFTR-genotype-class subgroup of the CFGP cohort, with the four-or-five-class definition pre-registered in advance, with effect-size heterogeneity across classes reported as a primary outcome and with explicit power notes acknowledging that some genotype-class subgroups will be small even within the 5,000-person total cohort.

JORDAN Given that proposal, what does Kar 2022 close, what does it leave open, and what would Runyu need to verify before committing?

ALEX Closes: the methodological argument for subtype stratification in modifier-locus discovery — when the locus exists, stratification reveals it; when stratification is skipped, the locus is invisible. That argument is now demonstrated at biobank scale on a non-CF disease and is no longer speculative. Closes: the observation that opposite-direction subtype-specific effects can survive replication in a held-out cohort, so the pattern is not a discovery-cohort artifact. Leaves open: whether the CFTR-genotype-class subgroups in CFGP are large enough to power within-class modifier scans even at moderate effect sizes — F508del-homozygotes are the bulk, but compound-heterozygote subclasses may number only a few hundred each. Until those are checked, this is an analytic direction the Kar paper licenses but does not pre-validate for our cohort.

JORDAN What is the one-line takeaway?

ALEX When a modifier locus has different effects in different molecular subtypes of a disease, you can only see it if you stratify the analysis by subtype before testing — and CFTR-genotype class is the natural CF-specific subtype on which to do that stratification.
