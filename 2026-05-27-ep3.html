Blurb

Collaco and colleagues at Johns Hopkins, with Garry Cutting as senior author, test whether ambient outdoor temperature affects CF outcomes. They discover in the U.S. CF Twin and Sibling Study (CFTSS, n=1,378) that warmer annual temperatures correlate with both higher P. aeruginosa colonization (OR 1.06 per °F) and worse lung function (FEV1 -0.34 percentage points per °F). They then independently replicate the P. aeruginosa finding in three more cohorts — CFF Patient Registry (n=16,439), Australian CF Data Registry (n=1,801), and ACFBAL (n=167) — and replicate the lung-function finding in CFF and (with marginal significance) ACFDR. The two effects of temperature are partially independent: conditioning on P. aeruginosa does not erase the lung-function effect, and vice versa. The proposed direction: treat ZIP-linked ambient temperature as a first-class covariate in any future CFTR PheWAS, with a low-cost pre-step of replicating the 2011 finding in the post-modulator CFF Patient Registry.

Plain-English glossary

  • ACFBAL: an Australian/New Zealand prospective CF cohort followed with bronchoalveolar lavage; n=167 in this paper.
  • ACFDR (Australian CF Data Registry): the Australian national CF registry; n=1,801 in this paper.
  • Ambient temperature: outdoor air temperature, here linked to subject ZIP/postcode and averaged annually from NCDC (U.S.) or Bureau of Meteorology (Australia) data.
  • Autosomal recessive: a disease that requires damaging variants on both copies of a gene to develop.
  • Bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL): a procedure that washes saline into a lobe of the lung and recovers the fluid to sample what's there — used in ACFBAL to genotype P. aeruginosa.
  • CFF (Cystic Fibrosis Foundation Patient Registry): the U.S. national CF registry; n=16,439 in this paper.
  • CFTR: the cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator gene; the chloride channel whose loss-of-function causes CF.
  • CFTSS (CF Twin and Sibling Study): a U.S. CF cohort enriched for twins and CF-sibling pairs; n=1,378 here. Used to separate genetic and environmental effects.
  • F508del homozygote: a patient with two copies of the F508del CFTR variant — the most common CF genotype.
  • FEV1 percent predicted: forced expiratory volume in one second, expressed as a percentage of the value predicted for someone of the patient's age, sex, and height. The standard CF lung-function metric.
  • Multivariate regression: regression with multiple predictors simultaneously, so each predictor's effect is adjusted for the others.
  • OR (odds ratio): ratio of odds of an outcome between two groups; here often expressed per degree Fahrenheit.
  • P. aeruginosa: Pseudomonas aeruginosa, a Gram-negative environmental bacterium that chronically infects CF airways and drives lung decline.
  • Public/private insurance: used in this paper as a socioeconomic-status proxy.
  • ZIP code / postcode-linked exposure: the technique of attaching environmental measurements to subjects based on their residence area code.

Methods to lift

  • ZIP/postcode-to-climate linkage — the basic technique of attaching a publicly-available climate variable to each registry subject via residence.
  • Family- and center-clustered regression — adjust standard errors for within-family (CFTSS) and within-center (CFF) non-independence.
  • Multi-cohort, multi-continent independent replication — discovery in one cohort, then independent re-test in three more before claiming the effect is real.
  • Mediation check via conditional regression — test whether one putative cause-effect (here, temperature → P. aeruginosa → FEV1) actually explains away the other (temperature → FEV1).
  • Public-versus-private insurance as SES proxy — limited but available across both U.S. cohorts.

ALEX Welcome back. Our third paper today is "Effect of temperature on cystic fibrosis lung disease and infections: a replicated cohort study," by Joseph M. Collaco and colleagues, with senior author Garry Cutting at Johns Hopkins. It was published in PLoS ONE in 2011. This is a CFF-cohort paper — the kind we're gradually working through in the second half of every reading day.

JORDAN It's older than most of the methods papers we've covered. Why this one, why now?

ALEX Because it's a foundational environmental-modifier paper for CF. The science is straightforward, but the *design* is exemplary — they take a finding from one cohort and replicate it independently in three more, on two continents. And the substantive finding — that ambient temperature affects both Pseudomonas aeruginosa acquisition and lung function in CF — feeds directly into how a future PheWAS would have to treat geography and climate as covariates rather than ignore them.

JORDAN OK, let's prime me. What do I need to know to follow along?

ALEX A lot of terms. CF — cystic fibrosis — is an autosomal recessive disease caused by mutations in the CFTR gene; autosomal recessive means you need a damaging variant on both copies of the gene to develop disease. CFTR is the cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator, a chloride channel in epithelial cells; broken CFTR means thick mucus in airways, pancreas, and elsewhere. Pseudomonas aeruginosa, or P. aeruginosa, is a Gram-negative environmental bacterium that colonizes CF airways and is the dominant chronic infection driving lung decline in older CF patients. FEV1 — forced expiratory volume in one second — is the standard lung-function metric; it's the volume a person can blow out in the first second of a forced exhalation, reported as a percent of predicted for someone of their age, sex, and height. Lung function in CF is reported on FEV1 percent predicted. The cohorts here: CFTSS is the U.S. CF Twin and Sibling Study — 1,378 subjects, all CF patients with a CF sibling, used because twins and siblings let you separate genetic from environmental effects. CFF is the Cystic Fibrosis Foundation Patient Registry, the U.S. national registry — 16,439 subjects in this paper. ACFDR is the Australian CF Data Registry — 1,801 subjects. ACFBAL is a prospective Australian/New Zealand cohort of 167 children studied with bronchoalveolar lavage — that's a procedure where saline is washed into and out of the lungs to sample what's there. OR is odds ratio; an OR of 1.06 per degree Fahrenheit means a 6% increase in odds for every degree warmer the patient's ambient annual temperature.

JORDAN That's the vocabulary. What's the question?

ALEX Two questions, paired. First: does ambient outdoor temperature affect whether a CF patient becomes infected with P. aeruginosa? Second: does ambient temperature affect lung function — FEV1 percent predicted? The motivation is that even patients with the *same* CFTR genotype show wildly different disease courses. Some F508del homozygotes — meaning two copies of the most common CF-causing variant — die in their twenties; others live to their fifties. About half of that variability in lung function is heritable, which leaves the other half to environment and chance. Secondhand smoke and socioeconomic status had already been shown to matter. The question Collaco and colleagues open: does something as basic as the temperature outside your window contribute to that environmental half?

JORDAN Was this contested before?

ALEX Less contested than unknown. There were some hints — registry-level work had noticed that southern U.S. CF centers reported earlier P. aeruginosa acquisition than northern ones — but it wasn't clear whether that was real, or driven by differences in screening practices, healthcare-access patterns, or socioeconomic confounding. So this paper sets up a clean replication design specifically to settle that.

JORDAN How did they do it?

ALEX They used multivariate regression — that's regression with multiple predictors, here including temperature, socioeconomic indicators, smoke exposure, and clinical variables — to test associations in the CFTSS discovery cohort. They tied each subject to an ambient annual temperature based on residence ZIP code or postcode, mapping to U.S. National Climatic Data Center or Australian Bureau of Meteorology temperature averages. Then they took the significant findings from CFTSS and tested them, *independently*, in three replication cohorts: CFF, ACFDR, and ACFBAL. In CFTSS they could cluster regression residuals by family, accounting for the non-independence of twins and siblings. In CFF they clustered by care center — a coarser but reasonable choice. ANOVA, chi-square, and t-tests were used as appropriate. They controlled for socioeconomic status using insurance type — public versus private — as a proxy, and for smoking exposure where available.

JORDAN Push back on a design choice.

ALEX The biggest one: ZIP-code-level temperature is a coarse exposure proxy. A patient living in a hot city might spend 90% of their life in air-conditioned indoor air at 70 degrees. So the temperature variable is really "outdoor regional climate around your address," not "the air your respiratory epithelium experiences." That's not fatal — the authors point out that any noise in the exposure measurement would *weaken* the observed association, so any signal they see is a lower bound. But it means the mechanism is necessarily ecological: temperature affects something *outside* the patient — likely the environmental P. aeruginosa reservoir — and the patient encounters it on the way out.

JORDAN Did they answer the question?

ALEX Yes, with strong replication on the P. aeruginosa side and meaningful replication on lung function. In the CFTSS discovery cohort, the presence of P. aeruginosa was associated with warmer annual temperature with an OR of 1.06 per degree Fahrenheit, p less than 0.001. This replicated independently in the CFF — OR 1.02 per degree, p less than 0.001 — in the ACFDR — OR 1.05 per degree, p equals 0.002 — and in ACFBAL — OR 1.09 per degree, p equals 0.003. Four cohorts, two continents, the same direction. Roughly: a CF patient in the warmest U.S. regions has on the order of double the odds of P. aeruginosa colonization compared to one in the coldest, even after adjusting for socioeconomic status.

JORDAN And lung function?

ALEX Warmer temperatures correlated with lower lung function in CFTSS — a 0.34 percentage-point drop in FEV1 percent predicted per degree Fahrenheit, p equals 0.005. Public insurance — their socioeconomic proxy — also pulled lung function down, 6.43 points lower than private insurance, p less than 0.001. Both findings replicated in the CFF subjects — temperature minus 0.31, p less than 0.001; insurance minus 9.11, p less than 0.001 — and in ACFDR the temperature effect was similar in magnitude — minus 0.23 — and approached but did not reach statistical significance at p equals 0.057, likely a power issue.

JORDAN Are the two findings — temperature on P. aeruginosa and temperature on lung function — confounded? If warm temperature causes P. aeruginosa and P. aeruginosa causes lung decline, the lung-function effect could be entirely mediated.

ALEX Excellent question, and they address it head-on. They show that the association between temperature and lung function is *minimally* influenced by P. aeruginosa status — meaning when you condition on whether the patient has P. aeruginosa or not, the temperature effect on FEV1 persists. And the reverse is also true: the temperature effect on P. aeruginosa is largely independent of lung function. So these are two partially separate environmental effects of temperature on CF, not one effect with a single causal chain.

JORDAN What's the mechanism guess?

ALEX For P. aeruginosa, the favored explanation is environmental ecology — warmer climates support more P. aeruginosa in soil and water, raising acquisition risk. They cite work showing that temperature affects P. aeruginosa biofilm and adhesion properties as well. For lung function, the mechanism is less clear; they speculate about ozone, allergens, viral co-infections, and indoor exposure patterns that scale with outdoor temperature, but the paper doesn't claim to settle this.

JORDAN Proposed direction for Runyu?

ALEX One specific direction. Treat ambient-temperature-at-residence as a *first-class covariate* in any CFTR-PheWAS analysis using the CFF cohort. Concretely: take each CFF patient with WGS, link their ZIP-of-residence to a temperature exposure variable using public NCDC or PRISM climate data, and rerun any modifier-locus association on lung function or P. aeruginosa with and without that covariate. If a modifier locus shifts in effect size when temperature is included, that signals a likely gene-by-environment interaction worth pursuing. The cheap version of the test — before any modifier-locus work — is to simply replicate the Collaco temperature effects on more recent CFF Patient Registry data fifteen years on, with modulator-era patients included, and see whether the effect size has changed in the CFTR-modulator-treatment era. That's a low-cost analysis with no genetic data needed, and the result would be informative either way. Formally: "In the CFF cohort with WGS and registry-linked residence data, evaluate whether known and novel CFTR-modifier variant associations with lung function and P. aeruginosa acquisition are confounded or modified by ambient-temperature exposure, and identify variants showing temperature-stratified effect heterogeneity. As a pre-step, replicate the 2011 Collaco temperature-and-FEV1 association in the post-modulator CFF Patient Registry to verify the environmental effect is still measurable in the current cohort."

JORDAN Gap-map: what does this paper close, and what does it leave open?

ALEX Closes: the question of whether ambient temperature has a non-trivial effect on CF outcomes. Four cohorts replicated it. It also closes the question of whether temperature on lung function is fully mediated by P. aeruginosa — it isn't. Leaves open: the mechanism for the lung-function effect specifically; the question of how temperature interacts with CFTR genotype — they didn't stratify by genotype because their cohorts weren't fully genotyped at that scale; whether modifier-locus effect sizes shift when temperature is included as a covariate — this is the immediately interesting gene-by-environment question for a future PheWAS. Also leaves open whether indoor air conditioning use, an obvious effect modifier, changes the picture — and ZIP-code temperature plus a binary AC-availability proxy from registry data could be the practical next step for replication. What we'd need to check before committing: confirm that the CFF Patient Registry contains residence-level data with enough granularity to link to climate exposure, and confirm that the WGS cohort's residence data hasn't been stripped during de-identification; verify whether the Therapy department, which owns the WGS, can release residence-linked subset data; check whether the CF registry post-2011 still tracks residential geography at the same granularity, since registry schemas evolve.
