Blurb

The first All of Us Research Program genomic-data release describes 245,388 clinical-grade whole-genome sequences delivered through a cloud-based Researcher Workbench under a "data passport" access model — median 28.6 hours from researcher sign-up to genomic data. AoU is, by design, the most ancestrally diverse large-scale WGS cohort to date: 77% of participants are from communities historically under-represented in biomedical research, 46% self-identify as a racial or ethnic minority, and 51.1% of WGS samples are non-European by computed genetic ancestry — versus roughly 95% European in the UK Biobank. The release identified more than 1 billion variants, including ~275 million previously unreported, and replicated published variant–phenotype associations at rates comparable to single-site EHR-linked biobanks across multiple ancestry groups. For Runyu's CFTR PheWAS proposal this paper functions as the obvious external replication cohort for any CFF-derived discovery, and as a methods source for ancestry classification, joint-call QC, and cross-biobank replication benchmarking.

Plain-English glossary

  • All of Us (AoU) — A large US-based research cohort run by the National Institutes of Health, aiming to enroll at least one million people, sequence their DNA, and link it to their electronic health records. Designed from the start to recruit a diverse population reflecting the actual US.
  • Ancestry classification — Assigning each person a label like "European" or "African ancestry" based on patterns in their DNA, not on self-reported race. Done by training an algorithm on a reference set of people whose ancestries are known.
  • Cohort harmonization — Making two studies use the same definitions for the same things — same disease codes, same variant naming, same ancestry labels — so results can be compared without translation.
  • DRAGEN — Illumina's software pipeline that converts raw sequencing reads into per-sample variant calls.
  • Electronic health record (EHR) — The digital version of a patient's medical chart: diagnoses, lab results, prescriptions, encounters. AoU links every participant's genome to their EHR.
  • Genome in a Bottle (GIAB) — A consortium that produces reference DNA samples with painstakingly verified "true" variant lists. Used to benchmark whether a variant calling pipeline is finding the right variants and not making things up. Krusche 2019 is the standard truth-set reference.
  • Genomic Variant Store (GVS) — AoU's cloud-native variant database. Variants live in a queryable schema; VCF files are rendered on demand instead of being the primary storage format. Built because conventional joint calling stops scaling around 50,000 individuals.
  • gnomAD — The Genome Aggregation Database. The largest publicly available reference of human variant frequencies. AoU adopted gnomAD's QC pipeline and ancestry labels so that downstream users can cross-reference allele frequencies without remapping.
  • HGDP + 1000 Genomes — Two reference panels of human DNA from globally diverse populations, combined into the 3,202-sample reference AoU used to train its ancestry classifier.
  • Joint calling — Running variant calling across many people simultaneously so that a variant seen in one person but missed in another gets a consistent decision everywhere.
  • Nirvana — Illumina's variant-annotation tool. Attaches biological context (gene name, protein change, transcript) to each variant.
  • PGRM (Phenotype Genotype Reference Map) — Bastarache 2023's curated benchmark of ~5,000 published variant–phenotype associations mapped to standardized billing-code-derived phecodes. A reusable yardstick for asking how well a new biobank reproduces known genetic findings, broken out by ancestry.
  • Phecode — A standardized phenotype code derived from clinical billing codes (ICD-9/ICD-10). Lets researchers define "this patient has condition X" consistently across health systems.
  • Principal component analysis (PCA) — A math tool that compresses thousands of genetic markers down to a handful of summary numbers. The first few principal components separate continental ancestries naturally and are the standard input to ancestry classifiers.
  • Replication cohort — A second, independent group of people used to confirm a finding from a first group. Replication separates real signals from flukes.
  • Researcher Workbench — AoU's secure cloud workspace. Researchers log in, run analyses there, and never download the raw data. Approval is via a "data passport" — vetted once, then self-serve workspaces — instead of per-project applications.
  • Summary statistics — Aggregated, person-stripped output of a genetic analysis (e.g., variant frequencies and effect sizes per group). Shareable without re-identifying anyone.
  • UBR (under-represented in biomedical research) — AoU's umbrella term for groups including racial and ethnic minorities, people with disabilities, sexual and gender minorities, older adults, and people from rural or lower-income backgrounds. 77% of AoU genomic participants meet at least one UBR criterion.
  • UK Biobank (UKBB) — The pioneering large-scale UK cohort (~500K participants); ~95% European-ancestry. The contrast point that motivated AoU's diversity goals.
  • Variant calling pipeline — The software chain that turns raw sequencing reads into a clean list of where each person's DNA differs from the reference genome.
  • VCF (Variant Call Format) — The standard file format for variant data: rows are genomic positions where someone differs from reference, columns are people.

Methods to lift

  • gnomAD-aligned ancestry classifier — classifier trained on a 16-PC PCA space derived from a 3,202-sample HGDP + 1000 Genomes reference panel and 151,159 autosomal SNPs; project new samples into the trained PC space and assign categorical ancestry labels (AFR/AMR/EAS/EUR/SAS/MID). Identical labels to gnomAD enables direct cross-tabulation with public allele-frequency references.
  • Joint-call QC framework from gnomAD v3.1 — flag samples on eight outlier metrics; standard pipeline of record for biobank-scale joint calling.
  • Validation against Genome in a Bottle reference samples using Krusche 2019 truth sets — SNV sensitivity >98.7%, precision >99.9%; indel sensitivity >97%, precision >99.6%.
  • PCA-on-unrelated-then-project pattern — compute PCA on the maximal independent set, project relatives into the same space rather than letting kinship distort the principal components.
  • Maximal independent set construction for unrelated-individual analyses (231,442 of 245,388 retained at 2nd-degree-or-closer cutoff). For CFF this contrast matters — CFF is family-enriched, so the lift is the framing (be explicit about retaining relatives via mixed models) rather than the algorithm.
  • PGRM (Bastarache 2023) as a cross-biobank replication benchmark — published variant–phenotype associations mapped to phecodes, evaluated as ancestry-stratified powered replication rates. A reusable defense against "is your biobank's phenotype data trustworthy."
  • Genomic Variant Store (GVS) — cloud-native variant storage where the VCF is rendered on demand from a queryable schema. Overkill for the CFF cohort but worth citing as the biobank-scale joint-calling state of the art.
  • Illumina DRAGEN + Nirvana for variant calling and functional consequence annotation on canonical ENSEMBL transcripts.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Two-stage discover-in-CFF / replicate-in-AoU design. The CFF Patient Registry has CF-specific longitudinal phenotyping that no general biobank can match — FEV1 trajectories, exacerbation counts, CFRD onset, sweat chloride, microbiology. AoU has scale and ancestral diversity that the CFF cohort cannot match — ~245K genomes, 51.1% non-European, ~11,000+ CFTR-carrier-level samples reportedly available through the Researcher Workbench. The proposed framing: use CFF as the discovery cohort for modifier signals leveraging registry-derived longitudinal phenotypes; use AoU as the external replication panel for index variants via EHR-derived CF and CF-adjacent phecodes. This converts the saturation and power risks of a small CF-specific cohort into a coherent design strength rather than two separate weaknesses.

What to verify first

  • Get a Researcher Workbench account. This is a separate access process from CFF data access — institutional data-use agreement plus self-service training and compliance attestation. Median time from sign-up to data is 28.6 hours, but the institutional agreement step happens once at the institution level and may already be in place at Runyu's host institution.
  • Check CFTR-carrier sample size in AoU at the target phenotype intersection. "~11,000 carriers" is the headline number, but the relevant denominator is carriers intersected with the specific outcomes we'd want to replicate (CF-related diabetes, lung function decline, etc.). Run the explicit power calculation before committing.
  • Confirm AoU's phenotype catalog covers our target outcomes in a form harmonizable to CFF Patient Registry phenotype definitions. Some CF-specific phenotypes (sweat chloride, sputum microbiology) will simply not be in EHR-derived AoU data; the replication strategy has to be built around what AoU actually has, not what we wish it had.
  • Audit AoU's CFTR variant call quality at clinically relevant positions — phasing of compound heterozygotes, calling of common pathogenic variants like F508del, indel sensitivity in the CFTR gene region — before treating AoU CFTR genotypes as ground truth for replication.

Gap-map note

AoU at 245,388 WGS is roughly 50× the size of the CFF WGS cohort and 51% non-European versus the CFF cohort's predominantly European composition. This argues for the two-stage design above rather than for trying to compete with AoU on scale. Tomorrow's paper (Zhou 2023, CF lung modifier WGS on the CFF cohort) is the closest published analog to what Runyu would propose and the right next read to scope the discovery side of that two-stage plan.
