Blurb (one paragraph)

Ge, Chen, Ni, Feng and Smoller 2019 in Nature Communications introduces PRS-CS — Polygenic Risk Score with Continuous Shrinkage prior — the now-default sumstats-based polygenic prediction method in modern biobank pipelines. The method places a Strawderman-Berger continuous shrinkage prior on per-SNP effect sizes within a Bayesian regression, with a global-scale parameter phi controlling overall sparsity and per-marker psi-j adapting locally. A Gibbs sampler with multivariate block updates of effect sizes within LD blocks gives both better LD adjustment and substantial computational speedup over LDpred. Simulations using UK Biobank data show PRS-CS dominates P-plus-T across all architectures and beats LDpred when training sample size is large. Real-data Partners HealthCare Biobank application across six diseases (BRCA, CAD, DEP, IBD, RA, T2DM) and six quantitative traits (height, BMI, HDL, LDL, cholesterol, triglycerides) shows 41.85% improvement over LDpred for breast cancer, 28.62% for rheumatoid arthritis, 18.17% average improvement on quantitative traits, and 48.16% average over P-plus-T. Proposed direction (conditional on CFGP WGS variant calls and CFFPR phenotype linkage): PRS-CS-derived polygenic background scores for each CFFPR-linked CFGP patient, built from public UK Biobank GWAS sumstats, used as covariates in CF-modifier-gene burden tests against the matched CFFPR phenotype panel. Pitfall to pre-register against: over-absorption — if a CF modifier gene itself contributes to the PRS being controlled for, the PRS covariate partially residualizes out the very signal you wanted to detect.

Plain-English glossary

  • PRS — Polygenic Risk Score: one number per person summarizing their genome-wide load of risk alleles.
  • PRS-CS — the method introduced in this paper. PRS via Continuous Shrinkage.
  • PRS-CS-auto — fully Bayesian version that learns the global scaling parameter from data without a validation set.
  • GWAS — Genome-Wide Association Study; scans millions of SNPs one at a time against a trait.
  • Summary statistics / sumstats — per-SNP effect sizes and p-values from a GWAS, shareable publicly without individual-level data.
  • SNP — single-nucleotide polymorphism; one-letter DNA variant.
  • LD — linkage disequilibrium; nearby variants inherited together more than chance.
  • LD reference panel — separately sequenced cohort (typically 1000 Genomes) used to estimate which SNPs correlate.
  • HapMap3 — ~1.4M curated common-SNP panel; standard restriction for PRS methods.
  • Bayesian regression — statistical framework using a prior probability distribution combined with data to give a posterior.
  • Prior / posterior — beliefs before / after observing the data.
  • Shrinkage — pulling effect-size estimates toward zero, especially for noisy ones.
  • Discrete mixture prior — "either causal with moderate-to-large effect, or exactly zero" — two boxes; used by LDpred.
  • Continuous shrinkage prior — one continuous distribution with mass near zero and heavy tails — PRS-CS.
  • Strawderman-Berger prior — the specific named prior PRS-CS uses for the local scaling layer.
  • Scale mixture of normals — a normal distribution whose variance is itself a random variable, giving heavy tails.
  • Gibbs sampler — standard MCMC algorithm for Bayesian models with conditionally conjugate priors.
  • LDpred — discrete-mixture-prior competitor PRS method (Vilhjálmsson 2015).
  • P-plus-T — LD-pruning then p-value-thresholding, the simplest PRS method.
  • Nagelkerke's R² — R-squared analog for logistic regression, the standard accuracy metric for binary-trait PRS.
  • 1000 Genomes Project (1KG) — public reference cohort used as LD reference panel.

ALEX Today's second paper is Ge, Chen, Ni, Feng and Smoller, 2019, in *Nature Communications*: "Polygenic prediction via Bayesian regression and continuous shrinkage priors." Tian Ge is first author at the Psychiatric and Neurodevelopmental Genetics Unit at Mass General Hospital and the Broad. The senior author is Jordan Smoller, who runs that unit. The paper introduces a method called PRS-CS — Polygenic Risk Score with Continuous Shrinkage prior — which is now, six years later, the default polygenic-score-from-summary-statistics method in most modern biobank pipelines, including the UK Biobank All-of-Us shared workflows. We're reading it because tomorrow's natural extension of the Bessonova registry work is to build modifier-gene scores on top of polygenic backgrounds, and you can't build the modifier score sensibly without first understanding how PRS-CS de-noises a million-marker score.

JORDAN Stop. Every term in that cold open spelled out as if I have never heard it. Bayesian regression, continuous shrinkage, polygenic risk score, summary statistics — all of them.

ALEX A polygenic risk score, abbreviated PRS, is a single number you compute for one person that summarizes their genome-wide load of risk alleles for a trait. The recipe: start with a large external genome-wide association study — GWAS — that scanned millions of single-letter DNA variants, called SNPs, single-nucleotide polymorphisms. Each SNP gets an estimated effect size — how much it shifts disease risk per copy. Then for any new person, you read their genotype, multiply each variant's copy count by its estimated effect, sum across the whole genome, and you get one number. That number is the PRS. Summary statistics — abbreviated sumstats — means the per-SNP effect sizes and p-values from the GWAS, not the individual-level genotype data. Most GWAS sumstats are shared publicly; individual-level data usually is not. So a method that works from sumstats alone is what most groups can actually run. LD is linkage disequilibrium: when two nearby variants on the same chromosome are inherited together more often than chance, they are in LD. If two SNPs are in tight LD and both show a GWAS signal, you don't know if both are causal or if one is causal and the other just rides along. LD reference panel means a separately sequenced group of people — usually the 1000 Genomes Project, abbreviated 1KG — used to estimate which SNPs are correlated with which. Bayesian regression: a statistical framework where, instead of estimating effect sizes by maximum-likelihood, you put a prior probability distribution on the unknown effect sizes — your beliefs before seeing the data — and combine it with the data to get a posterior — beliefs after seeing the data. The posterior mean effect sizes are what you use as your final estimates. Shrinkage means the prior pulls effect-size estimates toward zero, especially for noisy ones — it prevents over-fitting. Discrete mixture prior versus continuous shrinkage prior: a discrete mixture prior says "this SNP is either causal with some moderate-to-large effect, or it is non-causal with exactly zero effect" — two boxes. A continuous shrinkage prior says "every SNP's effect comes from one continuous bell-shaped distribution with most mass near zero and heavy tails" — no hard cutoff. The phrase scale mixtures of normals means the prior on each effect size is a normal distribution whose variance is itself a random variable, drawn from another distribution — that two-layer setup gives the heavy tails. Strawderman-Berger prior is a specific named choice for that second layer, used in PRS-CS. HapMap3 is a 1.4-million-SNP panel of common variants curated by the international HapMap project — most PRS methods restrict to this set because the effect-size estimates for rarer variants are too noisy. LDpred is the prior best-known summary-statistics PRS method, from 2015, which uses a discrete mixture prior — PRS-CS's main competitor. P-plus-T means LD-pruning then p-value-thresholding — the simplest PRS method.

JORDAN Now the question.

ALEX The question is technical but consequential. By 2019 the field had agreed that PRS would matter clinically — for risk stratification, early detection, complex-disease prevention. The bottleneck was prediction accuracy. The pre-existing methods — P-plus-T and LDpred — each had problems. P-plus-T discarded most of the signal by pruning. LDpred's discrete two-box prior — causal-or-not — fit some genetic architectures fine but failed for traits with mixed effect-size distributions and was computationally expensive. The motivation: can you design a prior that adapts automatically to the genetic architecture of the trait — sparse for some traits, polygenic for others — while also enabling fast multivariate updates that correctly handle LD between markers? The two technical wins PRS-CS promises are statistical and computational. Statistical: a continuous shrinkage prior with mass near zero plus heavy tails adapts automatically — strong shrinkage for noise, weak shrinkage for real signal — without you having to specify the fraction of causal SNPs up front. Computational: continuous priors allow conjugate block updates of effect sizes for SNPs in the same LD block, instead of updating each SNP separately. That is a massive speedup and a much better LD adjustment.

ALEX Methods. Equation one is the standard linear regression: a vector of phenotypes Y equals genotype matrix X times effect-size vector beta plus noise epsilon. PRS-CS places a prior on each beta — a normal distribution with mean zero and variance equal to a global scaling parameter phi times a marker-specific local scaling parameter psi-j. The global phi sets overall sparsity; the local psi-j adapts per SNP. The Strawderman-Berger prior is the specific choice for the distribution of psi-j. Two algorithm versions: PRS-CS, where phi is selected from a small grid of fixed values in a validation set, and PRS-CS-auto, fully Bayesian, where phi is automatically learned from the data using a half-Cauchy hyperprior — no validation set needed. They use an efficient Gibbs sampler, which is the standard Markov-chain Monte-Carlo algorithm for these models. LD reference: 1000 Genomes Project European sample, N equals 503. They restrict to HapMap3 SNPs. Simulations are run on UK Biobank genotype data: heritability fixed at 0.5, four genetic architectures of 100, 1000, 10,000, and 100,000 causal SNPs, four training sample sizes of 10,000 through 100,000, and a normal-mixture model as a fifth architecture. Validation N equals 3000, testing N equals 3000. Real-data application: Partners HealthCare Biobank, six diseases — breast cancer, coronary artery disease, depression, inflammatory bowel disease, rheumatoid arthritis, type 2 diabetes mellitus — and six quantitative traits — height, BMI, HDL, LDL, total cholesterol, triglycerides. They use Nagelkerke's R-squared for the binary traits and ordinary R-squared for the quantitative traits.

JORDAN One pushback on-air. The validation-and-testing split is two thousand people for validation, two thousand for testing per random split, with one hundred random splits averaged. That is fine for benchmarking, but for some of the real-data traits the Partners Biobank case counts are small — 884 breast-cancer cases, 753 rheumatoid arthritis cases, 750 IBD cases. The Nagelkerke R-squared numbers reported are averages over those one hundred splits, but the variance across splits is large enough that the relative-improvement claims for BRCA and RA — 41.85 percent and 28.62 percent over LDpred — could be sensitive to which random splits you happened to land on. PRS-CS being the better method is probably right; the specific magnitude of improvement on those two phenotypes is less certain than the headline reads.

ALEX That's fair. The simulation studies are the more controlled evidence. Onto results. In simulations, PRS-CS and PRS-CS-auto outperformed P-plus-T across all four genetic architectures. They beat LDpred when the training sample was large — at one hundred thousand training samples, LDpred actually degraded for the sparse hundred-causal-SNP architecture because its LD adjustment broke down, while PRS-CS stayed stable. In the real Partners Biobank data, the six binary diseases: PRS-CS beat LDpred by 41.85 percent on Nagelkerke's R-squared for breast cancer and 28.62 percent for rheumatoid arthritis, roughly tied on CAD, depression, and T2DM, and lost to LDpred by 10.24 percent on inflammatory bowel disease — the disease with the smallest GWAS training sample, consistent with the simulation result that LDpred outperforms PRS-CS at small training sizes. The six quantitative traits: PRS-CS beat LDpred by 8 to 32 percent — 27.75 percent for height, 32.05 percent for cholesterol — averaging 18.17 percent improvement. Averaged over all six quantitative traits, PRS-CS beat P-plus-T by 48.16 percent. PRS-CS-auto was slightly worse than PRS-CS for the diseases — most disease GWAS sample sizes weren't yet large enough to learn the global phi automatically — but it approached PRS-CS for the quantitative traits where sample sizes were larger. Did they answer the question? Yes. Continuous shrinkage priors with multivariate block updates beat discrete mixture priors and pruning-plus-thresholding for polygenic prediction, especially as sample sizes grow.

JORDAN One specific concrete research direction this paper suggests for Runyu?

ALEX Plain English first. Runyu's CFTR PheWAS is going to need to disentangle modifier-gene signals from population-wide polygenic background signals. If a CF patient with severe lung disease also has, by chance, a high polygenic risk score for asthma or COPD, then the modifier-gene effect you attribute to their SLC26A9 genotype might really be confounded by their asthma/COPD PRS. The way to clean that up is to compute a PRS-CS-derived polygenic background score for each CFFPR patient on each phenotype the PheWAS sweeps, then include the PRS as a covariate in the modifier-gene-by-phenotype regression.
