Blurb

Blackman, Hsu, Vanscoy, Collaco, Ritter, Naughton, and Cutting (J Clin Endocrinol Metab, 2009) used the CF Twin and Sibling Study to ask whether cystic-fibrosis-related diabetes (CFRD) has substantial heritability beyond CFTR itself — the prerequisite question for any CFRD genetic-modifier discovery program. In 1,366 CF patients from 109 centers, comprising 68 monozygous twin pairs, 23 dizygous twin pairs, and 588 sibling pairs, CFRD prevalence was 9%; mean age 15.8. After adjusting for known risk factors (age, pancreatic exocrine insufficiency, severe CFTR mutations, FEV1, BMI, glucocorticoid exposure), monozygous concordance for CFRD was 0.73 versus 0.18 for dizygous and non-twin siblings (P=0.002). Heritability was estimated at near 1.0 with a wide 95% CI of 0.42 to 1.0 — a definitive demonstration that modifier genes are the primary cause of CFRD beyond CFTR. This paper licensed the entire CFRD-modifier discovery program that followed (Blackman 2013 SLC26A9, Aksit 2020, etc.). Proposed direction for the CFF WGS cohort: a CFRD-anchored rare-variant burden phenome-wide scan in the CFF WGS cohort, targeting established CFRD modifier loci and a genome-wide gene-burden survey, with CFFPR-coded CFRD ascertainment. Open questions before committing: CFRD case count in the WGS subset, overlap with the Twin-Sibling cohort, and CFFPR diagnostic-code parity with Blackman's chronic-insulin-requiring definition.

Plain-English glossary

  • BMI z-score: body-mass index expressed as standard deviations from age-and-sex-matched population norms; the standard pediatric weight metric.
  • CFFPR (CF Foundation Patient Registry): the longitudinal clinical database that captures most US CF patient encounters; the phenotype source for any CFF-cohort modifier study.
  • CFRD (cystic-fibrosis-related diabetes): diabetes that develops in CF patients, distinct from type 1 and type 2; caused largely by progressive exocrine pancreatic damage spilling into beta-cell loss.
  • CFTR (Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane conductance Regulator): the gene whose mutations cause CF; encodes a chloride channel on the apical surface of secretory epithelia.
  • Concordance rate: in twin studies, the probability that one twin in a pair has a trait given the co-twin has it.
  • Dizygous (DZ) twin: fraternal twin, shares ~50% of genome with co-twin, same as a non-twin sibling.
  • F508del / p.Phe508del: the most common severe CFTR mutation; deletes phenylalanine at position 508 of the CFTR protein; ~70% of CF chromosomes worldwide carry it.
  • Falconer's formula: the classic twin-design equation that converts MZ and DZ concordances into a heritability estimate.
  • FEV1 percent predicted: forced expiratory volume in one second, expressed as a percent of an age/sex/height-predicted normal; the workhorse CF lung-function metric.
  • Genetic modifier: a gene other than the primary disease-causing one (CFTR here) that affects severity or variability of disease phenotype.
  • Heritability (broad-sense): the fraction of phenotypic variance in a population attributable to genetic differences between people.
  • Logistic regression: standard statistical model for binary outcomes (CFRD yes/no); estimates odds ratios and adjusts for covariates.
  • Monozygous (MZ) twin: identical twin, shares 100% of genome with co-twin.
  • NCT00037765: ClinicalTrials.gov identifier for the CF Twin and Sibling Study.
  • Nongenetic risk factor: here, glucocorticoid exposure duration — a modifiable environmental contributor to CFRD risk.
  • Pancreatic exocrine insufficiency (PI): failure of the exocrine pancreas to produce sufficient digestive enzymes; in CF, it's caused by progressive ductal scarring and acinar destruction.
  • TCF7L2 rs7903146: the major type 2 diabetes susceptibility variant; one of the candidate CFRD modifier variants that motivated this and the follow-on Blackman 2009 Diabetes paper.

Methods to lift

  • Twin-and-sibling concordance design — MZ vs. DZ/sibling pair concordance comparison; the cleanest non-genotype heritability estimator when a twin resource is available.
  • CF Twin and Sibling Study cohort — NCT00037765; 109 centers; the standing resource for any CF heritability or sibship-design analysis.
  • Multivariable logistic regression for CFRD risk factors — covariates: age, sex, CFTR genotype severity, pancreatic insufficiency status, FEV1%, BMI, glucocorticoid duration. Defensible covariate set for any CFRD modeling going forward.
  • Falconer heritability with bootstrap CI — explicit twin-design heritability output with confidence interval; standard for any heritability claim.
  • Family-history independent-predictor term — including "CF sibling with CFRD" as a binary covariate in the risk model; preserved as a sanity check for any future CFRD-prediction model.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: a CFRD-anchored rare-variant burden phenome-wide scan in the CFF WGS cohort (~5,000 patients), targeting established CFRD common-variant modifier loci (SLC26A9, TCF7L2, CFTR-2 cluster) for confirmation and a genome-wide gene-burden survey for novel discovery. CFRD ascertainment from CFFPR longitudinal records using a Blackman-compatible chronic insulin-requiring definition. Pre-specified covariate set mirroring this paper's regression: age, sex, CFTR severity class, pancreatic insufficiency, FEV1% slope, BMI z-score, cumulative glucocorticoid exposure. Power-justified threshold for novel-locus reporting (Bonferroni for known-locus confirmation; gene-burden Bonferroni for the genome-wide arm).

What to verify first

  • How many of the ~5,000 CFF WGS participants have CFRD coded in the registry? An effective case count below ~250 means the genome-wide arm is underpowered for novel modest-effect modifiers, though the targeted-locus confirmation arm remains feasible.
  • What is the overlap between the CFF WGS cohort and the original CF Twin and Sibling Study? Related individuals must be modeled through a genetic relatedness matrix (e.g., SAIGE-like LMM); ignoring relatedness inflates type-I error.
  • Does the CFFPR CFRD diagnostic code match Blackman's "chronic, insulin-requiring diabetes" definition, or does it capture a broader phenotype (impaired glucose tolerance, gestational, transient stress hyperglycemia)? A phenotype-definition mismatch will dilute any genetic effect.
  • Has the heritability of CFRD been re-estimated in a more recent twin/sibling resource? The 2009 estimate's wide CI (0.42–1.0) is what we'll inherit unless an updated number exists.
  • Is the cumulative glucocorticoid exposure variable available in CFFPR with sufficient resolution to enter the regression as in Blackman 2009? Without it, the nongenetic baseline isn't comparable across analyses.

Gap-map: closes / leaves open

  • Closes: Whether CFRD has substantial heritability beyond CFTR genotype (yes; lower-bound 42%). Whether familial clustering of CFRD is real after adjusting for shared CFTR mutation and shared environment (yes; family history is independently predictive). Whether the modifier-discovery program for CFRD is justified at all (yes; this paper is the licensing argument).
  • Leaves open: No specific modifier locus or variant identified (intentional — discovery is Blackman 2013's job). Whether CFRD heritability is constant across CFTR mutation classes (pooled estimate only). Whether the heritability estimate would replicate in a contemporary cohort with newer CFTR modulator-era patients (this is a pre-modulator cohort). Whether sub-phenotypes of CFRD (early-onset, post-transplant, ICU stress-hyperglycemia) share or differ in their genetic architecture.

ALEX All fair to slow down on. Let me walk through them. CF — cystic fibrosis — is a multi-system genetic disease caused by mutations in the CFTR gene, which stands for Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane conductance Regulator. CFTR encodes a chloride channel sitting on the apical surface of secretory epithelial cells, the cells lining your airways, your gut, your pancreatic ducts, your sweat glands. Lose chloride transport, you get thick mucus, recurrent lung infection, and progressive multi-organ dysfunction. CFRD — cystic-fibrosis-related diabetes — is the diabetes that develops in many CF patients, distinct from type 1 and type 2 diabetes. It's caused largely by progressive scarring and atrophy of the exocrine pancreas, which damages the insulin-producing beta cells alongside the digestive-enzyme-producing acinar cells. By age twenty roughly fifteen percent of CF patients have CFRD; by age forty, more than half do.

JORDAN And "modifier"? "Heritability"?

ALEX A modifier gene is a gene other than CFTR that affects how severe or how variable a CF phenotype is across patients who share the same CFTR mutation. So two patients can both be homozygous for the most common severe CFTR mutation — F508del, also written p.Phe508del — and yet one has CFRD at age fifteen and the other never develops it. That difference is what modifier biology is about. Heritability is a population-statistics term: the fraction of variation in a trait that's explained by genetic variation between people, as opposed to environmental variation. If heritability of CFRD within the CF population is high, that means modifier genes matter a lot; if heritability is near zero, then once you condition on CFTR genotype and pancreatic status, the rest is environment, age, treatment exposure, and chance.

JORDAN And how do you measure heritability without doing a genetic study?

ALEX The twin and sibling design is the classic move. The intuition is: monozygous twins — identical twins — share one hundred percent of their genome. Dizygous twins — fraternal twins — share fifty percent on average, the same as full siblings. If a trait is highly heritable, monozygous twin pairs should agree on it much more often than dizygous twins or non-twin siblings. The mathematical version of this comparison is called Falconer's formula and a bunch of refinements; the headline output is a heritability estimate, typically between zero and one. So Blackman is using twin and sibling concordance to derive a heritability estimate for CFRD without genotyping a single variant.

JORDAN Okay — so what was the question and why was it open before 2009?

ALEX By the mid-2000s, the field knew CFRD ran in CF families more often than chance — that is, if you had CFRD, your CF sibling was more likely than a random CF patient to also have CFRD. But "more likely" can be confounded by shared environment, shared CFTR mutation, and shared age. Without separating those out, the field couldn't say whether the familial clustering was genetic modifier-driven, environmentally-driven, or just CFTR-genotype-driven. Several smaller candidate-gene studies — including TCF7L2, the major type 2 diabetes risk variant — had shown nominal associations with CFRD, but none of them established the population-level point that there was substantial CFRD-specific heritability beyond CFTR. The contested question was whether it was worth the considerable expense to mount a multi-center CF modifier discovery program for CFRD. Blackman's twin-and-sibling analysis was specifically designed to answer that question definitively.

The cohort breakdown: sixty-eight monozygous twin pairs, twenty-three dizygous twin pairs, and five hundred eighty-eight sibling pairs, all with CF. Mean age fifteen point eight years, which is a young cohort by CFRD standards — most CFRD onset is in late teens to twenties, so this cohort will undercount lifetime CFRD risk but will capture early-onset cases.
