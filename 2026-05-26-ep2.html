One-paragraph blurb

Wang and colleagues (Ionita-Laza lab, Columbia) extend REGENIE — the standard two-stage GWAS framework — from linear regression to quantile regression, calling the result Regenie.QRS. Quantile regression estimates how a specific percentile of a trait's distribution depends on a variant, instead of just the average. In simulations across normal, heavy-tailed, dominance, and quantile-heterogeneous models, Regenie.QRS is the only method that controls type-one error across all significance thresholds while related individuals are included; against REGENIE, it shows 46% higher power under dominance models and 17–20% higher power under quantile-heterogeneous models at stringent thresholds. In UK Biobank applications, it recovers the G6PC2 glucose locus and the SORT1 cholesterol locus with effects strongest in clinically relevant tails. The proposed direction is to apply Regenie.QRS to continuous CF complication phenotypes in the CFF WGS cohort to surface modifier variants whose effects concentrate in disease-severity tails; the open questions are whether n≈5,000 supports informative quantile-tail tests and which CFF phenotypes are amenable to the continuous-trait quantile framework.

Methods to lift

  • REGENIE two-stage framework — Stage 1: ridge regression on genomic blocks builds per-block polygenic predictors. Stage 2: ridge regression with cross-validation combines blocks into a leave-one-chromosome-out polygenic prediction. Used here as the offset step in the quantile-regression pipeline.
  • Quantile rank score (QRS) test — variant-level score test for the τ-th quantile, derived from the asymptotic properties of quantile regression.
  • Regenie.QRS — subtract REGENIE's LOCO polygenic prediction from the trait, then perform QRS on the residual at each variant.
  • BLUP.QRS — same idea but using traditional BLUP rather than REGENIE for the polygenic-offset step.
  • PLINK2 QC pipeline — standard quality control (MAF ≥ 5%, genotyping rate ≥ 90%, HWE p ≥ 1e-4, individuals with > 10% missing genotypes removed).
  • FlashPCA — fast principal-component analysis used for ancestry adjustment (top 10 PCs as covariates).
  • KING — used to identify first-degree relatives for the extreme-relatedness simulation subset.
  • Rank-based inverse normal transformation — applied to traits before Regenie.QRS to make polygenic prediction well-behaved.
  • LD clumping (PLINK2) — r² 0.1, 1 Mb window — used to define independent loci across both methods.

What to verify first

  • Whether n ≈ 5,000 supports informative quantile-tail tests at τ = 0.1 and τ = 0.9 — back-of-envelope ~500 individuals per decile, sample-size adequacy at extreme thresholds is the binding constraint.
  • Which CFF phenotypes are amenable to continuous-trait modeling versus survival or binary endpoints not directly supported by Regenie.QRS (CFRD onset is right-censored; MI is binary; modulator-response is conditioned on treatment).
  • Whether rank-based inverse-normal transformation of CF phenotypes destroys the quantile-specific signal of interest — sensitivity to transformation choice should be tested empirically before committing.
  • Multiple-testing correction strategy across τ levels: per-quantile genome-wide threshold, or a unified composite test, or a hierarchical Bonferroni — the choice affects whether modest tail-specific signals survive correction.
  • Whether the CFF cohort has the multiplex-family / twin-sibling relatedness structure that Regenie.QRS specifically handles (yes, per prior catalog entries), and whether KING-derived kinship estimates are already available in the CFF data dictionary.
  • Whether Regenie.QRS's PNAS code release (as of 2025 publication) provides ready-to-use binaries that scale to a 5,000-sample / 30M+ variant WGS substrate, or whether substantial pipeline integration work is required.
  • Whether ancestry stratification is feasible at quantile resolution — Black/African-ancestry stratum in CFF is small (~90 individuals per Ling 2024 cohort baseline), and a per-stratum quantile-tail analysis would be very underpowered.

Gap-map: closes / leaves open

This is a biobank-methods episode and the standard CFTR-WGS gap-map does not strictly apply, but the methodological transfer story is the relevant gap:

Closes

  • That whole-genome quantile regression is computationally tractable at biobank scale, with proper type-one error control under relatedness — a problem that earlier marginal-QRS approaches could not solve.
  • That dominance-mode and tail-specific genetic architectures, which linear regression can systematically under-detect, can be recovered by a quantile approach.

Leaves open

  • Whether the method's power advantage holds at CF-cohort sample sizes (~5,000) rather than UKBB sizes (~400,000).
  • Whether quantile-specific signals at CF complication tails reflect real tail-biology (e.g., variants that protect against the worst lung disease specifically) or artifacts of phenotype distribution shape.
  • Whether existing CF modifier loci (chr11p13 EHF/APIP, chr20q13.2, SLC26A9, SLC6A14, ADAMTS5) show quantile-heterogeneous effects on CF outcomes — a low-cost first analysis to validate the framework on already-discovered modifiers.

ALEX Welcome to episode two. Today's paper is Wang, Wang, Wang, Masala, Fiorillo, Devoto, Cucca, and Ionita-Laza, published in 2025 in PNAS. The title is "Computationally efficient whole-genome quantile regression at biobank scale." Senior author Iuliana Ionita-Laza at Columbia. It's the second paper in our REGENIE-family thread.

JORDAN REGENIE? Quick refresher.

ALEX Sure. REGENIE is a statistical software tool developed at Regeneron in 2021 for running genome-wide association studies on biobank-scale data. The trick it pulled was a two-stage design — first fit a polygenic predictor of your trait using ridge regression on blocks of the genome, then use that prediction as an offset when testing each individual variant — that handles relatedness between participants and scales to hundreds of thousands of people cheaply. It became standard in UK Biobank GWAS very quickly. This paper extends the same idea from ordinary linear regression to quantile regression.

JORDAN Stop. What's quantile regression?

ALEX Good catch. Linear regression — the standard tool — estimates how the average value of a trait changes when you change a predictor. If a variant raises average blood glucose by 0.1 millimoles per liter, that's a linear regression effect. Quantile regression instead estimates how a specific percentile of the distribution changes. The 10th percentile, the 50th percentile, the 90th percentile, each gets its own effect estimate. The reason this matters is that a variant doesn't have to affect the whole distribution uniformly. It might raise the low tail dramatically while barely touching the high tail. Linear regression smears that asymmetry into a single average, and you miss the asymmetry entirely.

JORDAN An example would help.

ALEX The paper provides one. A variant near a gene called G6PC2 affects fasting glucose levels. Linear regression says the effect size is about 0.088 — a modest average bump. Quantile regression reveals the effect is roughly 0.10 at the 10th percentile of the glucose distribution, but only 0.02 to 0.03 at the 90th percentile. So the variant has a much bigger effect on people with already-low glucose than on people with already-high glucose. The biological interpretation: this variant acts like a guardian against hypoglycemia — it pulls up the low end without driving the high end. That's a mechanism story you can't see with linear regression alone, and it explains why this variant doesn't show up in type 2 diabetes GWAS.

JORDAN Some quick definitions before we go in. Heritability? Linkage disequilibrium?

ALEX Heritability is the fraction of variation in a trait that's attributable to genetic variation. Linkage disequilibrium, abbreviated LD, is the tendency for nearby variants to be inherited together. PLINK2 is a standard genetics command-line tool for quality control and analysis. MAF, minor allele frequency, is the frequency of the rarer of the two alleles at a given variant; common variants have MAF above 5 percent. Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium is a population-genetics expectation about allele and genotype frequencies; serious deviation from it usually means a variant was genotyped poorly. White British, in UK Biobank speak, is a population subgroup defined by self-reported ethnicity plus genetic ancestry clustering — used here for analytical homogeneity, not as a clinical statement. One more: LOCO, leave-one-chromosome-out, is the cross-validation trick GWAS uses to avoid having the variant under test contaminate the polygenic predictor.

ALEX Background and motivation. Standard GWAS has gotten very good at finding the average effects of variants on continuous traits. But for many phenotypes, especially clinical biomarkers, the interesting biology lives in the tails — in the people who get unusually low or unusually high values, not the people in the middle. Linear regression cannot distinguish a variant that shifts the whole distribution uniformly from a variant that mostly affects one tail. Earlier work showed that quantile regression run as a marginal test — meaning, one variant at a time without correction for the polygenic background — gives inflated false positive rates in biobank-scale data because it doesn't handle population structure or relatedness between participants. The motivating problem is: how do you do whole-genome quantile regression in a way that controls type-one error and uses related individuals — which UK Biobank is full of, and which the authors point out are very valuable for power.

ALEX Methods. The authors' tool is called Regenie.QRS — short for "Regenie applied to the Quantile Rank Score test." The pipeline has three steps. First, the genome is split into blocks; within each block, ridge regression is used to fit several polygenic predictors at different shrinkage levels — the same step REGENIE already does. Second, those block-level predictors are combined in a second-stage ridge regression with cross-validation to pick the best shrinkage parameter — also from REGENIE. The output is a leave-one-chromosome-out polygenic prediction of your trait. Third, and this is the new piece: subtract that polygenic prediction from your phenotype, and then run a quantile rank score test on the residual. Because the polygenic component has been projected out, the residuals behave like independent observations from each individual, even when the original individuals were related. They also derive a BLUP-based variant of the same idea — BLUP stands for best linear unbiased predictor, the older mixed-model alternative — for comparison.

JORDAN What's the comparison set?

ALEX They benchmark four methods: marginal quantile rank score, called QRS; REGENIE itself, the standard linear-regression baseline; the new Regenie.QRS; and BLUP.QRS. They run simulations under four data-generating models — a normal-error linear model, a heavy-tailed t-distribution-with-two-degrees-of-freedom error model, a model with dominance effects, and a heterogeneous model where some causal variants only affect upper quantiles.

JORDAN Pushback?

ALEX Worth flagging: the cohort sizes for the empirical applications are wildly asymmetric — UK Biobank with 382,402 individuals after quality control, paired against the SardiNIA cohort with about 6,150 individuals for replication. Replication rates are reported as "generally consistent but low," which the authors attribute to the small replication sample size, but it's a soft replication target. Also, quantile regression by construction requires individual-level data — you can't run it on summary statistics, which means it cannot be plugged into the existing meta-analysis ecosystem the way linear-regression GWAS can. And under saturated-GWAS regimes for highly studied traits, the authors are upfront that quantile regression often identifies fewer loci than linear regression because the latter has higher average power.

ALEX Results. Across all four simulation models and significance thresholds from 10 to the minus 4 down to 10 to the minus 8, Regenie.QRS is the only method that consistently controls type-one error within the 95 percent confidence band for the nominal rate. Marginal QRS shows widespread inflation — for example, 5.48 times ten to the minus six empirical false positive rate at a nominal 10 to the minus 6 threshold under the homogeneous model. BLUP.QRS is better but still inflated, especially in the full dataset with related individuals. REGENIE itself is well-controlled in most settings but can be elevated under the heterogeneous model. Power-wise, under the homogeneous linear model, Regenie.QRS achieves 0.78 power at alpha 0.05 versus 0.79 for REGENIE — essentially identical. Under the heavy-tailed t-distribution error model, Regenie.QRS hits 0.81 versus REGENIE's 0.80, with a meaningful gap above QRS at 0.73. Under the dominance model with 400,000 samples, Regenie.QRS achieves 46 percent higher power than REGENIE at alpha 0.05; at the stringent 10-to-the-minus-6 threshold, Regenie.QRS achieves 28 percent power versus essentially zero for REGENIE. Under the heterogeneous model, Regenie.QRS achieves 17 to 20 percent higher power than REGENIE at stringent thresholds. So when the genetic architecture departs from the linear-additive Gaussian default — which it does for many real traits — the quantile approach gains substantial power.

ALEX In real-data applications, the authors run Regenie.QRS on 28 continuous traits in UK Biobank that are shared with SardiNIA. REGENIE detects more loci overall — consistent with higher average power — but a meaningful subset of loci are detected only by Regenie.QRS, including the G6PC2 glucose example we discussed, and an LDL-cholesterol locus at SORT1 where the protective minor allele at rs12740374 has its strongest effect at the upper quantiles of the total cholesterol distribution. The SORT1 mechanism story is biologically clean: the variant creates a transcription factor binding site that increases SORT1 expression in liver, which lowers plasma LDL-cholesterol, and the effect is strongest in people who would otherwise be hypercholesterolemic — exactly the clinical group statins target. So quantile regression doesn't just find new associations, it characterizes the within-distribution shape of associations that linear regression also finds.

Plain English: a modifier variant that mostly matters at the bottom of the lung-function distribution — the patients with worst lung disease — could be invisible to a standard linear GWAS but visible to quantile regression. Formal one-sentence version: in the CFF WGS cohort, apply Regenie.QRS quantile-regression GWAS to continuous CF complication phenotypes — KNoRMA-derived lung function severity, BMI z-score, age at first chronic Pseudomonas, age at CFRD diagnosis, sweat chloride — testing whether quantile-specific effects at the extreme tails reveal modifier signals that the standard linear-regression scan misses.

JORDAN And beat seven — what's the gap, conditional on that proposal?

ALEX What this paper closes: it gives Runyu a software tool that is computationally tractable on a five-thousand-person cohort — far smaller than UK Biobank — and that correctly handles related individuals, which matters in CFF because of the multiplex-family and twin-sibling sub-cohorts. What it leaves open: first, we'd need to check whether five thousand individuals is enough to populate the upper and lower deciles densely enough that quantile regression at those quantiles is even informative — the authors test down to first-degree-relative subsets of about 47,000 individuals and note that as sample size shrinks, the relative power advantage of Regenie.QRS over QRS narrows. Five thousand may be smaller still. Second, we'd need to check whether CFF cohort phenotypes are best treated as continuous — KNoRMA and BMI z are continuous, but CFRD onset is right-censored survival, MI is binary, sweat chloride is bounded — and which of those Regenie.QRS in its current form actually supports. Third, the trait would need rank-based inverse normal transformation for the polygenic predictor stage to behave well, per the authors' stated practice, and we'd need to verify that this transformation doesn't destroy the very quantile-specific signal we're hoping to capture. Fourth, multiple-testing correction with quantile regression run at several quantile levels effectively multiplies the test count — we'd need a sensible global-versus-quantile-specific framework. Fifth, the CFF cohort's heterogeneous ancestry composition would require principal-component adjustment exactly as in the UKBB analysis, and the sample sizes per ancestry stratum would constrain stratified quantile-level analyses. None of these are blockers — they're things to check.
