Show notes — Lumsden 2020, APOE PheWAS in UK Biobank

Blurb

Lumsden and colleagues ran a hypothesis-free phenome-wide association study of APOE genotype against ~950 phecodes derived from hospital records in 337,484 unrelated white British UK Biobank participants. Using e3e3 as a reference and Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction at q=0.05 (p-threshold 6.1×10⁻⁴), they recovered the expected APOE-Alzheimer's and APOE-cardiovascular signals (OR 13.52 for AD in e4e4 homozygotes), plus surprising e4-associated protection against obesity, chronic airway obstruction, type 2 diabetes, gallstones, and chronic liver disease, and e2e2-associated increased risk for peripheral vascular disease, thromboembolism, aneurysm, peptic ulcer, cervical disorders, and hallux valgus. Continuous-biomarker sensitivity analyses (hippocampal MRI, LDL, HbA1c, BMI, spirometry) supported the categorical findings. The proposed direction this episode suggests: replicate the Lumsden template for CFTR — phecode-anchored, FDR-corrected, zygosity-preserving — but with careful adaptation to the CF-only cohort design choice problem (no e3e3-equivalent natural reference) and CFFPR-versus-ICD-phecode mapping. The open question: can a ~5,000-person CFGP-CFFPR cohort sustain the same case-count thresholds and FDR interpretation that worked at n=337k?

Plain-English glossary

  • Allele: One specific version of a gene. APOE has three common alleles: e2, e3, e4.
  • APOE / apolipoprotein E: Gene/protein that binds lipid particles and helps the body clear cholesterol via the LDL receptor.
  • Assessment center adjustment: Including geographic recruitment site as a covariate to control for regional differences.
  • Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction: Standard procedure to control the proportion of false discoveries when testing many hypotheses at once. At q=0.05 with ~950 tests, the p-threshold becomes 6.1×10⁻⁴.
  • Continuous biomarker sensitivity analysis: Re-running the analysis using the underlying continuous measurement (e.g. LDL cholesterol levels) instead of the binary disease label to check the finding holds biologically.
  • Forty principal components (PC1–PC40): Numerical summaries of fine-scale ancestry differences; including them as covariates controls for residual population stratification.
  • Genotype: The pair of alleles a person carries — e.g. e3e4 means one e3 and one e4 copy.
  • Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium: Statistical expectation for allele frequency distribution in a random-mating population; deviation can flag genotyping error.
  • HbA1c: Glycated hemoglobin — a blood biomarker reflecting average glucose over ~3 months. Standard diabetes marker.
  • Homozygote / heterozygote: Person with two identical alleles (homozygote, e.g. e4e4) versus two different (heterozygote, e.g. e3e4).
  • ICD-9 / ICD-10: International Classification of Diseases — the hospital diagnosis coding system used in clinical records.
  • LDL cholesterol: Low-density lipoprotein cholesterol; the "bad cholesterol" marker raised by APOE e4.
  • LDL receptor: Cell-surface receptor that docks APOE-coated lipid particles and pulls them into cells.
  • Logistic regression: Statistical model that estimates the odds of a binary outcome (has disease vs. doesn't) from one or more predictors.
  • NHS linkage: UK Biobank's linkage to long-term National Health Service hospital diagnosis records.
  • Odds ratio (OR): Ratio of the odds of disease in one group to the odds in the reference group. OR=1 is no effect; OR>1 means higher odds.
  • PheWAS (phenome-wide association study): Fix one genetic factor and scan it against many diseases at once — inverse of GWAS.
  • Phecode: A clinically meaningful disease grouping built on top of ICD codes, developed by Wei et al. 2017. ~1,859 phecodes from ~15,000 ICD codes.
  • Population stratification: Statistical artefact in which apparent gene-disease associations are actually reflecting ancestry differences between cases and controls.
  • Positive / negative control phenotype: A disease the genetic factor is known to affect (positive) or known not to affect (negative), used to validate the analysis pipeline before reporting novel findings.
  • rs429358, rs7412: The two SNPs whose combination at the APOE locus defines the e2/e3/e4 alleles.
  • Spirometry / FEV1 / FVC: Lung-function tests. FEV1 is the volume forcefully exhaled in one second; FVC is the total volume. Standard lung-disease severity markers.
  • UK BiLEVE array / UK Biobank Axiom array: The two genotyping chips used in UK Biobank, with 95% marker content similarity.
  • UK Biobank (UKBB): ~500,000-person UK population cohort recruited 2006–2010, linked to hospital records.

Methods to lift

  • Single-gene PheWAS design with zygosity preserved — build six genotype categories (e4e4, e3e4, e2e4, e3e3, e2e3, e2e2) and run five logistic regressions per phecode against the most common reference (e3e3), rather than collapsing to allele-dose. Preserves the ability to detect homozygote-specific effects.
  • Phecode translation — convert ICD-9/ICD-10 hospital codes into ~1,859 phecode buckets (Wei et al. 2017) before testing.
  • Case-count threshold — require ≥200 cases per phecode to test, leaving ~950–1,070 testable phecodes.
  • Covariate adjustment — age, sex, genotyping array, first 40 genetic PCs, assessment center.
  • Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction at q=0.05 — gave p<6.1×10⁻⁴ here, scales with number of tests.
  • Positive + negative controls before reporting novel hits — three positives (dementia, hyperlipidemia, IHD) and one negative (diaphragmatic hernia).
  • Continuous-biomarker sensitivity analyses — re-test each top categorical finding against the corresponding continuous biomarker (hippocampal volume, LDL, HbA1c, BMI, FEV1/FVC).
  • R package PheWAS by Carroll, Bastarache & Denny (2014) — used for the regression and visualization.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: run a hypothesis-free, phecode-anchored, Benjamini-Hochberg-corrected logistic-regression PheWAS using a structured CFTR-genotype category scheme that preserves zygosity (F508del/F508del vs F508del/other vs other/other, optionally further split by residual-function vs minimal-function classes), with the most common in-cohort genotype as reference, mirroring the Lumsden 2020 design. Add a parallel modulator-stratified arm (modulator-eligible vs modulator-ineligible) given that Trikafta and ivacaftor have changed CF natural history substantially in the post-2019 cohort.

What to verify first

  • That case counts per phecode-equivalent endpoint in the CFGP-CFFPR linked dataset will plausibly clear the 200-case Lumsden threshold given a ~5,000-person cohort. For most rare CF complications they will not.
  • That the FDR threshold remains interpretable at the much smaller test count when the cohort is ~5,000 rather than ~337,000 — the burden math is different.
  • That the CFFPR data dictionary can be mapped to phecode-equivalent endpoint groupings without losing the CF-specific phenotypes (modulator response, sweat chloride, pancreatic sufficiency, CFRD onset age, chronic Pa acquisition age).
  • A defensible choice of in-cohort reference genotype — no CFTR genotype is "neutral" the way e3e3 is for APOE.
  • Positive and negative control phenotypes appropriate to the CF cohort — pancreatic insufficiency as a CFTR-severity positive control; a negative control is genuinely hard inside CF and may need to come from a non-CFTR locus.
  • Reporting bias in registry-derived phenotypes — CF patients are hospitalized frequently and the primary admission reason is usually pulmonary, so secondary diagnoses (CFRD, liver disease, GI complications) may be differentially under-reported in patients not yet hospitalized for severe respiratory disease.
  • Ancestry distribution and population-stratification handling inside the CFGP cohort — Lumsden restricted to one ancestry; we'll need to choose between restriction and explicit PC adjustment.

Notes

  • Lumsden uncovered that the famously dangerous APOE e4 allele was protective for five common non-brain non-heart conditions — a strong illustration that the "candidate-gene era" intuition about a famous allele can systematically miss its real cross-disease footprint.
  • The e2e2 homozygote findings (peptic ulcer, aneurysm, hallux valgus) demonstrate why zygosity-preserving design matters: collapsing e2e2 into "any e2 carrier" would have buried these signals.
  • Case counts for the e2e2 findings were small (n=8 to n=63) — the authors flag this and call for replication. The lesson for the CFTR PheWAS is that very-rare-genotype findings deserve a "this is a hypothesis, not a finding" framing in any write-up.

ALEX Today's paper is "Apolipoprotein E (APOE) genotype-associated disease risks: a phenome-wide, registry-based, case-control study utilising the UK Biobank," by Amanda Lumsden, Anwar Mulugeta, Ang Zhou, and Elina Hyppönen, published in EBioMedicine in 2020. We're reading it because this is the single cleanest demonstration of what a focused, gene-anchored phenome-wide study actually looks like when you do it on UK Biobank data — and the design is almost exactly transferable to a CFTR PheWAS.

JORDAN OK before we go in, I'm already a little behind. APOE — what is that? And what's a PheWAS? You used that one yesterday but I want it again.

ALEX Both fair. APOE is a gene named after the protein it encodes, apolipoprotein E. The protein floats around in blood plasma and in cerebrospinal fluid — the fluid bathing the brain — and its job is to bind lipid particles (cholesterol-carrying particles) and dock them with a receptor on cell surfaces called the LDL receptor, the low-density-lipoprotein receptor. Through that docking job, APOE shapes how much "bad cholesterol" your body clears and how it gets shuttled around. There are three common versions of the APOE gene in human populations, called e2, e3, and e4. The version you carry is determined by which letter you have at exactly two positions in the gene — two single-nucleotide polymorphisms, or SNPs, named rs429358 and rs7412. Most people are e3-e3, meaning they inherited one e3 copy from each parent. Roughly fourteen percent of people worldwide carry at least one e4. Roughly eight percent carry at least one e2. The e4 version is one of the most famous common genetic risk factors in all of human genetics — it raises your risk of late-onset Alzheimer's disease up to fifteen-fold if you carry two copies. Now PheWAS, phenome-wide association study, takes one fixed genetic factor — here, APOE genotype — and tests it against a very large number of diseases all at once, instead of the usual one-disease, many-variants design of a GWAS. The output is a table where each row is a disease and the cell tells you whether your genetic factor of interest changes the risk for that disease.

JORDAN So they're asking "what does APOE do across the whole disease landscape," not just Alzheimer's.

ALEX Exactly. And the design choice that makes this transferable to a CFTR PheWAS is that they didn't pick a variant — they picked a gene, and structured their genotype categories around the biology of that gene's known three alleles. Background: APOE has been studied in hundreds of papers for forty years. The literature has converged on a few facts — e4 raises Alzheimer's risk, raises bad cholesterol, raises heart disease risk; e2 is the gentler version, usually protective for the same things. But beyond those headline findings, the literature is a mess of contradictory candidate-gene studies, often underpowered for the rarer e2-e2 homozygote, which is only about six in a thousand people. Outside of the brain-and-heart axis, the meta-analyses on APOE versus type 2 diabetes, versus gallstones, versus liver disease, versus stroke, versus cancer don't agree across studies and don't agree across ethnic backgrounds. The authors had a hypothesis-free question: if we use UK Biobank, which is large enough to detect both common and rare APOE-genotype effects, and we let all hospital-diagnosed conditions in the registry compete for attention at once, what does APOE actually associate with across the human disease phenome?

JORDAN What's UK Biobank again — quickly?

ALEX UK Biobank, UKBB, is a UK-government-funded population cohort of about half a million people recruited between 2006 and 2010 at ages 37 to 73. Each participant filled out questionnaires, gave blood urine and saliva for genotyping and biochemistry, and consented to long-term linkage to their National Health Service hospital records. So the "phenotypes" — the diseases — come from real hospital diagnosis codes accumulated over years, not from self-report. That's the raw substrate for any UK-Biobank PheWAS. Methods. They restricted analysis to three hundred thirty-seven thousand four hundred eighty-four unrelated white British participants — a deliberate ancestry restriction to keep population stratification from confounding the analysis, at the cost of generalizability. They directly genotyped rs429358 and rs7412 using the UK BiLEVE array or the UK Biobank Axiom array, and assigned each person to one of six APOE genotypes — e4e4, e3e4, e2e4, e3e3, e2e3, or e2e2. They threw out the seventeen people with the rare e1 allele. To turn hospital diagnosis codes into testable phenotypes they used a translation system called phecodes, developed by Wei and colleagues in 2017 — it groups roughly fifteen thousand ICD-9 and ICD-10 diagnosis codes from the hospital record into about eighteen hundred fifty-nine clinically meaningful phenotype categories called phecodes. ICD here is the International Classification of Diseases. JORDAN, phecodes are basically a "clinically sensible grouping" layer that sits on top of raw hospital codes — so instead of testing each fine-grained sub-diagnosis separately, you test the disease cluster. They required at least two hundred cases per phecode to test it, leaving them with somewhere between nine hundred fifty and one thousand seventy phecodes per analysis. For each phecode they fit a logistic regression — five regressions per phecode, one per non-reference genotype, using e3e3 as the reference — adjusting for age, sex, genotyping array, the first forty genetic principal components, and assessment center. To handle the multiple-testing problem they used the Benjamini-Hochberg false discovery rate procedure at q-equals-0.05, which gave them a p-value cutoff of 6.1 times ten to the minus four. They added three positive controls — dementia, hyperlipidemia, ischemic heart disease, all of which APOE is known to influence — and one negative control, diaphragmatic hernia, where they expected no signal. They then ran sensitivity analyses using continuous biomarkers — hippocampal volume from brain MRI, LDL cholesterol, triglycerides, HbA1c for diabetes, BMI and waist circumference for obesity, and spirometry for lung function — to ask whether the categorical disease findings tracked with the underlying continuous biology.

JORDAN One push-back on a design choice?

ALEX The "white British only" restriction is genuinely defensible — it makes the analysis cleaner — but it also bakes in the discovery limitation they later acknowledge: e4 raises diabetes and gallstone risk in Asian cohorts, and the opposite direction in white British. Replication in other ancestries is essential and the paper doesn't provide it.

JORDAN What did they find? Numbers please.

ALEX After the false discovery rate correction, APOE genotype associated with thirty-seven phecode outcomes, representing eighteen distinct diseases. Positive controls came in as expected. Alzheimer's odds ratio relative to e3e3 was three point sixty-nine for e3e4 carriers and thirteen point five two for e4e4 homozygotes — that's a thirteen-fold increase in Alzheimer's risk for the homozygote. Hypercholesterolemia and ischemic heart disease tracked the same direction. Then the new stuff. e4e4 homozygotes had lower risk of obesity, odds ratio zero point seven eight; lower risk of chronic airway obstruction, odds ratio zero point seven four; lower risk of type 2 diabetes; lower risk of gallstone disease; and lower risk of chronic liver disease and cirrhosis. So e4 — the famously dangerous allele — is protective for several common conditions outside the brain-and-heart axis. The other striking finding was the e2e2 homozygotes — only zero point six percent of the cohort — who had elevated risk of six distinct diseases: peptic ulcer, peripheral arterial thromboembolism of the lower extremity, "other aneurysm" (aortic and other arterial aneurysm but not cerebral or cardiac), unspecified peripheral vascular disease, noninflammatory cervical disorders, and hallux valgus (the bunion-type foot deformity). The biomarker sensitivity analyses backed up the categorical findings — e4 dose-dependent decrease in hippocampal volume, e4 dose-dependent increase in white-matter hyperintensities, e4 dose-dependent decrease in HbA1c, BMI, waist circumference, and so on. They answered their question — yes, APOE has pleiotropic effects across the disease phenome that the candidate-gene literature missed.

JORDAN So if I'm building a CFTR PheWAS, what direction does this suggest?

ALEX One specific concrete direction. In plain English: do for CFTR exactly what they did for APOE — build genotype categories that respect CFTR biology (severe versus residual-function alleles, F508del-homozygous versus F508del-heterozygous versus other-other, modulator-eligible versus modulator-ineligible), translate the registry into phecodes, run a logistic regression per phecode against a sensible reference, false-discovery-rate-correct, and report. The thing this paper teaches us specifically is the value of an a-priori reference genotype — they used e3e3 because it's the most common — and the value of stratifying by zygosity rather than collapsing to "any e4 versus no e4," because they would have missed the homozygote-specific signals in e2e2 and e4e4 if they had collapsed. The formal one-sentence version: conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage, run a hypothesis-free, phecode-anchored, Benjamini-Hochberg-corrected logistic-regression PheWAS using a structured CFTR-genotype category scheme that preserves zygosity, with the most common genotype as reference, mirroring the Lumsden 2020 design.

JORDAN And the gap conditional on that direction?

ALEX What this paper closes — the template for a single-gene PheWAS in a half-million-person biobank registry is now well-established. What it leaves open — three things. First, the cohort here is the general population. Cystic fibrosis patients in the CFFPR are not the general population — they're enriched for severe respiratory and gastrointestinal disease at baseline. So the "reference" group for CFTR-genotype comparisons inside a CF-only cohort is not analogous to e3e3 in the Lumsden design, because everyone in the CF cohort has CF. We need to think carefully about whether the comparison is "F508del homozygous versus everyone else with CF," "modulator-eligible versus modulator-ineligible," or "fully-pancreatic-sufficient versus fully-pancreatic-insufficient." Second, the phecode mapping from ICD-10 to phecodes was developed and validated on general-population hospital data. CFFPR uses a CF-specific data dictionary, not pure ICD, so we'd need to verify the mapping from registry variables to phecode-like phenotype clusters before reusing this design directly. Third, the Lumsden paper acknowledges reporting bias — hospital-diagnosed conditions get over-recorded for the visits a patient was already in hospital for. CFFPR has the same hazard squared because CF patients are in hospital often and their primary diagnosis is usually pulmonary. What we'd need to check before committing: that registry-derived phenotype counts are large enough per CFTR-genotype-category to clear the two-hundred-case threshold the Lumsden design used; that the false-discovery-rate threshold remains interpretable when the underlying cohort is ~5,000 rather than ~337,000; that we have positive and negative controls in the CF setting — pancreatic insufficiency would be a natural positive control for CFTR severity, but a negative control is harder; and that ancestry confounding inside the CFF cohort, which has its own ancestry distribution different from UK Biobank's, is handled with explicit principal components.

JORDAN One concrete thing to do this week?

ALEX Pull up the Lumsden 2020 supplementary table S4 — the per-phecode results table — and walk through how they reported each disease cluster. That's the format we will likely want to imitate.
