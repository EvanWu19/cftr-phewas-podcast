Blurb

FinnGen R5 leverages Finland's founder bottleneck to discover 30 new associations across 15 well-studied common diseases — most driven by low-frequency variants enriched > 2× in Finland vs. non-Finnish/Swedish/Estonian Europeans. The bigger PheWAS across 1,932 register-defined endpoints yields 893 phenome-wide significant signals at p < 2.6×10⁻¹¹. For our CFTR PheWAS, the transferable lesson is not the bottleneck logic but the register-based phenotype validation framework: reference-study correlation slopes between register- and research-defined cases are 0.7–0.95 with weighted r² > 0.89.

Limitation to flag

Unlike Finnish-isolate cohorts where founder drift creates testable low-frequency Finnish-enriched variants, the CFF cohort's analytical advantage comes from phenotype curation rather than allele-frequency enrichment, and we therefore expect to discover modifier loci of common-AF rather than rare-AF effect.

JORDAN Today we are on Kurki et al., Nature 2023, "FinnGen provides genetic insights from a well-phenotyped isolated population." PMID 36653562. Released as data freeze R5 — 224,737 Finnish participants — with the project goal of 500,000.

ALEX I want to flag the framing immediately. This is not just another biobank paper. It is an argument that bottlenecked populations are genetically informative in a way that big population biobanks like UKBB and AoU are not. We need to evaluate that claim against our CFF cohort, which has its own — different — bottleneck story.

JORDAN Set up the population-isolate logic for me.

ALEX Finland had a founding bottleneck about 120 generations ago, then a rapid expansion. The bottleneck increased the frequency of certain deleterious alleles by genetic drift, and the small founder pool means those deleterious alleles are concentrated on a small number of low-frequency variants — they define low-frequency as MAF between 0.1% and 5% — rather than spread across a much larger number of ultra-rare variants. The Finnish Disease Heritage is the textbook example: 36 mostly recessive Mendelian diseases more common in Finland than anywhere else.

JORDAN And the claim is this also helps for common-disease genetics.

ALEX That's the bet, and the paper tests it. They picked 15 well-studied common diseases — type 2 diabetes, atrial fibrillation, asthma, breast cancer, Alzheimer's, IBD, and so on — and ran GWAS in FinnGen with replication in the Estonian Biobank (EstBB, n=136,724) and UKBB. They found 30 new associations, primarily low-frequency variants, enriched in Finland.

JORDAN How are they defining "enriched"?

ALEX Greater than two-fold higher AF in Finnish individuals than in non-Finnish, Swedish, or Estonian European reference — they call this NFSEE — sourced from gnomAD v2.0.1. They explicitly carve Sweden and Estonia out of the European reference because of recent migration history; that's the sharper definition of "Finnish-enriched" than what you'd get from naive comparison to gnomAD's NFE.

JORDAN Worth flagging — population-genetics decisions are usually buried in supplements but matter for interpretation.

ALEX Right. Their methods table has 30 new associations across 15 diseases. The best example I want to flag for our purposes is the IBD signal at TNRC18: an intronic variant with AF 3.6% in Finnish, less than 1% in NFSEE, OR 3.2, P = 2.4 × 10⁻⁶¹. That's a single-variant effect that "eclipses the significance of signals at IL23R, NOD2 and MHC" — those are the canonical IBD loci that decades of GWAS have hit. They found a stronger signal because the variant simply isn't testable in non-Finnish cohorts at the AF needed for power.

JORDAN That's the textbook isolate-population win.

ALEX But there are 30 of these and the table shows the whole pattern. Forty-four of the non-HLA new associations had low-frequency lead variants enriched more than twofold in Finland. Twenty-five out of those 27 LD-pruned new associations had the variant essentially missing from existing eQTL studies like GTEx — which means even when you find a hit, you can't immediately do post-GWAS biology because the regulatory annotation infrastructure is built on non-Finnish samples.

JORDAN That last point is important. You discover a variant but you can't immediately mechanism-it.

ALEX Right. Then they do the bigger PheWAS — 1,932 disease end points across release 5. They report 2,733 genome-wide significant associations at p < 5 × 10⁻⁸ across 2,496 independent loci on 807 end points. At their phenome-wide significance threshold of P < 2.6 × 10⁻¹¹ — that's 5×10⁻⁸ divided by 1,932 endpoints — they have 893 PWS hits at 771 loci across 247 endpoints. Fine-mapping implicated 148 coding variants associated with 83 endpoints; 73 PWS-significant.

JORDAN Fine-mapping methodology?

ALEX They use SuSiE-based fine-mapping. PIP > 0.05 is their putative-causal threshold; PIP > 0.5 is high-confidence. They acknowledge calibration limitations in the accompanying methods paper. For a coding variant to be in a credible set with PIP > 0.05, it has to be both well-imputed and supported by the conditional analysis at the locus. Of those 148 coding variants, 17 had PIP > 0.5 — the high-confidence subset.

JORDAN Phenotyping side?

ALEX This is where FinnGen's design shows its hand. They built endpoints from a dozen national health registers — drug purchases, drug reimbursements, outpatient visits, in-patient visits, causes-of-death, and the cancer registry — combined using ICD codes plus the ATC drug coding. The reference-study correlation slopes for the 15 demonstration diseases are 0.7 to 0.95 against published disease-specific GWAS, with weighted r² between 0.89 and 0.97. That's the validation that says: "yes, our register-based phenotypes recover the same signals that hand-curated case definitions get."

JORDAN The CFFPR analogy is strong here.

ALEX This is exactly the analogy I want to push. CFFPR phenotypes are register-derived. Our concept-note will be challenged by reviewers asking, "is your phecode definition of CFRD or chronic Pseudomonas as good as a hand-curated research case?" The FinnGen reference-study slopes give us a defensible answer: register-based phenotypes recover 70-95% of the disease-specific GWAS effect, with r² above 0.89. That's the citation we use to push back.

JORDAN Good. What about the bottleneck claim — is it transferable to our cohort?

ALEX Yes and no, and this is where I want us to be careful. CF patients are not a population isolate in the FinnGen sense. They are an ascertained Mendelian-disease cohort. The drift logic that makes Finland enriched for low-frequency variants does not apply — our cohort is enriched for one specific allele set, the 2,000-plus CFTR variants, by ascertainment, not by drift.

JORDAN But the modifier-locus question is different.

ALEX Right, and that is the subtle point. CFTR-modifier loci segregate in the general population. Their AF in CF patients should be roughly similar to AF in matched-ancestry controls. So the FinnGen-style "low-frequency variants enriched in our cohort" framing does not give us free power — what gives us power is the ascertained phenotype heterogeneity. We are picking up modifier effects because every patient has the disease, so the case definition is high-quality and the comorbidity heterogeneity within cases is what we are scanning.

JORDAN So what do we actually lift from FinnGen for our analysis plan?

ALEX Three things. One, the register-based phenotype validation strategy. We replicate FinnGen's reference-study slope analysis on our CFFPR phecodes against published CF-specific case definitions for at least three anchor phenotypes: CFRD, meconium ileus, and Pseudomonas chronic infection. If our slopes are above 0.7 with weighted r² above 0.85, we cite Kurki 2023 as the methodological precedent for register-defined endpoints in genetics. Two, their Bonferroni threshold logic — 5×10⁻⁸ divided by phenome size — gives us a defensible PWS threshold. With our 1,000-ish active CFFPR phecodes, that would be 5×10⁻¹¹. Three, their PIP-stratified credibility-set analysis is the right post-GWAS framework: report PIP > 0.05 as putative-causal, PIP > 0.5 as high-confidence, with CFTR locus excluded because it's the disease-defining gene and would dominate every fine-map.

JORDAN What does this not transfer?

ALEX The 30-new-low-frequency-variant story does not transfer. We do not get a "population-isolate jackpot" with our 5,000-patient cohort. What we get is a phenotype-precision jackpot — the phenotype is so well-defined and the comorbidity tracking is so deep that the modifier-locus PheWAS is informative even at a sample size that would be useless in UKBB.

JORDAN That is actually the right way to position the gap-1 pitch.

ALEX It is. The FinnGen frame gives us a complementary argument: their power comes from population genetics; ours comes from phenotype curation. Both are answers to "why this cohort and not UKBB?"
