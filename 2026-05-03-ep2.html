Blurb

FinnGen is Finland's national biobank-plus-registry genetics project. In Release 5 (224,737 participants, target 500,000), the authors linked DNA from a network of Finnish biobanks to decades of nationwide electronic health records, drug-purchase, procedure, and cause-of-death registries. They turned that into 1,932 computable disease endpoints and ran a PheWAS across all of them. Because Finland went through a population founder event and bottleneck, certain DNA variants that are vanishingly rare in most of Europe are common enough in Finland to test statistically. They found 30 new associations across 15 well-studied diseases, plus 893 phenome-wide significant signals across the full 1,932 endpoints. The relevance for our work is not the founder-genetics story but the registry-to-endpoint architecture, which is the closest published analogue to what a CFTR PheWAS on the CF Foundation Patient Registry would have to build.

Plain-English glossary

  • AF (allele frequency) — how common a particular DNA variant is in a population, usually written as a percentage.
  • ATC — Anatomical Therapeutic Chemical classification, the standard codes used to label prescription drugs.
  • Biobank — a large research collection of biological samples (blood, saliva, etc.) paired with health information about the donors.
  • Bottleneck (population) — a sharp shrinkage in population size that wipes out genetic diversity, leaving descendants less varied than the ancestral group.
  • CFFPR — CF Foundation Patient Registry, the longitudinal clinical database the CF Foundation maintains for U.S. CF patients.
  • CFTR — the gene whose variants cause cystic fibrosis; the anchor gene for our proposed PheWAS.
  • EHR (electronic health record) — the digital file a clinical system keeps about a patient: diagnoses, prescriptions, procedures, etc.
  • Endpoint — a specific health outcome defined precisely enough that a computer can decide who has it.
  • Fine-mapping — the statistical step that narrows a genetic association down to the variants most likely to be causal.
  • Founder effect — when a small group splits off and becomes the ancestors of a later population, so the variants those few founders carried become disproportionately common downstream.
  • Genotyping array — a chip that measures DNA at a curated set of positions, much cheaper than full sequencing.
  • gnomAD — a public database of allele frequencies across major world populations, used as the reference for what's "rare elsewhere."
  • GWAS (genome-wide association study) — scan every common variant across the genome for association with one trait.
  • ICD (International Classification of Diseases) — the WHO's standardized codes for medical diagnoses; ICD-8, ICD-9, and ICD-10 are successive versions.
  • Imputation — statistically guessing the variants you didn't directly genotype, by comparing measured variants to a reference panel of fully sequenced people.
  • LD (linkage disequilibrium) — the tendency of nearby DNA variants to be inherited together as a block, so an association at one variant smears across its neighbors.
  • MAF (minor allele frequency) — the frequency of whichever version of a variant is rarer.
  • NFSEE — non-Finnish, non-Swedish, non-Estonian European; FinnGen's reference category for "other Europeans" when defining Finnish enrichment.
  • Phecode — a standardized way of grouping ICD codes into disease concepts designed specifically for PheWAS.
  • PheWAS (phenome-wide association study) — scan many health outcomes for association with one variant or gene.
  • REGENIE — a modern GWAS software package; "step 2" is its variant-association testing stage.
  • SAIGE — the mixed-model logistic regression GWAS tool FinnGen used; handles relatedness and rare outcomes.
  • Summary statistics — per-variant association results (effect size, p-value), shareable without releasing individual-level data.
  • UKBB (UK Biobank) — a roughly 500,000-person biobank in the United Kingdom; the standard "big and diverse" comparison cohort.
  • WGS (whole-genome sequencing) — reading essentially every base of a person's genome, in contrast to genotyping arrays which only measure curated positions.

Methods to lift

  • Registry-to-endpoint pipeline. Pull diagnoses, prescriptions, procedures, and cause-of-death from longitudinal records; combine into computable endpoint definitions; curate from a long candidate list down to a working catalog (FinnGen: 2,800 candidates curated to 1,932 endpoints).
  • Population-specific imputation reference panel. Build the imputation reference from genomes of the same population the study cohort comes from (FinnGen: 3,775 high-coverage Finnish whole genomes), so rare population-specific variants don't disappear into imputation noise.
  • Mixed-model GWAS engine. Use a tool like SAIGE (FinnGen) or REGENIE to handle relatedness among participants and unbalanced case-control ratios for rare endpoints.
  • Phenome-wide significance threshold. Standard genome-wide threshold (5 × 10⁻⁸) divided by the number of endpoints (FinnGen: 1,932), giving FinnGen's 2.6 × 10⁻¹¹ phenome-wide threshold.
  • Public release of summary statistics, not individual data. Share per-variant association results so others can replicate or meta-analyze, while keeping participant-level data behind governance.

A founder effect is what happens when a small group splits off from a larger population and becomes the ancestors of everyone afterward. Whatever DNA variants those few founders happened to carry get passed down to all their descendants, so rare variants from the founders can become surprisingly common in the descended population. Related concept, population bottleneck. A bottleneck is when a population shrinks dramatically, by famine or migration or isolation, and a lot of genetic diversity is simply lost because most lineages didn't make it through. Finland had both, a founding event followed by a bottleneck about a hundred and twenty generations ago.

JORDAN Allele frequency.

ALEX A DNA variant is just a position in the genome where some people have one letter and some have another. Allele frequency, often abbreviated AF, is just how common one of those letters is in a population. If three percent of Finnish chromosomes carry a particular variant, the AF in Finland is three percent. We'll also hear "minor allele frequency," MAF, which just means the frequency of whichever version is rarer.

JORDAN GWAS, PheWAS.

ALEX GWAS stands for genome-wide association study. You pick one trait, like type two diabetes, and you scan every common variant across the whole genome to see which ones are associated with that trait. PheWAS, phenome-wide association study, flips that around. You pick one variant, or one gene, and you scan every health condition you can measure in your data to see which ones are associated with it. GWAS is one trait, many variants. PheWAS is one variant, many traits.

JORDAN And the registry vocabulary.

ALEX EHR, electronic health record, just means the digital file your doctor and hospital keep about you, diagnoses, prescriptions, procedures. ICD stands for International Classification of Diseases. It's a standardized list of codes, maintained by the World Health Organization, that hospitals use to label every diagnosis. ICD-10 is the current version, ICD-9 is the older one, ICD-8 is older still. Endpoint just means the specific health outcome being studied, defined precisely enough that a computer can decide who has it and who doesn't. Last term, linkage disequilibrium, abbreviated LD. DNA variants that sit close to each other on a chromosome tend to get inherited together as a block, generation after generation, because not enough recombination happens between them to break them apart. So if one variant is associated with a disease, all its LD neighbors will look associated too, even if only one of them is doing the actual work.

JORDAN Okay. With that grounding, what were the FinnGen authors trying to do?

ALEX The motivating intuition is this. Most very large biobanks, the UK Biobank in particular, draw from genetically diverse populations. UKBB stands for UK Biobank, a biobank of about half a million people in the United Kingdom. Diverse populations are great for many things, but they have a property that limits one specific kind of discovery. Bad DNA variants in diverse populations are scattered across many millions of different ultra-rare variants, each one too rare to study individually. In a population that went through a founder event and a bottleneck, like Finland, the same total amount of bad genetic material gets concentrated onto a much smaller number of variants. Some variants that would be one-in-a-million elsewhere are one-in-fifty in Finland. That makes them statistically testable.

JORDAN So the hypothesis is, smaller cohort, but variants you can actually study.

ALEX Right. The Finnish Disease Heritage, a famous set of about thirty-six recessive Mendelian diseases that are far more common in Finland than anywhere else, is the proof of concept for this idea in Mendelian genetics, the kind of disease where one bad variant causes the disease. The open question that this paper takes on is whether the same logic helps for common diseases like diabetes and heart disease, where the genetics is spread across many variants of small effect. Their second move was to link genetic data to nationwide health registries. Finland has universal health care, and every interaction with the system, every drug purchase, every outpatient visit, every hospitalization, every cause of death, is recorded in national registries that go back decades. They linked all of that to biobank samples. So you get genetics on one side, and on the other side a near-complete medical biography for every participant. The question of the paper is whether founder enrichment plus deep phenotyping discovers things that bigger but shallower biobanks miss.

JORDAN And how do they actually build that?

ALEX They pulled samples from a network of regional Finnish biobanks, some collected fresh, some legacy collections. The release we're looking at, Release 5, has 224,737 participants. The eventual target is 500,000, which is the number you'll often hear cited for FinnGen overall. Step one, genotyping, which means measuring each person's DNA at a curated set of positions using a custom array, basically a chip designed specifically for Finnish genetics. Step two, imputation, which means statistically guessing the variants you didn't directly measure by comparing each person's measured variants to a reference panel of fully sequenced individuals. They built a Finnish-specific reference panel from 3,775 deeply sequenced Finnish genomes, which is critical because imputing Finnish genomes against a non-Finnish reference loses exactly the rare variants you most want to study.

JORDAN And the phenotype side.

ALEX They built endpoints, those computer-decidable health outcomes, by pulling ICD-10, ICD-9, and ICD-8 codes out of the registries, plus prescription drug purchases coded with a standard called ATC, plus medical procedures and cause-of-death codes. They started from more than 2,800 candidate definitions and curated those down to 1,932 disease endpoints. For the GWAS engine itself they used a tool called SAIGE, which is a mixed-model logistic regression method, just a flavor of statistical model that can handle the fact that lots of Finnish participants are related to each other and that most diseases are rare. They ran SAIGE for each endpoint and then meta-analyzed, statistically combined, with results from the Estonian Biobank and UK Biobank when they wanted replication.

JORDAN So with all that machinery, did founder enrichment actually buy them new biology?

ALEX Yes, and the numbers are striking. They first ran fifteen well-studied common diseases, things like type two diabetes, atrial fibrillation, asthma, breast cancer, Alzheimer's disease, inflammatory bowel disease. Across those fifteen, they found thirty new genetic associations that previous GWAS had missed. Forty-four of the non-immune-region new associations were driven by what they call low-frequency variants, AF below five percent, that were more than twice as common in Finland as in non-Finnish, non-Swedish, non-Estonian Europeans. They released all the per-variant summary statistics publicly on FinnGen.fi. No individual-level data, just the aggregate associations.
