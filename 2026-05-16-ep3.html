Blurb

Aksit et al. 2022 (American Journal of Human Genetics) is the CF Genome Project's joint scan of two CF complications — cystic fibrosis-related diabetes (CFRD) and meconium ileus (MI) — for shared genetic modifier architecture. In 4,569 pancreatic-insufficient CF patients with WGS, they identify 11 MI loci (6 unreported) and 12 CFRD loci (5 unreported). Three loci show formal pleiotropy: SLC26A9 and CEBPB increase risk for both traits in the same direction, while PRSS1 alleles run in opposite directions across the two traits. The SLC26A9 locus also harbors a second independent CFRD-only signal and a rare-variant SMMAT signal specific to CFRD. The episode suggests one possible direction for the CFTR PheWAS: a locked-locus pleiotropy scan of the Aksit-2022 lead variants against the full CFFPR phenome, conditional on dosage-callability checks, modulator-era stratification, and relatedness handling.

Methods to lift

  • Paired same-substrate GWAS — run discovery GWAS for two correlated traits on the same WGS cohort, then explicitly test cross-trait pleiotropy as a separate analysis step rather than as a sidebar.
  • Martingale residual for time-to-onset — convert age-of-onset CFRD into a quantitative residual per patient (Breslow model), then run linear regression downstream so the workflow harmonizes with the binary MI analysis.
  • SMMAT (Set-based Mixed Model Association Test) — rare-variant aggregation (MAF<1%) within genomic regions, run as a separate arm alongside common-variant single-variant testing.
  • Joint cross-trait mixed-effects logistic regression — model "CFRD or MI" as a binary outcome with a genetic-relationship-matrix random effect, to detect loci that drive shared risk for either complication.
  • Conditional analysis for independent signals at one locus — at SLC26A9, condition on the canonical lead variant to detect a second independent CFRD-only signal.
  • eQTL anchoring of unnamed loci — for hits without an obvious nearest gene, use GTEx eQTL evidence to anchor mechanism (e.g., CEBPB locus is an eQTL for LINC01273 in lung and subcutaneous adipose).
  • Direction-of-effect framing for pleiotropy — categorize pleiotropic loci as "same-direction" (SLC26A9, CEBPB) vs "opposite-direction" (PRSS1) and treat opposite-direction loci as mechanistically distinct.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage:

Locked-locus pleiotropy scan of the Aksit 2022 modifier-locus lead variants — SLC26A9 rs2036100 and rs6685188, CEBPB rs6095829, PRSS1 rs3757377, plus the chr16p12.1 CFRD lead variant — against the full CFFPR phecode-mapped phenome plus the CFFPR-curated CF endpoint panel (pulmonary FEV1 trajectory, chronic Pseudomonas acquisition, lung allograft, hepatobiliary disease, mortality, modulator response). Pre-register CFRD and MI as positive controls and the broader phecode panel as exploratory pleiotropy targets. Bonferroni-correct across phecodes within the locked locus set.

What to verify first

  • Genotype-dosage callability of all five lead variants from CFGP short-read WGS; the SLC26A9 region's high regional LD structure requires clean separation of the two independent signals.
  • CFFPR phecode-mapping consistency across the relevant time window (chronic Pseudomonas acquisition date and CFRD onset date have different ascertainment quality and resolution).
  • Bonferroni-correction posture: at five variants × ~900 phecodes the corrected α ≈ 1×10⁻⁵; whether to relax to FDR within the locked-locus set.
  • Modulator-era stratification: Aksit's cohort was largely pre-Trikafta; post-Trikafta phecode prevalence may differ for the lung-side endpoints.
  • Relatedness handling: CFGP substrate includes 60+ twin pairs and several hundred sibling pairs — mixed-effects models with a genetic-relationship-matrix random effect are mandatory.
  • PRSS1 mechanism check: the opposite-direction effect across CFRD and MI implies the same haplotype mediates two different downstream mechanisms; before extending PRSS1 to additional phenotypes, the direction-of-effect prediction needs a pre-registered hypothesis.

ALEX Today's third episode is Aksit, Ling, Pace, Raraigh, Onchiri, Faino, and colleagues, with Garry Cutting and Scott Blackman as senior authors, 2022, in the American Journal of Human Genetics — "Pleiotropic modifiers of age-related diabetes and neonatal intestinal obstruction in cystic fibrosis." This is the CF Genome Project's joint scan of two seemingly different complications — CFRD, the diabetes that develops in adolescent and adult CF patients, and meconium ileus, the neonatal intestinal blockage that happens in about a fifth of CF newborns — and asks whether the genetic modifiers overlap. It's the direct prior to the Aksit 2020 paper we covered on day one of this podcast and a load-bearing piece of the CFGP modifier-discovery program.

JORDAN Stop. I want every term before we move.

ALEX Right. CF, cystic fibrosis, is the disease caused by mutations in the CFTR gene; about 35,000 patients in the U.S. CFTR codes for a chloride channel on epithelial cells. CFRD is cystic fibrosis-related diabetes — a distinct form of diabetes that affects 19 percent of CF adolescents, 40 to 50 percent of CF adults, and more than 90 percent of CF patients with severe β-cell dysfunction by age 50. It looks somewhat like type 2 diabetes — slow decline in β-cell function and islet amyloid deposits — but unlike type 2 it doesn't usually involve insulin resistance. MI is meconium ileus — the very first stool of a newborn, called meconium, gets impacted in the small intestine and can't pass; in CF newborns this requires enema or surgical intervention and affects roughly 20 percent of CF babies. CFGP is the CF Genome Project — the whole-genome-sequencing resource on roughly 5,000 CF patients, drawn from the CF Twin and Sibling Study, the CFRD Study, the Genetic Modifier Study, and the EPIC observational study. CFFPR is the CF Foundation Patient Registry, the U.S. longitudinal clinical database that supplements the genetic cohort. Now the methods terms. WGS is whole-genome sequencing, where you read every position in a person's DNA rather than just the protein-coding parts. Pleiotropy is when one gene affects multiple traits — for example, if a variant influences both your CFRD risk and your meconium ileus risk, that variant is pleiotropic. PI, pancreatic insufficient, is the CF subtype where the pancreas has stopped producing enough digestive enzymes — most severe CF cases are PI, and PI patients are the ones who get MI and who develop CFRD. eQTL is expression quantitative trait locus — a variant that changes how much of a particular gene gets made. SMMAT stands for set-based mixed model association test — a method for rare-variant aggregation where you collapse many rare variants in a single gene or region into one test rather than testing each variant separately. Martingale residual is the residual from a survival model — used here to convert age-of-onset CFRD into a single quantitative number per patient, so that you can run linear regression instead of a more complex time-to-event model. F508del is the most common CFTR mutation, also written as p.Phe508del; the majority of severe CF patients carry at least one copy.

JORDAN What was the question?

ALEX Three earlier CF modifier studies — Blackman 2013 on CFRD, Sun 2012 on MI, and Aksit 2020 on CFRD-and-T2D overlap — had each found that variants near SLC26A9, CEBPB, and PRSS1 associated with their respective traits. The CFRD-and-MI correlation in CF patients was modest — about 5 to 10 percent shared risk after adjusting for CFTR severity — but it wasn't zero, and the prior modifier hits hinted that the same loci might be driving both. The question Aksit 2022 poses: when you run paired GWAS for both CFRD and MI on the same modern whole-genome-sequencing substrate, with explicit cross-trait pleiotropy testing, which modifier loci are shared and which are not? And do shared loci act in the same direction on both, or in opposite directions? The latter case — opposite directions — would be evidence that the same gene mediates two different mechanisms with opposing physiology.

JORDAN Now methods. How did they tackle it?

ALEX They started with 4,569 CF Genome Project individuals after sample QC, all pancreatic-insufficient. Three cohorts feed the substrate: the Genetic Modifier Studies, with 1,763 individuals; the CF Twin-and-Sibling Study plus the CFRD Study, with 1,582; and the EPIC observational study, with 1,224. Across the full cohort, 34 percent had CFRD on most-recent screening and 19 percent had a history of MI. 3,267 of the 4,569 had appeared in prior modifier GWAS — meaning a meaningful new-individual fraction was added by the CFGP substrate. CFRD was treated as a time-to-onset trait — they ran a Cox model and extracted the Martingale residual per patient, so the downstream regression is a linear model on a quantitative residual rather than a survival model on raw event data. MI was treated as a binary trait — affected or not. They ran logistic regression for MI and linear regression on the Martingale residual for CFRD, with covariates of sex, age at last CFRD screen, and the first four principal components for ancestry. For rare-variant analysis they used SMMAT — set-based mixed model association tests aggregating rare variants (minor allele frequency below one percent) within genomic regions. For joint cross-trait analysis they modeled presence of CFRD-or-MI as a binary outcome via mixed-effects logistic regression, with a genetic-relationship-matrix random effect to handle the relatedness from the twin-and-sibling substrate. Genome-wide significance was the standard 5×10⁻⁸ threshold.

ALEX My pushback on-air. The Martingale-residual-then-linear-regression workflow is a venerable trick — it lets you treat a time-to-onset trait like a quantitative trait — but it's not equivalent to a Cox proportional-hazards model. You lose information about the actual hazard shape, and you can mis-handle censoring. They defend the choice by showing similar betas and p-values whether they treat CFRD as Martingale-residual or as a dichotomous trait, but the equivalence holds at the lead variants, not necessarily at the gene-level rare-variant burden. The newer Aksit 2020 paper used proper Cox-model survival analysis. So this 2022 paper is doing pleiotropy testing on a slightly looser CFRD definition than the 2020 single-trait paper. That's not wrong — it's deliberate to harmonize with the binary MI analysis — but it means the CFRD p-values here are not directly comparable to the 2020 paper's.

JORDAN What did they find?

ALEX For MI they identified 11 loci. Six are previously unreported. Five reach genome-wide significance and are near SLC26A9, CLPS/SLC26A8, EMSY, ATP12A, and SLC6A14. The remaining six have suggestive evidence and are near SLC37A3, DOCK1, MNAT1, BCAR1, CEBPB, and one further locus. For CFRD they identified 12 loci. Five are previously unreported. TCF7L2 on chr10 and a chr16p12.1 locus reach genome-wide significance; six others — PRAMEF19, SLC26A9 with two independent signals, chr5q34, TFAP2B, ZNF316, and CEBPB — have suggestive evidence. Of these collections, three loci — SLC26A9, CEBPB, and PRSS1 — show variants associated with both MI and CFRD. At SLC26A9 the same alleles increase risk for both, with lead MI variant rs2036100 at p=7.3×10⁻⁸ and lead CFRD signal rs1874361 in high linkage disequilibrium with it. At CEBPB the same alleles also increase risk for both, with lead MI variant rs6095829 at p=5.2×10⁻⁷ and the corresponding CFRD lead variant rs2869963. At PRSS1, by contrast, the alleles run in opposite directions — the alleles that raise CFRD risk lower MI risk, and vice versa. The lead variants in the PRSS1 region — rs3757377 for CFRD at p=7.4×10⁻⁵ and rs1964986 — are in high linkage disequilibrium with each other but have opposite effect-size signs across the two traits. Aksit also separately discovered a second independent SLC26A9 signal — rs6685188, p=3.2×10⁻⁷ for CFRD — in a different LD block from the canonical SLC26A9 signal and not associated with MI. The rare-variant SMMAT analysis at SLC26A9 specifically — collapsing variants with allele frequency below one percent — yielded an independent rare-variant association with CFRD only, not MI. The interpretation is that within a single locus, different variant classes can carry different trait specificities. So they answered the question. There is genuine cross-trait pleiotropy at three modifier loci, two of which act in the same direction and one of which acts in opposite directions, and the SLC26A9 locus harbors both common-variant pleiotropy and rare-variant trait-specific signals.
