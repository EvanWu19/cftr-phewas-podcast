Blurb

Kalyta and colleagues — a Polish, Swiss, and British clinical-genetics group writing in Genes in 2023 — published a narrative review asking whether the textbook story of autosomal recessive disease holds up at biobank scale. The review pulls together case reports of symptomatic heterozygotes across neurological, neuromuscular, hematological, metabolic, and pulmonary diseases, contrasts them with two large biobank-scale findings (Barton 2022 in UK Biobank and Heyne 2023 in FinnGen), and discusses possible molecular mechanisms — undiscovered deep-intronic splice variants, dominant-negative effects, modifier genes, environmental exposures — for why the textbook recessive-versus-dominant binary breaks down. Plain-English glossary

  • Allele — one of the two copies of a gene a person inherits, one from each parent.
  • Allele frequency (AF / MAF) — the fraction of chromosomes in a reference population that carry a given variant; minor allele frequency is the rarer of the two.
  • Autosomal recessive disease — a disease that requires two damaged copies of a gene (one from each parent) to manifest; carriers of one damaged copy are usually thought to be unaffected.
  • Carrier (heterozygote) — a person with one damaged copy and one working copy of a recessive disease gene.
  • Compound heterozygote — a person with two damaged copies at the same gene but at different positions, e.g., one F508del CFTR allele and one G542X CFTR allele.
  • Deep-intronic splice variant — a damaging variant located far inside an intron, away from the standard exon boundaries; routinely missed by exome sequencing because exomes don't sequence deep introns.
  • Dominant-negative effect — a damaged protein that not only fails to function but actively interferes with the working copy of the protein, producing disease even with one damaged copy.
  • F508del / p.Phe508del — the most common CFTR pathogenic variant; a three-base-pair deletion removing one phenylalanine residue at position 508. Roughly 70 percent of CF chromosomes worldwide.
  • FinnGen — Finnish national biobank cohort; Finland's founder population enriches certain rare variants relative to the rest of Europe, making low-frequency-variant association studies more powerful.
  • Gene — a stretch of DNA coding for a protein.
  • Heterozygote — see Carrier.
  • Homozygote — a person with two identical copies of a variant.
  • PheWAS (Phenome-Wide Association Study) — analytic flip of GWAS; fix one variant or gene and ask which of thousands of medical phenotypes it associates with.
  • Penetrance — probability that a person carrying a pathogenic variant develops the disease.
  • UK Biobank (UKBB) — half-million-person British general-population research cohort with linked health records and DNA.
  • Variant — modern, neutral term for a position in DNA differing from the reference sequence.

Methods to lift

  • Narrative-review reading strategy — pair the case-report literature for a recessive-disease class with the matching biobank PheWAS for the same gene; the case reports define what the spectrum looks like in single individuals, the biobank quantifies whether the same effect is detectable on average across a population.
  • Heterozygote-modifier panel construction — select recessive disease genes for which a biobank-confirmed heterozygote phenotypic effect has been replicated in at least one large cohort (UKBB Barton 2022; FinnGen Heyne 2023; not yet AoU at scale), pre-register the gene list and the candidate phecode list before any test is run.
  • Variant-class subdivision within a recessive gene — distinguish loss-of-function carrier variants from missense / hypomorphic variants when scoring carrier status, since dominant-negative and haploinsufficient mechanisms predict different heterozygote phenotypic effects.
  • Sequencing-completeness audit — before claiming a heterozygote phenotype, document that whole-genome sequencing has been performed and that deep-intronic splice variants and copy-number variants have been called and reviewed; otherwise the apparent heterozygote may be an undetected compound heterozygote.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: a hypothesis-prioritized heterozygote-modifier scan against the CFFPR phecode catalog, restricted to a pre-specified panel of recessive disease genes for which biobank-confirmed heterozygote phenotypic effects have been replicated outside CF (anchored to the Barton 2022 UK Biobank panel and the Heyne 2023 FinnGen panel), with SERPINA1 alpha-1 antitrypsin as the lead candidate given respiratory-disease mechanistic relevance, and with the gene panel, the phecode list, and the carrier-status definition (loss-of-function only versus loss-of-function plus established missense pathogenic) pre-registered before any test is run.

What to verify first

  • Which recessive disease gene heterozygote effects in Barton 2022 and Heyne 2023 are robust to ancestry stratification rather than artifacts of European founder structure, since CFGP includes non-European admixed individuals (per Li 2011, today's Episode 3).
  • What fraction of CFGP participants carry one or more pathogenic variants in the candidate gene panel — too few carriers means the scan is underpowered before it begins.
  • Whether the CFFPR phecode catalog contains the relevant comorbidities for the candidate genes (alpha-1 antitrypsin lung and liver phenotypes, hereditary hemochromatosis iron-overload phenotypes, FMF-like inflammatory phenotypes) at high enough case counts to be testable.
  • Whether the CFGP / CFF Therapeutics Development Network has any published or internal view on heterozygote-modifier analyses on the WGS substrate, so duplicating their pipeline rather than building a parallel one remains the preferred default.
  • Whether the consortium variant calls have already been filtered against gnomAD frequency in a way that would preferentially remove the candidate carrier variants the panel is designed to test.
  • Whether the heterozygote effect at SERPINA1 in a CF-affected cohort produces a coherent biological signal — a CF patient with a single SERPINA1 carrier variant might show worse FEV1 trajectory than F508del-only matched controls, or might show no effect because CF lung disease already saturates the airway-injury phenotype.

JORDAN Okay. What problem motivated this review, and what was actually unknown before it was written?

ALEX The problem is the comfortable old textbook story about recessive disease. The textbook says: carriers of one damaged copy of an autosomal recessive disease gene are healthy. Two damaged copies cause the disease. One copy does nothing visible. That is how genetic counseling has been done for decades — when a person learns they are a CFTR heterozygote because their child was diagnosed with CF, the counselor reassures them that they themselves are not at any meaningful CF-related risk. Over the last decade, large biobanks with millions of people, electronic-health-record linkage, and dense genotyping have started to challenge that picture. Case reports going back to the 1980s suggested that some heterozygotes for some recessive diseases — Familial Mediterranean Fever, hereditary hemochromatosis, McArdle disease, alpha-sarcoglycanopathy — show milder, later-onset versions of the same disease. These reports were always controversial because the diagnostic workup was usually limited to Sanger sequencing or whole-exome sequencing, leaving open the alternative that a second damaging variant had been missed. The biobank era brought a new method. Instead of arguing about individual cases, you can ask the population question: do carriers of pathogenic variants for a recessive disease show, on average, a measurable phenotypic effect across hundreds of thousands of people? When you ask that way, the answer is sometimes yes. Kalyta and colleagues read the case-report literature and the new biobank literature side by side and try to make sense of why heterozygotes are usually asymptomatic, sometimes mildly affected, and occasionally indistinguishable from full disease. What was unknown before this review is whether the case reports and the biobank signals were telling the same story — and whether the dominant-versus-recessive textbook framework needs to be replaced.

The pattern was confirmed by Heyne and colleagues in FinnGen, who scanned 203 known recessive-disease genes and identified heterozygote-level phenotypic effects for variants in SERPINA1, NPHS1, CASP7, and GJB2. The authors of the Heyne study proposed reclassifying these as "recessive, with rare expressing heterozygotes." Did the review actually answer its own question? Partially. It did establish that symptomatic heterozygosity is real at the population level for some recessive disease variants, including CFTR p.Phe508del. It did not establish a unified molecular mechanism, and it did not give clinicians a usable tool for counseling individual heterozygotes — the population-level effect is real but small and the per-person prediction is still poor.
