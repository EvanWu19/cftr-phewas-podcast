Blurb

Jacobs, Belete, Bestwick and colleagues 2020 (J Neurol Neurosurg Psychiatry) ran the first PRS-by-environment interaction screen for incident Parkinson's disease in the UK Biobank — 1,276 incident cases against 500,406 controls. Marginal associations with PD replicated the textbook risk-factor list at FDR < 0.05 (family history of PD and dementia, non-smoking, low alcohol, depression, daytime somnolence, epilepsy, earlier menarche). A PRS built from external 2019 PD GWAS weights gave a top-vs-bottom-decile OR of 3.37 (95% CI 2.41–4.70) and modestly improved the PREDICT-PD algorithm. The signature G×E result: nominal (not FDR-surviving) negative multiplicative interaction between the PRS and diabetes (β = −0.40, p = 0.026), and a matching additive-scale attributable proportion of −0.39 (95% CI −1.03 to −0.03), implying diabetes confers more PD risk in low-PRS people than in high-PRS people. The episode suggests one possible direction for the CFTR PheWAS: an additive-scale RERI/AP interaction analysis between a CFTR-variant-burden severity score and a single environmental modifier (smoking, BMI, or diabetes) on a CF outcome in the CFF Patient Registry — conditional on a power audit and an outcome reframe from binary incident-disease logistic regression to a continuous longitudinal model for FEV1 trajectory.

Plain-English glossary

  • AP (attributable proportion) — proportion of disease in the doubly-exposed group attributable to interaction on the additive scale; derived from RERI.
  • CFFPR / CFF Patient Registry — the Cystic Fibrosis Foundation Patient Registry, a longitudinal US CF clinical-care registry.
  • CFRD — CF-related diabetes, a CF-specific form of diabetes driven by pancreatic beta-cell dysfunction.
  • CI — confidence interval.
  • FDR — false discovery rate; controls the expected proportion of false positives among declared positives.
  • G×E (gene-by-environment interaction) — situation where the effect of an environmental exposure depends on genotype, or vice versa.
  • GWAS — Genome-Wide Association Study; one-variant-at-a-time test of association with a phenotype.
  • ICD codes — International Classification of Diseases codes used to map hospital records to disease yes/no.
  • Incident vs prevalent — incident = diagnosed after enrollment (forward-in-time); prevalent = already diagnosed at enrollment.
  • OR (odds ratio) — ratio of the odds of disease in the exposed group to the odds in the unexposed.
  • PD — Parkinson's disease.
  • PheWAS — Phenome-Wide Association Study; one-variant tested against many phenotypes.
  • PREDICT-PD — a pre-existing clinical PD risk-prediction algorithm using non-genetic factors.
  • PRS (polygenic risk score) — per-person summary of GWAS-weighted risk allele load across the genome.
  • PRSice — software tool for building and selecting a PRS across multiple p-value thresholds.
  • Prodromal feature — an early symptom that appears before clinical disease.
  • RERI (relative excess risk due to interaction) — additive-scale interaction metric in logistic regression.
  • Townsend deprivation index — UK area-level socioeconomic deprivation score.
  • UKB / UKBB — UK Biobank; ~500k UK adults aged 40–69 at recruitment, prospectively followed with health-record linkage.

Methods to lift

  • Incident-case definition built from first-occurrence ICD-10 G20 in linked hospital records plus self-reported PD with date.
  • Multivariable logistic regression for each candidate exposure adjusting for age, sex, ethnicity, Townsend deprivation, with likelihood-ratio tests against the null model.
  • FDR control (5% and 10% thresholds) applied across all environmental and prodromal factors.
  • External-weight PRS construction using PRSice with 2019 Nalls PD GWAS summary statistics; train/test split (30/70) to pick p-value threshold without overfitting.
  • Continuous-PRS-by-exposure interaction tested on both multiplicative (interaction term + likelihood-ratio test) and additive (RERI / attributable proportion with bootstrap CIs) scales.
  • Sensitivity analyses: exclusion of under-60s, exclusion of non-White individuals, exclusion of self-report-only diagnoses, matched 4:1 case-control analysis.
  • Software stack: R 3.6.1, PRSice for PRS construction, standard logistic regression and bootstrap for RERI/AP CIs.

What to verify first

  • Smoking-history field in the CFF Patient Registry has sufficient density and time resolution to support pack-year-equivalent computation.
  • CFRD diagnosis and age-at-onset are reliably captured across enrollment years.
  • A sample-size power calculation for additive-scale interaction effects at CFF cohort size (~5,000) given plausible effect-size ranges from biobank literature.
  • A switch from binary-outcome RERI/AP to a continuous-outcome additive-interaction metric (e.g., linear mixed-model interaction term with marginal-effect contrast across exposure strata) is defensible and documented.
  • The 2019 Nalls-style external-weights pattern transfers — i.e., a published CF-modifier GWAS summary-statistics set exists at sufficient size and quality to serve as the external weights for a CF-modifier PRS analog.

ALEX Welcome back. Today's first paper is Jacobs, Belete, Bestwick and colleagues, 2020, in the Journal of Neurology, Neurosurgery and Psychiatry — "Parkinson's disease determinants, prediction and gene-environment interactions in the UK Biobank." The first author is Benjamin Meir Jacobs at the Preventive Neurology Unit at Barts and the London. We're reading it because it is one of the cleanest published worked-examples of how to run a gene-by-environment interaction inside a half-million-person biobank — and the methods, the statistical scaffolding, and the way the authors frame what they could and couldn't conclude are exactly the moves a CFTR-modifier study would have to make if it ever attempts a CFTR-burden-by-smoking or CFTR-burden-by-diabetes analysis on lung-function trajectories.

JORDAN Hold on. Before we touch a word of this — I want every term in that cold open spelled out as if I have never heard it. Parkinson's I know. UK Biobank, gene-environment, polygenic risk score, all of those — go from the top.

ALEX Good. Parkinson's disease, abbreviated PD, is the second-most-common neurodegenerative disorder. You lose a particular set of brain cells in a region called the substantia nigra; the clinical picture is tremor, rigidity, slowed movement. By the time someone is diagnosed, a big chunk of those cells is already gone — which is why a lot of effort goes into pre-diagnostic risk prediction. The UK Biobank, abbreviated UKB or UKBB, is a prospective cohort of just over 500,000 UK adults aged 40 to 69 at recruitment between 2006 and 2010. Every participant has linked health records, blood samples, baseline questionnaires, and most have genotyping done. A genome-wide association study — GWAS for short — is the standard tool where you test millions of single-letter DNA variants one at a time against a disease, asking which letter is more common in cases than controls. A polygenic risk score, abbreviated PRS, is the next step after GWAS: you take all the variants the GWAS flagged, weight each one by the effect it had in the discovery study, sum them up for each new person in a different cohort, and you get a single number that's their genome-wide load of PD-favoring alleles. Gene-environment interaction — written GxE or G×E — is the question of whether the effect of an environmental exposure depends on someone's genotype. "Effect of diabetes on Parkinson's risk depends on background polygenic risk" is a G×E claim. PheWAS, phenome-wide association study, is the reverse of GWAS — one variant, many phenotypes — which is what Runyu's long-term project is trying to set up. FDR is false discovery rate, the convention researchers use to control how many of their hits are likely to be flukes when they test many things at once. OR is odds ratio — the ratio of the odds of disease in the exposed group to the odds in the unexposed group. CI is the confidence interval around that estimate. ICD codes are International Classification of Diseases codes — the standard hospital-administrative codes for diseases — and UKB phenotypes are largely derived by mapping someone's ICD codes plus their self-report into a yes/no for each condition. PREDICT-PD is the name of a pre-existing clinical risk algorithm that combines non-genetic risk factors to predict PD. It existed before this paper; the authors did not invent it.

JORDAN Okay. Now what's the question they're actually trying to answer?

ALEX Three connected questions. One: which environmental and prodromal — meaning early, pre-diagnostic — factors predict incident Parkinson's in UK Biobank? "Incident" is a term of art here. It means the diagnosis happened *after* the person enrolled. It contrasts with prevalent disease, which is already present at enrollment. Incident analyses are more powerful for causality because exposure measurement happens before disease appears, so reverse causation is reduced. Two: does a PRS, built from external PD GWAS weights, improve a prediction algorithm — specifically PREDICT-PD — beyond what the non-genetic factors give you? And three, this is the part that matters most for us: does the effect of any of these environmental risk factors depend on background polygenic risk? Before this paper there had been a couple of attempts to look at gene-by-environment interactions in PD, but always at the level of one named locus times one exposure — variant in MAPT times smoking, say. Almost no one had asked the same question with a genome-wide PRS as the "gene" side. The motivation for asking it is that the latest PD GWAS at the time had identified ninety independent signals collectively explaining roughly 16 percent of disease liability, and the modifiable exposures the field had catalogued each had small effects, so the combined gene-plus-environment models were leaving a lot of the missing risk unexplained. Interactions might be sitting in that gap.

JORDAN Walk me through how they actually did it.

ALEX They started with 1,276 incident PD cases and 500,406 controls in UKB. For environmental and prodromal factors, they ran a multivariable logistic regression — a regression where the outcome is yes/no PD and the predictors are an exposure plus age, sex, ethnicity and Townsend deprivation status, which is a UK area-level socioeconomic index. They computed the false discovery rate across all risk factors tested and called anything below 5 percent strongly associated, anything below 10 percent suggestively associated. Then for the genetic side they used external GWAS summary statistics from the 2019 Nalls PD GWAS — that's "external" meaning the weights were estimated in a different study, so UKB was not used to fit them, which prevents overfitting. They split UKB participants of European ancestry into a 30 percent training set and a 70 percent testing set, ran PRSice — a standard PRS construction tool — across multiple p-value thresholds in the training set to pick the threshold that maximized PD prediction, and then froze that score and applied it in the held-out testing set. The PRS was then plugged into a logistic model with the same covariates to estimate the odds ratio between the top and bottom risk deciles. For the gene-environment interactions, they restricted the analysis to the testing set — again to avoid overfitting — and looked at eight risk factors that had crossed the FDR 10 percent threshold in the marginal analysis. They tested interactions on both the multiplicative scale, which means an interaction term in a logistic regression, and the additive scale, which uses a quantity called the relative excess risk due to interaction, abbreviated RERI, converted into an attributable proportion, abbreviated AP. ALEX is now going to push back on one design choice: they used the PRS as a continuous variable for the interaction tests rather than a dichotomized high-versus-low split. That gains statistical power but it means the AP-with-95-percent-CI numbers they report are tied to a one-standard-deviation increase in the PRS, which is not how a clinician will ever read it. The reader has to translate.

JORDAN And the results — did they actually find what they were looking for?

ALEX Partly. The marginal associations replicated almost all of the textbook PD risk-factor literature. At FDR less than 5 percent: family history of PD, family history of dementia, non-smoking, low alcohol consumption, depression, daytime somnolence, epilepsy, and earlier age at menarche all came up. At FDR less than 10 percent: diabetes and history of peptic ulcer. The PRS performed well. Comparing the highest PRS decile to the lowest, the odds ratio was 3.37 with a 95 percent confidence interval of 2.41 to 4.70. A higher PRS was associated with earlier age at PD diagnosis. When they added the PRS into the PREDICT-PD algorithm, the model improved — but modestly. The headline gene-environment finding is the one we read this paper for. Of the eight environmental factors tested for interaction with the PRS, none survived the FDR 5 percent threshold for multiplicative interaction. One did show nominal evidence: diabetes. Negative multiplicative interaction, beta of negative 0.40, p equals 0.026. The additive analysis pointed the same way: attributable proportion of negative 0.39, 95 percent confidence interval negative 1.03 to negative 0.03. So the diabetes effect on PD risk was bigger in people with *low* PRS than in people with high PRS. The interpretation the authors offered is interesting: maybe diabetes is a separate, non-genetic pathway to PD risk, and so it adds proportionally more in people who don't already have heavy genetic risk; or maybe the finding reflects differential access to certain antidiabetic medications between genetic-risk strata. They are explicit that the finding is nominal, did not survive multiple testing, and needs replication.

JORDAN Okay — assuming Runyu wanted to do something similar in the CF data, what is the one concrete thing this paper is suggesting?

ALEX One possible direction: an additive-scale interaction analysis, using the RERI / attributable proportion framework, between a CFTR-variant burden score and a single environmental modifier — for example smoking pack-years, or diabetes status, or BMI category — on a CF outcome that is well-measured longitudinally in the CFF Patient Registry, such as FEV1 percent predicted at a fixed age or rate of FEV1 decline. The CFTR-variant-burden score on the left-hand side is the rough analog of the PRS in Jacobs — it would be a per-person quantitative summary of how severe their CFTR variant load is, perhaps weighted by published functional consequence categories from the CFTR2 database. Plain-English version: take the CFF registry, line up patients by their CFTR severity score on one axis and by an exposure like ever-smoking on the other, and ask whether the effect of smoking on lung function is the same in patients with mild versus severe CFTR genotypes, or whether the interaction term carries independent signal. Formal one-sentence version: "Among CFF Patient Registry participants with linked CFF Genome Project WGS, test whether the additive-scale interaction between CFTR-variant burden (continuous severity score) and an environmental exposure (smoking, BMI, or diabetes) contributes statistically significant additional information for FEV1 trajectory after adjusting for age, sex, F508del-homozygous status, and clinically established modifier-gene PRS where available." Frame this as one possible direction this episode suggests, not the chosen direction.

JORDAN And the gap, given that direction?

ALEX Given that proposal, the Jacobs paper closes three things and leaves three open. It closes the question of how to operationalize a gene-by-environment interaction inside a biobank with both linked health records and genotyping — the RERI and AP framework is fully spelled out with the code references, and the train-test-split for PRS overfitting is a clean template to copy. It closes the question of whether a continuous polygenic measure can interact with a single environmental covariate in a real cohort. And it closes the question of how to report a nominal, non-multiple-testing-surviving finding honestly, which the authors handle well. What it leaves open for Runyu: first, the sample size question — Jacobs had 1,276 incident PD cases against 500,000 controls and *still* got only nominal evidence for a single interaction. The CFF cohort with ~5,000 patients total is much smaller than Jacobs's case sample for the genetic side, and there is no equivalent of the 500,000 controls. So a transferable design would have to lean on within-cohort variation in the exposure, not case-versus-control contrast. Second, Jacobs uses incident PD; FEV1 decline in CF is not an incident binary outcome — it is a continuous longitudinal trajectory — so the statistical machinery would need to switch from logistic regression to a linear mixed model or survival model, and the RERI/AP definitions are derived for binary outcomes, so a different additive-scale interaction metric would have to be selected. Third, the exposure ascertainment in the CFF registry is variable in granularity for the modifiers Runyu might want — smoking pack-years, diabetes status, BMI trajectory — and what we'd need to check before committing is whether the registry captures these at sufficient resolution and time-resolved frequency to support an interaction test. Verification caveats: we'd need to confirm the registry's smoking-history fields are populated densely enough across years; we'd need to confirm CFRD diagnosis and onset age are reliably captured; we'd need a power calculation for additive-scale interaction effects at the CFF cohort size given a plausible effect-size range from biobank literature. None of these are showstoppers; they're prerequisites.

Today's one-line takeaway: a polygenic risk score and a single environmental exposure can have a real interaction worth reporting even when the multiplicative test doesn't quite cross the FDR threshold, but reporting requires both scales — additive and multiplicative — and an honest power-and-replication caveat.
