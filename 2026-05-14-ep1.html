Blurb

Sun and colleagues combined whole-exome sequencing in 392,814 UK Biobank participants with imputed genotypes from 260,405 FinnGen participants — 653,219 individuals total — to meta-analyze coding-variant associations across 744 disease endpoints. They identified 975 associations, with more than a third previously unreported, and showed that variants previously labeled as causing single-gene Mendelian disorders have measurable, sometimes pleiotropic, effects at the general-population level. Finland's bottleneck-driven rare-variant enrichment provided the statistical power to reach into the 0.1–2 percent allelic spectrum that classical GWAS misses. Proposed direction (conditional): invert the Sun design to ask whether heterozygous CFTR pathogenic-variant carriers show altered phecode-based phenotype risk in UKB / FinnGen / All of Us, in addition to the in-cohort CFGP CFTR PheWAS that remains the project's primary scientific deliverable. Open question: whether CFF Registry phenotypes can be mapped onto the phecode scaffold the Sun pipeline assumes.

Plain-English glossary

  • Bottleneck population (genetic): A population descended from a small founding group, in which rare variants drift to higher frequency than in the parent population (Finland, Iceland, Ashkenazi Jews).
  • ClinVar: Public database that catalogs human variants and their clinical significance.
  • Coding variant: A DNA change that falls inside the protein-coding part of a gene.
  • CWAS: Coding-variant-wide association study — the design Sun uses, intermediate between GWAS and pure sequencing studies.
  • Endpoint (disease): A defined clinical phenotype used as the outcome in an association analysis.
  • Exome / WES: The ~2% of the genome that codes for proteins; whole-exome sequencing reads just that part.
  • FinnGen: Finnish national biobank-and-EHR genomics project; ~260k participants in this paper.
  • GWAS: Genome-wide association study; tests millions of mostly common variants against one trait.
  • Imputation: Statistical inference of un-typed variant genotypes from a reference panel and the typed variants you do have.
  • In-frame indel: Insertion or deletion that doesn't shift the reading frame.
  • Inverse-variance weighted meta-analysis: Combining effect-size estimates across studies, weighting each by inverse variance.
  • MAF (minor allele frequency): Frequency of the less-common allele in the population.
  • Mendelian disease: Disease caused predominantly by mutation in a single gene; usually rare.
  • Missing heritability: The gap between heritability estimated from family resemblance and heritability explained by known variants.
  • OMIM: Online Mendelian Inheritance in Man — the reference catalog of single-gene disease genes.
  • PheWAS: Phenome-wide association study; tests one variant or gene against many phenotypes.
  • Phecode: A standardized phenotype code derived from grouping ICD-9 / ICD-10 codes into clinically meaningful disease categories.
  • pLOF: Predicted loss-of-function variant.
  • REGENIE: Software for biobank-scale association testing; handles relatedness via a whole-genome regression step.
  • Sentinel variant: The lead-significance variant at a region after linkage-disequilibrium-aware clumping.
  • UKB / UK Biobank: UK population study of ~500k participants with genotype and EHR linkage.
  • VEP: Variant Effect Predictor — Ensembl tool that annotates variants by predicted functional consequence.

Methods to lift

  • REGENIE v1.0.6.7 — biobank-scale association software with a two-step whole-genome-regression-then-test design that handles relatedness without subsetting unrelated individuals.
  • Inverse-variance weighted meta-analysis — combining effect-size Z-scores across UKB and FinnGen to gain power, especially in the 0.1–2% MAF band where allele-frequency divergence between Finland and the UK is greatest.
  • Phecode + ICD-10 phenotype harmonization — building a shared phenotype scaffold across two biobanks with different native EHR coding systems.
  • gnomAD v2 variant annotation — categorical loss-of-function / missense / start-lost / stop-gained labeling for filtering and stratification.
  • Variant Effect Predictor (VEP) with SIFT and PolyPhen — for additional canonical-transcript-level annotation beyond gnomAD's pre-computed categories.
  • PLINK-based LD clumping at r²=0.1, ±500 kb — for defining independent associated regions, with HLA excluded as a single block.
  • MR-Clust clustered Mendelian randomization — for resolving multiple causal mechanisms within a single risk-factor → outcome relationship.
  • Three-condition significance: P<2×10⁻⁹ in meta-analysis, concordant direction in both cohorts, and P<0.05 in each cohort individually.
  • ACMG actionable-gene cross-reference — mapping significant hits to the ACMG SF v3 list to identify medically actionable findings.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: invert the Sun 2022 design from a Mendelian-gene-PheWAS-in-affected-cohort framing to a carrier-PheWAS-in-general-biobank framing. Among unrelated UK Biobank European-ancestry participants, ascertain heterozygous carriers of pathogenic CFTR coding variants (CFTR2 / ClinVar–annotated) and test for altered risk across the harmonized phecode set; replicate in FinnGen using the same variant filter and phecode mapping. This is a complementary arm to — not a substitute for — the in-CFGP CFTR PheWAS, and exists because the in-CFGP design tests phenotype variation within CF patients while the carrier-PheWAS design tests whether one functional copy of CFTR lost has any detectable phenotype in the general population.

What to verify first

  • Whether CFF Registry phenotype fields can be cleanly mapped onto the standard phecode scaffold (the phecode system is built from general-population ICD codes, not CF-specific clinical fields).
  • Whether CFGP WGS variant calls are harmonized with gnomAD v2 annotations the Sun pipeline assumes, or whether re-annotation is needed.
  • Whether UKB / All of Us / FinnGen institutional governance allows a Runyu-led carrier-PheWAS arm in addition to the in-CFGP work — i.e., whether the data access and authorship pathways are compatible with the team's primary CFGP-anchored deliverable.
  • Whether the heterozygote-carrier signal-to-noise in UKB at ~1-in-25 European-ancestry carrier frequency is adequate for detecting CF-relevant comorbidities at the effect sizes typical of CFTR carrier biology (chronic pancreatitis, sinusitis, male infertility, CBAVD).
  • Whether the Sun internal-replication criterion (P<0.05 in both cohorts) is strict enough for a CFTR-carrier design with much smaller per-locus power; a stricter Bonferroni-corrected replication threshold may be needed.

ALEX Right. UKB.

JORDAN And FinnGen?

ALEX FinnGen is the Finnish equivalent. It's a partnership between Finnish biobanks, universities, and industry. Around 260,000 Finns at the time of this paper, with electronic health records linked to genotype data. Finland matters here because the Finnish population went through what geneticists call a bottleneck — a small founding population that grew in relative isolation — which means certain rare variants happen to be enriched at much higher frequency in Finns than in the rest of Europe. Some of them more than fifty times more common.

JORDAN What does that buy you?

ALEX Statistical power. If a variant is 0.04 percent in the UK but 3 percent in Finland, you have many more Finnish carriers to study, so you can detect what that variant does to disease risk much more sensitively. So Finland is essentially a natural experiment that lets you study some rare variants as if they were common.

JORDAN Other terms. Exome sequencing.

ALEX Whole-exome sequencing — WES — means sequencing the roughly two percent of the genome that codes for proteins, the exons. Cheaper than whole-genome sequencing, and you get most of the variants likely to break a protein. UKB did exomes on about 450,000 participants total; this paper uses 392,814 of them.

JORDAN GWAS, PheWAS.

ALEX GWAS is genome-wide association study — pick one disease, scan millions of common variants, see which are associated. PheWAS is phenome-wide association study — pick one variant or gene, scan many diseases at once, see what it does across the phenome. This paper is, in spirit, somewhere between the two — they call it a coding-variant-wide association study, but the structural design is closer to a PheWAS because they test the same variants against 744 disease endpoints in parallel.

JORDAN Minor allele frequency.

ALEX MAF — minor allele frequency — is the proportion of chromosomes in the population carrying the less-common version of a variant. Below one percent is "rare." Below 0.1 percent is "ultra-rare." Above five percent is "common." This paper specifically targets the gap between rare and common — the 0.1 to 2 percent band — where neither traditional GWAS nor pure sequencing studies have had much power.

ALEX All right. Background and purpose. The big picture in human genetics has been split for decades. On one side, Mendelian diseases — caused by single mutations in single genes, usually very rare, very damaging. Cystic fibrosis is the textbook example: one gene, CFTR, and a couple thousand pathogenic variants drive the disease. On the other side, complex diseases — diabetes, hypertension, heart disease — where hundreds of common variants each push risk slightly up or slightly down.

JORDAN And those are studied with different methods.

ALEX Right. Mendelian genetics uses families and rare-variant sequencing. Complex-trait genetics uses GWAS, which is great at finding common variants but loses power dramatically as variants get rarer. There's a gap in between — variants that are too rare for GWAS to find reliably and not severe enough to be classified as Mendelian — and that gap is hypothesized to hold a lot of the so-called missing heritability.

JORDAN Missing heritability?

ALEX The gap between how heritable a trait is — how much identical twins resemble each other — and how much of that heritability you can actually explain with the variants you've found in your GWAS. For many complex traits, GWAS explains only a fraction. Where's the rest? One hypothesis is that it's in that 0.1 to 2 percent allelic spectrum.

JORDAN So this paper is trying to fill in that gap.

ALEX Exactly. By combining UKB exome data with FinnGen imputed genotypes, they get 653,219 people in their meta-analysis. Bigger than either cohort alone. And by using FinnGen specifically, they get to lean on Finland's enriched rare variants. Their stated motivation is to bridge the rare-versus-common divide and produce a compendium of coding-variant disease associations — for biology, but explicitly also for drug discovery. The whole final third of the paper is about medical actionability, ACMG actionable genes, and proposing carriers who might benefit from screening. Biogen is the funding sponsor here, so that's not incidental — drug-target validation is the commercial logic behind this work.

ALEX Methods. They harmonize phenotypes across the two biobanks using phecodes and ICD-10 mappings. They keep 744 specific endpoints clustered into 580 disease groups. They run REGENIE for the association tests in UKB.

JORDAN REGENIE?

ALEX REGENIE is a fast association-testing tool that handles binary case-control traits, accounts for relatedness between participants using a whole-genome regression model in step one, then runs the actual variant-level tests in step two. It's the modern standard for biobank-scale binary GWAS, alongside SAIGE which we covered earlier.

JORDAN And they meta-analyze across the two biobanks?

ALEX Inverse-variance weighted meta-analysis, which is the standard way to combine effect-size estimates from independent studies. They use gnomAD v2 annotations for variant categories — loss-of-function, missense, in-frame indel, stop-gained, et cetera — and they restrict their tests to coding variants with reasonable imputation quality.

JORDAN Significance threshold?

ALEX Three conditions, all required. First, P less than 2×10⁻⁹ — that's their genome-wide-like Bonferroni-corrected threshold. Second, concordant direction of effect in UKB and FinnGen. Third, P less than 0.05 in both biobanks individually. The combination matters because it means every association passes a discovery threshold and an internal-replication threshold simultaneously.

ALEX That's actually one place I want to push back. Their internal-replication-at-P-less-than-0.05 criterion is much weaker than a Bonferroni-corrected replication. With 318 to 479 sentinel variants you'd expect dozens to pass nominal significance just by chance. They acknowledge it implicitly — they always quote the meta-analysis P-value — but the design rewards consistency-of-direction more than it punishes false positives.

JORDAN Fair. What did they find?

ALEX Results. They identified 975 distinct associations, more than a third of them previously unreported. Of the 534 unique variants underlying those associations, 152 had been previously listed in ClinVar — the public clinical-variant database — but only seven of those had been formally annotated as pathogenic for the matching disease. So a big chunk of what they're calling new is actually variants that had been seen before, possibly even flagged as concerning, but never connected to a real population-level disease risk.

JORDAN Pleiotropy?

ALEX Lots. They give example after example. CHEK2 — a known breast-cancer gene — also associates with colorectal cancer, thyroid cancer, uterine fibroids, ovarian cysts, and multiple lymphoid and myeloid cancers when you actually scan systematically. That's the PheWAS dividend in action — once you test against the whole phenome, the single-disease framing of a gene fragments.

JORDAN Any specific numbers worth pinning down?

ALEX A few. The MYBPC3 variant rs370890951 — MYBPC3 is the cardiomyopathy gene — turns out to be associated with a roughly threefold risk of coagulation defects, P=9.8×10⁻¹³, in carriers. A DBH missense variant enriched fifty-fold in Finland — DBH causes orthostatic hypotension as a Mendelian disease — associates with reduced hypertension at P=1.3×10⁻²³. A CHEK2 frameshift deletion rs555607708 associates with myeloid and lymphoid leukemias and changes platelet volume and lymphocyte counts. The pattern across all of these is that mutations geneticists had thought of as private to single rare diseases are turning out to have measurable, sometimes counterintuitive effects at the population level.

ALEX Did they answer the question they posed? I'd say partly. They wanted to bridge rare and common variant studies. They did, in the 0.1 to 2 percent band — 145 of the 975 associations are in that band. But the gain is concentrated in genes where there's already a clinical phenotype to compare against.
