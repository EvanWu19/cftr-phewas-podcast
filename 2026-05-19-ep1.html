Blurb

Sinnott-Armstrong, Tanigawa, and colleagues 2021 (Nature Genetics) ran a harmonized pipeline across 35 routinely-measured blood and urine biomarkers in 363,228 unrelated UK Biobank participants, layering common-variant fine-mapping, protein-altering and copy-number-variant burden testing, HLA Bayesian model averaging, Mendelian randomization for causal inference, and multi-biomarker polygenic risk scores. They identified 1,857 biomarker-associated loci with 3,374 fine-mapped causal variants, nominated 51 causal biomarker → disease relationships via Mendelian randomization, and showed that combining 35 biomarker PRSs improves disease prediction for chronic kidney disease, type 2 diabetes, gout, alcoholic cirrhosis, and heart failure over single-disease PRSs alone — replicated in FinnGen R3 at n=135,500. Proposed direction (conditional) for the CFTR PheWAS: apply the biomarker-anchored architecture to CFFPR-linked CFGP whole-genome sequencing using routinely-recorded CF lab variables (sweat chloride, fecal elastase, FEV1 % predicted, BMI z-score, HbA1c, ALT) as biomarker anchors, with imported externally-built biomarker PRSs as the realistic-power version of the multi-PRS combination test. Open question: whether the architecture transfers to a five-thousand-person single-disease cohort where collider bias on CFTR loss-of-function status compromises the multi-PRS combination test.

Plain-English glossary

  • Biomarker: a routinely-measured lab value (e.g., total cholesterol, creatinine, urate, microalbumin) used clinically to monitor disease.
  • CNV (copy-number variant): a chunk of DNA duplicated or deleted, not a single-letter change.
  • FINEMAP: a fine-mapping tool that assigns each variant a posterior probability of being the true causal one rather than just LD-tagged.
  • FinnGen: the Finnish national biobank; ~135,500 participants by R3, used here as an independent replication cohort.
  • GWAS (genome-wide association study): testing millions of common variants for trait association across thousands-to-hundreds-of-thousands of people.
  • Heritability (SNP): the proportion of trait variation attributable to common-SNP genetic differences in the studied population.
  • HESS: Heritability Estimator from Summary Statistics; one of two SNP-heritability methods used here.
  • HLA region: a dense immune-system gene cluster on chromosome 6 with extreme polymorphism; analyzed separately.
  • LD Score regression: SNP-heritability estimator and population-structure diagnostic; LD-score intercept near 1 indicates well-controlled stratification.
  • Mendelian randomization (MR): causal inference design using inherited variants as instruments for biomarker → disease relationships.
  • MAF (minor allele frequency): the population frequency of the less-common allele; "rare" is <1%, "ultra-rare" <0.1%.
  • PAV (protein-altering variant): a single amino-acid substitution variant; sometimes harmful, sometimes benign in practice.
  • Pleiotropy: one gene affecting multiple traits or diseases.
  • PRS (polygenic risk score): weighted sum of many small-effect variant contributions, predicting genetic predisposition for a trait.
  • PTV (protein-truncating variant): a stop codon, frameshift, or splice disruption that destroys a protein; almost always loss-of-function.
  • SNP: single nucleotide polymorphism, a one-letter DNA difference.
  • snpnet / BASIL: the iterative-lasso polygenic-prediction package used here to build the per-biomarker PRSs.
  • UKBB (UK Biobank): ~500,000-participant UK research cohort with genome-wide genotyping and deep health data.

Methods to lift

  • Harmonized biomarker-level GWAS pipeline across many quantitative lab traits in one cohort, stratified by ancestry and meta-analyzed.
  • LD Score regression and HESS for SNP-heritability estimation across continuous biomarkers.
  • FINEMAP fine-mapping with posterior-probability credible-set construction at biomarker-associated loci.
  • Gene-level CNV burden testing across ~23,598 genes against quantitative biomarker phenotypes.
  • Bayesian model averaging for HLA allelotype association testing (handles the dense LD structure of the MHC region).
  • Two-sample Mendelian randomization across 35 biomarkers × 40 medically-relevant phenotypes to nominate causal relationships.
  • snpnet / BASIL iterative-lasso polygenic risk score construction in a held-out training/validation/test split.
  • Multi-PRS combination via multiple regression with biomarker PRSs as predictors plus the trait-specific PRS, evaluated for AUC and hazard ratio improvement in UKB and FinnGen.
  • Pre-treatment historical-balance / sensitivity testing across ancestry strata (predictive performance evaluated in held-out White British plus four additional ancestry groups).

ALEX Good morning Jordan. Today's paper is Sinnott-Armstrong, Tanigawa, and colleagues, 2021, in Nature Genetics, titled "Genetics of 35 blood and urine biomarkers in the UK Biobank." It's a sprawling biobank-methods paper from a Stanford-led group, with replication in the Finnish biobank FinnGen.

JORDAN Why are we reading it for a cystic fibrosis project?

ALEX Because it's the cleanest published template for turning routine lab tests into a layered genetic study — from common variants down to copy-number variants, through Mendelian randomization, all the way out to polygenic risk scores. If we ever want to use sweat chloride or other CF labs as quantitative phenotypes, this is the playbook.

JORDAN Before we go in, can we slow down on terms? Several things in that sentence I don't have yet.

ALEX Yes, let's prime the language first. A biomarker, in this paper, is just a routinely measured lab value — total cholesterol, creatinine, urate, that kind of thing. UK Biobank, abbreviated UKB or UKBB, is a UK research cohort of about half a million adults with both genome-wide genotyping and detailed health data; it's the world's most heavily mined biobank. FinnGen is the Finnish equivalent, smaller but genetically distinctive because Finland has population bottleneck history.

JORDAN What about the variant types you listed?

ALEX Three categories matter today. A single nucleotide polymorphism, or SNP, is a one-letter DNA difference. A protein-truncating variant, abbreviated PTV, is a SNP or small insertion-deletion that cuts a protein short — a stop codon early, a frameshift, a splice disruption. PTVs usually destroy the protein. A protein-altering variant, abbreviated PAV, is one letter changed that swaps an amino acid — sometimes harmful, sometimes silent in practice. A copy-number variant, abbreviated CNV, is a chunk of DNA that's duplicated or deleted, not just a single letter.

JORDAN And the human leukocyte antigen thing?

ALEX The HLA region on chromosome six is a dense cluster of immune-system genes. It's the most variable region in our genome, the place that drives transplant matching and a huge fraction of autoimmune-disease risk. It's a separate analysis arm here because the variant types don't behave like ordinary SNPs.

JORDAN One more. What's a polygenic risk score?

ALEX A polygenic risk score, or PRS, adds up the small effects of many common variants across the genome into a single number for each person, predicting their genetic predisposition for a trait. Think of it as a weighted DNA sum for cholesterol or kidney function or diabetes.

JORDAN And Mendelian randomization?

ALEX Mendelian randomization, MR, uses inherited genetic variants as natural-experiment instruments to test whether a biomarker actually causes a disease, rather than just correlating with it. The logic: your genotype was set at conception, before any of the diseases existed, so it can't be reverse-caused by disease.

JORDAN Okay, I have the vocabulary. What was the big-picture motivation?

ALEX Clinicians order lab panels every day — lipids, kidney function, liver enzymes, blood counts, hormones, glucose markers — to monitor disease. But the genetic architecture of these lab values, especially their causal relationships with diseases, has been studied piecewise rather than uniformly. Some traits like LDL cholesterol had been mined extensively; others, like rheumatoid factor or microalbumin in urine, almost not at all. The authors saw an opportunity. UK Biobank had measured more than thirty lab biomarkers on hundreds of thousands of people with full genotype data. Run one harmonized pipeline on all of them, then mine the results in a uniform way: where are the rare protein-altering hits with big effects? Which biomarkers causally influence which diseases? Can you use multiple biomarker PRSs together to predict disease better than any single PRS alone?

JORDAN So they're treating it as a methods showcase as much as a biology paper.

ALEX Right. Almost every analysis arm here — fine-mapping, MR, multi-PRS — is portable to any other lab measurement on any other cohort. For us, that includes sweat chloride, fecal elastase, FEV1, BMI z-score, the whole CF lab panel.

JORDAN One more piece of context. Why thirty-five biomarkers specifically?

ALEX Two reasons. Biological coverage — thirty-five spans the major organ systems clinicians care about: lipids, glycemic control, kidney function, liver function, hormones, bone metabolism, blood-cell counts. And statistical machinery — to build a multi-biomarker PRS model that improves prediction of complex diseases like type 2 diabetes or chronic kidney disease, you need many biomarker PRSs as candidate predictors, then let regression pick which ones add information. Five wouldn't be enough; thirty-five gives the model enough features to discover combinations.

JORDAN And the heritability numbers — that's how much of the variation in each lab value comes from genetics versus environment?

ALEX Exactly. Heritability is the proportion of variation in a trait attributable to genetic differences in the population studied. They report SNP-heritability using two methods called LD Score regression and HESS. The estimates span a huge range across the thirty-five biomarkers, from under one percent for lipoprotein-A on one method to almost sixty percent for total bilirubin on the other. A small handful had unusually concentrated genetic architecture — lipoprotein-A, total bilirubin, and direct bilirubin all had more than half their SNP-heritability coming from just the top one percent of loci. Most others were broadly polygenic — heritability spread across thousands of small-effect variants.

JORDAN How did they set up the actual analysis?

ALEX The substrate is 363,228 unrelated UK Biobank participants. They split into five ancestry strata — White British, non-British White, African, South Asian, East Asian — and analyzed each separately before meta-analyzing the first four. East Asian, just 1,082 people, was too small to pool reliably. They then ran association testing across three variant bins: protein-truncating, protein-altering, and synonymous-or-non-coding SNPs above a minor allele frequency of 0.1 percent.

JORDAN What does minor allele frequency mean?

ALEX How common the rarer of two variant alleles is in the population. Above one percent we call it common, below one percent rare, and below 0.1 percent ultra-rare. Most large biobank studies set 0.1 percent as the lower limit for variant-by-variant testing.

JORDAN Got it.

ALEX Beyond SNP testing they also ran CNV burden tests across 23,598 genes, and HLA allele tests in a Bayesian framework that handled the dense local linkage between HLA variants. To prioritize causal SNPs at significant loci, they applied a fine-mapping tool called FINEMAP, which scans each region and assigns each variant a posterior probability of being the true causal one rather than just along for the ride with a real signal.

JORDAN Push back on one design choice for me.

ALEX Honest pushback: the ancestry-stratified meta-analysis is appropriate, but the African subcohort here is 6,019 people. That's vastly underpowered relative to White British at 318,953. So while the headline analysis is technically multi-ancestry, the discovery is still essentially European in practice. Their own results will show European-built PRSs transferring poorly across populations, which is exactly the topic of our next episode.

JORDAN What did they actually find?

ALEX At the SNP level, more than ten thousand significant biomarker associations, 1,857 loci with at least one trait, and 3,374 fine-mapped variants with greater than ninety-nine percent posterior probability of being causal. Outside the HLA region they found 58 protein-truncating variant associations and 1,323 protein-altering associations with at least one biomarker. A few headliners: a stop-gain variant in ANGPTL8 lowering triglycerides; two protein-truncating variants in LPA lowering lipoprotein-A with downstream protection against coronary artery disease; a frameshift in SLC22A2 strongly lowering estimated glomerular filtration rate and raising creatinine; and a protein-truncating variant in COL4A4 strongly increasing urinary microalbumin and increasing kidney disease risk with odds ratio of nearly seven.

JORDAN Those are giant effects for rare variants.

ALEX That's the point. Rare variants of big effect are what tell you which gene is causal at a locus. SLC22A2 is in the solute-carrier family — the same family as SLC26A9 and SLC6A14, which we've catalogued as CF modifier loci. The HNF1B copy-number-variant signal across urea, eGFR, creatinine, and cystatin C is a reminder that structural variants can matter more than the SNP catalog suggests. They also found a pleiotropic locus at HGFAC, where one variant influences lipids, IGF-1, albumin, and calcium simultaneously.

JORDAN And the causal-inference layer?

ALEX Their Mendelian randomization confirmed previously-known relationships — urate causing gout, cystatin C causing stroke — and added some novel ones, like alanine aminotransferase causally raising diabetes risk and testosterone causally protecting against inflammatory bowel disease. Out of 35 biomarkers crossed against 40 diseases they nominate 51 causal relationships.

JORDAN And the headline multi-PRS story?

ALEX The multi-biomarker PRS model improved disease prediction for chronic kidney disease, type 2 diabetes, gout, alcoholic cirrhosis, and heart failure, both in UK Biobank and replicated in FinnGen at 135,500 participants. For chronic kidney disease, the single-disease PRS had a hazard ratio not significantly different from one — essentially no risk discrimination. The multi-biomarker PRS jumped the hazard ratio to 1.12 with a p-value near ten-to-the-minus-ten. For heart failure the single-disease PRS was 1.01 while the multi-biomarker version reached 1.08. For alcoholic cirrhosis, the single-disease PRS was 0.97 with a non-significant p-value, while the multi-biomarker version reached 1.18.

JORDAN Did they answer their core question?

ALEX Yes. Biomarkers are genetically structured, those genetics point toward causal disease pathways, and combining many biomarker PRSs together gives you better disease prediction than a disease-specific PRS alone for several common diseases. The FinnGen replication is the most credible part of the whole paper.

JORDAN What direction does this paper suggest for Runyu's CFTR PheWAS?

ALEX One specific possibility, plain English first. The CF Foundation Patient Registry — CFFPR — already records on every patient a panel of laboratory measurements that look just like biomarkers: sweat chloride, fecal elastase, FEV1 percent predicted, BMI z-score, hemoglobin A1c for CFRD screening, alanine aminotransferase for liver involvement. The Sinnott-Armstrong template — biomarker-level GWAS, then Mendelian randomization to test which biomarkers causally drive which CF complications, then a multi-biomarker PRS for individual outcomes — is directly portable to the CF setting.

JORDAN Can you state that formally?

ALEX Formal version: build harmonized biomarker-level GWAS for each routinely-measured CFFPR laboratory variable in the registry linked to the CF Genome Project whole-genome sequencing substrate, then construct multi-biomarker polygenic risk scores and test whether combining them improves prediction of pre-specified CF clinical endpoints — chronic Pseudomonas acquisition age, CFRD onset, FEV1 trajectory, BMI trajectory, CF-related liver disease — over single-endpoint PRSs alone. This is one possible direction the episode suggests; it is not a committed research design.

JORDAN What does the paper close, and what does it leave open for that proposal?

ALEX The paper closes the methodological feasibility — biomarker-level GWAS, fine-mapping, MR, multi-PRS combination are all demonstrated workable end-to-end at biobank scale and replicable in a smaller cohort. It closes the question of whether biomarker PRSs add predictive value over disease PRSs alone — yes, for several common cardiometabolic diseases. It leaves open whether the same architecture transfers down to a five-thousand-person cohort — the FinnGen replication was 135,500, twenty-seven times larger than the CF cohort.

JORDAN And on our verification side?

ALEX What we'd need to check, framed as questions and not facts: whether CFFPR captures enough longitudinal lab measurements per patient to derive stable biomarker phenotypes the way UK Biobank does; whether CF Genome Project whole-genome sequencing on roughly five thousand patients gives any reasonable single-variant GWAS power at the biomarker level; whether the multi-PRS combination logic survives the very different case-mix in a single-disease cohort where everyone has CFTR loss already; whether public CF-relevant biomarker GWAS sumstats exist outside the CF cohort to seed external-weight PRSs; whether the ancestry composition of the CFFPR-linked CF Genome Project cohort needs ancestry-aware analysis from the start. None of these are blockers, but each is a step that has to come before a proposal commitment.

JORDAN If the CF biomarker GWAS is underpowered for discovery, can we still use the multi-PRS idea?

ALEX Possibly, by importing externally-built biomarker PRSs and only testing the combination step inside the CF cohort. Train each biomarker PRS in UK Biobank or FinnGen, freeze the weights, score every CF Genome Project patient on each frozen biomarker PRS, then ask whether a regression combining multiple biomarker PRSs predicts CF endpoints better than the single closest-trait PRS. Power requirements for the combination test are far smaller than for discovery.

JORDAN Anything else worth flagging for verification?

ALEX Collider bias. Every CF Genome Project patient already has CFTR loss of function, which influences many of the same biomarkers Sinnott-Armstrong studied — bilirubin via liver involvement, glucose via CFRD, creatinine and cystatin C via long-term aminoglycoside antibiotics. Conditioning on having CF risks inducing spurious associations between biomarker PRSs and CF endpoints that don't reflect causal biology. Any concrete proposal would need a pre-registered collider-bias check.

JORDAN Tomorrow's tee-up?

ALEX Tomorrow we'll read Cavazos and Witte 2021, on whether including variants discovered in non-European populations rescues the ancestry-portability problem that this paper's own PRS results display. It's the natural companion piece.

JORDAN One-line takeaway for today?

ALEX When you have a deeply-phenotyped cohort with routine lab values, biomarkers are the anchor — uniform biomarker GWAS plus Mendelian randomization plus multi-biomarker PRSs is the most scalable path from genotype to disease prediction.
