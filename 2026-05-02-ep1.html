Blurb

Karczewski et al. (Nature 2020) assemble the gnomAD v2 reference dataset — 125,748 exomes plus 15,708 genomes, 141,456 individuals total — and use it to build a continuous, confidence-bounded constraint score (LOEUF) across every protein-coding gene. Roughly 3,063 genes show essentially no predicted loss-of-function variants (LOEUF < 0.35) and behave as a high-quality "essential / haploinsufficient" set. For the CFTR PheWAS side-project, this paper functions as infrastructure: every CFF whole-genome-sequencing variant should be annotated against gnomAD population frequencies, and every gene against its LOEUF score, before any gene-burden testing. CFTR itself, as an autosomal-recessive gene, is expected to (and does) sit near the genome-wide median of LOEUF — a feature of the metric, not a bug.

Plain-English glossary

  • Constraint — the gap between the number of pLOF variants observed in a gene and the number expected under no selection. High constraint means natural selection has been removing pLOF variants from the population.
  • ExAC — Exome Aggregation Consortium, the ~60,000-person predecessor to gnomAD; introduced the pLI metric in 2016.
  • Exome — the protein-coding 1-2% of the genome; cheaper to sequence than a whole genome.
  • gnomAD — Genome Aggregation Database; the largest publicly available reference catalogue of human genetic variation, cataloguing what variants exist in the general population and at what frequency.
  • Haploinsufficient — describes a gene where one broken copy is enough to cause disease, because a single functional copy cannot do the job.
  • LOEUF — Loss-of-function Observed/Expected Upper-bound Fraction; the constraint metric introduced by this paper. Observed pLOF count divided by expected pLOF count, with a 90% confidence interval, reporting the upper bound. Smaller LOEUF = more constrained.
  • LOFTEE — Loss-Of-Function Transcript Effect Estimator; the quality-control filter that flags high-confidence pLOF variants and removes likely false positives (e.g., terminal truncations, rescued splice sites).
  • MAF — Minor Allele Frequency; the fraction of chromosomes in a population carrying the rarer of two variants at a position. Common = MAF >1%, rare = <1%, ultra-rare = <0.1%.
  • Methylation — a chemical modification of cytosine bases; methylated cytosines mutate roughly 10x faster than unmethylated sites and the mutation-rate model accounts for this.
  • Missense variant — a single-letter change that swaps one amino acid for another in the resulting protein. May or may not affect function.
  • Mutation rate — how often a given DNA letter mutates per generation; depends on local sequence context (the letter and its two neighbors) and methylation status.
  • Neutral expectation — how many variants would be expected in a stretch of DNA if no selection were acting; computed from mutation rate and sequence context.
  • pLI — probability of being Loss-of-function Intolerant; ExAC-era binary-ish constraint metric, superseded (but not replaced) by LOEUF.
  • pLOF — predicted Loss-Of-Function variant; a variant likely to break the protein. Three categories: stop-gained (premature STOP), frameshift (insertions/deletions that scramble the reading frame), and essential splice-site variants (disrupt RNA splicing).
  • Synonymous variant — a single-letter change that, due to redundancy in the genetic code, leaves the amino acid unchanged. Treated as approximately neutral and used to calibrate the mutation-rate model.

Methods to lift

  • gnomAD v4 constraint table (per-gene LOEUF, observed/expected per consequence class) — download from gnomad.broadinstitute.org; default to canonical-transcript values.
  • LOFTEE VEP plugin for high-confidence pLOF annotation — apply as a filter for genome-wide gene-set tests; on CFTR specifically, treat LOFTEE as advisory rather than as a hard filter to avoid losing potentially functional C-terminal disruptions.
  • Decile stratification — pre-register that "constrained" = LOEUF < 0.35 (top decile-equivalent) and "tolerant" = bottom decile; report enrichment within strata, not pooled across the genome.
  • Synonymous calibration check — replicate the paper's r ≈ 0.979 expected-vs-observed synonymous correlation as a QC check on CFF variant calling.
  • Population-frequency annotation — annotate every CFF WGS variant with gnomAD allele frequency stratified by ancestry subset.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Annotate every CFF whole-genome-sequencing variant against gnomAD v4 population allele frequency, and every gene against its gnomAD LOEUF score, before any rare-variant burden analysis is run. Pre-stratify gene-level tests by LOEUF decile (or by the LOEUF < 0.35 threshold) and report results within strata so that baseline constraint heterogeneity is not confused with disease-association signal (Karczewski et al., Nature 2020).

ALEX Morning, Runyu. Today we are reading the gnomAD v2 paper — Karczewski and colleagues, Nature 2020, "The mutational constraint spectrum quantified from variation in 141,456 humans." This is the paper that gave the field its standard reference catalogue of human DNA variation and a per-gene score called LOEUF. If you only ever read one population-genetics methods paper, this is the one.

JORDAN 141,456 humans. That is an oddly specific number.

ALEX That is the whole sample size. We will unpack what that number is doing for us in a moment.

JORDAN Before we get into it, can you set up the vocabulary? I keep hearing these words and I want clean definitions.

ALEX Yes, let me lay them out one at a time. First, gnomAD. That stands for Genome Aggregation Database. It is the largest publicly available reference catalogue of human genetic variation — basically a giant table that says "out of about 141,000 mostly healthy people we sequenced, here are all the DNA letters where some of them differ from the reference, and here is how often each variant shows up."

JORDAN So it is a frequency lookup.

ALEX Exactly. Next, MAF — minor allele frequency. At any position in the genome where there is variation, you have a more common letter and a rarer letter. MAF is just the fraction of chromosomes carrying the rarer letter. Common variants have MAF above 1 percent, rare variants are below 1 percent, ultra-rare are below 0.1 percent.

JORDAN Got it. What is pLOF?

ALEX Predicted loss-of-function. These are variants that we think break the protein the gene codes for. Three flavors. Stop-gained, where a single letter change creates an early STOP signal so the protein is truncated. Frameshift, where insertions or deletions knock the reading frame out of register so everything downstream is garbage. And essential splice-site variants, which scramble the cut-and-paste of RNA so the mature message is wrong. Add those three together and you get pLOF — predicted loss-of-function.

JORDAN And missense, synonymous?

ALEX Missense — a single letter change that swaps one amino acid for another in the protein. Might matter, might not. Synonymous — a single letter change that, because of redundancy in the genetic code, leaves the amino acid identical. Usually treated as silent.

JORDAN Mutation rate?

ALEX How often a given DNA letter mutates per generation. Crucially, the rate depends on context — what letters are next door, and whether the site is methylated. Methylated cytosines mutate roughly ten times faster than other sites.

JORDAN Neutral expectation?

ALEX If a stretch of DNA were under no selection at all — nothing penalizing or rewarding mutations there — how many variants would we expect to see in a sample of 125,000 people? You compute that from the mutation rate and the sequence context. That is the neutral expectation.

JORDAN And constraint is the gap between observed and expected.

ALEX Right. If a gene is essential, natural selection has been quietly removing pLOF variants in it for thousands of generations, so we see fewer than the neutral expectation. High constraint equals strong selection against breaking that gene. Low constraint means the gene tolerates being broken without fitness consequence.

JORDAN Haploinsufficient?

ALEX One broken copy is enough to cause disease. We have two copies of every gene. Haploinsufficient genes cannot do their job with just one functional copy, so a single pLOF variant produces a phenotype.

JORDAN LOEUF and LOFTEE.

ALEX LOEUF — loss-of-function observed/expected upper-bound fraction. It is just observed pLOF count divided by expected pLOF count, with a confidence interval, and you report the upper bound of that interval. Smaller LOEUF means more constrained. LOFTEE — Loss-of-Function Transcript Effect Estimator — is the quality-control filter that flags which pLOF calls are high-confidence versus likely false positives.

JORDAN OK. With that vocabulary, what was the question this paper set out to answer?

ALEX Background first. There was a predecessor dataset called ExAC — Exome Aggregation Consortium — about 60,000 people, published in 2016. ExAC introduced the idea of constraint scores per gene, including a metric called pLI, the probability of being loss-of-function intolerant. pLI essentially asks "is this gene in the haploinsufficient class — yes or no — with what posterior probability?" That was useful, but at 60,000 people the confidence intervals on observed-versus-expected pLOF for any given gene were wide. Many genes were too small or too rare in observation to classify confidently.

JORDAN So you could not really tell if a gene was constrained or just under-sampled.

ALEX Exactly. And there was a deeper conceptual issue. pLI is binary-ish — a gene is haploinsufficient or it is not. But intuition and biology both say constraint is continuous. Some genes are extremely intolerant, some are mildly intolerant, some are completely permissive, and there is a smooth gradient between them. ExAC could not really resolve that gradient.

JORDAN So the question for this paper is —

ALEX Two parts. First, can we more than double the sample size to about 140,000 people? Second, with that bigger sample, can we build a precise, continuous constraint score across every protein-coding gene in the human genome — about 19,704 genes — and have it be reliable enough that downstream studies can use it as a prior?

JORDAN Why does that matter for medical genetics?

ALEX Because every clinical lab interpreting a patient variant wants to know "is this gene one where breaking it is plausible disease-causing?" Constraint is the cleanest population-genetic answer. And every rare-variant association study wants to know "which genes should I prioritize, and which should I down-weight?" Same answer. So a high-quality constraint table becomes infrastructure — every later paper consumes it.

JORDAN A reference resource, not a one-off finding.

ALEX Exactly. The deliverable is a table, not a discovery.

JORDAN Walk me through how they did it.

ALEX Four pieces. Piece one, data assembly. They aggregated 199,558 exomes — exome means just the protein-coding 1 to 2 percent of the genome — and 20,314 whole-genome sequences. After harmonizing across studies, removing duplicates, removing samples failing quality control, they had a final analysis set of 125,748 exomes and 15,708 genomes. Total 141,456 individuals. Harmonization is non-trivial — different studies used different sequencing platforms, different variant callers, different reference builds. They re-processed everything through a single pipeline.

JORDAN Piece two.

ALEX The mutation-rate model. For every possible single-letter change in the genome, they estimated a baseline mutation rate using sequence context — the letter and its two neighbors — and methylation status. That gives them, per gene, an expected count of synonymous variants, missense variants, and pLOF variants under the assumption of no selection.

JORDAN How do they know the model is calibrated?

ALEX They use synonymous variants as the calibration target, because synonymous changes are presumed near-neutral. Predicted-versus-observed synonymous correlates at r equals 0.979. That is a tight calibration check. If the model gets synonymous right, you trust it on pLOF.

JORDAN Piece three.

ALEX LOFTEE. Predicted loss-of-function annotations from off-the-shelf tools are notoriously dirty. Lots of "stop-gained" variants are actually in the last exon and the protein still works. Lots of "essential splice" variants are rescued by alternative splicing. LOFTEE applies stringent first-principles filters — removes terminal truncations, removes rescued splice sites, keeps the variants that should genuinely cause nonsense-mediated decay. Applying LOFTEE leaves them with 443,769 high-confidence pLOF variants.

JORDAN Piece four.

ALEX LOEUF computation. Per gene, observed high-confidence pLOF count divided by expected pLOF count from the mutation model. Compute a 90 percent confidence interval. Report the upper bound. That is LOEUF. Smaller equals more constrained.

JORDAN And the result?

ALEX A complete constraint spectrum across every protein-coding gene. They bin genes into deciles of LOEUF — about 1,920 genes per bin. The first decile is the most constrained 10 percent of genes. They identify roughly 3,063 genes where pLOF variants are essentially absent at LOEUF below 0.35 — these are the genes where breaking even one copy is so deleterious that natural selection has cleared pLOF carriers.

JORDAN Is that the haploinsufficient list?

ALEX It overlaps strongly with known haploinsufficient genes — those are concentrated in the top decile, validating the metric. Olfactory receptor genes, which we already know are tolerant of disruption — most of us have dozens of broken olfactory receptors with no consequence — fall in the bottom, permissive deciles. Autosomal recessive genes fall in the middle, because selection on AR genes acts on homozygotes, not heterozygotes, so the per-allele depletion is weaker.

JORDAN Where does CFTR sit?

ALEX CFTR is an AR gene, so the prediction is middle of the distribution. The supplementary constraint table confirms that — CFTR's LOEUF is roughly around the genome-wide median, not in the top constrained decile. That is the right answer biologically. CFTR carriers — heterozygotes for one pLOF — are clinically silent. Selection only fires when you inherit two broken copies.

JORDAN Did they answer their question?

ALEX Yes. They built the constraint resource. Today, gnomAD constraint is THE standard population-genetic prior for variant interpretation. Every clinical lab consults it. Every rare-variant association study uses it. The paper has been cited many thousands of times because the table itself is the deliverable.

JORDAN So how do we use this for our project?

ALEX Plain English first. Before we run any rare-variant analysis on the CFF whole-genome sequencing data, we should annotate every variant with two things — its frequency in gnomAD across the general population, and its gene's LOEUF score. Those two annotations tell us, first, whether a variant is genuinely rare or merely an artifact of CFF being a small isolated cohort, and second, whether the gene the variant sits in is one where pLOF actually matters or one where it is biologically tolerated. Without those annotations, we cannot interpret a burden test.

JORDAN Formal version for the proposal.

ALEX Annotate every CFF WGS variant against gnomAD v4 — that is the current major release as of 2024 — for population frequency, and every gene against its LOEUF score, before any gene-burden analysis. Stratify or weight gene-level tests by LOEUF decile so that constraint heterogeneity does not masquerade as biological signal.

JORDAN That sounds like infrastructure work, not analysis.

ALEX It is infrastructure. And it has to be in place before we look at any results, otherwise we will be tempted to retrofit annotations to whatever signal pops up.

JORDAN What does this paper not close for us?

ALEX Conditional on the previous step — gnomAD answers population-frequency reference and per-gene constraint annotation. That is a lot. What it does not close — first, gnomAD's CF-affected representation is tiny. CF patients are a sliver of the gnomAD cohort. So allele frequencies for CF-relevant variants in gnomAD are general-population frequencies, which will systematically differ from frequencies in CF patients. We need to be careful when filtering CFF variants by gnomAD frequency — what is rare in gnomAD may be common in CF.

JORDAN Second?

ALEX Constraint scores assume general-population evolution. CFTR's LOEUF reflects overall selection on the gene, but near specific disease-causing positions there is a different selection regime — those positions are under stronger negative selection than the gene-wide average. LOEUF may understate constraint locally even while being well-calibrated globally.

JORDAN Verification caveats before we lean on this?

ALEX Three. One — confirm we can access gnomAD v4, either via the web browser at gnomad.broadinstitute.org or via local download of the constraint table. Two — write the annotation pipeline before we look at any analysis results; the choice of annotation framework, transcript model, and consequence priority changes the variant interpretation, and we cannot let those choices be made post-hoc. Three — decide on the LOEUF threshold for "constrained" — for example, the conventional cutoff of below 0.35 — before we unblind any test results, so we are not picking the threshold that gives us the answer we want.

JORDAN What is on deck for tomorrow?

ALEX Karczewski 2022 — "Genebass" — same group, same gnomAD infrastructure, but now run as a phenome-wide rare-variant association study across UK Biobank exomes. That is the methodological template for our CFTR PheWAS. Today's takeaway — gnomAD gives us the population frequency and the constraint score; we cannot do rare-variant work without both, and we annotate before we analyze.
