How did this episode land for you?

One-paragraph blurb

Wu and colleagues built and validated the canonical crosswalks from the World Health Organization's International Classification of Diseases tenth-revision codes — both ICD-10 and the U.S. clinical-modification version ICD-10-CM — to the phecode system that underpins essentially every phenome-wide association study. They mapped more than seventy-five percent of all official ICD-10 and ICD-10-CM codes to phecodes; coverage rose above ninety percent for codes actually observed in the Vanderbilt and UK Biobank databases. Reproducibility of chronic-disease phenotype assignment across the U.S. ICD-9-to-ICD-10 transition was seventy to seventy-six percent at Vanderbilt; the negative-control acute-disease reproducibility was below ten percent, exactly as expected. A PheWAS for a known lipoprotein-A variant, rs10455872, replicated cleanly across both maps with nearly identical odds ratios for coronary atherosclerosis and chronic ischemic heart disease. One possible direction this episode suggests is pre-registering a CFFPR-anchored CFTR PheWAS phenotype catalog defined entirely at the phecode level using the Wu maps, enabling clean comparison with UK Biobank and All-of-Us cohorts on the same phenotype substrate.

Plain-English glossary

  • BioVU: The Vanderbilt University Medical Center deidentified DNA biobank used as the PheWAS test substrate in this paper.
  • GEMS: General Equivalence Mappings — a Centers-for-Medicare-and-Medicaid-Services product that crosswalks ICD-10-CM to ICD-9-CM and vice versa.
  • ICD-9-CM / ICD-10 / ICD-10-CM: International Classification of Diseases, ninth and tenth revisions; the CM versions are U.S. clinical-modification variants used for billing.
  • LPA / rs10455872: The lipoprotein-A gene and a single-nucleotide variant in it known to increase risk for coronary heart disease.
  • NLM: National Library of Medicine — maintains several biomedical-terminology crosswalks used as inputs to the mapping.
  • OHDSI: Observational Health Data Sciences and Informatics — a research consortium that maintains the OMOP common data model and concept-relationship tables.
  • Phecode: A curated hierarchical grouping of ICD codes into clinically meaningful disease phenotypes, with built-in exclusion criteria for controls. The standard PheWAS phenotype unit.
  • PheWAS: Phenome-Wide Association Study — for a single variant or gene, scans thousands of phenotypes for association.
  • SNOMED CT: Systematized Nomenclature of Medicine Clinical Terms — a comprehensive clinical terminology used as an intermediate mapping step.
  • UMLS: Unified Medical Language System — an NLM-maintained metathesaurus of biomedical vocabularies; provides Concept Unique Identifiers (CUIs) used to chain mappings.

Methods to lift

  • Multi-resource chained mapping: GEMS (CMS) for ICD-10-CM-to-ICD-9-CM, UMLS Concept-Unique-Identifier equivalents for cross-vocabulary chaining, OHDSI concept relationships, NLM SNOMED-CT maps. Direct string-matching of code descriptions handles the easy cases; the chains handle the rest.
  • Hierarchical-child preference: when an ICD code maps to both a parent and a child phecode, keep the child to preserve granularity.
  • Polyhierarchical retention: when an ICD code maps to phecodes in different families (e.g., sickle-cell-disease-with-splenic-sequestration → both 282.5 and 289.5), keep both. New feature relative to the ICD-9-CM original phecode design.
  • Two-database coverage check: report (a) total unique codes mapped, (b) unique codes used in cohort mapped, (c) total code-instances mapped — three different denominators answering different questions.
  • Reproducibility audit: define the same patient cohort across pre- and post-transition windows and compute case-overlap percentages; chronic disease should reproduce ~70–75%, acute disease <10% as a negative control.
  • Comparative-PheWAS validation: pick a strong-effect variant with known phenotype associations (e.g., LPA rs10455872) and verify that both maps recover the same top phecode hits with similar odds ratios — establishes that downstream genetic-association results are equivalent across the maps.
  • Manual review of unmapped codes: separate "should not map" (Chapter 20, 21 codes for external causes / Z-codes) from "could-have-mapped-but-didn't" to characterize coverage limitations honestly.
  • R PheWAS package incorporation: the new maps were released in PheWAS package version 0.99.5-2, making them immediately usable.

ALEX Today's second episode is Wu and colleagues, 2019, published in JMIR Medical Informatics, titled "Mapping ICD-10 and ICD-10-CM Codes to Phecodes: Workflow Development and Initial Evaluation." First author Patrick Wu was a medical-scientist-training-program student at Vanderbilt at the time; senior authors Wei-Qi Wei and Joshua Denny are the people who effectively built the modern phecode system. The paper has been cited about four hundred and seventy-seven times.

JORDAN That's a *lot* of citations for what sounds like a database paper.

ALEX That's the point — this is the single most-cited piece of phenotyping infrastructure in the whole PheWAS world. Every comorbidity endpoint we'd run for a CFTR PheWAS would sit on top of this mapping. So even though it's not a flashy biological-discovery paper, it's load-bearing for the entire field.

JORDAN Okay, slow me down. What's a phecode?

ALEX A phecode is a curated grouping of billing-and-diagnosis codes — specifically, codes from the International Classification of Diseases — into clinically meaningful disease phenotypes that work well for genetic-association studies. The original phecode system, built by Joshua Denny in 2010, took the ICD-Ninth-Revision-Clinical-Modification — abbreviated ICD-9-CM — coding system and condensed its codes into custom phenotype groups. The most recent iteration of phecodes has roughly eighteen-hundred-sixty-six hierarchical phenotype codes mapping to about fifteen-thousand-five-hundred-fifty-eight ICD-9-CM codes.

JORDAN And ICD codes — those are billing codes?

ALEX ICD codes are diagnosis codes. They were developed by the World Health Organization originally to track mortality and morbidity, but they're used worldwide in clinical billing, electronic health records, and increasingly in research. ICD-9 came out in 1979, ICD-10 in 1990, and the U.S. created clinical-modification versions — ICD-9-CM and ICD-10-CM — that add extra specificity for U.S. billing requirements. The U.S. switched from ICD-9-CM to ICD-10-CM in October 2015.

JORDAN What's PheWAS, again?

ALEX PheWAS is phenome-wide association study. The mirror image of genome-wide association study. In GWAS you take one phenotype — say, FEV1 lung function — and scan thousands of variants looking for ones that associate with it. In PheWAS you take one variant or one gene and scan thousands of phenotypes looking for which ones it associates with. The phenotypes in PheWAS are almost always phecodes, because phecodes are the clean abstraction layer between raw billing codes and biologically meaningful disease definitions.

JORDAN Why a separate abstraction layer rather than just running PheWAS directly on ICD codes?

ALEX Two big reasons. First, ICD codes are noisy. The same disease can be coded with dozens of different ICD codes that differ in granularity, in left-versus-right anatomy, in stage of disease. Treating each ICD code as a separate phenotype dilutes signal across many low-power tests. Second, ICD codes do not have built-in exclusion criteria for controls. If you're testing for type-two-diabetes, you don't want patients with type-one-diabetes or with secondary diabetes mellitus or with abnormal-glucose-but-not-yet-diabetic to be in your control group, because case-control contamination kills statistical power. Phecodes have explicit hierarchical exclusion rules: when you're testing phecode 250.2 — type-two diabetes — the controls automatically exclude anyone with phecode 250.1, type-one diabetes, or phecode 249, secondary diabetes mellitus, or phecode 250.4, abnormal glucose. That hierarchy is baked into the system.

JORDAN Why was a new mapping paper needed in 2019?

ALEX Because the original phecode system was built on ICD-9-CM, and the world moved to ICD-10. By 2019, most U.S. health systems had crossed over to ICD-10-CM, and most international cohorts including the UK Biobank had always used ICD-10. The two systems are not interchangeable. ICD-9-CM was a numeric system — like 474.11 for hypertrophy of tonsils. ICD-10-CM is alphanumeric — J35.1 for the same condition. ICD-10-CM is also about ten-fold more granular for some categories like diabetes, and the structural organization differs in semantics and groupings.

JORDAN So a researcher with ICD-10 data could not just run the old phecode map.

ALEX Right. A handful of prior studies had tried to do indirect mapping by first converting ICD-10 back to ICD-9-CM and then mapping ICD-9-CM to phecodes — but they only handled ICD-10, not ICD-10-CM, and they didn't validate the result. The Wu paper's stated goals were to build a beta map for both ICD-10 and ICD-10-CM to phecodes, to evaluate how much of the codebase actually mapped, and to demonstrate that PheWAS results from the new map would be similar to PheWAS results from the gold-standard ICD-9-CM map.

JORDAN Walk me through the methods.

ALEX The data substrate was two databases. Vanderbilt University Medical Center — VUMC — had more than two-and-a-half years of ICD-10-CM billing data from October 2015 to June 2017, covering more than three million unique patients. The UK Biobank had more than two decades of ICD-10 data from April 1995 to March 2015, on inpatient encounters for the half-million UKBB cohort.

JORDAN And the mapping itself?

ALEX They used a stack of resources. Direct string-matching of code descriptions — for example, ICD-10-CM H52.4 "Presbyopia" matches phecode 367.4 "Presbyopia" exactly, no further work needed. That covered five-hundred-fifteen ICD-10-CM codes. Indirect mapping through the General Equivalence Mappings — abbreviated GEMS — which is a Centers-for-Medicare-and-Medicaid-Services product that crosswalks ICD-10-CM to ICD-9-CM. They could then chain GEMS to the existing ICD-9-CM-to-phecode map. GEMS covered eighty-two-thousand-two-hundred-eighty-seven ICD-10-CM codes. They also used the Unified Medical Language System — UMLS — concept identifiers, the Observational Health Data Sciences and Informatics concept relationships — OHDSI — and the National Library of Medicine maps. For ICD-10 without the clinical modification they couldn't use GEMS at all, so they relied on string matching plus the UMLS, OHDSI, and NLM crosswalks alone.

JORDAN What about codes that don't have an obvious one-to-one mapping?

ALEX There were two situations. First, some ICD-10-CM codes map to both a parent phecode and a child phecode lower in the hierarchy — they kept only the child phecode in those cases, to preserve the more granular meaning. Second, some ICD-10-CM codes mapped to phecodes in completely different families. The example they give is sickle-cell disease with splenic sequestration mapping to both phecode 282.5 "Sickle cell anemia" and phecode 289.5 "Diseases of spleen." They kept both, which creates a polyhierarchical structure that wasn't present in the original ICD-9-CM phecode system.

JORDAN I'd push back on that — keeping both could double-count patients in two phenotype groups simultaneously.

ALEX That's a real design concern. The authors acknowledge it implicitly by calling out the polyhierarchical change as a new feature. For a CFTR-PheWAS user, the practical implication is that some patients will appear as cases for multiple phecodes that aren't part of the same parent-family — which can be fine for hypothesis-generating PheWAS but creates an interpretation challenge for any per-phenotype confidence-interval calculation that assumes phenotype independence.

JORDAN How did the coverage check come out?

ALEX It came out very well at the unique-code level. Of all possible ICD-10-CM codes — about ninety-four-thousand-two-hundred-one of them in the 2018 UMLS release — eighty-two-thousand-three-hundred-three or eighty-seven-point-three-seven percent mapped to at least one phecode. Of all possible ICD-10 codes — twelve-thousand-twenty-seven of them — nine-thousand-sixty or seventy-five-point-three-three percent mapped. When restricted to *codes actually used in the cohorts* the coverage is even higher: ninety-four-point-four percent of the thirty-six-thousand-eight-hundred-fifty-eight ICD-10-CM codes used at VUMC mapped, and ninety-three-point-two-four percent of the six-thousand-two-hundred-forty-five ICD-10 codes used in the UK Biobank mapped. By total instances of code use across all patients, ninety percent of ICD-10-CM and eighty-four percent of ICD-10 code-instances mapped to phecodes.

JORDAN What about the unmapped codes?

ALEX Most of them were not biological-disease codes. The unmapped ICD-10 codes were largely personal-history or family-history codes that don't really represent active disease. The unmapped ICD-10-CM codes were largely Chapter Twenty external causes of morbidity and Chapter Twenty-One factors influencing health status — the "Z codes" — plus Chapter Nineteen injury-and-poisoning codes. So the unmapped subset is mostly not actually missing-from-phecodes; it's just non-disease-classification codes that legitimately shouldn't be in a phenotype-discovery list.

JORDAN And the reproducibility check?

ALEX They built a cohort of three-hundred-fifty-seven-thousand-seven-hundred-twenty-eight VUMC patients who had at least one ICD-9-CM code in the January-2014-to-June-2015 window and at least one ICD-10-CM code in the January-2016-to-June-2017 window. Mean age forty-five, fifty-five percent female. The question was: for patients who were *cases* for a chronic disease in the ICD-9 era, what fraction are also cases for the same disease in the ICD-10 era? For chronic diseases — hypertension, hyperlipidemia, type-one-diabetes, type-two-diabetes — they got seventy to seventy-six percent reproducibility. For the acute-disease negative control — intestinal infection — they got eight percent reproducibility, which is exactly what you'd want: an acute disease shouldn't be the same patient in two non-overlapping eighteen-month windows.

JORDAN That's a clever negative control.

ALEX It's also methodologically important because it shows the reproducibility number is doing real work — it's not just measuring something obvious like "did the patient come back." When they manually reviewed thirty patients who were chronic-disease cases in the ICD-9 window but not in the ICD-10 window, the reasons were almost entirely administrative: the patient was coded outside the eighteen-month window, the patient came in fewer than two times during the ICD-10 period, the patient was seen only by a specialist who didn't re-bill the chronic comorbidity. Zero cases were missed because the ICD-10-CM phecode map itself was wrong.

JORDAN What about the PheWAS validation?

ALEX They ran a PheWAS for a known lipoprotein-A variant, rs10455872, using thirteen-thousand-nine-hundred adult patients from the Vanderbilt BioVU biobank who had been genotyped at the variant and had at least one ICD-9-CM and one ICD-10-CM code in the relevant windows. They ran one PheWAS on ICD-9-CM-derived phecodes and one on ICD-10-CM-derived phecodes, adjusted for age, sex, and race. Two top hits replicated cleanly across both maps: coronary atherosclerosis showed P less than 0.001, odds ratio 1.60 with ninety-five-percent confidence interval 1.43 to 1.80 on *both* maps — exactly the same point estimate. Chronic ischemic heart disease was P less than 0.001 on both, odds ratio 1.56 on ICD-9-CM versus 1.47 on ICD-10-CM. Same direction, similar magnitude.

JORDAN So what does this suggest for the CFTR PheWAS project?

ALEX It suggests something operational and concrete. Any CFFPR-linked PheWAS we'd ever run will have ICD-10-CM as its native phenotype substrate, because CFFPR is U.S.-based and the U.S. has been on ICD-10-CM since October 2015. If we ever want to harmonize CFFPR phenotypes with UK Biobank data — which is ICD-10 — or with All-of-Us data — which is mixed ICD-9-CM-ICD-10-CM-and-other — we need a single phecode crosswalk that handles all three. The Wu mapping is the canonical one. Adopting it verbatim means our CFTR PheWAS phenotypes will be comparable to thousands of existing PheWAS analyses on the same phecode catalog.

JORDAN Translate that into a formal sentence.

ALEX One possible direction this episode suggests: pre-register a CFFPR-anchored CFTR PheWAS phenotype catalog defined entirely at the phecode level using the Wu 2019 ICD-10-CM-to-phecode crosswalk for U.S.-era CFFPR data and the Wu 2019 ICD-10-non-CM-to-phecode crosswalk for any UK Biobank or other international comparator data, with the full phecode hierarchy including the built-in exclusion criteria applied uniformly, and with a documented per-phecode reproducibility audit on the CFFPR substrate before any genome-wide test is run.

JORDAN Conditional on that proposal — what does Wu close and what does it leave open?

ALEX It closes the question of whether ICD-10-CM-to-phecode and ICD-10-to-phecode mapping is feasible — both maps now exist, validated, peer-reviewed, and freely available through the PheWAS R package. It closes the question of whether PheWAS results from the two coding eras are comparable for chronic-disease endpoints — same direction, similar effect sizes at established loci like LPA. It closes the question of how to validate a new phecode map — the reproducibility + negative-control + comparative-PheWAS triad is the canonical evaluation. What it does *not* close: first, CFFPR is not VUMC and is not UK Biobank. CFFPR has a curated CF-specific endpoint catalog already in place — CFRD onset, chronic Pseudomonas acquisition, lung allograft, mortality — that the Wu mapping was not designed for. We'd need to check whether each of those curated CFFPR endpoints maps cleanly to a phecode equivalent or whether the curated definitions should stay separate from any phecode-only PheWAS. Second, the reproducibility numbers — seventy to seventy-six percent for chronic disease — imply that twenty-four to thirty percent of patients drop out across the ICD-9 to ICD-10 transition for administrative rather than biological reasons. CFFPR's transition handling at member centers in October 2015 deserves a per-center audit. Third, the polyhierarchical issue Jordan flagged earlier matters operationally for any phecode hit interpretation. Fourth, the LPA variant they validated with is a strong-effect common variant. Subtler associations may not transfer as cleanly across the maps.

JORDAN Tomorrow's tee-up?

ALEX Tomorrow we're back on the CFTR-WGS sheet with Uden and colleagues 2025 — a follow-up replication and mechanistic paper on TNFR1 as a CF survival modifier, with full air-liquid-interface epithelial transcript analysis. Different paper genre from today's mapping infrastructure piece, but it's where actual gene-level CF science lives.
