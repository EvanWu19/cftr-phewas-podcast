Blurb

Sng and colleagues at CSIRO built RAPpoet — a driver-worker parallel orchestration template for UK Biobank's cloud Research Analysis Platform — and used it to run the first comprehensive CAD association analysis on the 145,207-genome UKB WGS release. They ran logistic regression, REGENIE, and the random-forest method VariantSpark in parallel, then fed summary statistics into PolyFun for fine-mapping. PolyFun pinpoints rs10757274 as the most likely causal SNV at the long-known 9p21.3 CAD locus, and VariantSpark recovers rs28451064 at 21q22.11 — a known CAD locus that single-SNV logistic regression missed at genome-wide significance in this dataset. They report 94% runtime reduction and 44% cost reduction versus naive sequential processing. The proposal it suggests: run a parallel-tools comparison (linear GWAS + REGENIE + tree-ensemble ML) on a 5,000-patient CF cohort with WGS, and see whether the ML sensitivity gain that appears at UKB scale survives at CF scale. The open question is whether tree-ensemble methods retain that sensitivity when n drops from tens of thousands of cases to a few thousand.

The lesson is "here is how a small team actually ran a whole-genome study on UK Biobank's new cloud-only data, and here is how they made it cheap enough."

JORDAN Hold on — before we go in, what do I actually need to know to follow along?

ALEX Good. Let's set ground rules. UK Biobank, or UKB, is a half-million-person research cohort in the UK with linked health records and now whole-genome sequencing on every participant. Whole-genome sequencing — WGS — means they read essentially every letter of every participant's DNA, not just the common-variant chips that earlier biobank work relied on. A genome-wide association study — GWAS — is the workhorse technique where, for every variant in the genome, you ask, "do people who carry this variant have more of disease X than people who don't?" A SNV is a single nucleotide variant, a one-letter DNA change. A locus is a region of the genome, identified by chromosome and band — so 9p21.3 means the short arm of chromosome 9, region 2, sub-band 1.3.

JORDAN And "fine-mapping"?

ALEX When a GWAS hits a region, it doesn't tell you *which* variant in that region is the actual cause. Variants near each other inherit together — what geneticists call linkage disequilibrium, or LD — so the hit you see is one of dozens of correlated variants. Fine-mapping is a statistical narrowing step that tries to assign each variant a probability of being the true causal one. The tool the authors use is called PolyFun — Polygenic Functionally informed fine-mapping — which is a method that mixes the GWAS evidence with prior knowledge about which variants live in functionally important parts of the genome.

JORDAN And the other tools?

ALEX REGENIE is a now-standard whole-genome regression method, designed for biobank-scale data — it builds a global polygenic predictor first, then uses that to control for genetic relatedness when it tests each variant. VariantSpark is the authors' own machine-learning tool, a random-forest variant of GWAS that can pick up combinations of variants that single-variant tests miss. ML stands for machine learning. And RAP — the Research Analysis Platform — is UK Biobank's cloud computing environment, hosted on DNAnexus. UKB no longer ships data to researchers' institutions for the new WGS data; they ship the computation to UKB's cloud. That single fact reshapes everything else.

JORDAN So why this paper, why now?

ALEX In November 2023, UKB released WGS on 145,207 participants — that's roughly half a petabyte of data. There's no realistic way to download that. You analyse it where it sits, on the RAP. But the RAP's standard tutorials assume you process one file at a time. With over a hundred thousand per-sample VCF files, that doesn't work. Sng and colleagues built a parallel orchestration tool called RAPpoet, ran a real GWAS for coronary artery disease — CAD — on the WGS, and reported what it cost and what it found. The paper is half methods and half compute-engineering postmortem, and the postmortem is the more useful half for someone in our position.

JORDAN Define CAD too, please.

ALEX Coronary artery disease — narrowing of the arteries that supply the heart muscle. Cases were identified from electronic health records using ICD-9 and ICD-10 billing codes — those are the World Health Organization's standardized disease codes that hospitals enter for billing — plus the UK's surgical procedure codes called OPCS-4. So a "case" here is anyone whose record lists heart-attack, angina, or coronary-revascularization codes. ICD = International Classification of Diseases. After their quality control they had 24,954 CAD cases and 25,858 controls, all of self-reported white British ancestry, none more closely related than third cousins.

JORDAN That's the methods. What about the science motivation?

ALEX CAD is one of the most-studied diseases in human genetics. There's a textbook locus on 9p21.3 that's been replicated in hundreds of studies but the *causal* variant — the actual nucleotide doing the damage — has been argued about for fifteen years. Earlier work used array-genotyped or imputed data, where you only see common variants directly and infer the rest. WGS lets you actually see rare and structural variants in the region. So the substantive question is: now that we can see every letter, does fine-mapping converge on a single causal SNV at 9p21.3, or does the picture stay murky?

JORDAN And the engineering motivation?

ALEX Biobank-scale WGS broke the old workflows. You used to bring data to your compute. With UKB-RAP, you bring compute to data, in someone else's cloud, on someone else's billing meter. So the question becomes: can a small team get a publication-worthy analysis done on this platform without burning their grant on idle EC2 hours? That is *exactly* the question Runyu would face if the CFF were ever to host its WGS on a similar cloud platform, which is the trajectory most cohorts are heading toward.

JORDAN OK. How did they actually do it?

ALEX Three pieces. First, the cohort definition I mentioned — ICD-and-procedure-based case ascertainment, strict sample QC, ancestry restriction to self-reported white British so that population stratification — that's the problem of false hits from comparing people of different ancestries — doesn't contaminate the results. Second, the orchestration. They built RAPpoet, which is shorthand for "RAP parallelisation orchestration engine template." It's a driver-worker pattern. A small driver script lives on the user's laptop and orchestrates the jobs; worker scripts inside the RAP project handle the actual computation across thousands of pVCF files in parallel.

JORDAN pVCF?

ALEX Project-VCF — a file format that holds variant calls for many samples in one column-rich file. They used "bring your own license" instances on the RAP, picked instance types matched to each step's CPU and memory needs, and report a 94% speedup and 44% cost reduction versus the naive sequential pipeline. Third, the analytical layer — they ran three association methods in parallel: standard single-SNV logistic regression, REGENIE, and VariantSpark — and then fed the GWAS summary statistics into PolyFun for fine-mapping.

JORDAN You said you'd push back on a design choice.

ALEX The most defensible-but-arguable one: restricting to genetically inferred white British samples. They do it to keep the ancestry signal clean, which is statistically reasonable, but it means the fine-mapping result they get only applies to that population. A causal SNV that's frequent in Europeans might be rare or even absent elsewhere, and a CAD risk locus that's important in South Asians could be entirely missed. The paper acknowledges this and frames it as a methods demonstration rather than a global CAD map, which is honest, but the framing matters for whether the result generalizes.

JORDAN Now the results. Did they answer the question?

ALEX Partly yes, partly informatively no. The 9p21.3 question — they ran PolyFun on the WGS and report that rs10757274 emerges as the most likely causal SNV in the region, with the highest posterior inclusion probability — a number between zero and one indicating "this is the causal variant" — among the candidates. That's a real fine-mapping payoff: fifteen years of "somewhere in 9p21.3" reduced to a single nucleotide. The caveat is the analysis is in Europeans only, and rs10757274 sits in a long non-coding RNA locus called ANRIL whose mechanism is still being worked out.

JORDAN And the other locus?

ALEX This is the part I think matters most for us. The ML method, VariantSpark, picked up rs28451064 in 21q22.11 — a known CAD risk SNV — that the standard single-SNV logistic regression did not flag at genome-wide significance in this dataset. REGENIE also detected it. The interpretation is not that ML is magic; it's that the random-forest method is sensitive to non-additive contributions and to combinations of nearby variants, so for some loci it pulls a signal out of the noise that the single-variant test misses. They're careful to say this is *sensitivity*, not necessarily specificity — ML methods are notorious for finding patterns that don't replicate. So the result is "two genuine CAD loci recovered, one of them only because we ran a non-standard method alongside the standard one."
