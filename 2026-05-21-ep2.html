Blurb

Collins, Brand, Karczewski, Zhao and colleagues (Nature, May 2020) built gnomAD-SV — the first sequence-resolved population-scale catalog of structural variation from 14,891 short-read whole-genome-sequenced samples (14,237 after QC), cataloging 433,371 SVs across six canonical classes (deletion, duplication, multiallelic CNV, insertion, inversion, translocation) and 11 complex subclasses. The average genome contains ~7,400 SVs and 179.8 SV-altered genes (144.3 pLoF, 24.3 copy-gain, 11.2 IED); 25–29% of rare per-individual heterozygous gene-inactivation events come from SVs rather than SNVs. SV mutation rate is 0.29 de novo events per generation. Selection metrics derived from SNVs (pLI, LOEUF) predict tolerance to SV disruption (APS correlates significantly). 15,634 common SVs sit in strong LD with published GWAS hits — many SNV GWAS signals may actually tag SVs. Proposed direction (conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage): a CFTR-locus and CF-modifier structural-variation catalog built from CFGP WGS with population-frequency comparison against gnomAD-SV, focused on gene-disrupting SVs that SNV-focused pipelines miss, with downstream linkage to CFFPR phenotype data. Open question: whether CFGP WGS depth and read length support the multi-algorithm gnomAD-SV discovery pipeline at adequate sensitivity.

Plain-English glossary

  • APS — absolute proportion of singletons; SV-class selection metric analogous to LOEUF.
  • CNV — copy-number variant; SV that changes the number of copies of a chunk of DNA (deletion or duplication).
  • complex SV — SV combining two or more breakpoints and classes (delINV, dupINVdel, etc.); 11 subclasses defined in this paper.
  • gnomAD — Genome Aggregation Database; the SNV-focused population variant reference.
  • gnomAD-SV — the SV-focused companion catalog released by this paper.
  • IED — intragenic exonic duplication; SV duplicating a subset of a gene's exons.
  • indel — small insertion or deletion (<50 bp); not classified as an SV.
  • inversion — SV that flips a chunk of DNA end-for-end without changing copy number.
  • LOEUF — loss-of-function observed/expected upper-bound fraction; gene-level constraint score from gnomAD-SNV.
  • MCNV — multiallelic copy-number variant; locus with more than two population copy-number states.
  • pLI — probability of loss-of-function intolerance; gene-level constraint, older sibling of LOEUF.
  • pLoF — predicted loss-of-function; variant predicted to knock out a gene.
  • singleton — variant observed as exactly one allele across the entire sample; the rarity tail of the frequency spectrum.
  • SNV — single-nucleotide variant; one-letter DNA difference.
  • SV — structural variant; rearrangement of ≥50 bp of DNA.
  • translocation — SV that moves DNA from one chromosome to another.
  • Watterson estimator — population-genetics method for projecting de novo mutation rates from observed allele-frequency spectra in unrelated individuals.
  • WGS — whole-genome sequencing; reads the entire ~3 Gb human genome (vs. WES which reads only the 1–2% exome).

ALEX Welcome back. Episode two of today's batch is "A structural variation reference for medical and population genetics" by Ryan L. Collins, Harrison Brand, Konrad J. Karczewski, Xuefang Zhao and colleagues, published in Nature in May 2020. This is the gnomAD-SV paper — the first sequence-resolved, population-scale catalog of structural variation built from whole-genome sequencing. It has been cited over 850 times in five years.

JORDAN Why does CFTR-PheWAS work need a structural-variation reference?

ALEX Because the field's standard discovery substrate is the single-nucleotide variant — the SNV — and the standard exome chip and exome-sequencing data miss almost everything else. A structural variant is anything that rearranges at least 50 base pairs of DNA: deletions, duplications, inversions, translocations, complex multi-breakpoint events. Those events can knock out genes, flip regulatory regions, and create dosage changes that no single-letter substitution would produce. If you only look at SNVs, you systematically miss the variants that are most likely to break a gene cleanly. Until 2020, nobody had built a population-scale catalog of these for matched human samples.

JORDAN Vocabulary primer first. I keep hearing WGS, SNV, indel, SV, CNV — what are they?

ALEX WGS — whole-genome sequencing — reads essentially the entire 3-billion-base-pair human genome. WES — whole-exome sequencing — reads only the 1 to 2 percent of the genome that codes for protein. SNV is a single-nucleotide variant — one letter changed. An indel is a small insertion or deletion under 50 base pairs. An SV is a structural variant, 50 base pairs or larger. A CNV — copy-number variant — is the subset of SVs that change the number of copies of a chunk of DNA, either deletions or duplications. A multiallelic CNV, abbreviated MCNV, is a locus where you can find more than two copy-number states across the population, like 0, 1, 2, 3, or 4 copies. An inversion flips a chunk of DNA end-for-end without changing copy number. A translocation moves DNA from one chromosome to another. A complex SV is anything that combines two or more of these.

JORDAN And pLoF, LOEUF, pLI?

ALEX Three measures of gene constraint. pLoF stands for predicted loss-of-function — a variant that is predicted to knock out the gene by introducing a premature stop codon, breaking the splice site, or deleting the gene. pLI is the probability of loss-of-function intolerance — a number from zero to one that measures how strongly a gene appears to reject heterozygous loss-of-function variants in the general population. LOEUF, the loss-of-function observed over expected upper bound fraction, is the modern replacement for pLI, also derived from SNV data in gnomAD. Both are gene-level metrics that come from counting how many pLoF SNVs you actually see in a gene versus how many you'd expect under neutral mutation. APS — the absolute proportion of singletons — is a fourth term we'll meet: it measures selection against SVs the same way LOEUF measures selection against SNVs, by asking what fraction of variants of a given class are present in only one allele across the population.

JORDAN OK. What was the problem they wanted to solve?

ALEX Two interlocking problems. First, SV discovery from short-read whole-genome sequencing is hard. Each SV class — deletion, duplication, inversion, complex — has different detection signatures in the read data, and no single algorithm catches them all. The team had previously published an SV discovery pipeline that combined results from multiple algorithms across 519 quartet families with autism, but that was just over two thousand individuals. Second, the human-genetics community had a SNV reference catalog — gnomAD — that had transformed clinical variant interpretation. There was no equivalent for SVs, so when a clinical lab found an SV in a patient's genome, they had no population-frequency reference to ask "is this rare or common?" That was the gap, and as long as the gap existed, SVs were essentially uninterpretable in clinical practice except in the most extreme dominant cases.

JORDAN What did they actually do?

ALEX They analyzed 14,891 short-read WGS samples at average 32-times coverage, aggregated from large-scale sequencing projects. After quality control they retained 14,237 samples. They ran a multi-algorithm SV-discovery pipeline that handles six canonical SV classes — deletions, duplications, multiallelic CNVs, insertions, inversions, translocations — plus 11 subclasses of complex SVs. They benchmarked against long-read sequencing from a smaller subset of the same samples and against the previous 519 autism quartet dataset, where molecularly validated de novo SVs gave them an independent ground-truth comparison for their mutation-rate estimates. They cross-referenced against the standard human-genetics annotation tracks: GTEx tissue-expression data, the NHGRI-EBI GWAS catalog, the chromatin-state map from ChromHMM, the VISTA enhancer database, and ultraconserved-element annotations. The end product was gnomAD-SV — 433,371 SVs catalogued and released as a public resource with population allele frequencies stratified by ancestry.

JORDAN Push back on something they did.

ALEX The biggest caveat is short-read coverage. Short-read WGS — typically 150 base pair reads — is fundamentally bad at calling SVs in repetitive sequence, and the human genome is heavily repetitive. The team is explicit: their sensitivity in repetitive regions is reduced, their estimates of mutation rates almost certainly underestimate the truth, and their inversion catalog in particular is a floor not a ceiling. Long-read sequencing now exists and would resolve many of the SV classes they had to estimate around. So gnomAD-SV is a reference catalog at one technology generation that will need updating as long-read population-scale data arrives. The other pushback is ancestry composition — the 14,237 samples are heavily European-skewed, with smaller representation of African, East Asian, Latino, and South Asian populations. SV allele frequencies vary by ancestry, and the example they call out — duplications of NPHP1 at 2q13 being up to 4.6-fold more frequent in East Asian samples than in others — means the reference can mis-flag rare-vs-common status when applied to non-European patients. The recommended use is to apply a population-matched 0.1 percent allele-frequency screening threshold rather than the headline global frequency.

JORDAN What did they find?

ALEX A lot. The average genome in gnomAD-SV contains roughly 7,400 structural variants. About 179.8 genes per genome are altered by biallelic SVs — 144.3 by predicted loss of function, 24.3 by copy gain, and 11.2 by intragenic exon duplications. About 11.6 of those genes are completely inactivated by homozygous loss-of-function SVs in the average individual. When you restrict to rare SVs at allele frequency under 1 percent, the per-genome count drops to 10.2 altered genes, of which 5.5 are pLoF, 3.4 copy-gain, and 1.3 intragenic exon duplications. Crucially, the companion SNV gnomAD paper estimated 122.4 pLoF short variants per genome and 16.3 rare ones. So 25 to 29 percent of rare heterozygous gene-inactivation events per individual come from SVs, not SNVs. If your discovery substrate is SNV-only, you are missing about a quarter of the rare gene-knockout signal across the genome. They estimated the genome-wide SV mutation rate at 0.29 de novo SVs per generation — roughly one new SV every 2 to 8 live births, with rates varying markedly by SV class — and that estimate matched their independent observation in the 519-quartet families. They identified 15,634 common SVs in strong linkage disequilibrium with published GWAS hits, meaning a lot of GWAS signals attributed to SNVs may actually be tagging nearby SVs that are the true causal variants. And the gene-level pattern of selection against SVs — measured by APS — correlated significantly with both pLI and LOEUF, demonstrating that SNV-derived constraint metrics partially predict tolerance to structural disruption. The implication is that LOEUF can be used to prioritize candidate genes for SV-modifier work even when SV-specific constraint estimates are unavailable.

JORDAN A concrete research direction this episode suggests?

ALEX One possible direction: build a CFTR-locus structural-variation interpretation layer. CFTR sits at chromosome 7q31.2 and spans about 250 kilobases of genomic DNA. CFTR has roughly 2,000 known disease-causing variants — but those are overwhelmingly SNVs and small indels found via Sanger sequencing of exons or via the established multiplex commercial CF panels. Large structural variants in CFTR — gene-spanning deletions, large duplications, inversions, complex events involving the CFTR introns — are reported sporadically in clinical case literature but have never been systematically catalogued across a CF cohort. The Ling 2024 paper's BMI signal at ADAMTS5, which we'll cover later today, also illustrates the broader principle: rare-variant haplotype effects can hide entirely in populations where the haplotype is rare. Formally: a CFTR-locus structural-variation catalog built from the CFF Genome Project WGS, with population-frequency comparison against the gnomAD-SV reference for non-CF baseline, focused on identifying gene-disrupting SVs in CFTR or its known regulatory neighbors that would not be detected by exome chip or SNV-focused WGS analysis pipelines, with downstream linkage to CFFPR phenotype data to ask whether SV-carrier status modifies disease severity beyond the SNV-defined CFTR genotype class.

JORDAN What does the paper close and what is left open under that direction?

ALEX What it closes: a population-frequency baseline for non-CF SV interpretation is now available, and the SV-discovery algorithms used in the gnomAD-SV pipeline are documented. The methods are reusable. What is left open: nearly everything CF-specific. We do not know what fraction of the CFGP WGS samples have had SV-aware analysis at all — historic CFTR-modifier work has been SNV-centered, and SV calling is a separate computational stack that may or may not have been run. We do not know whether the residual CFTR genotype variance not explained by the canonical SNV-defined mutation classes is partially attributable to undetected SVs in CFTR itself, in the regulatory neighborhood, or in unrelated modifier loci. We do not know the carrier frequency of large CFTR deletions in CFGP versus gnomAD-SV. Things we would need to check before committing: whether the CFGP sequencing depth and read length are compatible with the multi-algorithm SV discovery pipeline used in gnomAD-SV; whether the CFGP variant calls already include any SV layer that could be inventoried directly; whether the CFFPR-WGS sample-linkage is structured to support phenotype regression on SV-carrier status; whether the family-based subset of the CFGP — the Twin and Sibling and multiplex cohorts — has the joint-segregation data that would allow de novo SV mutation rate estimation comparable to the Watterson-estimator approach gnomAD-SV used; and whether existing CFTR SV reports in the CF clinical literature — for instance the well-known CFTRdele2,3 21kb deletion — are already represented in the CFGP variant set or remain to be catalogued.
