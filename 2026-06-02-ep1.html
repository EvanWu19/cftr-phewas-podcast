Blurb

Yang et al. (2026, Briefings in Bioinformatics) introduce MixEHR-SAGE, a PheCode-guided multi-modal topic model that reads a patient's hospital record as a "document" of diagnoses (ICD-10), medications (ATC), and procedures (OPCS-4), and learns over 1,000 interpretable disease "topics." Instead of labeling each person a binary case/control, it assigns a continuous disease risk score per phenotype. Applied to ~370,000 UK Biobank participants, these scores predicted incident type 2 diabetes (T2D) and leukemia from baseline records, and — when used as the trait in a GWAS — recovered loci the binary approach missed (PPP1R15A for T2D; JMJD6/SRSF2 for leukemia, externally supported by FinnGen and MAGMA). The proposed direction for a CFTR PheWAS: build continuous, multi-modal CF severity scores from registry data and test whether they out-power binary phecode definitions for detecting CF genetic modifiers. The open question is whether topic modeling — built and validated at 350K scale — can be fit stably on a ~5,000-patient CF cohort.

Plain-English glossary

  • ATC (Anatomical Therapeutic Chemical): standardized international dictionary for classifying drugs/medications.
  • Collapsed variational inference: an efficient approximate-math algorithm for fitting probabilistic models like topic models to very large datasets.
  • Continuous risk score: a number on a sliding scale of how strongly a patient resembles a disease, replacing a hard yes/no case label.
  • EHR (Electronic Health Record): a patient's digital hospital file (diagnoses, prescriptions, procedures).
  • GMM (Gaussian Mixture Model): a clustering method used here to initialize the topic priors.
  • GWAS (Genome-Wide Association Study): scans the whole genome for genetic variants (SNPs) more common in people with a trait than without.
  • ICD (International Classification of Diseases): the standardized diagnosis-code dictionary (e.g., E11.9 = type 2 diabetes).
  • Incident case: a new diagnosis recorded after a baseline date (vs. a prevalent case already diagnosed before).
  • LD (Linkage Disequilibrium): the tendency of nearby genetic variants to be inherited together; used here for "clumping" GWAS hits into independent loci.
  • Locus (plural loci): a specific location in the genome.
  • MAGMA: a gene-based association test that aggregates SNP signals to the gene level.
  • OPCS-4: the British standard codes for surgical and operative procedures.
  • PheCode (Phenotype Code): a research-friendly grouping of related ICD codes into ~1,300 disease categories.
  • Phenotype: any observable trait or condition of a person.
  • PheWAS (Phenome-Wide Association Study): takes one genetic variant and tests it against many phenotypes.
  • SNP (Single Nucleotide Polymorphism): a single-letter difference in DNA between people.
  • T2D (Type 2 Diabetes): the common adult, insulin-resistance form of diabetes.
  • Topic model: statistical method that discovers clusters of co-occurring "words" (here, medical codes) across "documents" (here, patient records).
  • UKB (UK Biobank): ~500,000-person British research cohort with genetic + health-record data.

Methods to lift

  • Multi-modal phenotyping: combine diagnoses + medications + procedures, not just diagnosis codes, to define phenotypes.
  • Seed-guided topics: initialize topic clusters with expert PheCode→ICD mappings so discovered topics align with named diseases (interpretability without pure supervision).
  • Continuous risk score as GWAS trait: use the per-patient topic mixture as a quantitative trait; run linear regression GWAS (PLINK) alongside the binary-label logistic GWAS to compare yield.
  • PLINK: open-source workhorse for genotype QC and association testing.
  • LD clumping (PLINK): collapse correlated SNP hits into independent loci.
  • External replication discipline: confirm novel loci in an independent biobank (FinnGen) plus a gene-based test (MAGMA) before believing them.
  • Incident-vs-prevalent split: validate predictive scores against diagnoses recorded after baseline, with PheCode-based control exclusions.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: Construct continuous, multi-modal phenotype severity scores from CF patient-registry diagnoses, medications, and procedures, and test whether using those scores as quantitative traits improves power to detect CFTR-modifier signals relative to binary phecode definitions.

What to verify first

  • Whether a topic model (or a simpler continuous-severity model) can be fit stably on a ~5,000-patient cohort — the method was built/validated at ~350K scale.
  • Whether the CF registry stores medications and procedures in coded, machine-readable form (ATC/OPCS-equivalent) rather than free text.
  • The ancestry composition of the CF cohort (this analysis was European-ancestry-only; portability is untested).
  • Whether a continuous CF severity score even has more usable variance than a binary outcome for the specific endpoints of interest (lung function is already continuous; CFRD onset less so).
  • Whether registry follow-up depth supports an incident-vs-prevalent design for time-to-onset endpoints.

ALEX A PheCode is a tidied-up grouping of ICD codes. Researchers noticed that the raw ICD dictionary is too fine-grained and inconsistent, so they hand-built "Phenotype Codes" — PheCodes — that bundle related ICD codes into about thirteen hundred research-friendly disease categories. A phenotype, just so it's said plainly, is any observable trait or condition of a person. Now, GWAS: a Genome-Wide Association Study scans the whole genome looking for genetic spelling differences — single-letter variants called SNPs, single nucleotide polymorphisms — that show up more often in people with a disease than without. And PheWAS, the thing this whole podcast orbits, flips that around: a Phenome-Wide Association Study takes one genetic variant and asks which of hundreds of phenotypes it's linked to.

JORDAN And "topic modeling"? That sounds like something from text analysis, not medicine.

ALEX It is borrowed from text analysis, and that's the clever part. A topic model is a statistical method that looks at a pile of documents and discovers hidden "topics" — clusters of words that tend to appear together. Think of how the words "engine," "tire," and "mileage" cluster into a topic you'd call "cars." Yang's team treats each patient's medical record as a document and each medical code as a word, then lets the model discover clusters of codes that hang together — which turn out to be diseases. Their model is named MixEHR-SAGE.

JORDAN So the whole paper is about a better way to define disease from records. Why does that need fixing? What was broken?

ALEX Here's the motivating problem. The standard way to run a PheWAS or GWAS is to draw a hard line: you either have the PheCode for a disease or you don't. Case or control, one or zero. But hospital records are an imperfect snapshot. Someone in the early stages of T2D, already on metformin, already showing eye complications, might not yet have the diabetes diagnosis code formally entered. The binary system calls that person a "control" — a healthy comparison — when biologically they're anything but. That misclassification weakens every downstream genetic analysis, because you're comparing a muddy "sick" group against a contaminated "healthy" group.

JORDAN So the binary label throws away information about how sick someone is.

ALEX Exactly. And the second problem: PheCodes only use ICD diagnosis codes. They ignore the prescriptions a patient is on and the surgical procedures they've had — which are often richer signals of disease than the diagnosis code itself. A metformin prescription tells you a lot. A laser eye procedure for diabetic retinopathy tells you a lot. The standard approach is blind to those. Yang's argument is: use all three streams — diagnoses, medications, procedures — and replace the hard yes/no with a continuous risk score, a number on a sliding scale of how strongly each patient resembles each disease.

JORDAN Alright, that motivation lands. How did they actually build it? Walk me through the methods.

ALEX They took UK Biobank — UKB, a famous research cohort of about 500,000 British adults aged 40 to 69 with both genetic data and linked hospital records. They fed MixEHR-SAGE three modalities: ICD-10 diagnosis codes, self-reported medications mapped to ATC codes — ATC is the Anatomical Therapeutic Chemical system, another standardized dictionary, this one for drugs — and OPCS-4 codes, which are the British standard for surgical and operative procedures. The "SAGE" part is "seed-guided." They seed the model with expert knowledge: each PheCode points to its known ICD codes, and the model uses that as a starting hint, so the topics it discovers line up with real, named diseases instead of arbitrary clusters.

JORDAN How does it learn? Is this one of those things that takes a supercomputer a month?

To handle half a million people they processed the data in mini-batches of a thousand records at a time. The output is over a thousand interpretable phenotype topics, and for each patient, a mixture — what fraction of their record belongs to each disease topic. That mixture becomes the continuous risk score. Here's a design choice I'd push back on, though: they excluded rare diseases entirely because the sample sizes were too small, and they restricted the genetic analysis to people of European ancestry — after quality control, 370,885 unrelated European-ancestry individuals.
