How did this episode land for you?

Blurb

Wang and colleagues from the Strug lab developed SS2 (Simple Sum 2), a two-stage frequentist colocalization test that handles four pathologies of the chr3q29 CF lung-modifier locus that break standard colocalization tools: high LD across the region, allelic heterogeneity with multiple independent eQTL signals, overlapping samples between the GWAS and the eQTL study (85 of 94 eQTL samples are in the 6,365-patient ICFGMC GWAS), and summary statistics from meta-analysis with related individuals within sub-studies. SS2 first tests whether any eQTL exists at the locus (stage 1) and only then runs the colocalization test (stage 2), with Bonferroni correction across genes surviving stage 1. Applied to chr3q29: stage 1 p=3.16×10⁻⁷ and stage 2 p=1.31×10⁻⁵ for MUC4 in CF nasal epithelia; MUC20 stage 1 p=0.083 (does not pass). Across 564 gene-by-tissue pairs spanning a 1-Mb window and 14 CF-relevant GTEx tissues, MUC4 in CF nasal epithelia survived Bonferroni at adjusted stage 2 p=1.49×10⁻³ as the only lung-relevant gene. COLOC posterior was 0.76 (below the 0.8 threshold), COLOC2 was essentially zero, SMR raw p=0.003 going to adjusted p=0.379, SMR-multi adjusted p=0.03 — none of the standard methods gave a clean answer. SS2 is implemented in the LocusFocus web tool. The CF-direction this episode invites is to apply SS2 systematically across all five Corvol 2015 CF lung-modifier loci to produce a definitive gene-attribution table — with the caveat that the chr3q29 mucin cluster has high-frequency CNV at a 7–19 kb tandem-repeat region that SNV-based methods like SS2 cannot resolve.

Plain-English glossary

  • Allelic heterogeneity — more than one independent causal variant exists at the same locus; each variant independently affects the trait.
  • Bayesian colocalization (COLOC, COLOC2, ENLOC) — compute posterior probabilities for various colocalization hypotheses given the data and a prior.
  • Bonferroni correction — multiply the per-test p-value by the number of tests to control family-wise error rate when you do many tests.
  • Colocalization — statistical test of whether a GWAS signal and an eQTL signal at the same chromosomal region share a causal variant.
  • Composite null hypothesis — the null isn't a single scenario but a union of several distinct scenarios (no GWAS no eQTL; no GWAS but eQTL; GWAS but no eQTL; both at independent variants), all of which must be rejected to claim colocalization.
  • CNV (copy-number variant) — large structural variant where a stretch of DNA is duplicated or deleted, often spanning kilobases to megabases.
  • eQTL (expression quantitative trait locus) — DNA variant that influences expression level of a particular gene.
  • Frequentist colocalization (SS, SS2, SMR, SMR-multi) — compute p-values from a test statistic, asking how likely the data would be under the null.
  • GTEx (Genotype-Tissue Expression project) — public resource of eQTL summary statistics across ~50 human tissues from organ donors.
  • HNE (human nasal epithelia) — primary airway epithelial cells obtained by nasal curettage; CF-tissue-relevant surrogate for lower-airway biology.
  • GWAS-PW — extension of COLOC that empirically estimates priors from genome-wide data; algorithm folded into COLOC2.
  • JLIM — colocalization test contrasting one-shared-variant vs distinct-variants likelihoods; requires individual-level expression data for permutation.
  • LD (linkage disequilibrium) — correlation between nearby DNA variants because they get inherited together.
  • MUC4 / MUC20 — sister mucin genes clustered at chr3q29 encoding membrane-spanning "tethered" mucins involved in airway mucociliary defense.
  • PWCoCo (pairwise conditional + colocalization) — alternative method for multi-signal regions; iteratively conditions on each independent signal.
  • S-PrediXcan / TWAS / S-MultiXcan — colocalization-adjacent methods using transcriptome imputation weights from a reference panel.
  • SS / SS2 (Simple Sum / Simple Sum 2) — frequentist colocalization tests developed by the Strug lab; SS2 is the two-stage version that controls Type-I error across all four composite-null scenarios.
  • SMR / SMR-multi (Summary-based Mendelian Randomization) — colocalization-adjacent methods that use SNPs with eQTL p<5×10⁻⁸ as instrumental variables.

Methods to lift

  • SS2 two-stage colocalization framework — stage-1 set-based eQTL test followed by stage-2 Simple Sum colocalization, with Bonferroni correction at each stage; controls Type-I error across all four composite-null scenarios.
  • LocusFocus — web application implementing SS2 colocalization between user-supplied GWAS summary statistics and GTEx-supplied or user-supplied eQTL summary statistics (locusfocus.research.sickkids.ca).
  • LeBlanc decorrelation correction — adjusts summary statistics for sample overlap between the GWAS and eQTL studies; required when overlap exceeds a few percent.
  • Cholesky-transformed genotype LD matrix — LD-matrix construction approach that respects the meta-analysis-with-related-individuals structure of the GWAS substrate.
  • PEER factor + RIN + PTPRC/CD45 covariate adjustment — standard eQTL-analysis covariates including immune-cell-content adjustment used by the Strug eQTL pipeline (FastQTL v.2.0).
  • 564-gene-by-tissue Bonferroni grid — explicit multiple-testing framework when colocalization is tested across 50 genes in a 1-Mb window × 14 CF-relevant GTEx tissues, with stage-2 threshold further adjusted by the number of stage-1 survivors.
  • Empirical FWER simulation — modeling the simulated locus on the actual MUC20/MUC4 and SLC6A14 LD patterns; mixing H03 and H04 null scenarios at varying proportions to interrogate composite-null Type-I error.

ALEX Welcome to episode two for May 25, 2026. The paper is Fan Wang and colleagues — Wang, Naim Panjwani, Cheng Wang, Lei Sun, and Lisa Strug from the Strug lab at Toronto and SickKids — published February 2022 in the American Journal of Human Genetics, titled "A flexible summary statistics-based colocalization method with application to the mucin cystic fibrosis lung disease modifier locus." The CF Foundation funded this work and they're applying their new method to a CF-specific lung-modifier locus we've talked about before, so this episode straddles methods and CF biology.

JORDAN Before we go in. Colocalization. We've heard the word, but what does it actually mean?

ALEX Good — let me unpack it carefully. Colocalization is a statistical technique that asks a simple question with deep biological implications. Suppose you have a chromosomal region — say a few hundred thousand base pairs of DNA — where you see two different statistical signals. One is a GWAS signal: variants in this region associate with a disease, in our case CF lung function. The other is an eQTL signal: variants in the same region associate with how much some nearby gene is expressed, in our case mucin-4 in airway epithelium. The two patterns sit on top of each other on the genome. The question is — are they driven by the same underlying causal variant, or are they two different variants that happen to be near each other? If they share a causal variant, we say the signals colocalize, and we can claim biological mechanism: the DNA variant causes a change in MUC4 expression, which causes a change in lung function. If they don't share a variant, then the GWAS and eQTL signals are coincidentally adjacent, and we cannot draw the mechanistic arrow.

JORDAN So colocalization is the bridge between "this region matters statistically" and "we know what gene the region is actually regulating."

ALEX Exactly. It is the workhorse method for assigning a candidate causal gene to a GWAS locus, which is the rate-limiting step in moving from GWAS to therapeutic target.

JORDAN OK and primer round. Eight terms I need defined. GWAS. eQTL. summary statistics. LD. allelic heterogeneity. Bayesian versus frequentist. composite null hypothesis. and what is MUC4 specifically.

ALEX GWAS — genome-wide association study — scans a million-plus common DNA variants across a cohort, testing each one for association with a trait. eQTL — expression quantitative trait locus — a DNA variant that influences how much a particular gene gets transcribed into RNA. Summary statistics are the per-variant outputs of a GWAS — for each variant, you get a regression beta, a standard error, a p-value, and effect-allele frequency. You don't need individual-level data to do follow-up analysis, just these per-variant summaries. LD — linkage disequilibrium — is the correlation between nearby DNA variants because they get inherited together as a block. High LD makes statistical fine-mapping hard: many variants look equally guilty because they're carrying each other's signal. Allelic heterogeneity is when more than one independent causal variant exists at the same locus — that is, two different variants in the same region each independently affect the trait. Bayesian methods like COLOC compute posterior probabilities — the probability of various hypotheses given the data and a prior. Frequentist methods like the one in this paper compute p-values — the probability of seeing data this extreme if the null hypothesis is true. Composite null hypothesis means the null isn't a single scenario, it's a union of several distinct scenarios that all need to be rejected together to claim colocalization. And MUC4 — mucin-4, tracheobronchial — is one of a family of mucin genes that encode the gel-forming proteins that make up airway mucus. In cystic fibrosis, airway mucus is abnormally thick and obstructs the airways, which is the central airway pathology in CF.

JORDAN And what's the locus we're talking about?

ALEX The chr3q29 locus contains both MUC4 and a sister gene MUC20 — mucin-20, cell-surface-associated — clustered together in a complex, highly polymorphic stretch of chromosome 3. This locus was identified as genome-wide significant for CF lung function severity in Corvol 2015 — the foundational five-locus CF Gene Modifier Consortium meta-analysis — but the GWAS itself can't tell you whether MUC4 or MUC20 is the mediating gene, because they're sitting on top of each other and both are biologically plausible.

JORDAN OK background and purpose. What problem motivated the work?

ALEX Several layered problems. The Strug group had two pieces of data in hand. One — GWAS summary statistics from the International CF Gene Modifier Consortium's 6,365-patient meta-analysis of CF lung function severity, with a clear signal at chr3q29 between MUC4 and MUC20. Two — eQTL summary statistics for both MUC4 and MUC20 in primary human nasal epithelial cells from 94 CF patients in the same consortium. Visually, when you overlay the GWAS signal and the MUC4 eQTL signal at the locus, they look like they line up. But — and this is the key motivation — none of the existing standard colocalization tools, applied to this locus, gave a clean answer that MUC4 was the responsible gene. COLOC said posterior probability 0.76 for MUC4, just below the conventional 0.8 threshold. COLOC2 said 0.0007 — much worse, completely missed it. SMR said p-value 0.003 raw, which goes to 0.379 after correcting for testing 143 gene-by-tissue pairs at the locus, not significant. SMR-multi did slightly better at adjusted p-value 0.03 but still middling. So they had a locus that everyone in CF biology believed was real — the GWAS signal had replicated at meta-analysis scale, the eQTL had clear visual coincidence — but the standard statistical machinery wouldn't certify the mechanistic claim.

JORDAN Why was the locus breaking the standard methods?

ALEX Four specific complications, all of which the paper enumerates. First — high LD across the region, meaning many SNPs are statistically interchangeable from the summary statistics' point of view. Second — allelic heterogeneity, meaning more than one independent causal signal in the region. The eQTL peak is much wider than the GWAS peak, which is the visual signature of multiple eQTL signals in LD. Third — overlapping samples. Of the 94 patients in the eQTL study, 85 are also in the 6,365-patient GWAS. This creates statistical correlation between the two sets of summary statistics that increases false-positive risk if not handled. Fourth — the GWAS summary statistics come from a meta-analysis that includes sib-pairs and other related individuals within sub-studies. Most colocalization methods assume unrelated independent samples; the meta-analysis-with-related-individuals structure is not standard.

ALEX The previous version of the Strug-group method — called Simple Sum, SS — handled high LD and allelic heterogeneity well, but had a known problem. Under one of the four null scenarios — specifically, when there's a strong GWAS signal at the locus but no eQTL signal at all — SS would over-call colocalization. So the Strug group had been using SS with an ad hoc fix: restrict its use to regions that already showed strong eQTL signal. This is a defensive hack — it controls the false-positive rate, but it discards locus-gene pairs where the eQTL signal is real but moderate.

JORDAN And the new paper fixes that.

ALEX Yes. The new method is called SS2 — Simple Sum two-stage. The fix is structural: rather than restricting analysis with an ad hoc rule, build the two-stage logic into the test itself. Stage one — a gene-based set test asking whether there is any eQTL signal at the locus at all. If stage one passes, only then proceed to stage two — the actual Simple Sum colocalization test. The stage-two significance threshold is Bonferroni-corrected for the number of genes that survived stage one. The framework controls Type-I error across all four scenarios of the composite null hypothesis — no GWAS signal and no eQTL, no GWAS but eQTL present, GWAS present but no eQTL, both present but at independent variants. It also accommodates summary statistics from meta-analysis with related individuals, and the overlapping-samples problem is handled by an existing decorrelation correction from LeBlanc and colleagues.

JORDAN Push back on a design choice. The paper picks COLOC, COLOC2, SMR, and SMR-multi as comparators. What about all the other methods?

ALEX The paper acknowledges eCAVIAR, ENLOC, GWAS-PW, JLIM, and S-PrediXcan as alternatives and explains why they didn't compare against each. eCAVIAR and ENLOC don't explicitly handle the composite null. GWAS-PW empirically estimates priors and is folded into COLOC2 essentially. JLIM requires individual-level expression data for permutation. S-PrediXcan and the TWAS family rely on prediction weights inferred from non-CF reference data — but the Strug group already had CF nasal epithelial expression directly, so importing external weights would have been less faithful than using the in-cohort data directly. The choice to compare against COLOC, COLOC2, SMR, and SMR-multi is defensible, but it does mean we're not getting a head-to-head against eCAVIAR or JLIM. That's a limitation worth knowing.

JORDAN Results. Did SS2 give them the answer they wanted?

ALEX Yes — and crucially, it gave a clean answer where the other methods didn't. Applied to the chr3q29 locus with MUC4 eQTLs from CF nasal epithelia: stage-one eQTL test p-value 3.16 × 10⁻⁷; stage-two colocalization p-value 1.31 × 10⁻⁵. After Bonferroni correction across 564 gene-by-tissue pairs tested across the locus and CF-relevant GTEx tissues, MUC4 in nasal epithelia survives as significant at adjusted stage-two p-value 1.49 × 10⁻³. For MUC20 in the same tissue, the stage-one test doesn't even pass — uncorrected p-value 0.083, so the stage-two test is not applied. The conclusion is clean: at the chr3q29 CF lung-modifier locus, the eQTL evidence prioritizes MUC4 as the responsible gene, not MUC20. The SS2 also extended the analysis across 50 genes within a 1-megabase window in 14 CF-relevant GTEx tissues, totaling 564 gene-by-tissue pairs; only 39 pairs surpassed the colocalization threshold, and MUC4 remained the only lung-relevant gene to do so. The simulations comparing power across the four methods on the same locus structure show SS2 was the most powerful method under several alternative scenarios — particularly when there are multiple eQTL signals in the region and one of them colocalizes.

JORDAN Did they answer the question?

ALEX Yes. The question was — at chr3q29 in CF lung disease, which gene mediates the GWAS signal? The answer is MUC4. The caveat the discussion section flags is that the chr3q29 region contains a known polymorphic 48-base-pair tandem repeat region ranging from 7 to 19 kilobases, with high-frequency copy-number variation that is highly variable across individuals — the genotyping array data suggests substantial CNV at the mucin cluster that the GWAS summary statistics don't fully capture. So the SS2 prioritization of MUC4 is the best gene-level answer the SNV-based data can give, but the actual causal variant may be a CNV that the SNV summary statistics tag but don't resolve.
