Blurb

Kingdom and Wright (2022) review what happens to the textbook concept of penetrance once you stop sampling from clinic and start sampling from population biobanks like UK Biobank and All of Us: penetrance for monogenic variant carriers averages around 60 percent or lower, the "Wellderly" cohort is full of healthy adults carrying supposedly pathogenic variants, and the mechanisms responsible — variant location, nonsense-mediated decay, allelic imbalance, cis- and trans-acting modifiers, somatic mosaicism, polygenic background, environment — are mostly cataloged but not yet decomposed. The proposed direction this episode suggests is a variance-decomposition study in the CFF whole-genome sequencing cohort that asks how much of CF severity is CFTR genotype versus polygenic background versus a candidate-modifier-gene burden. The open question is whether the CFF data are actually structured to support such a decomposition — we have not inspected them.

Methods to lift

  • Penetrance comparison frame: clinical cohort vs. population cohort — for any candidate "modifier" or pathogenic variant, report penetrance estimates separately in the disease-ascertained cohort and in a matched biobank slice, then triangulate.
  • Variance-decomposition framing — partition phenotype variance into causal-variant, allelic-balance, trans-modifier, and global-modifier components rather than reporting only association p-values.
  • Polygenic-risk-score overlay on monogenic carriers — adapt Fahed et al. 2020 (familial hypercholesterolemia) approach: stratify monogenic-variant carriers by PRS quintile and compare phenotype severity.
  • Modifier-gene candidate list for CF — Table 3 names TGFB1 (lung disease severity), IFRD1 (age of chronic infection onset), DCTN4 (Pseudomonas timing); each cite needs independent-replication audit.
  • Trinucleotide-repeat copy number as severity quantitative trait — analogy for any CF dataset where a quantitative carrier-state variable could predict severity gradient.

A genetic variant is just a place in the DNA where some people carry a different letter than the reference human genome. A phenotype is the trait or outcome you can actually see in the person — having lung disease, being tall, having a particular cholesterol level. A genotype is the combination of DNA letters the person carries at a place of interest. Penetrance is yes-or-no: out of all the people who carry a particular genotype, what fraction actually develop the expected phenotype? Fully penetrant means everybody with the genotype eventually shows the disease. Incompletely penetrant — or reduced penetrance — means only some fraction does. Variable expressivity is a different question. Among the people who do develop the phenotype, how severe is it, and what shape does it take? Some are mild, some are severe, some have one set of symptoms and others have a different set. The review also names a third concept called pleiotropy, which is when different variants in the same gene cause genuinely different diseases. Penetrance, expressivity, and pleiotropy overlap in practice, but they are three different questions.

ALEX Let me sweep them. NGS is next-generation sequencing, the umbrella term for the modern high-throughput DNA reading machines. WES is whole-exome sequencing, which reads only the roughly two percent of DNA that codes for protein. WGS is whole-genome sequencing, which reads everything, coding and non-coding. NMD is nonsense-mediated decay, a cellular quality-control system that destroys messenger RNA transcripts containing premature stop codons before they can be translated into broken proteins. LoF is loss-of-function, meaning a variant breaks the gene. GoF is gain-of-function, meaning a variant makes the gene do something extra or different. PTV is a protein-truncating variant — a variant that introduces a premature stop or causes a frameshift, cutting the protein short. SNP, single nucleotide polymorphism, is a single-letter change in the DNA that is common enough in the population to be called a polymorphism. UTR stands for untranslated region, the parts of a gene's messenger RNA at the 5-prime and 3-prime ends that are not translated into protein but help regulate when and how much protein gets made. TAD is a topologically associating domain, a region of the genome whose 3D folding keeps certain regulatory elements close to certain genes. JORDAN Okay, ground covered. Why does this question matter for what we are doing?

ALEX Because the working assumption when CFTR was cloned in 1989 was clean Mendelian inheritance. Two broken copies of CFTR, you get cystic fibrosis. One broken copy, you are a healthy carrier. That assumption held in clinic for a long time because cystic fibrosis clinics see the patients who are sick — they do not see the people walking around with two CFTR variants who never showed up. Kingdom and Wright are reviewing what happens when you stop sampling from clinic and start sampling from population biobanks like the UK Biobank and All of Us, which enroll hundreds of thousands of relatively healthy adults regardless of disease status. When you do that for monogenic-disease genes broadly — not just CFTR — penetrance estimates start collapsing. The headline number from one biobank study they cite, Goodrich and colleagues 2021, is that penetrance for monogenic variant carriers averages around 60 percent or lower for most conditions. In the "Wellderly" cohort, healthy people who reached age 80 with no chronic disease, 70 percent carried at least one heterozygous deleterious variant in a gene on the ACMG actionable list. In another elderly cohort — ASPREE — about 1.3 percent of healthy older adults carried a previously identified pathogenic variant including in Lynch syndrome and familial hypercholesterolemia, and never had the disease. The frame they keep returning to is ascertainment bias: clinical cohorts overestimate penetrance because they recruit from people who are already sick. Population cohorts may underestimate it because they enroll healthier-than-average people. The true population penetrance lives in between, and we cannot know it without looking at both. That frame matters because the cystic fibrosis literature has historically been built almost entirely on patient registry cohorts.

Kingdom and Wright argue the field has good lists of pathogenic variants but very thin catalogs of the modifiers that change how those variants land in a person.
