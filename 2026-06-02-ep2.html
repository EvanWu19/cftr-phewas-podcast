Blurb

Namjou et al. (2026, Frontiers in Genetics) build a multi-ancestry polygenic risk score (PRS) for the antibody (EBNA1 IgG) response to Epstein-Barr virus, then use it as a genetic stand-in for a blood measurement most people never had. Using UK Biobank (9,695 with serology; 80/20 discovery/validation split), an African-ancestry GWAS (N=4,365), and the French Milieu Intérieur cohort, they ran GWAS (PLINK), built the PRS with PRS-CSx (723,164 variants), and validated it (AUC 0.65 in UKB, 0.72 in Milieu Intérieur). The dominant GWAS signal sat in the HLA/MHC region (rs6927022, meta p=8.21×10⁻¹¹³); weaker novel signals appeared near NRG3 and within ARMC3 on chr10. Projecting the PRS onto all of UK Biobank, a PheWAS found a positive link to multiple sclerosis and inverse links to celiac disease and type 1 diabetes — directions confirmed against measured antibody titers. The transferable trick for a CFTR PheWAS: build a PRS for a CF endophenotype measured only in a subset, project it onto the full registry cohort, and scan its phenome-wide correlates.

Plain-English glossary

  • Additive genetic model: assumes each extra copy of a variant changes the trait by a fixed amount.
  • Antibody response / serology: the amount of a specific antibody in blood, and the lab measurement of it.
  • AUC / AUROC (Area Under the ROC Curve): 0.5 = coin-flip prediction, 1.0 = perfect; here a discrimination metric for the PRS.
  • EBNA1 (Epstein-Barr Nuclear Antigen 1): an EBV protein; anti-EBNA1 IgG marks past/settled infection.
  • EBV (Epstein-Barr Virus): near-ubiquitous herpesvirus; linked to some cancers and autoimmune disease.
  • FDR (False Discovery Rate): multiple-testing control (Benjamini–Hochberg); caps the expected fraction of false positives.
  • FUMA: web platform for functional annotation and gene mapping of GWAS results.
  • Genome-wide significance: the strict p < 5×10⁻⁸ threshold for a believable GWAS hit.
  • GWAS (Genome-Wide Association Study): genome-wide scan for variant–trait associations.
  • Heritability: the fraction of trait variation attributable to genetics (EBNA1 response ≈ 32–48%).
  • HLA / MHC: Human Leukocyte Antigen / Major Histocompatibility Complex — the dense, hyper-variable immune-gene region on chr6 that dominates many immune traits.
  • IgG: the antibody class marking established immune memory.
  • MFI (Median Fluorescence Intensity): the lab readout proportional to antibody quantity.
  • OR (Odds Ratio): how much a unit change in the predictor multiplies the odds of an outcome.
  • PCs (Principal Components): mathematical summaries of ancestry included as covariates to control population structure.
  • PheWAS (Phenome-Wide Association Study): one predictor tested against many phenotypes.
  • PRS (Polygenic Risk Score): one number summing many small genetic effects to estimate predisposition.
  • PRS-CSx: multi-ancestry Bayesian "continuous shrinkage" method for building portable PRS across populations.
  • R²: fraction of trait variance explained by the model.
  • SNP (Single Nucleotide Polymorphism): single-letter DNA difference.

Methods to lift

  • PRS-as-proxy design: build a PRS for a biomarker in a measured subset, then apply it to a much larger cohort lacking the measurement.
  • PRS-CSx: multi-ancestry PRS construction with a shared continuous-shrinkage prior — improves portability when ancestries differ.
  • 80/20 discovery/validation split within one cohort, plus an independent external validation cohort (Milieu Intérieur).
  • Extreme-quantile evaluation (top 10% vs bottom 10%) to demonstrate PRS signal — note this inflates apparent performance vs. full-distribution prediction.
  • PheWAS R package: case = ≥2 phecode occurrences on different days; control = none; ≥20-case minimum; FDR 0.05.
  • Cross-check genetic associations against the measured biomarker to confirm the PRS behaves like the real trait.
  • FUMA for gene prioritization from GWAS summary statistics.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: For a CF-relevant quantitative endophenotype measured in only a subset of patients (e.g., sweat chloride, residual chloride secretion, a circulating biomarker), develop a multi-ancestry PRS in the measured subset, project it onto the full CF cohort, and conduct a phenome-wide scan to map its clinical and complication correlates.

What to verify first

  • Whether the measured-subset discovery sample is large enough to build a PRS with real signal (this study had thousands; a CF endophenotype substudy may have dozens–hundreds).
  • Whether the candidate CF endophenotype is heritable enough to predict at all (EBNA1 response ≈ 32–48% heritable; confirm an analogous estimate exists).
  • Whether the trait's genetics is concentrated in one locus (e.g., HLA-like) — if so, a single-variant proxy beats a polygenic score.
  • Ancestry composition and availability of matched LD reference panels for PRS-CSx in the CF cohort.
  • That any PheWAS hits are treated as associations for follow-up, not mechanisms (no causal claim, possible misclassification in registry phenotypes).

ALEX Our second paper today is from 2026, in Frontiers in Genetics, by Bahram Namjou, Michael Lape, Matthew Weirauch, Kenneth Kaufman, and Leah Kottyan. The title is "Polygenic risk score and phenome-wide association study of the Epstein-Barr virus antibody response." We're reading it for a specific trick it pulls: it turns a blood-test measurement that only some people have into a genetic prediction that everyone can have — and then asks what that prediction is connected to across the whole medical record.

JORDAN That already sounds useful, but I need the words first. "Polygenic risk score," "antibody response," "Epstein-Barr" — give me the primer before we get into it.

ALEX Let's build it piece by piece. Epstein-Barr virus, or EBV, is an extremely common virus — most adults have been infected by adolescence, usually without serious illness, though it's linked to certain cancers and autoimmune diseases. When your body meets a virus, it makes antibodies — proteins that recognize and tag the invader. The amount of a specific antibody in your blood is your antibody response, and measuring it is called serology. This paper focuses on antibodies against one EBV protein called EBNA1 — Epstein-Barr Nuclear Antigen 1 — and specifically the IgG type of antibody, which marks past, settled infection. The measurement they use is a fluorescence readout abbreviated MFI, median fluorescence intensity — basically, how brightly the test lights up, which scales with how much antibody is present.

JORDAN Got it — EBNA1 IgG is a number from a blood test. Now, "polygenic risk score"?

ALEX A polygenic risk score, or PRS, is a single number that sums up the small genetic effects of many — often hundreds of thousands of — variants across your genome, each nudging a trait up or down a little. "Poly" meaning many, "genic" meaning genes. No single variant matters much, but added together with the right weights, they estimate your genetic predisposition for something. So a PRS for EBNA1 response estimates, from DNA alone, whether you're genetically inclined toward a high or low antibody level — even if no one ever drew your blood.

JORDAN And the rest of the alphabet soup — GWAS, PheWAS, HLA?

ALEX GWAS, again, is a Genome-Wide Association Study — scan every variant, find the ones associated with the trait. The variants are SNPs, single-letter DNA differences. HLA stands for Human Leukocyte Antigen — a dense cluster of immune-system genes on chromosome 6, also called the MHC or major histocompatibility complex. It's the most genetically variable region in the human genome and it dominates a lot of immune traits. And PheWAS — Phenome-Wide Association Study — is our recurring move: take one genetic predictor and test it against hundreds of diseases to map its reach.

JORDAN Okay. So what was unknown that made this study worth doing?

ALEX Two things. First, we knew from twin and family studies that how strongly you respond to EBV is partly inherited — heritability estimates run from roughly a third to a half. And we knew the HLA region was the biggest known driver. But nobody had built a portable genetic predictor — a PRS — that you could apply to a huge cohort where the antibody was never measured. Second, EBV antibody response has tantalizing links to disease, especially multiple sclerosis — MS, an autoimmune disease of the nervous system — and lupus. So if you could predict EBV response from DNA, you could ask, at population scale, which diseases track with it. That's the gap: a scalable genetic proxy plus a systematic phenome scan.

JORDAN Walk me through how they built and tested the predictor.

ALEX They started in UK Biobank, where EBNA1 IgG had actually been measured in 9,695 people. They split that: about 80 percent — 6,662 unrelated white British individuals — became the discovery set, where they ran the GWAS, and the other 20 percent — 1,965 people — was held back for validation. Crucially, they also pulled in GWAS summary results from 4,365 people of African ancestry from a previous study, and a separate European cohort from the Institut Pasteur in France called Milieu Interieur, about 1,000 people, as a second validation. The GWAS itself used PLINK with an additive genetic model, adjusting for age, sex, and ten principal components — those are mathematical summaries of ancestry that soak up population structure so it doesn't masquerade as a real signal.

JORDAN And the PRS-building tool? You hinted multi-ancestry matters here.

ALEX They used PRS-CSx. The base method, PRS-CS, uses Bayesian statistics with a "continuous shrinkage prior" — a principled way to shrink uncertain variant effects toward zero so you don't overfit. The "x" version does this across multiple ancestries at once, sharing information between European and African data to make the score travel better between populations. The final score used 723,164 variants. Here's a design choice worth poking: they defined PRS "success" by contrasting the top 10 percent of antibody levels against the bottom 10 percent — the extremes. That's a legitimate way to show a score has signal, but it inflates how impressive the numbers look compared to predicting across the whole middle of the distribution, where most people live.

JORDAN So did it work? Did they answer their question?

ALEX Partially, and honestly reported. The GWAS confirmed the expected: the strongest signal was smack in the HLA region — variant rs6927022, with a p-value around ten-to-the-minus-113 in the combined meta-analysis. A p-value that small means the association is astronomically unlikely to be chance. Outside HLA, they surfaced two newer, weaker signals on chromosome 10 — one near a gene called NRG3, one inside ARMC3 — that just reached or approached genome-wide significance, which is the strict 5-times-ten-to-the-minus-8 threshold the field uses. The PRS validated with an AUC — area under the curve, a 0-to-1 measure where 0.5 is a coin flip and 1.0 is perfect — of 0.65 in UK Biobank and 0.72 in the French cohort. They themselves say that's not good enough for clinical prediction, but it's fine for research stratification.

JORDAN And then the PheWAS — what lit up?

ALEX This is the payoff. They projected the PRS onto the full UK Biobank and ran a PheWAS using the PheWAS R package, defining a case as someone with at least two occurrences of a phecode and controlling the error rate with a method called FDR — false discovery rate — at five percent. The genetically predicted EBNA1 response was positively associated with multiple sclerosis, p around ten-to-the-minus-10 — consistent with the strong epidemiological EBV-MS link. But then a twist: it was inversely associated with several other autoimmune conditions, most strikingly celiac disease, at an extraordinary p-value, and type 1 diabetes. And they cross-checked: people with MS really did have higher measured antibody levels, people with celiac lower. So the genetic proxy behaved like the real thing.

JORDAN What about those two chromosome-10 hits, NRG3 and ARMC3? Are those just statistical curiosities, or is there a story?

ALEX They treat them as hypotheses, not conclusions, which is the right posture for signals that barely cross the line. The ARMC3 region is intriguing because it had been previously linked to antibody glycosylation — the sugar decorations on antibodies that tune how they function — so it's biologically suggestive that the same region would shape antibody response. The NRG3 signal is more of a question mark; they say it'll need fine-mapping to know what gene and what tissue it acts through. They also ran a pathway analysis — grouping the prioritized genes to see if any biological process was over-represented — and the top hit involved purinergic receptor signaling. Again, flagged as a lead to chase, not a settled mechanism. And separately, they confirmed the PRS could predict EBV seropositivity — whether you've been infected at all — though with the caveat that fewer than 5 percent of each cohort was actually seronegative, so there wasn't much signal to work with there.

JORDAN Why would the same antibody response push toward one autoimmune disease and away from another? That seems contradictory.

ALEX It's the most interesting puzzle in the paper, and they're careful not to over-claim. Their read is that a PRS for an immune trait is partly an HLA-driven object, and HLA alleles famously pull in opposite directions for different autoimmune diseases — the same immune-gene neighborhood that raises celiac risk can lower another condition's. So "EBV antibody response" as a genetic construct is really a bundle of shared immune architecture, not a clean single mechanism. And they stress repeatedly: PheWAS shows association, not causation.

JORDAN I'm on a cystic fibrosis team. Where's the thread for me?

ALEX Here's one possible direction this episode suggests — and it's the trick, not the EBV biology, that transfers. In CF research, lots of meaningful measurements exist for only a subset of patients. Sweat chloride is measured widely, but a deeper endophenotype like residual chloride secretion — measured in a rectal-biopsy assay we'll actually talk about in the next episode — exists only in small research substudies. The plain-English idea: take a CF quantitative trait that's well measured in a subset, build a PRS for it in that subset, and then carry that score genetically onto the whole registry cohort that never had the measurement — and run a PheWAS to see what clinical outcomes the genetically predicted trait tracks with.

JORDAN Give me the formal one-liner.

ALEX Roughly: "For a CF-relevant quantitative endophenotype measured in only a subset of patients, develop a multi-ancestry PRS in the measured subset, project it onto the full CF cohort, and conduct a phenome-wide scan to map its clinical and complication correlates." That's the conditional artifact.

JORDAN And the gap — what's proven versus what I'd have to check?

ALEX Conditional on that direction, here's the split. What this paper closes: it's a clean template that a PRS can serve as a genetic stand-in for an unmeasured biomarker, that multi-ancestry PRS-CSx improves portability across populations, and that PRS-driven PheWAS recovers known biology, lending it credibility. What it leaves open is mostly about feasibility at CF scale. PRS-CSx still needs a reasonably powered discovery GWAS — they had thousands of people with the measurement; a CF endophenotype substudy might have dozens to a few hundred, so a thing we'd need to check is whether the discovery sample is large enough to build a score with any real signal. We'd also need to verify the trait is actually heritable enough to predict — EBNA1 response had 30-to-50 percent heritability; not every CF measurement will. And because HLA so dominated this analysis, we'd want to check whether a CF endophenotype's genetics is similarly concentrated in one locus or genuinely polygenic, since a one-locus trait doesn't really need a polygenic score. Finally, the same causation caveat applies: any PheWAS hits would be associations to follow up, not mechanisms — none of this presumes you've inspected the CF genetic data or fixed a design.
