Blurb

The founding PheWAS paper. Denny and colleagues at Vanderbilt built a translation table mapping ICD-9 billing codes into 776 disease groupings ("phecodes"), then used the first 6,005 European-Americans in BioVU to demonstrate that scanning a single SNP across the phenome — rather than scanning the genome for one trait — was methodologically defensible. They picked five SNPs with seven previously published GWAS associations as positive controls; four of seven replicated at p between 2.8×10⁻⁶ and 0.011, and 19 previously unknown SNP-phecode associations cleared p<0.01. The 2010 paper's role in the field is to be the methodology citation in every later PheWAS introduction — and the paper that put "PheWAS" into the literature. For a future CFTR PheWAS, it gives the row-versus-column grid logic; the actual machinery comes from later calibrated tools (SAIGE 2018, SAIGE-GENE+ 2022).

Methods to lift

  • ICD-to-phecode translation table — researcher-curated rollup grouping ~15,000 ICD codes into ~776 disease groupings (now grown to ~1,800+ in current phecode versions). Vanderbilt-maintained reference; later canonical Wei 2017 and Wu 2019 extensions to ICD-10.
  • Case/control by phecode rule — for each phecode, "case" = patient has at least one billing instance of any ICD code mapped to that phecode; "control" = patient has none of those ICD codes; ambiguous cases are typically excluded from controls (so a patient with a related-but-different phecode is not used as control).
  • Phecode-by-SNP grid logic — for each (SNP, phecode) pair, run a regression of case/control status on genotype with appropriate covariates; report a p-value per cell.
  • Multiple-testing reasoning — report at p<0.01 for descriptive scans plus Bonferroni or FDR for confirmatory thresholds; this paper used p<0.01 for descriptive feasibility, not discovery.
  • Reference cohort — BioVU at Vanderbilt; the prototype EMR-linked biobank that anchors the entire Vanderbilt PheWAS pipeline.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: one direction this paper suggests is a Denny-style PheWAS-grid scan of the established CFF-cohort modifier loci (SLC26A9, SLC9A3, EHF, SLC6A14, TCF7L2, plus the Zhou 2023 secondary signals) against a CFFPR-derived endpoint catalog, with multiple-testing correction calibrated to the active endpoint count and the dependence structure between endpoints (per Karczewski 2022 phenotype-pruning logic). Modern calibration via SAIGE 2018 for binary endpoints with case prevalence below ~2% and a standard logistic mixed model for more balanced endpoints. This is one of several directions opened by the paper, not a committed study design.

What to verify first

  • CFFPR endpoint catalog — what structured fields the registry tracks, how comparably they are recorded across centers, and which qualify as PheWAS-suitable (binary, sufficiently prevalent, harmonized).
  • CFFPR-to-phecode translation — whether existing CFFPR fields can be used directly or need translation into a phecode-style format; whether such a translation table exists in the literature.
  • Modifier-locus inventory — committing to the row list (which loci to scan) is a separate scientific decision; gap analysis is the deliverable that pins this down.
  • Prior precedent — Aksit 2022 (today's ep3) is the closest CF-cohort published precedent; the gap analysis must be honest about how much of this design space it has already covered.
  • Longitudinal vs snapshot endpoints — Denny 2010 used snapshot ICD status; the CFFPR records longitudinal trajectories. Mixed-model PheWAS extensions live in SAIGE 2018 and Karczewski 2022, not in this paper.

PheWAS: demonstrating the feasibility of a phenome-wide scan to discover gene-disease associations.

Denny JC · Bioinformatics · 2010

ALEX Good morning. Today's second paper is Denny and colleagues, 2010, "PheWAS: demonstrating the feasibility of a phenome-wide scan to discover gene-disease associations," published in Bioinformatics, volume 26, issue 9, pages 1205 to 1210. Lead author is Joshua Denny at Vanderbilt University, senior author is Dana Crawford. This is the founding PheWAS paper. The acronym we keep using — phenome-wide association study — was coined here. We're reading it because it is a very small, very early-2010s, six-thousand-person proof-of-concept that, fifteen years later, became the methodological backbone of every biobank phenome-wide scan we've covered so far — UKBB, FinnGen, AoU, Genebass. Reading the founding paper is worth a session because the limitations the original authors named in 2010 are still the live limitations today.

JORDAN Vocabulary first. Some of this is repeating from yesterday and I want it.

ALEX Yes. DNA is the molecule that stores genetic information. A variant is a position in the genome where people differ; a single-nucleotide polymorphism, abbreviated SNP, is the most common kind of variant — one DNA letter swapped for another. Genotype is the specific letter pair you carry at a SNP. Allele is one of the alternative letters. A gene is a stretch of DNA that codes for a protein. GWAS, genome-wide association study, takes one trait and scans the genome for SNPs associated with it. PheWAS, phenome-wide association study, is the inverse — take one SNP and scan many traits for the SNP-trait associations. A biobank is an organized research cohort that links DNA samples to electronic health records.

JORDAN And what's a phecode and why does it keep coming up?

ALEX That's the central concept of this paper, so let me set it up. Hospitals bill insurance using a structured diagnosis-code system called ICD, the International Classification of Diseases. The 2010 version in use in the United States was ICD-9. ICD-9 has on the order of fifteen thousand codes — far too granular to use as the analysis unit in a phenome-wide scan, because most codes are seen only a handful of times and many codes describe variations of the same underlying condition. The Vanderbilt team built a translation table that maps groups of related ICD-9 codes into broader disease groupings they called phecodes — at this point, in the 2010 paper, they had 776 disease groupings. So a phecode is a researcher-curated rollup of ICD-9 billing codes into a single disease unit suitable for use as the dependent variable in a regression. Every PheWAS we've covered uses descendants of this same Denny lab phecode system, occasionally extended to ICD-10 in later papers like Wu 2019.

JORDAN A few more terms before we go in?

ALEX Two. Electronic medical record, abbreviated EMR, is the digital chart hospitals keep on every patient — diagnoses, lab values, medications, billing codes. EMRs are the data substrate for biobank phenotyping. BioVU is Vanderbilt's biobank — the local cohort this paper used, which links Vanderbilt EMRs to DNA samples drawn from leftover clinical blood. That's the vocabulary. Add ICD, phecode, EMR, and BioVU to the carry-over list.

JORDAN Tell me what they were trying to prove.

ALEX A specific feasibility claim. By 2010 there had been roughly three years of GWAS at scale — Wellcome Trust Case Control Consortium 2007, type-2 diabetes meta-analyses 2008, the standard SNP-by-disease pairs were known. The Vanderbilt team observed that the natural inverse — pick a known risk SNP and check what other diseases it associates with in EMR-linked biobank data — had never been done systematically. There were two reasons it hadn't been done. First, no one had built a translation table from ICD-9 codes into a disease-level analysis unit at the right granularity. Second, there was no benchmark for whether such a scan could replicate known associations using nothing but billing-code-derived phenotypes — billing codes are notoriously noisy, and researchers were skeptical that a SNP-disease association proven on careful research-grade phenotyping would survive a switch to ICD-9-only outcomes. The paper's job was to settle both questions with a small, clean proof of concept.

JORDAN How did they design it?

ALEX They picked five SNPs that were already established risk loci in the published GWAS literature. The SNPs collectively had seven previously reported disease associations across atrial fibrillation, Crohn's disease, carotid artery stenosis, coronary artery disease, multiple sclerosis, systemic lupus erythematosus, and rheumatoid arthritis. They then took the first six thousand and five European-American participants in BioVU — Vanderbilt's biobank — for whom DNA had been genotyped at those five SNPs, and pulled their full ICD-9 billing-code histories from the Vanderbilt EMR. They ran their newly-built code-translation table against each participant's billing history, producing a 776-by-six-thousand matrix of phecode case-or-control status. For each of the five SNPs they then ran a PheWAS — for each of the 776 phecodes, regress phecode status on SNP genotype with appropriate covariates, and report the p-value. Five SNPs times 776 phecodes equals 3,880 association tests in total. Two binary outcomes for the success of the design. First: of the seven previously reported SNP-disease associations, how many does the EMR-and-billing-code PheWAS recover? Second: among the remaining association tests, are there novel signals or only random noise?

JORDAN I want to push back on something. Three thousand eight hundred eighty tests at a five-times-ten-to-the-minus-eight Bonferroni — they had no chance of declaring genome-wide significance. What were they actually claiming?

ALEX That's the right pushback. They were not claiming statistical discovery in the modern PheWAS sense. They were claiming methodological feasibility — that the technique could replicate known signals at p-value thresholds appropriate for a 3,880-test grid, which is around p less than 0.0001 by Bonferroni or p less than 0.01 if you accept FDR. So when they report results, they report at p less than 0.01, and they're explicit that follow-up validation is required. The 2010 framing is "is this a defensible technique," not "is this a discovery engine." It became a discovery engine only after biobanks reached UKBB and AoU scale — meaning hundreds of thousands rather than six thousand participants — which is where every modern paper we've covered lives.

JORDAN So what did the 2010 numbers actually say?

ALEX Four of the seven previously reported SNP-disease associations were replicated at p-values between 2.8 times ten to the minus six and 0.011. Three of seven were not replicated. Both numbers matter. The four-of-seven replication tells you that EMR-derived ICD-9 phenotypes are noisy but not so noisy that a clean published association is invisible. The three-of-seven non-replication tells you that ICD-9 billing codes are systematically lossier than research-grade phenotyping, and which SNP-disease pairs survive depends on how cleanly the disease maps to ICD-9. Crohn's disease, atrial fibrillation, systemic lupus — those have specific ICD-9 codes and replicated. The ones that didn't replicate were conditions where the ICD-9 mapping is ambiguous or where prevalence in BioVU is too low at six thousand participants. Beyond replication: the paper reports nineteen previously unknown statistical associations between the five SNPs and other phecodes at p less than 0.01. They do not claim those nineteen are discoveries. They explicitly call them "previously unknown statistical associations" requiring further evaluation.

JORDAN Did they answer their feasibility question?

ALEX Yes. The four-of-seven replication at appropriate p-values, paired with non-trivial novel-signal density in the rest of the phenome, demonstrated that ICD-9-based phenome-wide scanning was a working method. The 2010 paper's role in the field is exactly this — it's the methodology citation in the introduction of every later PheWAS paper, and it's the paper that put the word "PheWAS" into the literature. Whether the four replications and nineteen novel signals were biologically informative was not the point. The point was that a phenome-wide scan on EMR-linked biobank data was a defensible thing to attempt.

JORDAN One specific direction this paper opens for the CFTR PheWAS — plain English.

ALEX Plain English first. Denny 2010 says that if you have a cohort of people, their DNA, and their billing codes, you can systematically scan the entire phenome — not just one disease at a time — for unexpected gene-disease links. We have something better than Denny had in 2010. We have about five thousand cystic fibrosis patients with whole genome sequencing and a curated registry — the CFF Patient Registry — that already organizes their clinical history into structured fields. We don't even need to start from raw ICD billing codes; the registry has already done much of the rollup. So the natural question is: which CFTR-modifier locus, scanned across the registry's full set of structured outcomes, picks up unexpected signal — pleiotropy beyond the one or two phenotypes that locus was originally discovered against?

JORDAN And the formal version?

ALEX One direction this paper suggests is: take the modifier loci already established in the CFF cohort GWAS and WGS literature — the ones we've been cataloging, including SLC26A9, SLC9A3, EHF, SLC6A14, TCF7L2, and the Zhou 2023 secondary signals — and run a PheWAS-style scan of each locus against the curated phecode-equivalent endpoints in the CFFPR. Use Denny 2010's grid logic — one variant against many endpoints, with multiple-testing correction calibrated to the actual endpoint count — and use modern calibration tools — SAIGE for binary phecodes, with appropriate handling of phecode pruning per Karczewski 2022 / Genebass methods we covered Friday. To be clear, this is one of several directions the paper suggests, not a committed study design. We have not committed to a research design and we have not seen the CFF whole-genome variant calls.

JORDAN Given that proposed direction, where's the gap?

ALEX Conditional on a Denny-style PheWAS scan of CFFPR endpoints against established CFTR-modifier loci, this paper's logic — one variant scanned across many phecodes — closes the question of whether such a design is methodologically defensible. It leaves four things open that we'd need to verify before committing. First, the CFFPR endpoint catalog is not a phecode list. The registry has structured fields like CF-related diabetes status, chronic Pseudomonas colonization, FEV1 percentile, BMI z-score, transplant status, modulator drug initiation date — these are not ICD phecodes. We would need either a translation step from CFFPR fields into a phecode-style endpoint list, or to commit to using CFFPR fields directly and separately publish the endpoint definitions. Second, the multiple-testing correction needs to know the actual endpoint count and the dependence structure between endpoints — Karczewski 2022's pairwise r-squared less than 0.5 phenotype-pruning recipe, which we covered Friday, is the right machinery here. Third, Denny 2010 used six thousand participants and replicated 4 of 7 known signals; CF cohorts at five thousand should have similar power for moderate-effect modifier signals on common phenotypes, but underpowered for rare endpoints. Fourth, the registry's longitudinal phenotype layer — repeated FEV1 measurements per patient over years — is not what Denny 2010 used; Denny used snapshot ICD-9 status. Using longitudinal phenotypes correctly requires mixed-model PheWAS not covered by this 2010 paper, and that machinery comes from the SAIGE 2018 and SAIGE-GENE+ 2022 papers.

JORDAN And what would Runyu need to verify before this becomes a real plan?

ALEX Three things. One, we'd need to inventory the CFFPR's structured endpoint catalog and decide which fields qualify as PheWAS-suitable endpoints — binary, sufficiently prevalent, comparably defined across reporting centers. Two, we'd need to commit to a modifier-locus list. The "scan all loci against all endpoints" framing is a methodological grid; the question of which loci to put in the rows is a separate scientific decision and depends on how comprehensive we want the gap analysis to be. Three, we'd need to establish whether prior CFF-cohort PheWAS-style work exists that we might be replicating rather than extending.
