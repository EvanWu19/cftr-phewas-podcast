Blurb

Collaco and colleagues at the Cutting laboratory at Johns Hopkins used 812 CF patients from the US CF Twin and Sibling Study, with missing data supplemented by the Cystic Fibrosis Foundation Patient Registry, to test whether secondhand smoke exposure interacts with CFTR genotype and modifier-gene genotype to alter cystic fibrosis lung-function trajectory. Of patients with reported home secondhand smoke data, 23.2 percent were exposed; smoke exposure associated with a 9.8 percentile-point cross-sectional and 6.1 percentile-point longitudinal FEV1 decrease independent of socioeconomic status. Three significant gene-environment interactions were reported: CFTR non-F508del homozygotes (additional −12.8 percentile points, P=0.001), TGFβ1 −509 TT homozygotes (−22.7 percentile points, P=0.006), and TGFβ1 codon-10 CC homozygotes (−20.3 percentile points, P=0.005). Proposed direction (conditional): re-examine the gene-environment interaction question in the modern CFGP whole-genome-sequencing cohort against the four loci already established in the recent CF-modifier GWAS literature. Open question: whether CFFPR secondhand smoke exposure data is harmonized cleanly enough across years to support modulator-era stratified analysis.

Plain-English glossary

  • CBAVD: Congenital bilateral absence of the vas deferens — a male-fertility phenotype linked to milder CFTR variants. Not discussed in Collaco 2008 directly but relevant to the broader CFTR-carrier-phenotype literature.
  • CFTR: Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane conductance Regulator — the CF disease gene on chromosome 7, encoding a chloride-ion channel on epithelial cell surfaces.
  • CFFPR: Cystic Fibrosis Foundation Patient Registry — the US national CF registry, which Runyu's team works on.
  • CF Twin and Sibling Study: Cutting-lab family-based CF cohort, enrolled twins and full siblings concordant for CF, started in the 1990s.
  • Cross-sectional FEV1: Single-time-point measurement of forced expiratory volume in one second.
  • F508del (ΔF508): The most common CF-causing variant — a 3-base deletion removing phenylalanine at amino-acid position 508 of CFTR.
  • FEV1: Forced expiratory volume in one second — the standard pulmonary function test summary statistic; reported as percentile of an age/sex/height-matched CF reference.
  • Gene-environment interaction: A statistical model where the effect of an environmental variable on outcome depends on a genetic variable, captured by a non-zero interaction term.
  • Longitudinal FEV1: Rate of FEV1 change over multiple time points.
  • MeSH: Medical Subject Headings — the controlled-vocabulary indexing system PubMed uses to tag papers.
  • Modifier gene: A gene whose variation modulates the severity or features of disease caused by a different primary gene (here, CFTR).
  • Recessive: Inheritance pattern where two affected copies of a gene are required for the disease phenotype.
  • Secondhand smoke: Environmental tobacco smoke from someone else's smoking, in this paper measured by home-exposure and maternal-smoking-during-gestation questionnaire items.
  • TGFβ1 / TGFB1: Transforming Growth Factor Beta 1 — a fibrosis- and immune-modulating cytokine encoded by TGFB1; one of the earliest-replicated CF modifier genes. Two candidate-gene-era SNPs anchor most of the literature: the −509 promoter SNP and the codon-10 SNP.
  • Twin-and-sibling design: Family-based study design comparing within-pair phenotype variation between relatives concordant for disease; controls for shared genetic background and shared early-life environment.

Methods to lift

  • Family-based twin-and-sibling enrollment substrate — within-pair design controls for shared genetic background and shared early-life environment, dramatically reducing noise versus a population-cohort design.
  • CFFPR-data supplementation — Cutting-lab approach to fill missing variables in the Twin-and-Sibling substrate using the national registry; a precedent for linking the family-based and registry data sources Runyu's team has access to.
  • Cross-sectional and longitudinal FEV1 endpoints, reported as percentile-points — age/sex/height-normalized CF-reference percentile, not raw spirometry; the convention in CF modifier literature.
  • Smoke exposure measurement from questionnaire — home secondhand smoke and active maternal smoking during gestation as two separate variables; questionnaire-based and thus subject to recall bias.
  • Stratified regression with formal gene-environment interaction terms — testing whether the smoke effect differs across genotype subgroups via interaction terms in the regression model.
  • Socioeconomic-status confounding check — explicit SES adjustment to verify the smoke effect is not driven by an SES-smoke correlation.
  • Candidate-gene CFTR / TGFβ1 stratification — pre-specified genotype subgroups (CFTR F508del-homozygote vs non-homozygote, TGFβ1 −509 TT vs other, TGFβ1 codon-10 CC vs other) drove the interaction tests.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage: re-examine the Collaco 2008 gene-environment interaction question in the modern CFGP WGS cohort, replacing the candidate-gene TGFβ1 −509 and codon-10 SNPs with WGS-callable common-variant lead SNPs at the established modern CF lung-modifier loci — MUC4 / MUC20 (chr3q29), AGTR2 / SLC6A14 (chrXq22-q23), EHF / APIP (chr11p13), and CHP2 (chr16). Test for statistical interaction between locus-level genotype and CFFPR-recorded secondhand smoke exposure on cross-sectional and longitudinal FEV1, with mixed-effects models that handle repeated measures and family-based relatedness from the twin-and-sibling substrate. Stratify or interaction-test for modulator-era patients separately.

What to verify first

  • Whether CFFPR has a clean, year-stratified, longitudinal secondhand-smoke-exposure variable linkable to CFGP genotype data, and whether the CFFPR exposure-capture protocol has changed in ways that introduce era-confounding.
  • Whether the original CF Twin and Sibling Study families remain represented in the CFGP cohort and whether the within-family power-advantage that helped Collaco can be preserved.
  • Whether modern CF-modifier-GWAS lead SNPs at MUC4 / MUC20, AGTR2 / SLC6A14, EHF / APIP, and CHP2 are reliably called from CFGP WGS — and whether their effect-size estimates are stable across the cohort.
  • Whether the TGFβ1 −509 and codon-10 candidate variants are still in the modern CFGP variant call set and whether subgroup sizes for the rare TT and CC homozygote categories survive modern QC filtering.
  • Whether ivacaftor and Trikafta era patients should be analyzed separately, since modulator therapy substantially changes the airway biology that smoke exposure interacts with — the Collaco 2008 cohort predates the modulator era entirely.
  • Whether smoking-prevalence decline in the U.S. since 2008 has left enough exposed CF patients in the modern cohort to power gene-environment interaction tests at the effect sizes Collaco reports.
  • Whether questionnaire-based secondhand-smoke measurement in the registry era has the same exposure misclassification profile across genotype subgroups — differential misclassification biases interaction tests.

Gap-map: closes / leaves open

Closes:

  • That secondhand smoke exposure is associated with lower CF lung function independent of socioeconomic status, in a cohort that the Cutting lab built on the same substrate Runyu's team can access today.
  • That CFFPR-derived smoke-exposure variables can be used in CF gene-environment work, at least at 2008-era data-collection standards.
  • That gene-environment interaction signals of ~20 percentile-point magnitude are detectable in the Twin-and-Sibling substrate.
  • That CFTR genotype (F508del-homozygote vs non) and TGFβ1 modifier genotype each modulate the smoke-FEV1 effect at conventional statistical significance.

Leaves open:

  • Whether the same interaction signals replicate in modulator-era CF cohorts.
  • Whether the candidate-gene TGFβ1 hits replicate in modern WGS-era genome-wide modifier analyses (no published WGS-era replication of TGFβ1 candidate SNPs that we are aware of).
  • Whether the modern CF lung-modifier GWAS loci (MUC4 / MUC20, AGTR2 / SLC6A14, EHF / APIP, CHP2) show analogous gene-by-smoke interaction.
  • Whether secondhand-smoke gene-environment interaction extends beyond the lung phenotype to CFRD, hepatobiliary disease, or other CF complications.
  • Whether the within-family power-advantage of the Cutting twin-and-sibling design is preserved in the post-CFGP-WGS analytic framework, or whether a population-style mixed-effects model dilutes it.
  • Whether population-scale (not family-scale) CF cohorts can ever match the interaction-detection power Collaco achieved on 812 patients within families.

ALEX Episode three. Today's CFTR cohort paper is "Interactions between secondhand smoke and genes that affect cystic fibrosis lung disease," published in JAMA in 2008. First author J Michael Collaco, senior author Garry Cutting, both at Johns Hopkins. PMID 18230779. I have to flag something at the top — this paper is closed-access at EuropePMC despite having a PMC ID, so what we have to work with is the published abstract, the journal metadata, and the MeSH headings. We're going to do this episode at the abstract-and-substrate level, not the full-text level. I'll say so on-air again when we get to the numbers.

JORDAN Got it. Why this paper today?

ALEX Because it's one of the foundational JAMA-tier CF gene-environment papers, and it was the first to make a clean quantitative claim that secondhand smoke exposure has a measurably larger effect on CF lung function in people with specific modifier genotypes than in others. Gene-environment interaction is a hard concept to do well at any cohort size, and CF is one of the rare diseases where the field tried it seriously. Reading this paper tells us how the Cutting lab built a substrate — the CF Twin and Sibling Study — that could even ask the question.

JORDAN All right, hold on, plain English. Cystic fibrosis itself. I want a clean primer.

ALEX CF is a recessive single-gene disease. The gene is called CFTR — Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane conductance Regulator — on chromosome 7. CFTR encodes a chloride-ion channel that sits on the surface of secretory epithelial cells. When CFTR is broken, the airway surface liquid in the lungs dehydrates, mucus thickens, mucociliary clearance fails, and the lungs progressively scar and get infected. The lung disease drives most CF morbidity and mortality, though the disease also affects pancreas, intestine, sweat glands, sinuses, and the reproductive tract.

JORDAN F508del?

ALEX The single most common pathogenic variant in CFTR — a deletion of three bases that removes a phenylalanine at amino-acid position 508. About 70 percent of CF patients of European descent carry at least one copy of F508del; about 40 to 50 percent are F508del homozygous. The convention in the literature is "F508del homozygotes" — the genetically most-uniform CF subgroup, which is why family-based and twin-based CF research often restricts to that genotype.

JORDAN FEV1.

ALEX Forced expiratory volume in one second. The standard pulmonary-function metric. You blow into a spirometer as hard and fast as you can, and the device measures how much air you can push out in the first second. FEV1 is the most widely used summary of CF lung-disease severity. The numbers in this paper are reported as percentile-points — meaning where a patient falls in the FEV1 distribution for their age, sex, height, and CF reference population, rather than raw liters per second.

JORDAN Gene-environment interaction. What does that mean precisely?

ALEX An effect of one factor — say, smoke exposure — that *depends on* the level of another factor — say, a person's TGFβ1 genotype. Mathematically, it shows up as a non-zero interaction term in a regression model that includes both the environmental variable and the genetic variable. Biologically, the claim is that smoke exposure hurts more in some genotypes than others. Statistically, gene-environment interaction is notoriously hard to detect — it requires more samples than main-effect tests, the interaction effect has to be biologically real, and the environmental variable has to be measured accurately.

JORDAN TGFβ1.

ALEX Transforming Growth Factor Beta 1. A cytokine — a small signaling protein — that drives tissue scarring, immune modulation, and fibrosis. TGFβ1 was one of the earliest replicated CF lung-disease modifier genes. The candidate-gene-era work pointed to two specific positions in the gene: the −509 promoter SNP, where the T allele is associated with higher TGFβ1 expression, and the codon 10 SNP, where the C allele encodes the proline-containing variant. The 2008 paper uses both.

JORDAN Twin and sibling study.

ALEX A family-based research design. You enroll pairs of relatives — identical twins, fraternal twins, full siblings — all of whom have CF. The within-pair comparison lets you control for shared genetic background and shared early-life environment, which dramatically reduces the noise compared to a population-cohort study. The Cutting-lab US CF Twin and Sibling Study is the substrate behind a large fraction of CF modifier-genetics work in the 2000s. CFFPR — the Cystic Fibrosis Foundation Patient Registry — supplements the data, which is interesting from your perspective because that's the registry Runyu's team works on.

ALEX Background and purpose. By 2008 the CF modifier-genetics field had spent about a decade looking at why CF patients with the same CFTR genotype — even the same F508del-homozygote diagnosis — had wildly different lung disease severity. Some F508del homozygotes had near-normal FEV1 well into adulthood; others were transplanted as teenagers. The candidate-gene literature had implicated TGFβ1 and a handful of other genes as modifiers, but the effect sizes were modest and replication was inconsistent.

JORDAN And environment?

ALEX The known environmental risk factors for CF lung disease were limited but real. Direct active smoking — rare in CF patients because of disease severity — was known to harm. Smoke exposure from a parent or housemate had been suspected but inconsistently shown. The contested question Collaco and the Cutting lab set up was: does secondhand smoke exposure interact with CFTR genotype or modifier genotype to produce larger lung-function effects than smoke exposure alone or genotype alone?

JORDAN Why does that question matter for our project?

ALEX Two reasons. First, mechanistically, if smoke exposure changes risk *more* in some patients than others, that means the gene tells you something actionable for the patient — counseling, environmental intervention, prognosis. Second, methodologically, gene-environment interaction is one of the harder things to do in CF cohort genetics, and the substrate Collaco uses — twin-and-sibling pairs with CFFPR supplementation — is the same substrate Runyu's team has access to today, just expanded by a decade and a half plus whole-genome sequencing.

ALEX Methods. The substrate is 812 CF patients with reported secondhand smoke exposure data and 780 with reported maternal-smoking-during-gestation data. The cohort was recruited between October 2000 and October 2006 and is primarily US-based. The CF Twin and Sibling Study did the primary data collection; CFFPR supplemented missing values. Patients ranged from infants to adults — the MeSH terms list adolescent, adult, child, child preschool, infant, female, and male — which is important because the FEV1 analysis has to handle age-stratified reference values.

JORDAN Outcomes?

ALEX Two — cross-sectional and longitudinal lung function. Cross-sectional FEV1 is a single time-point measurement; longitudinal FEV1 is the rate of change over multiple measurements. They report effects on both. The exposure measure is questionnaire-based secondhand smoke exposure in the home and active maternal smoking during pregnancy.

JORDAN Statistics?

ALEX Regression analysis stratified by environmental and genetic factors, with formal interaction terms for gene-by-smoke combinations. They explicitly tested whether socioeconomic status confounded the smoke-FEV1 relationship and report that it did not — a reasonable check given that smoke exposure correlates with SES in many cohorts.

ALEX One design choice I want to push back on. The study is retrospective and questionnaire-based on the smoke-exposure side, which means the exposure measurement is much noisier than the genotype measurement. Gene-environment interaction tests are especially sensitive to differential exposure misclassification — if smoke exposure is reported more accurately in some subgroups than others, that bias propagates into the interaction term. The paper acknowledges the limitation implicitly by emphasizing the within-twin-and-sibling design, but the SES-confounding test doesn't actually address exposure misclassification.

ALEX Results. I'll flag again — these numbers come from the abstract, not from the full text we don't have.

JORDAN Right.

ALEX 188 of 812 — 23.2 percent — were exposed to secondhand smoke in the home. 129 of 780 — 16.5 percent — had mothers who smoked actively during pregnancy. Home secondhand smoke exposure associated with a 9.8 percentile-point decrease in cross-sectional FEV1, P less than 0.001, and a 6.1 percentile-point decrease in longitudinal FEV1, P=0.007. Both effects survived after adjustment for socioeconomic status.

JORDAN Gene-environment interactions?

ALEX Three significant ones. First, CFTR non-F508del-homozygotes had an additional 12.8 percentile-point decrease in FEV1 from smoke exposure, P=0.001. Second, TGFβ1 −509 TT homozygotes had a 22.7 percentile-point decrease, P=0.006. Third, TGFβ1 codon-10 CC homozygotes had a 20.3 percentile-point decrease, P=0.005. Each interaction is reported on top of the main smoke effect.

JORDAN Did they answer the question?

ALEX Partly. They demonstrated that any home secondhand smoke exposure was associated with lower CF lung function across multiple measures, and they demonstrated three specific genotype-stratified subgroups in which the smoke effect was substantially larger than in the rest of the cohort. The biological story is consistent — TGFβ1 promotes fibrosis, smoke promotes airway inflammation, and the two should plausibly interact at the cellular level. But the paper does not directly demonstrate the mechanism, only the statistical interaction at the population scale.

ALEX Beat six. What specific direction does this paper suggest for Runyu's work?

ALEX One direction is the following. The 2008 substrate — CF Twin and Sibling Study plus CFFPR data — is a 17-year-younger version of what's available now. The CFGP whole-genome-sequencing cohort, which is built on substantially overlapping enrollment infrastructure, has variant calls across the entire genome rather than the TGFβ1 candidate-SNP set Collaco used. So one extension of this paper is a CFGP-WGS-era re-examination of the secondhand smoke gene-environment interaction question — for any modifier-gene candidate locus, does smoke exposure amplify the effect on lung-function trajectory? Specifically including the loci already implicated in the modern CF-modifier GWAS — MUC4 / MUC20, AGTR2 / SLC6A14, EHF / APIP, CHP2 — that we've catalogued in earlier episodes.

JORDAN Formal version?

ALEX "Among CFGP whole-genome-sequenced CF patients with linked CFFPR longitudinal lung-function data and CFFPR-recorded secondhand smoke exposure history, do common-variant lead SNPs at published CF lung-disease modifier loci — MUC4 / MUC20 at chr3q29, AGTR2 / SLC6A14 at chrXq22-q23, EHF / APIP at chr11p13, and CHP2 at chr16 — show statistical interaction with secondhand smoke exposure on cross-sectional and longitudinal FEV1, using a mixed-effects model that handles repeated measures and accounts for family relatedness from the twin-and-sibling substrate?"

JORDAN What does Collaco close?

ALEX It establishes that the CF Twin and Sibling substrate is statistically powered to detect gene-environment interactions of the size we'd want to find — a 20-percentile-point FEV1 difference in a TGFβ1-CC homozygote subgroup is not subtle. It also establishes that secondhand smoke exposure can be measured well enough from CFFPR questionnaires to detect a real signal. The lung-function endpoint mechanics are documented.

JORDAN What does it leave open?

ALEX Several things. Whether the candidate-gene-era TGFβ1 effects replicate in the modern WGS-era CFGP cohort with a much larger sample size and proper multiple-testing control. Whether secondhand smoke exposure measurement quality in CFFPR has changed since 2008 — what variables CFFPR captures now, how they're harmonized over time, and whether smoking-prevalence shifts in the U.S. have left enough exposed CF patients to power modern gene-environment tests. Whether the interaction signal generalizes beyond TGFβ1 to other published CF lung-modifier loci — the candidate-gene-era literature suggested it might, but population-scale tests have not been published.

JORDAN Verify first?

ALEX We'd need to check several things. Whether CFFPR has a clean, year-stratified, longitudinal secondhand-smoke-exposure variable that can be linked to the CFGP genotype substrate. Whether the same CF Twin and Sibling Study families are represented in both the 2008 substrate and the modern CFGP — and if so, whether the within-family-design power that helped Collaco can be preserved or has been diluted. Whether modern CF-modifier-GWAS lead SNPs at MUC4 / MUC20, AGTR2 / SLC6A14, EHF / APIP, and CHP2 are even called consistently across CFGP samples and whether genotype dosage at those SNPs is reliable enough for gene-by-smoke interaction tests. Whether the TGFβ1 −509 and codon-10 candidate variants are still available in the modern CFGP variant call set and whether the homozygote-by-homozygote subgroup sizes survive the modern cohort filtering. Whether modulator-era patients — those on ivacaftor or Trikafta — should be analyzed separately, since the modulators substantially change the airway biology that smoke exposure interacts with. None of these are knowable from the 2008 paper, and the modulator question in particular only became a serious confounder after 2012 / 2019 and is not addressable by anything in Collaco. They're "what we'd need to check," not "what we know."
