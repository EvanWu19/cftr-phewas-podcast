Blurb

Van Hout, Tachmazidou, and the Regeneron-GSK Genetics Center collaboration published in Nature in October 2020 the announcement paper for the first batch of UK Biobank whole-exome sequencing — 49,960 participants, sequenced by Regeneron under contract to UKBB and GSK. The paper is the foundational resource for biobank-scale rare-variant gene-burden testing: 4.75 million coding variants observed within targeted regions, of which 198,269 are predicted loss-of-function (99.6 percent at MAF below one percent), with 97 percent of all 19,396 surveyed genes having at least one carrier of an LoF variant in this cohort and 69 percent having at least ten — the threshold gene-burden tests need to be properly powered. The paper demonstrates the resource by burden-testing 1,730 phenotypes, surfacing new associations including PIEZO1 with varicose veins, COL6A1 with corneal biomechanical resistance, MEPE with bone density, and IQGAP2 plus GMPR with red-blood-cell traits; documents that approximately two percent of unselected middle-aged Britons carry a clinically actionable pathogenic variant; and shows that BRCA1 and BRCA2 cancer penetrance in an unselected biobank cohort runs lower than the textbook clinical-genetics estimates because the latter were generated from severely-affected ascertained families. The proposed direction this episode suggests, conditional on later inspection of CFGP variant calls and the modulator-era CFFPR phenotype linkage, is an external rare-variant gene-burden replication on UKBB exomes for the candidate modifier-gene list locked from any CFGP-internal scan, against a pre-registered CF-adjacent ICD10 phenotype list (bronchiectasis, idiopathic pancreatitis, malabsorption, congenital absence of the vas deferens, early-onset airway disease). The open question is whether CF-cohort modifier signal — detected against a background where every individual already has full CFTR-driven disease — actually transfers to a CFTR-functional general population, since the modifier effects might be silent without a CFTR-disease background.

Plain-English glossary

  • Allele — one of the two copies of a gene a person carries, one from each parent.
  • Allele frequency / minor allele frequency (MAF) — the fraction of chromosomes in a reference population that carry a given variant; minor allele frequency refers to the rarer of the two letters at that position.
  • American College of Medical Genetics (ACMG) — the U.S. clinical-genetics professional body that publishes the standard scoring framework for classifying variants as pathogenic / likely pathogenic / uncertain significance / likely benign / benign, plus the tier-three list of genes considered clinically actionable when an unexpected pathogenic variant is found.
  • Burden test — a statistical method that aggregates carriers of any rare loss-of-function variant across an entire gene into a single combined carrier count, then asks whether that aggregated group has different disease rates than non-carriers; lets you find a gene that matters even when each individual variant is too rare to test alone.
  • Exome — the protein-coding portion of the genome, roughly one to two percent of total DNA, where most disease-causing variants are concentrated.
  • Hospital Episode Statistics (HES) — UK NHS-administered records of inpatient hospital episodes, with diagnoses encoded as ICD10 billing codes; the standard phenotype source for UKBB association studies.
  • ICD10 — International Classification of Diseases version 10; the World Health Organization's standard hospital diagnosis-coding system used in NHS records.
  • Imputation — statistical inference of untyped genetic variants from a reference haplotype panel; works well for common variants but increasingly poorly for rare variants below one-percent allele frequency.
  • Loss-of-function variant (LoF / LOF) — a variant predicted to disable the protein product of a gene; includes premature stop codons, frameshift insertions or deletions, splice-site disruptions, and start-codon losses.
  • LOFTEE — a software annotation tool for filtering predicted-LoF variants down to a high-confidence subset by removing technical artifacts and variants in low-confidence positions.
  • Pathogenic variant — a variant judged by clinical geneticists to cause disease using ACMG criteria.
  • Purifying selection — the evolutionary process by which damaging variants get removed from the population over generations because their carriers leave fewer descendants; explains why nearly all LoF variants are rare.
  • Regeneron Genetics Center (RGC) — the in-house human-genetics arm of Regeneron Pharmaceuticals; performed the actual sequencing and variant-calling pipelines for this paper.
  • UK Biobank (UKB / UKBB) — half-million-person British general-population research cohort recruited 2006-2010, with linked NHS medical records and DNA.
  • Variant — modern, neutral term for a position in DNA where a person's letter differs from the reference genome sequence; replaces the older word "mutation."
  • Whole-exome sequencing (WES) — laboratory technique that selectively reads out only the protein-coding portion of the genome; contrasts with whole-genome sequencing which reads everything.

Methods to lift

  • IDT xGen Exome Research Panel v1.0 + supplemental probes — the exome-capture chemistry used by Regeneron for the UKBB WES sequencing pipeline; supplemental probes were added to boost coverage at underperforming loci. 39 megabases of target territory across 19,396 genes.
  • Average-coverage and per-site-coverage QC — average coverage above twenty-fold at 94.6 percent of targeted bases, with per-individual variant-missingness and per-variant variant-missingness both held below ten percent and Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium P-value above 10⁻¹⁵ as variant-level QC thresholds.
  • LOFTEE-based LoF filtering — high-confidence LoF subset (169,881 of the 198,269 raw LoF variants pass LOFTEE filtering) is the analysis-grade variant set; raw LoF counts include technical artifacts that LOFTEE removes.
  • Per-gene carrier-count threshold for burden-test power — the rule-of-thumb threshold cited here is ten or more carriers per gene before per-gene burden testing is properly powered; 69 percent of the 19,396 surveyed genes meet this threshold at n=49,960.
  • WES-versus-imputed-sequence comparison as quality benchmark — concordance R² between WES and imputed sequence ranged from 32.2 percent at MAF below 0.01 percent to 95.2 percent at MAF above one percent; this calibration is reusable for any future paper claiming new rare-variant signal.
  • Asthma-enriched ascertainment for the 50K WES batch — sixteen point five percent of the WES cohort had asthma diagnosis codes versus thirteen point five percent in the full UKBB; flag-and-track for any future analysis using this WES subset against the full UKBB cohort as comparator.
  • Medical-actionability survey — apply the ACMG tier-three actionable-gene list (in 2020 this was a 59-gene list, now expanded to roughly 73) as a flag-and-count audit on any new biobank-scale WES dataset; the two-percent population baseline observed here is the comparator.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFGP WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage:

An external rare-variant gene-burden replication analysis on UKBB whole-exome sequencing data (the now-published 454,787-exome release covered by Backman 2021 supersedes the 49,960-exome batch covered here for power), restricted to the candidate-modifier-gene list locked from a CFGP-internal discovery scan (SLC26A9, EHF, APIP, CHP2, CEBPB, PRSS1, the chr20q13.2 region candidates, plus any newly-discovered loci), tested against a pre-registered phenotype list constructed from biobank-grade ICD10 codes for CF-adjacent endpoints (bronchiectasis, idiopathic pancreatitis, malabsorption, congenital bilateral absence of the vas deferens, early-onset airway disease, and pancreatic exocrine insufficiency), with the variant-class definition (LoF only versus LoF-plus-damaging-missense) and the MAF cutoff (1 percent versus 0.1 percent versus 0.01 percent) pre-registered in a publicly-deposited analysis plan before any test is run.

What to verify first

  • Which candidate-modifier genes from any CFGP-internal scan have at least ten LoF carriers in the UKBB 454,787-exome release (the carrier-count threshold for properly-powered per-gene burden testing).
  • The case-count for each pre-registered CF-adjacent phenotype in the UKBB ICD10 catalog — congenital bilateral absence of the vas deferens is the rarest and may be sub-powered.
  • Whether the candidate-gene panel size keeps the multiple-testing burden manageable (a panel of ten to twenty genes against five to ten phenotypes is roughly 50-200 tests; a panel of two hundred genes is a different multiple-testing regime entirely).
  • Whether ancestry stratification is needed given the UKBB 93.6 percent European versus CFGP somewhat more admixed composition documented in Li 2011 — the modifier-effect direction and magnitude should be reported per-ancestry-stratum, not pooled.
  • Whether any prior consortium or third-party publication has already done a CF-modifier-gene-centric UKBB external replication on overlapping candidate genes; if so, the design here builds on that rather than duplicates.
  • Whether the CFTR pathway is sufficiently functional in non-CF UKBB participants that modifier-gene effects are detectable at all in the absence of a CFTR-disease background — this is the substantive transferability question, not just an ancestry-and-power question.
  • Whether the asthma-enrichment ascertainment in the original 50K WES batch carries through to the 454K release (mostly resolved as the larger cohort dilutes the enrichment, but worth confirming).

The UK Biobank, which we will abbreviate UKBB or just UKB, is a half-million-person British research cohort that recruited middle-aged adults between roughly 2006 and 2010, collected blood samples, and linked everyone's genetic data to their National Health Service medical records, so researchers can ask which genetic variants associate with which diseases. Whole-exome sequencing, abbreviated WES, is a laboratory technique that selectively reads out only the protein-coding portion of the genome — the exome — which is roughly one to two percent of the total DNA but contains the parts most likely to disrupt protein function when mutated. A variant is the modern, neutral term for a position in DNA where one person's letter differs from the reference genome. An allele is one of the two copies of a gene a person carries; minor allele frequency, abbreviated MAF, is the fraction of chromosomes in a reference population that carry the rarer of the two letters at a given position. A loss-of-function variant, abbreviated LoF or LOF, is a variant predicted to disable the protein product of a gene — examples include premature stop codons, frameshift insertions or deletions, splice-site disruptions, and start-codon losses. A pathogenic variant is one judged by clinical geneticists to cause disease using the American College of Medical Genetics scoring framework. A burden test is a statistical method that aggregates the carriers of any rare LoF variant across an entire gene into a single combined count and asks whether that aggregated carrier group has different disease rates than non-carriers — it lets you find a gene that matters even when each individual variant within the gene is too rare to test on its own. ICD10 is the International Classification of Diseases version 10 — the standard hospital billing-code system used in UK National Health Service records to encode diagnoses. The Regeneron Genetics Center is the in-house human-genetics arm of Regeneron Pharmaceuticals; they did the actual sequencing for this paper under contract to UKBB and the GSK collaboration. JORDAN Okay. What was the problem this paper was solving — what was unknown or impossible before it was published?

ALEX Two things. First, the UKBB had been a major resource since around 2018 when its array-based genotype data and imputed sequence data went out to the world. Array genotyping reads roughly 800,000 pre-selected common variant positions per person, then statistical imputation extends that to several tens of millions of variants by inferring untyped sites from haplotype reference panels — this works very well for common variants present at MAF above one percent, but it works increasingly poorly for rarer variants, and it works essentially not at all for variants below MAF 0.1 percent. The variants most likely to be functionally important and clinically interpretable — the loss-of-function variants in single genes that produce Mendelian-style large effects — are overwhelmingly rare, so the imputation-based UKBB data was missing them by construction. To get those, you have to sequence directly. The second thing was that even when individual research groups had run WES on smaller cohorts, the WES studies were often disease-enriched — recruited because the participants had a specific disease being studied — so the rare-variant catalog they produced was not generalizable to the population. UKBB sequenced its WES participants from a cohort recruited essentially at random from middle-aged Britons, with one mild enrichment we'll come back to, so the catalog of rare variation produced here would describe what coding rare variation actually looks like in a general adult population at unprecedented scale. Before this paper, the field had nothing this big.

JORDAN How did they actually do it? Mechanically.

ALEX They selected 50,000 participants out of the 502,543-person UKBB cohort. Selection was not strictly random — they prioritized people with whole-body magnetic resonance imaging data from the UKBB Imaging Study, people with linked hospital episode statistics, people with linked primary healthcare records, and they intentionally enriched for one disease, asthma — all participants whose hospital records contained ICD10 codes J45 or J46 — to ensure the cohort had enough asthma cases for downstream association testing. That last decision is worth flagging. Forty samples were dropped during sequencing for quality-control failures or participant withdrawal, leaving 49,960 individuals. The exome capture used the IDT xGen Exome Research Panel version 1.0 supplemented with extra probes to boost coverage at underperforming loci. The capture target was 39 megabases of DNA spanning 19,396 genes. Average coverage across targeted bases exceeded twenty-fold at 94.6 percent of sites — meaning each base was read at least twenty independent times by the sequencer in nearly all individuals. They observed 4,752,777 variants within the targeted regions, of which 1.2 million were synonymous, 2.5 million were missense, and 198,269 were predicted loss-of-function variants affecting at least one transcript of the gene. ALEX should push back on a design choice here. The ascertainment enrichment for asthma — sixteen and a half percent of the WES cohort had asthma diagnosis codes versus thirteen point five percent in the full UKBB — biases any future analysis that uses this WES subset against the full UKBB as a comparator. It does not bias rare-variant catalog generation, which is the headline contribution, but anyone who wants to use this WES batch as the discovery cohort for any non-asthma trait needs to remember that the cohort is mildly selected.

JORDAN What did they find — what is the headline?

ALEX Several headline numbers. First, the rare-variant catalog. Of those 198,269 LoF variants, 99.6 percent had MAF below one percent — which is the expected pattern from purifying selection: damaging variants get removed from populations before they become common. The median individual carried 120 LoF variants in their genome — meaning every healthy person walking around carries about 120 disabled gene-copies that are tolerated either because the other allele compensates or because the gene is not essential. Almost all genes — over 97 percent of the 19,396 surveyed genes — had at least one carrier of an LoF variant in this cohort, and over 69 percent of genes had at least ten carriers. That is the property burden tests need: you need ten or more carriers per gene before per-gene burden testing is properly powered. Second, the comparison against imputed sequence data. WES detected 198,116 LoF variants in 46,911 European-ancestry participants while imputed sequence data on the same individuals detected only 13,561 LoF variants — about a fourteen-fold improvement, exactly because imputation cannot reach the rare-variant tail. Third, the burden-testing demonstration. They ran rare-variant gene-burden tests on 1,730 phenotypes and found new signals: PIEZO1 LoF carriers had elevated rates of varicose veins, COL6A1 LoF carriers had altered corneal biomechanical resistance, MEPE LoF carriers had altered bone mineral density, and IQGAP2 plus GMPR LoF carriers had altered red-blood-cell traits. Fourth, the medical-actionability survey. About two percent of this 50,000-person general-population sample carried a pathogenic variant in one of the American College of Medical Genetics' tier-three actionable-variant gene list — meaning two out of every hundred middle-aged Britons carry a clinically meaningful variant their doctor doesn't yet know about. They also examined cancer penetrance in BRCA1 and BRCA2 carriers — variants traditionally associated with very high cancer risk turned out to confer somewhat lower-than-textbook penetrance in this unselected general-population cohort, because clinical genetics studies historically over-sampled severely affected families. Did this paper answer the question it set out to answer? Yes, fully. It established that biobank-scale WES is feasible, that the resulting rare-variant catalog supports gene-burden testing, and that the burden-testing yields biologically interpretable new associations.
