How did this episode land for you?

Blurb

Hess et al. (Mol Psychiatry 2021) proposed and tested the first "polygenic resilience score" for a complex disease. In schizophrenia, where a polygenic risk score (PRS) explains roughly 6–7% of liability, they asked the inverse question: among unaffected controls who carry the heaviest polygenic risk burden (top 10% PRS), what additional, orthogonal common-variant signal distinguishes them from equal-risk cases? Using the 51-study PGC-2 meta-analysis, they identified 3,786 high-risk resilient controls vs 18,619 risk-matched cases, ran a GWAS comparing the two, and built a polygenic resilience score from 80,822 LD-independent SNPs (with risk-associated SNPs and LD partners explicitly excluded for orthogonality). The score replicated significantly in three independent samples (MGS, iPSYCH, deCODE) at modest effect (OR 1.12, P=0.0044 at the best p-value bin), explaining only ~0.07% of liability — vastly less than the 46.9% in (overfit) discovery. Proposed direction for the CFTR PheWAS: derive an analogous CF lung-severity resilience score within the CFF WGS cohort. The framework is portable; the open question is whether n≈5,000 patients (versus Hess's ~22,000 risk-matched subjects in discovery) provides any power at all at the high-risk tail.

Plain-English glossary

  • AD (autosomal dominant): One bad copy of a gene is enough to cause disease.
  • AR (autosomal recessive): Both copies must be bad to cause disease. CF is AR.
  • CFFPR: Cystic Fibrosis Foundation Patient Registry — the US registry that tracks essentially every diagnosed CF patient longitudinally.
  • CFF WGS cohort: ~5,000 CF patients with whole-genome sequencing data held by the CF Foundation Therapy department.
  • CFTR: Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane conductance Regulator — the gene mutated in CF; a chloride/bicarbonate channel.
  • deCODE Genetics: Icelandic population-genetics company holding a large population-based biobank, used as Hess replication set #3.
  • GRAS: Gottingen Research Association for Schizophrenia — one of the PGC-2 contributing studies.
  • GWAS: Genome-Wide Association Study — testing millions of SNPs for frequency differences between cases and controls.
  • iPSYCH: Lundbeck Foundation Initiative for Integrative Psychiatric Research — Danish registry-linked psychiatric genetics consortium; Hess replication set #2.
  • LD (linkage disequilibrium): Statistical correlation between nearby SNPs because they tend to be co-inherited. Used here to enforce that resilience SNPs are orthogonal to known risk SNPs.
  • MAF (minor allele frequency): Fraction of the population carrying the less-common allele at a SNP.
  • MGS: Molecular Genetics of Schizophrenia — US/Australian SZ case-control study; Hess replication set #1.
  • Mendelian disease: Disease caused by mutation in a single gene with a clean inheritance pattern (e.g., CF, sickle cell). Contrasts with polygenic.
  • OMIM: Online Mendelian Inheritance in Man — curated catalog of Mendelian gene-disease links.
  • Orthogonality (in this paper): Resilience SNPs must lie outside risk loci and outside LD partners of risk loci, so the resilience signal is not just inverse-risk.
  • PGC (Psychiatric Genomics Consortium): International collaboration that pools psychiatric GWAS data; PGC-2 is the wave-2 SZ meta-analysis.
  • PheWAS: Phenome-Wide Association Study — testing one genetic variant or score against many phenotypes; the inverse layout of GWAS.
  • Plink: Open-source software for GWAS-scale variant analysis.
  • PRS (polygenic risk score): A single number per person summing genome-wide risk-allele dosages weighted by effect sizes from a discovery GWAS.
  • Resilient control: In Hess's design, an unaffected person whose PRS sits in the top 10% — high genetic risk burden but never developed the disease.
  • SNP: Single Nucleotide Polymorphism — a single-base position in the genome where the population carries multiple letters.
  • SZ: Schizophrenia.
  • WGS: Whole-Genome Sequencing — reads (in principle) every base of the genome.

Methods to lift

  • Risk-matched case-control design — identify the top-decile PRS controls, match SZ cases at the same PRS window, run a fresh GWAS comparing the two groups.
  • Orthogonality enforcement via LD exclusion — drop any SNP associated with the disease at P<0.05 or in LD (R²>0.2 within 1Mb) with such SNPs, so resilience signal cannot be inverse-risk.
  • Plink v1.9 — single-SNP logistic regression with PC adjustment for population stratification.
  • Inverse-variance fixed-effect meta-analysis — pool per-study summary statistics into a single meta GWAS (R metafor package).
  • Polygenic resilience score formulation — weighted allele-count sum across ten p-value bins (0.0001 → 1.0); Nagelkerke pseudo-R² for variance-explained estimation.
  • Discovery-vs-replication overfitting check — variance explained in independent samples drops to liability-scale h² of ~0.07%; report both numbers to flag overfitting honestly.

Proposed direction (conditional)

Conditional on inspection of CFF WGS variant calls and registry phenotype linkage:

Derive a polygenic resilience score for CF lung disease severity, defined as the additive contribution to mild-lung-phenotype status from common variants orthogonal to known CF lung-severity risk loci, using a Hess-style risk-matched case-control GWAS within the CFF Patient Registry's WGS cohort. Risk loci to define orthogonality against: chr5p13 (SLC9A3 region), chr11p13 (EHF/APIP), chr20q13.2 (Wright 2011), plus any additional Corvol 2015 / Zhou 2023 modifier loci that survive replication. Lung-severity phenotype: KNoRMA or equivalent CFFPR-derived severity quantile.

What to verify first

  • Whether the CFF WGS variant call set has passed QC for common-variant polygenic scoring (imputation INFO ≥0.9, MAF ≥5%, principal-component-derived ancestry assignments).
  • Whether a CF lung-severity PRS built from prior modifier-GWAS summary stats (Wright 2011, Corvol 2015, Zhou 2023) spreads the cohort enough at the upper tail to define a meaningful high-risk subgroup.
  • Whether the CFF Patient Registry lung-function phenotype can be dichotomized into "affected severe" vs "unexpectedly mild" in a way that matches the case-control structure Hess assumes — and whether KNoRMA or an alternative survivor-bias-adjusted phenotype is the right severity axis.
  • Whether the cohort is sufficiently ancestry-homogenous (or, conversely, sufficiently diverse) for principal-component-based stratification correction at the scale Hess applied.
  • Whether sample size at the high-risk PRS tail of an n≈5,000 cohort allows even a sensitivity analysis of the 90th-percentile cutoff that Hess flagged as arbitrary.
  • Whether the 22,000-subject discovery → 0.07% liability-scale h² ratio in Hess implies that a 5,000-subject CF cohort would deliver any detectable resilience signal at all — i.e., a power calculation should precede any analytic commitment.

ALEX Welcome back to the CFTR PheWAS reading podcast. Today's first paper is Hess, Tylee, Mattheisen, and colleagues with the Schizophrenia Working Group of the Psychiatric Genomics Consortium, the iPSYCH Danish initiative, and deCODE Genetics, published in Molecular Psychiatry in 2021, titled "A polygenic resilience score moderates the genetic risk for schizophrenia." The reason we're reading it: the entire literature on CFTR modifiers asks "what makes severely affected patients severely affected?" — but this paper asks the inverse question. Given the same heavy genetic burden, who stays unaffected, and can we measure that protection with a single score? If the answer is yes for schizophrenia, the framework is directly portable to "given the same severe CFTR genotype, who has unexpectedly mild lung disease?"

JORDAN Hold on. Schizophrenia is not cystic fibrosis. Why are we starting with a psychiatry paper?

ALEX Because the *method* travels. The paper is a proof-of-concept for a new kind of polygenic score that, if it works at all, applies to any disease where genetic risk varies a lot from person to person and where some people defy that risk. CF fits that pattern: even Phe508del homozygotes — patients with the most common severe CFTR genotype — have wildly different lung function, age at first Pseudomonas infection, and survival. So the question of the day is: how does this method work, and would Runyu be able to port it to the CFF registry?

JORDAN Okay. Before we go deeper, I need a primer. You're about to say a lot of words I don't fully know. Start with the basics.

ALEX Right. Let me lay them down in order. A SNP is a single-nucleotide polymorphism — one position in the DNA where the population carries two different letters, say A versus G. A GWAS is a genome-wide association study — you measure millions of SNPs in thousands of cases and controls and ask which SNPs differ in frequency between the two groups. The result is a long list of SNPs each with a P-value and an effect size, usually an odds ratio. A PRS is a polygenic risk score — you take the GWAS results, weight each SNP by its effect size, and sum across SNPs to give each person a single number representing their total inherited risk burden. Higher PRS, more risk.

JORDAN And "schizophrenia," in this paper's terms?

ALEX SZ, schizophrenia, is a chronic mental disorder affecting roughly one percent of the population, highly heritable — around 80 percent of risk is genetic. The PGC, the Psychiatric Genomics Consortium, is the big international collaboration that has done the foundational SZ GWASes, and their PGC-2 wave-2 dataset is the substrate for this paper. The PRS derived from PGC-2 explains about 6 to 7 percent of liability for SZ — meaningful, but well short of the 80 percent total heritability. The gap between what the PRS explains and what twin studies say should be there is called the "missing heritability" problem.

JORDAN And "resilience" — that's the new word.

ALEX Resilience here is not the psychology version. It is not coping, social support, or having a sense of purpose. The authors define genetic resilience operationally: heritable variants that reduce the *penetrance* of risk variants. Penetrance is the probability that someone carrying a risk genotype actually expresses the disease. So resilience alleles let high-risk people stay unaffected. Crucially — and this is one of two key postulates — resilience is not the same as protection. A "protective" allele is the alternate allele at a known risk SNP, the one that controls carry more often than cases. A resilience allele lives somewhere else in the genome and ameliorates the effect of risk loci without being at a risk locus itself. That orthogonality assumption is something we should remember when we get to results.

JORDAN So the question they're asking is: among people who carry a heavy load of SZ risk alleles, why are some of them not sick — and is that buffering itself heritable and scoreable?

ALEX Exactly. The motivation goes back to a 2016 paper by Chen and colleagues called the Resilience Project, which screened nearly 600,000 genomes to find people who carry recessive disease-causing mutations but never expressed the disease. That worked for rare Mendelian disorders. Hess is asking: can we do the analogous thing for *polygenic* disorders, where risk is spread across thousands of small-effect variants instead of concentrated in a single locus?

JORDAN That feels like a CF-relevant question stated in psychiatry language.

ALEX Hold that thought. Let's do methods.

ALEX The strategy has three phases. Phase one: take the PGC-2 SZ meta-analysis — 51 separate studies, roughly 45,000 cases plus an order of magnitude more controls, mostly European ancestry — and use it to compute a PRS for every subject in every study. Phase two: identify high-risk controls. They set an arbitrary threshold at the 90th percentile of PRS *within controls*, and called anyone above it a "resilient control" — high genetic risk but somehow no diagnosis. They then matched those resilient controls against SZ cases whose PRS fell in the same window. This yielded about 3,786 high-risk resilient controls versus 18,619 equally-high-risk cases. Phase three: run a GWAS comparing those two groups. The variants whose frequencies differ between the high-risk-resilient and high-risk-affected groups are the candidate resilience alleles.

JORDAN What's stopping that GWAS from just re-discovering risk variants?

ALEX Good catch — that's the orthogonality enforcement step. Any variant that was associated with SZ risk at P less than 0.05 in the original meta-analysis, *or* in linkage disequilibrium — meaning statistically correlated through inheritance — with such a variant at R-squared above 0.2 within one megabase, gets discarded from the resilience GWAS. They also strip out the major histocompatibility complex region and the chromosome 8 inversion, both of which have unusual LD that breaks standard polygenic methods. The remaining SNPs go into the resilience-score formula: count protective alleles, weight by the log odds ratio from the resilience GWAS, sum across SNPs at various P-value cutoffs from 0.0001 up to 1.0.

JORDAN One pushback before results — they set the resilient threshold at the 90th percentile arbitrarily. What happens if you move it?

ALEX They flag this as a design choice in the supplementary tables and don't extensively explore sensitivity. That's a real limitation. The threshold defines who counts as resilient at all, so anyone trying to port this method needs to decide whether their cutoff is principled or just convenient.

ALEX Now results. In the discovery sample, resilience scores were significantly associated with resilience status — a p-value smaller than ten to the negative three hundredth, which is essentially the limit of the floating-point arithmetic. Up to 46.9 percent of the variance in resilience status was explained by the score in discovery. That number drops dramatically — almost all of it overfitting — when applied to three independent replication samples: the Molecular Genetics of Schizophrenia study, the Danish iPSYCH study, and the Icelandic deCODE sample. Combined, those samples gave 8,119 high-risk controls and 1,437 high-risk cases. In meta-analysis the resilience score still replicated significantly, with the strongest effect at the P-less-than-0.3 SNP bin — odds ratio 1.12 per standardized unit increase in resilience score, two-tailed P equals 0.0044. The variance explained in replication averaged just 0.042 percent on the observed scale, or 0.07 percent on the liability scale.

JORDAN That feels like a long way from 46.9 percent.

ALEX It is, and that's the most important number in the paper. The discovery sample massively overfit, and the true effect in fresh data is small but reproducible. None of the individual SNPs reached genome-wide significance — the conventional threshold of P less than 5 times ten to the negative eighth — but seven loci reached "suggestive" association at P less than ten to the negative fifth. The authors do a power analysis showing that roughly 2,030 more high-risk controls would be needed to push the weakest of those suggestive SNPs to genome-wide significance. Did they answer the question they posed? Partially. They demonstrated the framework works — resilience scores predict caseness independent of risk scores, with a significant interaction term — but they did not deliver a polished, deployable resilience score. They delivered a proof of concept.
